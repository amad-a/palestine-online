Palestinian refugees and struggling with hopelessness

By Mahmoud al-Khatib

Less than 24 hours after statements made by U.S. President Clinton indicating that he wished to see the Palestinian refugees free to live any place they want, the U.S. State Department rushed to withdraw these statements and explain them. The Palestinian Authority officials had snatched these statements considering them as a change in the U.S. Administration’s policy and that Mr. Clinton was supporting the right of Palestinian refugees to return to their homeland. But the PA officials were disappointed and frustrated when James Foley, the spokesman for the U.S. State Department, announced that it was only a slip of the President’s tongue (who is famous for having all kinds of slips)!

Those who watched the press conference between President Husni Mubarak of Egypt and the U.S. President saw how Mr. Clinton was embarrassed and surprised by an Egyptian journalist’s question that made a comparison between the U.S. stand regarding the repatriation of the Kosovo refugees and its stand towards the return of the Palestinian refugees to their homeland occupied by the Jewish State in 1948. I think Mr. Clinton was embarrassed because he was not aware of the Palestinian refugees’ ordeal. This made him contradict the official policy of the U.S. Administration. For, if Mr. Clinton knew that the Palestinian refugee issue was listed on the agenda of the final status talks between the PLO and Israel, he would have said so to escape from any possible embarrassment.

Mr. Foley, under pressure by the Jewish and Zionist lobbies in the U.S. Administration, rushed to apologize and to drop statements made by his boss. He said that the U.S. would not interfere in an issue that is supposed to be discussed by the parties concerned.

The Zionists, of course, would not let such statements by Mr. Clinton be snatched up by the international news agencies because the right of return for the Palestinian refugees is a highly sensitive issue. There is almost unanimous agreement among the Israelis in rejecting the right of return to Palestinian refugees. The Zionists do not give a glimmer of hope that these refugees could return home although United Nations resolutions state that they should be allowed to return, and that those Palestinians who opt otherwise should be compensated.

The Israelis ask where and how can more than 4 million Palestinian refugees be absorbed into the Jewish State. This is an unfair question because the Palestinian refugees are the real owners of the land on which the Zionists are now living. The fair solution, in my opinion, is that the Zionists go back to where they came from: be it Russia, Europe, the United States or the Arab countries. Until this end can be achieved, the Palestinian refugees have enough space to live in their original homeland. According to recent surveys, 78% of the Israelis live in only 15% of what is now called Israel, while the other 22% live in the remaining 85% of that area. The absolute majority of the Jews live in a dozen towns or so. Only 2.7% of the Israeli Jews exploit Palestinian land. Therefore, only 154,000 rural Jews control 17,325 square kilometres, which is the home and heritage of more than 4 million refugees.

The Israelis have never asked themselves why more than two thirds of the Palestinian population are refugees and homeless.

The Zionist regime does not only reject the return of Palestine refugees to their homeland, but it also strongly rejects the return of the displaced Palestinians who were forced to leave their homes in the West Bank following the Arab-Israeli war in June 1967. There are some 800,000 displaced persons who left the West Bank and migrated to the East Bank. The West Bank at the time was a part of Jordan and that’s why the Palestinians who left the West Bank in 1967 were classified as displaced persons. But the Zionist regime insists that there were only 200,000 displaced Palestinians according to the UN figures. Yes, this number was somehow correct, but this Palestinian population grew naturally over the last three decades. Their children who were born in exile have the right to return with their parents to their homeland. Despite that, the Israelis, during the quadrilateral committee meetings on the displaced persons, suggested that they would allow only a few thousand of the 200,000 displaced Palestinians (without their children) to go back to the West Bank every year. This means that the last displaced Palestinian maybe be allowed to return home after 50 or 60 years!

The question of the Palestinian refugees is one of the other complicated and serious issues that were adjourned to the final status negotiations: the status of Jerusalem, the Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza, the borders and the water. It is not fair to let these issues be negotiated between the tyrant Zionist regime and the insufficient and corrupt Palestinian Authority. There is no balance of power between the two parties and hence the outcome of the negotiations on these serious issues would be disastrous for the Palestinian people in general. The PA is not the honest Palestinian leadership that can defend the legitimate rights of the Palestinian refugees and the Palestinians in general. This leadership has abandoned the refugee cause from the beginning when it, in 1993, recognized the Zionist usurpation and occupation regime before it could get any considerable “relinquishment” from the Zionists.

In the coming future, the Palestinian refugees can live any place they want, according to Mr. Clinton’s slip of the tongue, except living in their own homes and land on which the Israelis are now building their State.

Murder, expulsion -- and silence *

By Abdel-Azim Hammad

After the Wye River Memorandum was signed, I asked the US ambassador about its possible long-term political implications. Does an agreement signed by a Likud premier, which promises to hand over territory to the Palestinian Authority, mean the renunciation by the Likud of its policy of "transfer" (emptying the West Bank of its Palestinian population to remove the final hurdle to the establishment of Greater Israel)? Surprised by the question, the ambassador assured me that he had never heard anyone in the Likud -- or, for that matter, in Israel -- mention a plan for the "transfer" of Palestinians, except the extremist Rabbi Meir Kahan (assassinated some years ago in New York) and his followers, who are isolated from mainstream political life in Israel.
I was just as surprised by his answer. True, none of the Zionist and Israeli leaders has ever mentioned plans to "transfer" the Palestinian population or to purge it through ethnic cleansing. But their actions have been far more eloquent than any statements could ever have been.
The argument regarding Zionist and, later, Israeli ethnic cleansing practices had thus moved from the realm of academia to formal diplomatic confrontation within the context of the peace process. Israel immediately refused any grounds for comparison between the Palestinian refugees and the Kosovar Albanians, anxious to avoid any suggestion that the same approach should be adopted regarding the Palestinian refugees. Israel has always maintained that the Palestinian refugees left their country voluntarily, and therefore relinquished their right to repatriation. As "proof", it trundled out the moth-eaten myth of Palestine as a land without a people. In a statement published in the Sunday Times on 15 May 1969, Golda Meir denied the very existence of the Palestinian people, concluding that Israel could not have expelled or expropriated them, since "they do not exist!"
Now the wall of silence surrounding the rights of the Palestinian refugees and the ethnic cleansing campaigns carried out by all Israeli governments came tumbling down. We need to collect all the information we can about the ethnic cleansing of the Palestinians. Any mention of the practice inside and outside Israel will help Arab diplomats counter the American and Israeli propaganda war and win over public opinion, especially in the West. Israeli propaganda must be revealed for what it is, once and for all. Israel claims that the Palestinians left their villages voluntarily or were told to do so by the Arab leaders. But when global attention questions these claims, Israel claims that the Palestinians never existed in the first place.
Israel Shahak has given a detailed account of Israel's repressive practices, writing that "Israeli bulldozers crushed villages, fields and cemeteries". Benny Morris, one of the most prominent Israeli new historians, confirms that, out of a total of 475 villages inside Israel, 418 were wiped out for good. The Arabs have long known it, and the new historians admit it willingly: Palestine was never "a land without a people for a people without a land". Its people did not leave it willingly: they were massacred and expelled. Morris, too, writes that, as early as the '30s, members of the Jewish Agency, among them Ben Gurion, planned to establish a Jewish state with the smallest Arab minority possible. According to the same historian, Ben Gurion, who was particularly deft at political manoeuvres, struggled relentlessly to abrogate a clause in the Liberal Party programme (which was not his party) demanding ethnic cleansing. At the same time, he showed no compunction in carrying out the same policy. While his followers understood his message, there were no explicit statements to enrage world opinion, for Israel needed the support of the UN, which was about to confer legitimacy upon the establishment of Israel.
On 2 November 1947, only days before the General Assembly adopted the resolution for the partition of Palestine, Ben Gurion warned his followers that the major problem facing the Jewish state was its population. The Jews, he said, constitute 60 per cent of the area to be assigned by the United Nations to Israel. There would be no guarantee, therefore, for the establishment of a Jewish regime, or a strong, stable Jewish government. He urged them to adopt a new position, revise all their ideas and think in terms of statehood. This meant the displacement, transfer and ethnic cleansing of the Palestinian population.
The Jewish Agency immediately set up a committee for this purpose. Its records have allowed Morris to demonstrate that some 460,000 Palestinians were evicted by the threat or actual use of force. The massacres in Deir Yassin and other villages are instances of a consistent policy, with a clear goal: to decimate the Palestinian population. The Israeli claim that Ben Gurion's statement meant encouraging new waves of Jewish immigrants to Israel is untenable. Immigration to Israel in the '30s was already an ongoing process, which had begun at the end of the 19th century. No revision of policies was required in this respect.
Even the records of the Israeli transfer committee present us with half-truths, however. The United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees in the Middle East (UNRWA) has placed the number of Palestinian refugees forced to leave their homeland in accordance with Ben Gurion's plans at 900,000 in 1948 -- in other words, nearly double the number of refugees declared by the Israeli committee. The truth is that since 1937, the concept of eviction or ethnic cleansing was part of the policy of the Zionist movement, as revealed by the documents of the committee established by the British to inquire into the causes for the Great Arab Revolt of 1936 and to suggest solutions for the problems in Palestine. Official Israeli historians claim that the idea of eviction was imposed on the Zionist agenda through its deliberate insertion into the recommendations of this committee. But the new school of historiography in Israel has discovered that the Zionist movement in fact imposed the recommendation on the British. This is of little consequence, however. What matters is that eviction and displacement constituted an established Israeli strategy, recognised and implemented, regardless of its certificate of ownership.
Israel's ethnic cleansing campaign created the refugee problem. While the UN has recognised the right of Palestinian refugees to repatriation, Israel will not implement the resolution. President Clinton found himself cornered on this matter. He was forced into recognising their right to return, since the right had been already recognised for the Kosovo Albanian refugees. NATO went to war against Yugoslavia to ensure that the right of repatriation was fully implemented. The world must not allow silence to shroud this human tragedy any longer.

* Al-Ahram Weekly

Nine Hundred Years and Two Crusades

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

On the morning of Friday, 23 Sha’ban 492 H (15 July 1099 AD), Jerusalem was raped by an estimated 1 million Crusaders who came all the way from Europe to allegedly liberate local Christians from Islamic oppression. Jerusalem was consequently turned into a blood pool. Between 60,000 and 70,000 Muslims and Jews were butchered. This was the ultimate conquest of a trail of blood that extended for thousands of miles. Nine hundred years later, Jerusalem finds itself once again in bondage. The invading crusade this time does not hoist the cross but rather the Star of David. The similarities are striking.

Both campaigns originated in Europe, both used religion to justify aggression and butchery, and both were in essence motivated by purely mundane (secular) rather than religious considerations. In both cases, too, it was the weakness and disunity of the Muslims that contributed to the success of the invaders. Reading through Ibn Kathir’s Al-Bidayah wan-Nihayah, one is struck by the resemblance of the Muslim condition 900 years ago to the modern-day situation. Since the beginning of the 12th volume of his historic encyclopaedia, Ibn Kathir reiterates with every annual event profile the phrase ‘inna lillahi wa-inna ilayhi raji'un’ (to God we belong and to Him is our return), a phrase which Muslims utter when afflicted with severe calamity such as death or great loss.

Muslim land was divided among feuding sultans who quarreled not only with each other but even with close relatives or associates over power and wealth. To secure dominance over other competitors, a Muslim sultan would collaborate with the invading Crusaders by divulging sensitive information to them and helping them devour more Muslim towns and villages. When the news of the fall of Jerusalem reached Baghdad, the nominal capital of the Caliphate, people wept, rushed to the mosques for prayer and urged mobilization. As crowds gathered just outside Baghdad in preparation for a march toward the Holy City, news came that the Crusaders totaled a million warriors and that up to 70,000 of the city’s inhabitants had been massacred. The prospects seemed too gloomy and in the absence of a political will to rush to the aid of vanquished Jerusalem, the crowds dispersed and the governors continued their indulgence and bickering.

Describing the situation of the Muslims when Jerusalem was invaded in 1099, an Arab poet known as Abul-Muzaffar Al-Abyurdi lamented that tears instead of spears spoke in response. From his perspective, the fall of Jerusalem reflected the collapse of religious values and the absence of faith from the hearts of Muslims. Fear of the enemy, which made Muslims opt for inaction, was indicative of the erosion of taqwa (fearing the Almighty). According to him, it was obsession with living and anxiousness to avoid death that brought humiliation and dishonour to the world of Islam. In conclusion, he regrets that as a result of such degradation, the Muslims of the time had the willingness to neither support faith nor protect honour. The Muslims took several decades before they woke up from their slumber.

After 88 years, a Kurdish Muslim leader, who emerged out of Egypt and united the community under his command, marched toward Ash-Sham and liberated the Holy City. The city embraced its Muslim liberators who entered its gates peacefully on Friday, 27 Rajab 583 H (2 October 1187). Salah-ud-Din al-Ayyubi, who became a legend, did indeed lead the Ummah in its rise out of the dark tunnel. But he did not emerge out of the blues; he was the culmination of an awakening, of a revival movement, that took three quarters of a century to prevail. In some ways, the 12th century awakening resembles today’s Islamic awakening. In some other ways, the liberation of Jerusalem after 88 years of European occupation promises an imminent end to modern-day Zionist occupation of the first Qiblah. In less than 40 years from now, this vision may just prove to be true.

* Director of the London-based Liberty for Human Rights in Muslim World

Will Fatah disintegrate after Arafat?

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem - Fatah, the mainstream faction of the PLO and the backbone of Yasser Arafat’s Palestinian self-rule authority, is undergoing mounting internal divisions which some observers interpret as “early signs of a power struggle in preparation for the post Arafat era.”

According to insiders in Fatah, the bulk of this brewing struggle and intra-faction strife within the widely heterogeneous movement takes place below the surface, often assuming manifestations such as stepped-up polarization, vying for control of some existing Fatah institutions, as well as seeking to curry favour with the Israelis and the Americans.

“Fatah will disintegrate into many smaller factions, and self-serving bands if the Fatah leadership doesn’t take prompt action to instill genuine democracy within the movement,” warned Marwan al-Barghouthi last month.

Barghouthi, a Legislative Council member who also assumes the post of Secretary-General of the Fatah Higher Committee in the West Bank, pointed out that Fatah, like the PA, remained a centralized movement run almost single-handedly by Yasser Arafat.

Interestingly, Barghouthi has apparently fallen into disfavour with PA Chairman Yasser Arafat.

Last month, Arafat reportedly refused to meet Barghouthi in Ramallah until he amended his relations with Arafat’s Secretary, Ramzi Khouri, and other top officials in the President’s office.

According to the insiders mentioned above, Barghouthi had earlier accused Khouri of “erecting a stone wall between the President and the people and embezzling and squandering large sums of money on friends.”

Fatah discontent with Arafat has reached the boiling point

Much of the indignation and internal friction within Fatah can be found in the Gaza Strip where discontent with Aafat’s nepotistic rule has reached the boiling point.

Generally, there are two main rival camps involved in the current acrimony: the returnees or “Tawansah” (e.g. those who returned with Yasser Arafat from Tunis five years ago and effectively became the privileged class) and indignant local Fatah activists who feel “let down” and “betrayed” by the PA leadership.

In late June, a group of disgruntled officers, all of whom are native Gazans, circulated a communiqué signed by “the free officers” in which they lashed out at the Palestinian leadership, accusing it of being “penetrated by Zionist agents, mercenaries and hangers-on who call themselves Fatah but indulge in granting key jobs to Israeli collaborators while slamming the doors in the face of true patriots.”

“We would like to remind you of those who stole and embezzled the financial allocations earmarked for the policemen, spending the money on recreation tours abroad and paying for the luxurious flats and smart cars in Gaza and Ramallah…at a time when the vanquished members of the security forces are denied their overdue salaries.”

The communiqué went on slamming “the sycophants and hangers-on around Arafat” saying, “Yes, there are certain people who have the right to eat in exquisite restaurants and drink (alcoholic beverages) in the Tahuna and Beach bars, and then pass on the bills to Ramzi Khouri (head of Arafat’s office).” Last year, the expenses of the President’s office reached US$ 142 million, prompting the donor countries to “slim down” subsequent donations.

The “free officers” addressed the Palestinian people, calling on them to “put an end to the traitors and thieves whose scheme is to steal and embezzle as much as they can.”

The communiqué named several high-ranking PA security officials, including Mahmud Sehweil, Amin al Hindi, Jebril Rajoub and Ghazi al Jabali, warning that “nobody shall be able to protect those traitors from the wrath of the people because nobody has the right to collaborate with the Zionists at our people’s expense.”

Initially, the Palestinian Authority sought to ignore the leaflet which was distributed throughout the Gaza Strip as well as in parts of the West Bank. However, after Israeli and British papers, especially the Independent, published the contents of the leaflet, PA Gaza police chief Ghazi al-Jabali dismissed in a brief press conference the “free officers” as “a small group with no roots.”

He pointed out that 13 “low-ranking officers” had been arrested in connection with the leaflet, adding that they would be tried for “defamation and libel.”

None the less, most observers in the occupied territories doubt the PA claims that the leaflet was “an isolated phenomenon.” They point out that a state of profound frustration with the PA is permeating all sectors of the Palestinian populace, including people considered traditional supporters of Fatah.

Indeed, the problem extends far beyond the mere outpouring of anger at the ubiquitous aspects of corruption, nepotism and cronyism throughout the PA, and touches on the integration of the security agencies, the main base of Arafat’s survival.

One of the most conspicuous examples of this strife among the security agencies is the not-so-secret struggle between Preventive Security Force (PSF) Jebril Rajoub and General Intelligence chief Tawfik Tirawi.

Last month, the increasing rivalry between the two prompted Arafat to create still another new security agency which he called the “Special Forces”, to be added to the numerous security agencies which are, at least nominally, answerable to Arafat.

The new security agency, to be headed by Col. Bashir Nafei’, a prominent Fatah activist in the Qalandia refugee camp, just north of Jerusalem, is apparently designed to neutralize PSF chief Jebril Rajoub, especially after it became clear that Rajoub was seeking to conclude “arrangements and understandings” with the Israelis and Americans “behind Yasser Arafat’s back.”

In any case, Rajoub is not the only PA official seeking to curry favour with the Israeli Shabak and the American CIA. Tirawi, too, has demonstrated “audacious obsequiousness” in this field, so much so that it can be said that he has earned the title of “Israel’s premier man” within the PA security establishment.

This probably explains why the Israeli intelligence establishment demanded two years ago that the PA crackdown on Hamas and Islamic Jihad activists be carried out by Tirawi’s mukhabarat rather than by Rajoub’s PSF, as had been the case before.

Of course, Arafat readily gave his consent, and all the arrests of Islamist activists were carried out by the General Intelligence ever after. Today, there are over 300 mostly Islamist detainees interned by Tirawi, without charge or trial.

Multiple Fatah factions

In light of the above, it is safe to conclude that Fatah will, in the not-too-distant future, disintegrate into many “Fatahs”, e.g. Tirawi’s Fatah, Rajoub’s Fatah, Barghouthi’s Fatah, Fatah of the Returnees, Fatah of the West Bank and Fatah of the Gaza Strip, with unpredictable ramifications on the Palestinian people.

It is true that manifestations of this disintegration will not appear in their stark forms before the disappearance of Arafat, the symbol around which and through which Fatah remained somewhat intact for all these years.

When Dreamers are Left to Die… A Story of a Syrian Man

By Ramzy Baroud

Waiting in his cell for death or a miracle is exactly what Yasser El-Muazen must do after the Tel Aviv District Court shattered his hope of release and of survival.

Yasser, a Syrian man, was arrested by Israeli forces in occupied South Lebanon, the area known by Israel as the “Security Zone”. In early 1989, the Syrian fighter was sentenced to spend 25 years of his life with thousands of his Palestinian and Arab comrades incarcerated in Israel.

Yasser’s sentence followed a brief trial where an Israeli court found him guilty for his attempt to carry out an attack in Metulla, near the Lebanese border.

The tragedy of Yasser is a story of two parts¾ each is as sorrowful as the other.

Most of Israel’s demands presented to the Palestinian Authority (either in a friendly manner or a harsh one) were fulfilled as expeditiously as possible.

Yet, the most basic Palestinian demands, which have been ignored by Israel year after year, are being added to the Israeli record of disrespect for peace, justice and mutual agreements.

Yasser and thousands of Palestinian and Arab prisoners most likely do not fall in the “mutual agreements” category, which have been breached repeatedly by Israel.

Consequently, Yasser and his comrades must wait for Israel to start respecting its commitments to peace and to end its violation of human rights.

For such a miracle to take place, the wait is expected to carry on for many years. And waiting shall be very hard for those inhumanly crammed into very small cells for years to come.

But it is even harder for Yasser El-Muazen, who suffers from complete kidney failure, which makes the pain of isolation and imprisonment a less serious matter than his physical pain.

For some time, Palestinian human rights organizations have been calling on Israeli courts to order Yasser’s release, for death is imminent if a kidney transplant is not carried out immediately.

Finally, the Tel Aviv District Court agreed to review his case and make a decision concerning his release.

To ensure positive results, Yasser’s lawyer and many concerned organizations rushed to the court with all the necessary medical records showing the seriousness of Muazen’s condition.

Also, Syrian doctors confirmed that one of Yasser’s nine brothers who lives with his family in Damascus was a great match for the needed kidney.

On 5 July, the Israeli court delivered its final verdict. Yasser’s release would jeopardize the security of the State of Israel; therefore his appeal was denied.

Yasser, weak, sick and no longer able to walk independently, was escorted back to his cell after his last hope of freedom slowly faded. Yasser’s story stubbornly forces us to ask the question: Why should Palestinian and Arab prisoners be held accountable for defending their legitimate rights, while thousands of Israeli soldiers who participated in the massacring of innocent women and children are roaming the land carefree and untroubled?

Perhaps the hailed peace miracle that rewarded the general of Israel a Nobel Peace prize fell short of concerning itself with the welfare of those who needed it the most¾ the oppressed Palestinians.

Sending a man back to his cell, knowing that his death can no longer be postponed, is an international crime that must be investigated.

If Yasser were an Israeli, his story would reach the Four Corners of the earth. But when it comes to a poor, Arab fellow from Syria, the world’s attention span fails to understand the pain and anguish that he must feel every moment in his damp, dark prison cell.

Well, should we just add Yasser to the great list of mournful events in recent Arab and Palestinian history? Should we forget the young man who was hung by his feet for days by Israeli interrogators because “he had a dream”?

I hope we don’t. But I am certain that the dreamer shall never stop dreaming until his soul abandons his ailing body.

Only then will Yasser’s fight be over!

Securing Occupation:

The Real Meaning Of The Wye River Memorandum

Dr. Norman G. Finkelstein

(1-3)

As a formal document, the Wye River Memorandum breaks no new ground. Its stated purpose is merely to reaffirm and “facilitate implementation” of “prior agreements.” Nonetheless the memorandum illuminates the process set in motion at Oslo and dispels lingering illusions. In these remarks, I will first sketch the crucial historical background, then analyze the document and, finally, consider the prospects for a just settlement.

Background

The aim of the mainstream Zionist movement from its inception a century ago has been to create a Jewish state in Palestine. Ideally, this meant a state with a homogeneously Jewish population; for practical purposes, a state with an overwhelming Jewish population, tolerating a small Arab minority of perhaps 20 percent.1

The main obstacle to the realization of this goal was the indigenous Arab population. In his recently published quasi-official history of Israel, British historian Martin Gilbert argues that “there was a strong desire among the Labor Zionists to live together with the Arabs, and not, as many of the extremists hoped, to make them subordinate to Jewish nationalist needs, or even to drive them out of Palestine altogether.” Scholarship does not sustain this claim. Labor Zionism was committed to the “building of a Jewish society by Jews alone, from foundation stone to rafter” in “all of Palestine” (Anita Shapira). Accordingly, as Zeev Sternhell shows in an important study, “nobody fought against the Arab worker more vigorously than [Labor Zionists]; nobody preached national, economic, and social segregation with more determination than the Labor movement.”2

Faced with indigenous resistance, European conquest movements in the post-Columbus era typically resorted to the most brute force: extermination. Yet, by the early twentieth century this extreme option was no longer available. The Zionist movement thus set its sights on “population transfer” — the euphemism for expulsion — of the indigenous population. Indeed until after World War II, international opinion acquiesced in expulsion as a means of resolving ethnic conflicts.3 Historian Benny Morris observes that, for the Zionist leadership, “transferring the Arabs out” was seen as the “chief means” of “assuring the stability and ‘Jewishness’ of the proposed Jewish state.” During the 1948 war, the Arab population was effectively expelled from the conquered areas of Palestine, completing the first phase of Zionist conquest.4

In the course of the June 1967 war, Israel conquered the long-coveted West Bank and Gaza,5 as well as the Sinai and Golan Heights. In this second phase of conquest, the Zionist leadership confronted the same dilemma as earlier in the century: it wanted the land but not the people. The options available for resolving this dilemma, however, had considerably narrowed. Not only extermination but expulsion as well was no longer politically tenable.6 The Zionist movement accordingly opted for encirclement: appropriating as much of the resources (especially water) and land as was feasible while confining the Arab population to native reservations. This is the essence of the Allon Plan, first formulated in the late 1960s and the operative framework of the Oslo process.

Israel’s partial withdrawal option fell afoul, however, of the international consensus that formed after the June 1967 war for resolving the Arab-Israeli conflict. Embodied in U.N. Resolution 242, this consensus called for a full Israeli withdrawal from occupied Arab land in exchange for an Arab commitment to full peace with Israel. It bears recalling that the root of Israel’s enduring quarrel with the international community has been the demand not for a Palestinian state but for full, as against partial, withdrawal. Indeed, 242 made no mention at all of a Palestinian state, referring merely to a “just settlement of the refugee problem.” The Allon Plan is not incompatible with a Palestinian state: what to call the arid patches of land ceded to the Arab natives is a matter of semantics. For Israel, the crux has always been its claim to “territorial revision” (Abba Eban).7

After the June war, Israel called for partial withdrawal on all the Arab fronts. Egypt offered in February, 1971, to sign a bilateral peace treaty if Israel fully withdrew from the Sinai. Israel refused. In the name of “security,” it demanded retention of part of Sinai, Moshe Dayan famously declaring that, “we prefer Sharm-el-Shaykh without peace to peace without Sharm-el-Shaykh.” Once Egypt proved itself a military force to reckon with in the October 1973 war, Israel came around, agreeing at Camp David in 1978 to the peace terms it rejected in 1971. A core Zionist tenet, Zeev Sternhell observes, is “never giving up a position or a territory unless one is compelled to by a superior force.” Israel did continue to bargain hard at Camp David, demanding (unsuccessfully) to retain control of the oil refineries, settlements, and airfields it had built in Sinai. Yet Sharm-el-Shaykh figured not at all in these intense, often bitter, negotiations. Israel abandoned Sharm-el-Shaykh — its crucial “security” asset — without even a whimper. It is an instructive lesson in the substance, or lack thereof, of Israel’s “security” concerns.8

Confronting, in the first years of the intifada, the compound force of Palestinian civil resistance and widespread international outrage, Israel considered the prospect of full withdrawal. But the challenge to Israeli power soon receded. As the intifada lost momentum, a concatenation of events — Iraq’s destruction in the Gulf War, the demise of the Soviet bloc, the open alignment of the Arab regimes with the United States, the PLO’s precipitously declining fortunes — convinced Arafat to cut a deal with Israel, accepting partial withdrawal in exchange for the trappings of statehood. The PLO’s capitulation at Oslo did not result from political ineptitude. Uri Savir’s account of the negotiations shows that the Palestinian negotiators did, at every crucial juncture in the Oslo process, raise the right objections. The problem was, they had no power.9

Once Arafat conceded, as he effectively did at Oslo, that the West Bank and Gaza were “disputed territories,” both sides having equal title,10 it was inevitable that, in the ensuing battle over percentages, a 50-50 split would be held up as the legitimate “compromise.” Yet Netanyahu deserves the lion’s share of credit for recasting public discourse. By tenaciously claiming that Israel had title to all and Palestinians to none of the West Bank, Netanyahu turned any withdrawal into an Israeli concession. Who could then expect Israel to “give away” more than 50 percent of “its” land for peace? Before Netanyahu, full withdrawal in exchange for full peace was the legitimate compromise, Labor’s partial withdrawal the illegitimate one; after Netanyahu, partial withdrawal in exchange for full peace became the legitimate compromise, zero withdrawal the illegitimate one. Redefining the poles of debate with his pugnacious theatrics, Netanyahu has effectively legitimized the Labor Party’s rejectionist stance, in the process also managing to “lower,” as he put it, “the level of Palestinian expectations.” Apart from “extremists,” no one any longer speaks about full withdrawal. Indeed, the call for full withdrawal is now equated with the call for zero withdrawal, as pundits condemn the “extremists on both sides.”

Living Islam In The West:

An Interview With Shaykh Faisal Mawlawi

Shaykh Faisal Mawlawi, the Chairman of Lebanon’s Islamic Jama`ah, underscores the necessity of comparative jurisprudence (fiqh al-muwazanat) when dealing with many issues related to the Islamic presence in the West, especially when it comes to the traditional jurisprudential classification of the world into an Islamic realm and a battlefield realm. He considers it timely to develop this concept in light of the West’s status as a realm of accord and missionary activity.

He explains some sensitive concepts of problematic implications for Muslims living in the West, such as al-muwalat (allegiance) and al-tashabbuh bi al-kuffar (adopting kafer manners and means), which in a full-fledged Islamic way of life are prohibited. He adds that a Muslim should gauge his interest in “spreading the word” of Islam while living in the West as opposed to maintaining the identity of his children, even if that means relocating into an Islamic country. He also says a cost-benefit analysis should be considered between marrying a scriptural woman to obtain legal residency or citizenship and the possible advantages or disadvantages of such a marriage. What follows is the text of the discussion.

Q:

Among the Muslims living in the West there is a heated discussion about the traditional fiqhi classification of the world into an abode of Islam and an abode of war, which carries on to varying comments about the presence of Muslims in the West as well as their social interactions. All this back-and-forth debate is presumably based upon Islamic legal theories and practice. How could this historical fiqhi classification be brought up to date in light of Muslims residing in the West?

A.

The classification of the world into an Islamic domain and a hostile domain has been one of the most contentious classifications among Muslim legal scholars. This is true at the definitional level of each camp, and it is also true for those who added another domain: dar al`Ahd (the jurisdiction of trust). This is any area of the world that enters into a trust arrangement with the Muslims. My opinion is that the traditional bi-polar interpretation is no longer valid in our age and time. The Islamic portion of the world no longer meets the qualifications outlined by the `ulema, prominent among which is the implementation of Islamic laws and practices. And according to such (traditional) classification those countries that are assumed to be war-areas no longer meet the original qualifications. The fact is, most Muslims living in these “war-areas” feel secure in their life and possessions. Further, Muslims in these areas are not subject to pressures to renounce their Islam, as may have been the case in the past.

I am inclined to say that all countries inhabited by Muslims should be considered an Islamic domain even if Islamic laws and procedures are not implemented. The “hostile realm,” I think, should be confined to non-Islamic countries or countries that are in a state of war with the Muslims such as Israel in occupied Palestine. The rest of the territories in the world qualify for the “jurisdiction of trust” concept. The reason is that there are many agreements and arrangements between the states of these territories and Muslim states through the United Nations. These states are not - by any definition - concerned with the application of Islamic laws. I am inclined to say that these areas-of-trust should be considered an open field of da`wah for those who carry Allah’s faith. This concept can extend to include all the countries of the world even if they are not in a state of “understanding” with the Muslims. This jurisdiction would gain a status similar to the one which the Muslims experienced in pre-Medinan Mecca with respect to Muslims socializing with others.

Q:

Some Muslims living in the West try to justify legal irregularities such as not paying their taxes by saying that the West is in a state of war or is considered hostile territory (dar al-harb). They cite a legal opinion of Abu Hanifah which says that contracts or transactions between Muslims and belligerent citizens living external to the Islamic political order are null and void. Could you shed some light on this opinion of Abu Hanifah concerning such contracts in light of Muslims who live in the West.

A:

Abu Hanifah — may Allah be pleased with him — divides the world into two spheres: one Islamic, the other belligerent. He does not apply to the belligerent area of the world the same legal considerations that other faqihs do. The Hanafi school of thought permits a Muslim to relocate peacefully within the belligerent sphere of the world; in this case and according to the Hanafi interpretation of Islamic laws a Muslim must abide by the laws of that non-Islamic land. A Muslim is also expected to transact and “make money” in all legal ways within this hostile area. The Muslim is not permitted to act in any dishonorable manner.

The point of contention in the Hanafi denomination concerns generating wealth through contracts considered valid by non-Muslims but considered invalid by Muslim standards, such as usurious transactions. Even this type of opinion restricts its adherents from being involved in any cheating or betrayal. Whatever transaction takes place between a Muslim and a non-Muslim in non-Islamic territoriality should be done by the consent of both sides. Therefore, there is nothing in the Hanafi school of thought that would sanction a Muslim who has entered peacefully into the (Western) non-Islamic hemisphere by obtaining a visa to turn around and carry on illegal and unlawful activities financial or otherwise.

Q:

How can we remain obedient to secular laws and Islamic laws if they clash? For example, Western laws prohibit having more than one wife, but Islam permits it with restrictions. How can we counterpoise ourselves in such a situation?

A:

There is no problem when there is an Islamic law that permits something and another secular law that bans it, because a Muslim may or may not partake of the “extra-legal” (al-mubah). Once a Muslim moves into the West he (implicitly) agrees to forego the mubah which is outlawed in that particular country. So far so good. These types of problems are quite rare. The only incident I can recall is this hijab issue. It is obvious that when secular and Islamic laws collide, a Muslim is expected to honor his Islamic law whenever possible. Whenever a Muslim finds himself in a “legal bind” he should choose the less detrimental, as Allah says: Be observant of Allah to the best of your ability (fa-ittaqu Allaha ma istata`tum).

Q:

There seems to be an age-old debate about the validity of women covering the face. How can we redress this with Muslim women living in the West?

A:

This issue has vibrated with scholarly differences past and present. I tend to believe that uncovering the face is perfectly valid. It is preferable for women to cover their faces in Islamic countries because such behavior in Islamic countries is well-thought-of and respected. In non-Islamic countries I would say that it suffices for Muslim women to wear their hijab without covering their face, which in these countries is less eye-catching than to cover the face. Besides, the image of Islam is better served in such societies.

Q:

Divine law has permitted marriage with people of the Book. In fact, marrying women of the Book is, Islamically speaking, legal, if they are chaste, even if the civil benefits of legal residency were not to accrue from such a marriage. Q:

Concerning the issue of raising children in the West, how can a Muslim find an accommodation between his fear of his children assimilating into the West and losing their Islamic character, which would have him think about returning to his country of origin, and the desire to establish an Islamic presence in the West?

A:

The purpose of teaching Islam is an Islamic requirement for residing in a non-Muslim country. Muslim families in such a context are obviously vulnerable. The determining factor would depend on the person involved, his country of origin, and his country of residence. Some Muslims may come from countries whose moral standards and practices are as bad as they are in his Western country of residence, if not worse. According to the Talmud, Jesus was executed by a proper rabbinical court for idolatry, inciting other Jews to idolatry, and contempt of rabbinical authority. All classical Jewish sources which mention his execution are quite happy to take responsibility for it; in the talmudic account the Romans are not even mentioned.

The more popular accounts - which were nevertheless taken quite seriously - such as the notorious Toldot Yeshu are even worse, for in addition to the above crimes they accuse him of witchcraft. The very name Jesus’ was for Jews a symbol of all that is abominable, and this popular tradition still persists. The Gospels are equally detested, and they are not allowed to be quoted (let alone taught) even in modern Israeli Jewish schools.

Secondly, for theological reasons, mostly rooted in ignorance, Christianity as a religion is classed by rabbinical teaching as idolatry. This is based on a crude interpretation of the Christian doctrines on the Trinity and Incarnation. All the Christian emblems and pictorial representations are regarded as ‘idols’ - even by those Jews who literally worship scrolls, stones or personal belongings of ‘Holy Men’.

The attitude of Judaism towards Islam is, in contrast, relatively mild. Although the stock epithet given to Muhammad is ‘madman’ (‘mesbugga’), this was not nearly as offensive as it may sound now, and in any case it pales before the abusive terms applied to Jesus. Similarly, the Qur’an - unlike the New Testament - is not condemned to burning. Most rabbinical authorities agree that Islam is not idolatry (although some leaders of Gush Emunim now choose to ignore this). Therefore, the Halakhah decrees that Muslims should not be treated by Jews any worse than ‘ordinary’ Gentiles. But also, no better. Again, Maimonides can serve as an illustration. He explicitly states that Islam is not idolatry, and in his philosophical works he quotes, with great respect, many Islamic philosophical authorities. He was, as I have mentioned before, personal physician to Saladin and his family, and by Saladin’s order he was appointed Chief over all Egypt’s Jews. Yet, the rules he lays down against saving a Gentile’s life (except in order to avert danger to Jews) apply equally to Muslims.
