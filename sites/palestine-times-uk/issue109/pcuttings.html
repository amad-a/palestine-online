Press Cuttings

The Los Angeles Times, 29 June 2000

Jerusalem- Israeli and Palestinian leaders told Secretary of State Madeleine Albright on Wednesday that they are far from reaching a deal to settle their decades-old conflict but promised to attend a summit if President Clinton calls one.

     The ambiguous end to Albright's third trip to the Middle East in a month seemed to rule out the sort of early July extravaganza that Clinton clearly wants--and Albright was sent to the region to set up. But it left open the possibility of a high-stakes meeting involving the three leaders later in the year.

     By agreeing to attend a summit if it is called, Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak and Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat put unexpected pressure on Clinton. The U.S. president has sought to play the role of mediator but clearly has been uncomfortable with suggestions that he could or should dictate an outcome.

Ha’aretz, 30 June 2000

Everyone seems to be making threats these days. Palestinians are threatening Israelis, Israelis are threatening Arabs, Jews are threatening Jews, West Bank settlers are threatening the life of Prime Minister Ehud Barak. In the past, when there were tough negotiations with the Egyptians, warnings were issued. But there never were any threats comparable to those we are hearing now. The recent talks with the Syrians were also tough, but both sides guarded their tongues. Despite his inflexible positions, the late Syrian President Hafez Assad carefully refrained from making any threats.

Yet we now see a developing "culture of threats" where each side hears only the threats being made by the other side. When Chief of Staff Shaul Mofaz said the Israel Defense Forces might use tanks and planes should there be an eruption of Palestinian violence in the territories, the Palestinians interpreted that as a vulgar threat. (from an article by By Ze'ev Schiff)

Ha’aretz, 28 June 2000

For the first time, a chief rabbi of Israel has declared that the Temple Mount should remain under Palestinian administration even after the permanent settlement is signed.

A written declaration to that effect, on official Chief Rabbinate stationery, was sent by Sephardi Chief Rabbi Eliahu Bakshi-Doron with an Israeli delegation to an interfaith meeting with Muslim and Christian Palestinians that was held in Milan.

The Israeli delegation included rabbis, among them the personal emissary of Rabbi Bakshi-Doron.

Another proposal made by the Israeli delegation called for the establishment of an interfaith council that would be responsible foe all the holy places in Jerusalem, particularly the Temple Mount.

The Chicago Tribune, 27 June 2000

Jerusalem- (Israel’s) Prime Minister Ehud Barak's government survived two parliamentary threats to his coalition on Monday, clearing the way for him to focus on peace negotiations with the Palestinians.

Barak was caught in a crisis earlier this month, when three of the six parties in his coalition voted for a preliminary opposition motion to hold elections three years early.

Two of the defecting parties were opposed to the far-reaching concessions Barak is reportedly ready to make to the Palestinians. But the most important defector--Shas, the second-largest party in the coalition--was seeking guarantees for its network of religious schools and radio stations.

Shas extracted the guarantees it wanted, and when two follow-up votes came up Monday, its members voted with Barak.

The Los Angeles Times, 25 June 2000

"Hafez Assad is dead, and he did not sign," read the headline in Al-Nahar, Lebanon's leading Arabic-language newspaper, a day after the Syrian leader suffered a fatal heart attack on June 10. Indeed, Assad never did sign a peace treaty with his Israeli enemies, a move that earned him much respect in the Arab world, at least among those who regard a settlement as tantamount to surrender.

     Esteem, however, is different than political influence. It was Assad's fate to die even as his once-vaunted Lebanon policy came under considerable stress. When Israeli forces withdrew unilaterally from southern Lebanon, Syria was denied the military leverage over Israel that it enjoyed through its support of Hezbollah, and, in the process, it became more difficult for Assad or his successor to negotiate an acceptable deal on the Golan Heights.
