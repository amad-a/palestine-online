Palestinians reaffirm their attachment
to homeland on Land day

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Palestinians in Palestine and the Diaspora observed on 30 March the annual anniversary of “Yawm al Ardh” or “Land Day,” by reaffirming and reasserting their attachment to their occupied homeland and determination to deliver Palestine from the shackles of the Zionism.
The Land Day anniversary acquired particular significance this year as Israel and the Palestinian Authority continued to hold open and secret talks over the fundamental issues of the Palestinian question, such as the future of Jerusalem, the fate of more than four million refugees awaiting for
more than fifty years repatriation.
The anniversary also comes as the confiscation of private Arab land continues on both sides of the green line, a sinister racist practice that never stopped ever since the Zionist regime was established in Palestine in 1948.

Protests in Nablus
As in previous years, Palestinians marked the anniversary with massive demonstrations, parades, rallies and clashes with Israeli occupation soldiers.
The most violent clashes took place in the town of Nablus, where as many as 10,000 people took to the streets, chanting anti-Zionist slogans affirming the right of the dispossessed Palestinians to repatriation and restitution.
“We shall never come to terms with the arrogation of our homeland,” read one of the large placards carried by the demonstrators.
Another sign read “ Israel shall never enjoy a moment of peace as long as a Palestinian child is alive.”
Much of the activity in Nablus was carried out by the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas), as the movement’s leadership had earlier called on supporters to participate heavily in rallies and protests marking Land Day.
Around one hundred masked Hamas supporters torched the Israeli flag, vowing to continue the struggle and holy Jihad until “we get our full right back.”
“Palestine is in our hearts, Palestine is al Msjidul Aqsa (Aqsa Mosque), it is the third holiest Islamic shrine after the Holy Ka’aba and the Prophet’s Mosque in Madina. Hence, Palestine is part of our Islamic belief,” said one masked man, reading from a prepared text.
Many demonstrators marched toward the nearby Zionist colony of Alon Moreh, where the Israeli occupation authorities have been seeking to seize additional Arab land to expand the settlement.
There, the demonstrators hurled stones on Israeli soldiers, who responded with firing rubber bullets and tear gas, injuring as many as 20 people.
Most of the injured sustained light injuries and were released from hospital, according to local hospital sources.
In Ramallah, Hebron, and Bethlehem, Palestinian youngsters stoned Israeli occupation soldiers with stones, forcing Israeli vehicles to travel through bypass roads.
Moreover, Israeli settlers in much of the West Bank were told to stay in-door lest they be attacked by Palestinians.
In Jerusalem, a demonstration at Ras al Amud, where a Jewish colony is being built, attracted only hundreds of participants, in contrast to the organizers’ promises.
The demonstration was organized by the chief PLO man in East Jerusalem, Faisal Huseini, a traditional supporter of Yaser Arafat.
Observers said the low turnout was probably a reflection of mounting public disenchantment with Arafat’s regime.
Earlier on the day, Arafat himself addressed Palestinians through the PA-run radio and television.
He called on Palestinians everywhere to “unite in order safeguard our holy homeland in face of the forces of oppression and aggression.”

Across the Green Line
In 1948-Palestine (Israel), the one million-strong Arab community observed the Land Day with massive rallies in all major Arab towns and villages.
In Sakhnin, where the original Land Day came into being a quarter of a century ago, thousands of Palestinians held a big rally during which Palestinian flags were raised.
The demonstrators were soon attacked by police and special police forces, using tear gas and rubber-coated bullets.
At least 25 protesters were injured, one of them seriously.
The “muwajahat” or clashes lasted for four hours, as crack border police soldiers traced the protesters into the town, using tear-gas into populated areas.
At the town of Um al Fahem, in the southern Triangle, thousands of townspeople planted olive trees at al Rawha, an adjacent area the Israeli army has been seeking to seize from its owners.
In 1998, fierce clashes erupted between the people of Um al Fahem and Israeli occupation troops, forcing the Israeli government of Benyamin Netanyahu to suspend an order to confiscate the land.
The Land Day commemorates the killing by the Israeli army of six Palestinians at the village of Sakhnin on 30 March, 1976.
The Palestinians were then protesting the expropriation of their lands in the Galilee by the Israeli government for the purpose of expanding Jewish settlements in the region.
Since then, Land Day has become a symbol of Palestinian determination to
resist the theft of their land, which has been going unabated since the establishment of Israel in 1948.
Hundreds, probably thousands, of other Palestinians have since then been killed, maimed or made homeless in the fight to retain Palestinian land.

Land: the crux of the matter
Indeed, the land is the crux of the matter of the Palestinian question.
Even the Zionists themselves admit that grabbing Palestinian land, often at gun point, is neither legal, nor moral, nor just.
One Zionist writer, Y. Ben Porat, a well-known writer wrote in 1972 that “One truth is that there is no Zionism, no settlement, no Jewish state, without the evacuation of Arabs and confiscation and enclosure of their land.”
Even David Ben-Guirion, the first Prime Minister of Israel, recognized, in his own way, the injustice the Zionists meted out to the Palestinians.
He wrote in 1949 “why should the Arabs make peace? If I were an Arab leader, I would never make terms with Israel. That is natural. We have taken their country. Sure, God promised it to us, but what does that matter to them? Our God is not theirs. They only see one thing: we came here and stole their country. Why should they accept that ?”

Pope visits al-Haram al-Sharif

Occupied Jerusalem - Amid heavy security, the Pope of the Vatican, Pope John Paul II, paid a courtesy visit on Sunday, 26 March, to al-Haram al-Sharif of Jerusalem, the Muslim world’s third holiest shrine.

The Mufti of Jerusalem, Sheikh Ikrema Sabri, and several Muslim Ulema (scholars), as well as several Jerusalemite leaders, including PLO Jerusalem official Faisal Husseini, received the Pope.

The Supreme Muslim Council of Jerusalem, which oversees Islamic holy places in the city, called on the Pope to do all he could to end the Israeli occupation of Jerusalem.

Sabri told the Pope that without ending the Israeli occupation of Jerusalem, there would be no peace or justice or stability in this part of the world.

“This is an occupied land which Israel seized in 1967,” Sabri told the Pope.

“There will be no peace, no security, no stability in the absence of justice, and justice will not be achieved until this city of ours is made free.”

The Pope responded to Sabri’s remarks, saying the followers of the three monotheistic religions (Islam, Christianity and Judaism) should be able to live together in peace and tranquillity.

Palestinian officials earlier handed the Pope a petition, urging him to work toward ending the Israeli occupation of Jerusalem.

The petition was symbolically signed by hundreds of Jerusalemite notables, Muslim and Christian alike.

During his visit to the Holy Land, the Pope refrained from making explicit political statements favouring either Israel or the PA.

During his visit to the Deheisheh refugee camp outside Bethlehem on Wednesday, 22 March, the Pope refrained from making an explicit reference to the Palestinian refugees’ right of return to their homeland.

He instead spoke of the refugees’ right to live in a homeland and earn a decent living, a statement that did not meet Palestinian expectations.

On Sunday, 26 March, the Pope visited al-Buraq Wall, which the Jews call the Western Wall.

There, the Pope read a printed and signed copy of a plea for forgiveness that he made at the Vatican earlier this month for the historic sins of Christians.

“We are deeply saddened by the behaviour of those who in the course of history have caused these children of yours to suffer and, asking your forgiveness, we wish to commit ourselves to genuine brotherhood with the People of the Covenant.”

Jews pray at the Wall to mourn the destruction of the First and Second Temples and insert written prayers and supplications to God into crevices between the stones.

The Pope was heckled by extremist Jews who protested at the “Wailing Wall being made impure by the presence of the Pope.”

At least two Jews were arrested as they were carrying torn-up Vatican flags and other anti-Christian signs.

On 23 March 1999, the Pope made a pilgrimage to the Holocaust Memorial Museum in West Jerusalem. On 25 March of the same year, the Pope asked Israel to rescind a license granted to Muslims to build a mosque opposite the Church of the Annunciation in the Arab town of Nazareth.

The mosque is slated to be built on land belonging to the Muslim Wakf, but Christians have voiced worries that the planned mosque would undermine the dominating presence of the towering church.

Muslim officials and dignitaries received the Pope and in deference to him cancelled a prayer-demonstration at a Tabariya mosque that Muslims are trying to recover from the Israeli authorities.

Israel confiscated hundreds of mosques in 1948 and converted many of them into museums or bars, offending Muslims.

The Pope returned to Rome on 26 March, ending one of his most important visits abroad.

Internally displaced Palestinian refugees
re-affirm right of return

Occupied Jerusalem – PT

On Saturday, 11 March, internally displaced Palestinians in pre-1967 Palestine (Israel) joined Palestinian refugee communities in exile to reaffirm their right of return.

“No peace with Israel without the implementation of our right to return to our homes and properties” is the demand which mobilizes not only millions of Palestinian refugees in the Arab world and the West, but also the approximately 250,000 Palestinians who have remained—displaced and disowned—inside 1948-Palestine.

The public Rally for the Right of Return, organized by the National Committee for the Defense of the Rights of the Internally Displaced in the sports hall of the Nazareth municipality, was attended by some 850 participants, including activists from displaced communities, Palestinian political parties and movements, representatives of Palestinian local councils and public institutions in Israel, as well as solidarity delegations from the occupied Syrian Golan Heights, refugee camps in the occupied West Bank and the PLO.

Hundreds of letters of support sent by Palestinian parties, institutions and activists in all parts of Palestine, Lebanon, Syria, Kuwait and Canada, as well as the statements of the speakers at the Return Rally, namely Ramiz Jeraiyseh/Mayor of Nazareth, Muhammad Zeidan/Head of the Arab Monitoring Committee, Abdelhakim Al-Zreikhi/PLO Refugee Department, Jamal Shati/PLC, Salman Fakhr Al-Din/Syrian community in the Golan Heights, and Muhammad Jaradat/BADIL, addressed two major common themes:

1. The determination of Palestinian refugees and those internally displaced to continue the struggle for their right of return (“with my teeth I will cling to my land...” in the words of the late Palestinian poet Tawfiq Zayyad), which is expressed in the current series of popular conferences and rallies launched in all parts of Palestine: al-Far’ah (1995), Bethlehem (1996), Gaza (1996), Tulkarem (1999), and in Nazareth (1995 and 2000);

2. The need for united Palestinian action towards a solution of the Palestinian refugee question—the core issue of the Palestinian/Arab-Israeli conflict—based on the implementation of international law and United Nations resolutions, in particular UN resolution 194 (Right of Return).

In a step towards the required united action, Mayor Jeraiyseh announced on behalf of the Arab Monitoring Committee that the right of return of internally displaced Palestinians will feature as the central issue of the public events organized on this year’s Palestinian Land Day (30 March 2000).

Attorneys Wakim Wakim and Suleiman Fahmawi, speaking on behalf of the National Committee for the Defense of the Internally Displaced, presented the Committee’s Manifesto and clarified that:

1. The National Committee is the representative of the internally displaced Palestinians in Israel, while the PLO is the sole representative of the Palestinian people, including internally displaced Palestinians. Any political agreement signed by the PLO with Israel that excludes the right of return will be considered null and void by refugees and the internally displaced;

2. Israel continues to violate the basic rights of its Palestinian citizens, not only their right to property is denied, but even their right to vote and their right to a candidate is being questioned. Expectations of a gradual democratization of Israel are thus based on illusions.

The speakers of the National Committee called for broad support of the internally displaced by all Palestinian social and political institutions, for the immediate adoption of the file of the internally displaced by the Palestinian leadership, and for a joint and intensive effort at documentation of Palestinian evictions and displacement during the 1948 Nakba.

Israeli guest speaker Tedi Katz summarized his research findings on the previously poorly documented Israeli massacre in the Palestinian village of Tantura.

He drew the attention of the audience to the fact that the mosque and graveyard of Tantura were ploughed and transformed into a sea-side parking lot, a measure which strongly contradicts Israeli sensitivity to the desecration of Jewish graveyards all over the world.

He emphasized that no Israeli government interested in peace will be able to escape the refugee question and reminded the audience that accurate documentation of destroyed villages and lost properties is a precondition for a strong Palestinian argument and negotiations in the future. If the history of the 530 Palestinian villages destroyed and depopulated in 1948 is not recorded now, while eyewitnesses are still alive, they will be lost forever.

The Rally’s cultural programme expressed Palestinian refugees’ determination to return in emotional terms. The Saffouri-dance theatre group gave an artistic account of the search for a lost village.

Deheishe camp’s IBDA’A children again succeeded to move the audience by depicting Palestinian persistence in their struggle against all odds, as well as the power of the young Palestinian generation, ready to take up and complete the cause of their parents and ancestors.

Israeli Military Forces Commit Cold-Blooded
Killing of a Palestinian Woman

Hebron- PT

At midnight, 20 March 2000, Halima Abdel Aziz Al-Aloul (Al-Sharouf), 45, mother of 12 children, was killed by the Israeli Occupation Forces in the Hebron District in the West Bank.

She was shot by Israeli soldiers in the area between the two villages of Kharas and Sourif. The soldiers were reportedly reservists from the settlement of Kiryat Arba’a whom the Israeli army allowed to erect roadblocks and checkpoints on roads used by Palestinians.

The settlers of Kiryat Arba’a, as indeed the rest of the Jewish settlers everywhere in the West Bank, are considered among the most murderous of the Jewish community, as they are indoctrinated to hate non-Jews in general and Palestinians in particular.

According to the affidavit submitted to Palestinian human rights organization al-Haq, based in Ramallah, her husband, Mohammad Ibrahim Al-Tous, 48, approached a military checkpoint between Kharas and Sourif known as the Wadi al-Aazaz.

As he neared the checkpoint, he realized he did not have his insurance document for the car, so he sought to turn back from the checkpoint.

At this point, the soldiers/settlers fired at least 20 rounds at the car, without any warning to stop.

His wife, seated in the passenger seat, was hit six times in the back, head, chest and neck. She died immediately and was later taken to the hospital for an autopsy.

At the same time, the husband was also shot in the back. Al-Tous drove 1 kilometre before he fainted.

Citizens took him to the Al-Ahli Hospital in Hebron to the surgery unit to remove the bullet; however, they could not find it.

He was injured in the liver and other internal organs by bullet fragments.

Initially, the Israeli occupation authorities claimed the car was intent on running over the soldiers.

However, later on, probably under media pressure, the Zionists began a token investigation into the incident. The soldiers were neither arrested nor prosecuted, and were reportedly transferred to another locality to carry out policing duty.

Soldiers saw the occupants of the car

According to Al-Tous, the soldiers were able to see the occupants of the car, given that it was close to a military checkpoint, which itself is fully lit and there was a full moon.

What is even more reprehensible is that the soldiers allowed the driver to continue driving for 1 kilometre knowing that injuries were inflicted to the occupants of the car. Shooting at civilians without warning is a violation of the principles and minimum international standards regarding the use of firearms. The intention of the soldier(s) was to obviously shoot to kill.

The murder of al-Sharouf coincided with the visit by Pope John Paul II to Palestine.

Jewish settlers have committed numerous crimes against the civilian Palestinian population in the Hebron area.

In 1983, for example, settlers dressed in military uniforms stormed the Islamic college in Hebron, killing two Shariah students and injuring many others. The Israeli occupation authorities then claimed the perpetrators were Palestinians and that the car they were driving carried Arab license plates. The murderers were later apprehended and spent a few years in jail before they were pardoned by the former Israeli President Haim Herzog.

In 1994, Baruch Goldstein, an American Jewish immigrant from Brooklyn, committed his gruesome massacre of Palestinian worshipers at the Ibrahimi Mosque. Twenty-nine worshipers were instantly killed and scores were injured. The settlers of Kiryat Arba’a erected a marbled memorial plaque around Goldstein’s grave at the settlement. Extremist Jews from around the world make pilgrimage to the site.

Palestinian councillors admit Arafat is
the biggest obstacle to democracy in Palestine

Dura (West Bank)- Palestinian Legislative Council members representing Fatah, the mainstream faction of the PLO, admitted during a public meeting in Dura on 12 March that Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat remains the “main obstacle to democracy in Palestine.”

Responding to a torrent of angry questions and criticisms by citizens disenchanted with the PA regime, three Council members admitted, ostensibly under audience pressure, that Arafat was impeding democracy and the separation of power, and that Palestinians had little hope of genuine political reform as along as Arafat was alive.

Muhammed al-Hurani, a veteran Fatah leader, admitted that “democracy in Palestine couldn’t prosper and flourish under present circumstances.”

When asked to clarify what he meant by “present circumstances,” Hurani said, “You know no President would volunteer to give up some of his powers and our President is no exception.”

Hurani said he and his fellow councillors were trying to “snatch our rights from him (Arafat) little by little, step by step, occasionally using gentle words and resorting to tacit pressure if necessary.”

Asked why the Legislative Council has not succeeded in bringing about the much-needed separation of powers, Hurani said, “You know…we are a third world country; we are not in Sweden.”

“But we struggled for 30 years, and justifying the lack of freedom by the fact that we are a third world country is just unconvincing…There are many third world countries that have a flourishing democracy; India has a democracy, poor countries in the heart of Africa have a democracy. Besides, our enemy, Israel, has a democracy of her own…so why can’t we have democracy and have freedom of speech and an independent judiciary?” retorted an angry citizen.

Another councillor, Minister of Transport Ali al-Kawasmeh, sought to glorify the “Palestinian democracy.”

“Look everywhere around us in the Arab world…do they have a similar level of freedom of speech as we do?”

However, another person from the audience interrupted him saying, “You think after all these years of suffering and struggle we should be compared to these police States around us…we want real freedom and real democracy, and if Syria or Iraq is your model, please don’t try to impose it on us.”

Kawasmeh retorted by arguing that, “Democracy has its limits and one has to be careful and be sure about what he says and writes.”

However, a citizen retorted saying, “It seems that your knowledge of democracy is similar to my knowledge of the Japanese language. In a democracy, the courts, not the police, decide whether something said or written is legal or illegal…Here, everything is in the hand of one person, he is Abu Ammar (PA Chairman Yasser Arafat), he is the President, he is the judiciary, he is the police, he is the Palestinian Authority.”

On the expiry of the Council’s term, many citizens confronted the three councillors with the fact that the Legislative Council was no longer legal.

One of the councillors, Suleiman Abu Sneineh, a lawyer, said the dissolution of the Council now would benefit Israel, pointing out that the current Council achieved international recognition, arguing that it was foolhardy to scrap all of this now.

On hearing this, one citizen asked sarcastically, “Do you think we are a bunch of dummies here…why are you insulting our intelligence like this…in every democracy, when the term of a Legislative Council expires…a new election is organized….I wonder how many more years we have to wait until the open-ended term of your damned Council will end.”

According to the Oslo Accords, the raison d’être of the PA, the term of the PA, its President Yasser Arafat, and the Legislative Council end upon the expiration of the accords on 4 May, 1999.

PA mistreats Professor for attacking corruption

Occupied Jeruslaem- On 18 February, the notorious secret police of the Palestinian Authority arrested Dr. Abdul Sattar Qassem, a Political Science professor at the Najah National University in Nablus.

Since then, Qassem has been held without charge or trial in a Jericho prison, where some security elements said he is being mistreated.

Family sources said Qassem was in need of medical care. So far, his lawyer and human rights representatives have been barred from visiting him.

Qassem has been an outspoken critic of the Palestinian Authority and the overall Oslo process, which he described as a sell-out of Palestinian rights.

In 1995, Qassem was shot four times by pro-Arafat gunmen, who sought to serve him a warning to “shut up or else...”

His latest book, The Road to Defeat, blasts Arafat for putting up with widespread corruption within the Authority and for not doing anything to curb human rights violations by his security forces.

During October of 1999, Qassem co-signed a petition blasting the performance of the PA at every conceivable level. The petition, dubbed as “Petition-20”, drew widespread reactions from Palestinians inside Palestine and in the Diaspora.

The petitioners specifically laid blame on Arafat, breaking a well-established taboo in Palestine. On 27 November, 1,000 copies of the petition were distributed.

When the petition was released, Qassem was arrested by Arafat’s secret police. He was the first of 11 petitioners to be arrested. The remaining nine petitioners could not be arrested owing to the immunity given them as members of the Palestinian Legislative Council. One petitioner who was not arrested, Mo’awya Al-Masr, a member of the Council, was shot three times. Abdul Jawad Salah, also a Council member, was severely beaten by the police.

Qassem and another co-signer from the Dura area, Ahmed Shaker Dudin, received the lion’s share of mistreatment.

While in jail, Qassem was interrogated by the Attorney-General of the Palestinian Authority, Khaled Al-Kidra, himself a target of corruption allegations.

Qassem was threatened with trial by a military court.

By January 2000, though, all of those arrested were released without charges being filed after they pledged to appear in court if summoned or face a $70,000 fine.

Qassem started teaching in the new semester soon after his release. Then the secret police arrested him again on 18 February.

Qassem stated that he was never physically abused during his first arrest, but reports by the Palestinian Society for the Protection of Human Rights and the Environment suggest that he is not as lucky this time and is being tortured.

Qassem was released in January only to defuse mounting political pressure. Now, as things have quieted down, the Palestinian Authority has decided to settle the score.

It is not exactly known why Qassem was re-arrested. But sources close to the security agencies suggest that the arrest has to do with Qassem’s attacks on the personal leadership of Yasser Arafat.

Indeed, Qassem has been quite outspoken in criticizing the autocratic style of Arafat’s leadership, saying this leadership has caused untold harm to the Palestinian people and their cause.

Qassem, though, never did anything or committed any offense that violates the written laws of the PA.

However, it is all too clear that the PA is an autocratic police State (without a State), and non-conformists are persecuted, arrested and might even get killed.

Qassem is a brave fighter for liberty, human rights and civil liberties.

He should be let down.

PA continues to suppress free press within self-rule areas

Hebron (West Bank)- The Palestinian Authority (PA) has continued to crack down on press freedom within the autonomous enclaves. The following two recent incidents shed light on this.

On Saturday, 25 March, the administrative governor of Hebron, Abdul Fattah al-Jueidi, summoned to his office at “al-Mukata’a” a number of local cameramen who work for international news agencies and television networks.

According to Palestinian sources, Jueidi questioned the cameramen concerning their coverage and filming of the riots at the Deheishe refugee camp near Bethlehem, which occurred on Wednesday, 22 March, immediately following the papal visit to the camp.

One cameraman intimated that Jueidi warned them against “exposing us and making us the laughing-stock of the world.”

He reportedly described the videotape images of the riots as “scandalous” and “besmirching the image of the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian people as a whole.”

The cameramen reportedly undertook not to film scenes deemed detrimental to Palestinian national interests “as defined by the PA.”

Earlier, the PA reportedly ordered the printed and electronic media throughout the West Bank not to cover the Deheishe riots in which as many as 80 people were hurt.

The second incident took place on 21 March when PA General Intelligence (GI) officers in Hebron summoned Khalid Amayreh for questioning in connection with an internet posting in which the Hebron-based journalist described in detail how he had been questioned by GI officers on 11 February regarding “non-conformist news and view” in the local weekly, Akhbar al-Khalil (Hebron Times), which Amayreh edits.

Amayreh said the officers this time were “more unfriendly than the previous time.”

“Two of them used very abusive and obscene language,” said Amayreh.

Amayreh said the questioning was “more acrimonious” than before and lasted for close to three hours.

PA deputy urges Palestinians to protect
“Legislative Council” from Arafat

Occupied Jerusalem- A Palestinian Legislative Council member called on the Palestinian people to protect and safeguard the Council from “the hegemony of the executive power.”

Speaking during a Democracy-day forum at the village of Kufr Nima near Ramallah, Ghazi Hananiya, deputy speaker of the Legislative Council, said the Palestinian people were duty-bound to protect the Council from the excesses and hegemony of the executive power.

“This is the house of the people, and the people should protect their house.”

However, Hananiya stuttered and sought to evade the subject when a citizen asked him who was attacking the Council and whether he was alluding to Yasser Arafat.

Another lawmaker, Qaddura Fares, exhorted the audience to accept the PA as it is, arguing that it was the product of years of struggle and suffering.

Fares was confronted with angry reactions.

Some in the audience asked him, “Do you mean we should condone all this corruption and lawlessness…just because some people within the PA had a history of struggle?”

Knesset member says Israeli law is racist in nature

Occupied Jerusalem- An Arab member of the Israeli parliament (the Knesset) attacked the Israeli justice system, saying discrimination between Arabs and Jews was assuming flagrant proportions.

Hashem Mahamid of the United Arab List castigated the justice system’s policy of using double standards when dealing with Arab and Jewish prisoners.

He pointed out that a life sentence for a Jew in reality means a few years, while it means “imprisonment till death” for an Arab.

“A Jewish prisoner often receives a commuted sentence, a pardon or parole and is allowed family visits, but an Arab prisoner is denied these rights and is not allowed even to walk in his father’s funeral or visit his grave.”

Mahamid said the situation of Arab political prisoners in Israel was particularly appalling.

He said the prison conditions of those prisoners were much worse than the conditions of Jewish prisoners.

Mahamid said the Israeli justice system and Israeli officials did not deal with these issues based on the principle of justice and equality for all but on the basis of race.

Report: Rabin offered Arafat a “mini-State” in 1995

Occupied Jerusalem- A Palestinian source revealed that the assassinated Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin had offered Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat a small State in Gaza and Jericho in 1995.

The PA semi-official daily al-Haya al-Jadida quoted the unidentified high-ranking source as saying that in 1995 Rabin proposed that Arafat “immediately declare a Palestinian State in Gaza and Jericho in which case Israel would lend recognition to it.”

The source said Rabin told Arafat then that declaring a Palestinian State would make the peace process much easier since the negotiations between the two sides would be between two States.

The Palestinian source reportedly described Rabin’s offer as “lacking cohesion.”

According to the report, Arafat did not respond either positively or negatively to the offer.
