Muslim David trounces Zionist Goliath near Nablus

Hebron- From Khalid Amayreh

Hamas Mujahideen or “freedom fighters” dealt a huge blow to an Israeli army elitist undercover unit on 27 August when Israeli soldiers disguised as Arabs raided the village of Aseera al-Shamailya near Nablus, 100 kilometres north of Jerusalem, for the purpose of killing a Hamas fugitive.

The soldiers, who belonged to the notorious murderous unit code-named “Duvedevan” (Cherry), entered the village shortly after the fall of darkness for the purpose of “liquidating” Mahmoud Abu Hannoud, Hamas’ top guerrilla in the West Bank. (It is believed that soldiers of the same unit were involved in the murder of the 73-year-old mukhtar of the village of
Sarda, Mahmoud Abdullah, on 15 August, during what the Hebrew press called a secret drill to storm Palestinian villages.)

Initially, the soldiers opened fire randomly at a number of houses, suggesting that they didn’t know the exact whereabouts of Abu Hannoud.

At least two villagers were injured, one of them is Nidal Daghlas, a schoolteacher whom the Israeli army later branded as “terrorist.”

Daghlas sustained moderate wounds, and the Zionist soldiers refused to allow first aid to be given to him, allowing him to bleed very badly.

It seemed that somehow Abu Hannoud had received word–probably from a double collaborator–that Israelis were in town and that their intention was to kill him. This gave the Islamist fighter time to think and flee to Nablus, 3 kilometres away. Nablus is under full PA control and is effectively off limits to the Israeli army.

As Abu Hannoud moved extremely cautiously from his hideout, heading for Nablus, he apparently encountered and surprised four of the unit’s soldiers, who must have thought that he was still in the house. He opened fire at them, killing three soldiers and seriously injuring a fourth.

Abu Hannoud was also lightly to moderately injured in the shoulder and in the arm, but that did not prevent him for fleeing the site, taking advantage of the dense olive orchards which gave him cover against the Israeli helicopters that were searching for him with flares and laser lights.

After a laborious and extremely risky walk-and-hide manoeuvre, Abu Hannoud was able to reach the closest PA police roadblock. There, Abu Hannoud was taken to hospital, thus escaping miraculously from what looked like an inescapable death.

The Israeli army has suggested that the three soldiers might have been killed by friendly fire, a suggestion the local villagers dismiss as a ruse to boost the morale of the Israeli public.

During the two-hour episode, many villagers took to the streets, shouting “God is Great” and hurling stones at the soldiers. The ensuing commotion, said one source at Aseera Shamaliya, was instrumental in confusing the Israelis and consequently enabling Abu Hannoud to escape to Nablus.

Stunned by the death of the three soldiers, the Israeli army called in three helicopter gunships which trained spotlights on the houses and the adjacent fields in an effort to locate Abu Hannoud, but, of course, to no avail.

Having failed to apprehend the Islamist fighter, the army, which called in troop reinforcements, conducted a house-to-house search in the village, harassing and terrorizing residents. Later on, an army bulldozer levelled the house of the schoolteacher Daghlas, without giving the family
a chance to remove their furniture or their belongings.

According to eyewitnesses, Israeli soldiers beat Daghalas mercilessly on the spot, despite his injury, to extract information from him on the whereabouts of Abu Hannoud. Daghlas repeatedly told the soldiers that he had not seen anything. He was later transferred to a helicopter and taken to an unknown destination.

In addition to Daghlas, the occupation authorities arrested three citizens from the village. They are the imam of the local mosque, Sheikh Dhirar Hamadneh, Ahmed Khalil and Abdullah Odeh.

Israel accuses Abu Hannoud of masterminding a number of suicidal bomb attacks in Jerusalem which left many Israelis dead and injured.

The Israeli government demanded that the PA extradite Abu Hannoud to Israel to stand trial.

However, the Israeli request was flatly rejected by the PA security chief in the West Bank, Jebril Rajoub, who remarked that “only over our dead bodies will we hand him to the Israelis.”

The PA has never formally handed over any Hamas fighters to Israel since this would cause a great embarrassment for the PA and portray it as a bunch of collaborators working for Israel.

Nonetheless, the PA said it would try Abu Hannoud before its notorious “State Security Court” for “endangering Palestinian national interests.”

Moreover, following the battle of Aseera Shamaliya, and in an apparent effort to obtain a certificate of good conduct from the Zionists, PA security agencies launched a campaign of arrests among Hamas supporters at the Najah University.

According to Palestinian sources in Nablus, the Preventive Security and the Mukhbarat (intelligence) arrested as many as 15 Islamist students on suspicion of having connections with Abu Hannoud.

The arrests were apparently coordinated with another campaign of arrests targeting Islamist students by the Israeli occupation authorities.

The semi-official Palestinian daily, al-Ayyam, reported on Tuesday, 29 August, that the Israeli army arrested as many as 25 Palestinian university students studying at Najah University in Nablus and Al-Quds universities in Jerusalem.

There is a strong security and intelligence coordination between the two sides, and it is mainly targeted against the Islamists who oppose Yasser Arafat’s capitulatory policies vis-à-vis the Zionist regime.

Meanwhile, Hamas praised Abu Hannoud’s “bravery and heroism” in the face of hundreds of Israeli soldiers.

Hamas also held the Palestinian Authority responsible for the safety and well-being of Abu Hannoud.

Hamas spokesman in Gaza Mahmoud al-Zahhar censured the PA saying, “Those heroes should be treated with utmost respect, not thrown into jail.”

The movement issued a detailed communiqué on 28 August, vowing to continue in the path of struggle and Jihad until Muslims recovered their rights from Zionist hands.

The bloody encounter at Aseera Shamaliya comes at a time when Israeli-Palestinian contention over the paramount issue of Jerusalem is rising, and with the peace process at a real crossroad.

Hamas and the Islamic Jihad have recently threatened to launch an “all-out” war against Israel if Jewish groups continue to encroach upon Muslim holy places at Jerusalem’s Haram al-Sharif.

On 25 August, thousands of Islamists in Nablus and Amman took to the streets to warn the PA as well as other Arab regimes against accepting any agreement that would leave East Jerusalem, particularly the Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, in Israeli hands.

Observers in Palestine believe the latest episode at Aseera Shamaliya could induce more Palestinian and Arab youths to join the ranks of Hamas, especially if the peace process ended in a dead end, a prospect that is not very unlikely given the present impasse.

Meanwhile, Lawyer Husam Arafat has declared that a team composed of many Palestinian lawyers was formed to defend the Qassam Brigades commander Mahmoud Abu Hannoud, who was expected to stand trial at the Palestinian Authority’s State Security Court.

Arafat, spokesman for the team, said the lawyers called for forming the team after the PA declared it would try him before the State Security Court.

PA police official Ghazi Jibali and attorney-general Khaled Al-Qudra had said that Abu Hannoud would stand trial on Tuesday, 29 August.

Arafat underlined that many lawyers had volunteered to defend Abu Hannoud and that his team would soon hold a press conference to explain its defense plan to the public.

Jerusalem, the crux of the matter

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

Israeli authorities reopened Jerusalem’s Haram Al-Sharif to visitors on 14 August following a four-day closure in the aftermath of clashes between Jewish extremists and Palestinian Muslims. The site, at which the Al-Aqsa and Dome of the Rock mosques are located, is considered the third holiest shrine in Islam.

Clashes erupted when thousands of Jews gathered on the Haram Al-Sharif grounds to commemorate the “annual day of sorrow,” marking the destruction of the second Jewish temple which some Jews believed to have stood at the same site.

However, almost 30 years of excavations in and around the area failed to produce any tangible proof that the temple is beneath the Aqsa Mosque, a fact recognized by many Jewish experts on archaeology.

The extremists, representing a myriad of religious groups, did not even try to hide their goal. Converging at one of the gates of the Haram Al-Sharif, they shouted, “Let us rebuild the temple.” Eventually, the throngs were forced out by Palestinians, but not before making their point.

Only a few days earlier, discussions by Israel’s High Rabbinate regarding a proposal to build a large synagogue in the heart of the Haram Al-Sharif were postponed until “a more appropriate time.” Debate over the proposal was believed to have been shelved owing to behind-the-scenes pressure from the Israeli government, mindful of the likelihood of angry reactions by Palestinian Muslims.

The new synagogue was suggested by the Rabbi of Haifa, Yoshaf Hacohen. According to Hacohen, this project would “enable Jews to gain a foothold at the Temple Mount.” In the view of Muslims world-wide, a “foothold” would only be a prelude to demolishing Al-Aqsa Mosque and building a synagogue in its place.

Officially, successive Israeli governments have refrained from taking concrete steps to alter the status quo at Haram Al-Sharif, prevailing since Israel occupied the area in 1967, mainly owing to the overwhelming religious sensitivity surrounding the issue.

However, with Jerusalem currently the focus of Palestinian-Israeli negotiations, and with the Palestinians and the Muslim world insisting that the city become the capital of a Palestinian State in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, it seems the Israeli government is beginning to reconsider its erstwhile policy on that matter.

A manifestation of that change of policy on the Haram Al-Sharif occurred at Camp David when Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak reportedly proposed to Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat that in the context of a final settlement for the Old City of Jerusalem, Israel should control the area “underneath” the two mosques on the Haram Al-Sharif property.

Barak never explained exactly what he meant by controlling the area underneath the mosques, a task which he apparently left to Jewish extremists and the High Rabbinate to clarify “at the appropriate time.”

Jews pray at the Western Wall, behind which the Dome of the Rock and the al-Aqsa Mosque compound can be seen. The mosque compound, the third holiest site in Islam, was reopened recently after visitors were barred from entry during the week preceding because of minor clashes between Jewish extremists and Muslims.

The proposal reportedly shocked the Palestinian leadership as it was the first time an Israeli Prime Minister came so close to articulating Jewish extremists’ interest in demolishing the al-Aqsa Mosque.

Arafat was said to have firmly told Barak and U.S. President Bill Clinton that no Arab or Muslim leader could sign away the al-Aqsa Mosque, a stance reiterated by Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak several times in recent days. Predictably, Palestinian leaders from across the political spectrum understood the message inherent in the ominous proposal.

“This is a criminal proposal–a direct invitation to war,” said Jerusalem’s mufti, Ikrema Sabri, in reaction to the proposal, adding that “these criminal intentions could set the entire region on fire because Muslims are not going to sit idle while their holy places are taken away from them.”

Sabri told reporters in Jerusalem that al-Aqsa Mosque “was, is and always will be an exclusively Islamic holy place that belongs to the entire Muslim nation. It is an integral part of the Muslim faith.”

An even stronger reaction came from Hamas, whose founder and leader, Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, warned that “the destruction of al-Aqsa Mosque would be followed by the destruction of Israel. We’ll set Israel on fire if they dare to attack al-Aqsa Mosque.”

In addition, the League of Palestine’s Islamic Religious Scholars issued a strongly worded statement on 10 August, warning Jews against playing with fire.

Calling the proposal a blatant challenge to a billion-and-a-half Muslims which flies in the face of all religious, moral and legal norms, the League’s statement warned Muslims that al-Aqsa Mosque was in grave danger.

Attempts by extremist Jewish groups to enter the area are not new to the Palestinians. The most notorious of these occurred in 1990 when an organization called Solomon’s Group entered the Haram Al-Sharif, an action that led to skirmishes and intervention by Israeli police in violation of unofficial arrangements for protecting the area. As clashes escalated, 17 Muslims were killed.

The current showdown over the Haram Al-Sharif serves to highlight the religious dimension of the conflict in Palestine. This situation was further complicated by the recent manifestly racist slurs by the mentor of the Israeli ultra-orthodox Shas Party in which he called Arabs “snakes” and said that God regretted having created them.

PA gives green light to
“beauty contest” in West Bank

Occupied Jerusalem - The Palestinian Authority is reportedly allowing a tourist company with Israeli connections to organize a so-called “beauty contest” in Ramallah on the West Bank in October, according to reports published in the Palestinian Arabic press.

The Ramallah-based Arabic daily al-Ayyam reported on 10 August that the PA tourism ministry approved plans to that effect. The paper reported that the ministry agreed in principle to allow the organizers to go ahead. However, the tourism ministry denied any involvement, accusing the organizer of lying.

“Those people are spreading lies about us...we have never given them any consent in this regard and we published a statement to that effect in the main dailies,” said Tawfik Abu Eita, the acting minister of tourism.

“I can assure you that the Ministry of Tourism has nothing to do with these ads,” he added during a telephone interview from his office in Bethlehem.

Abu Eita, however, did not rule out the possibility that the organizer received an okay from some influential person in the tourism ministry.

“He might have got an okay behind our back...everything is possible, but this okay will be worthless,” said Abu Eita.

The paper said the contest would be held on 10 October, nearly two weeks after the erstwhile deadline for the declaration of Palestinian Statehood. The organizer of the contest, a private tourist company named Magic Touch, defended the organization of a beauty contest in the mostly conservative Palestinian society.

A company official argued that the organization of the contest would be conducive to the establishment of the Palestinian State.

Asked how promiscuous acts as such would or could expedite the establishment of Palestinian statehood, the official said, “It depends on how you view it...you think it is profanity, we think it is art.”

The official, Hussam Mayeer, was quoted as saying that “there is no conflict between Palestinian traditions and beauty queen contests.”

However, several Palestinian feminist leaders lambasted the planned event as “visual prostitution” and “injurious to women’s dignity, honour and image.”

“This is a blind imitation of the West….this is totally unacceptable,” said Zainab Ghenemi, Public Relations Officer at the PA Ministry of Planning and International Cooperation.

“I don’t know how this nudity will advance the cause of women in Palestine...I fail to understand how stripping women from their clothes in full daylight and exposing their fine parts to the hungry eyes of spectators can be considered a form of art.”

Palestinian lawmaker Hanan Ashrawi also criticized plans to that effect saying, “This is not going to advance the cause of women; it will only assert negative stereotypes about women.”

Ashrawi indirectly accused the organizers of viewing women as merely sexual objects.

“I don’t know why we should seek to perpetuate the concept that the woman is only a commodity.”

The Mufti of Palestine, Ikrema Sabri, castigated the holding of beauty contests as “a manifestation of a promiscuous culture.”

“Islam, and I understand other religions as well, prohibits this form of nudity and promiscuity; I call on the organizer to reconsider this vile act.”

Sabri, a PA official, reportedly pressed the tourism ministry to cancel the event, arguing that the holding of such profanity in Palestine–not far away from the Aqsa Mosque–would put the Palestinian people to shame and besmirch its holy struggle for liberation and freedom.

Other Palestinian Islamic leaders also lashed out at the PA for allowing or at least acquiescing to the scheme. Sheikh Nayef Rajoub opined that those who are promoting promiscuity in Palestine were Zionist tools to spread moral decay and corruption among our people.

“Their goal is to corrupt the Palestinian woman and strip her of chastity and dignity,” he said, adding that “this has always been an undeclared Zionist goal...and now we have Palestinians who are readily willing to carry it out.”

Rajoub pointed out that Israel and Zionists in general feel deeply satisfied when they see Palestinian women cast off their modesty and Hijab. He pointed out that the former Israeli premier Shimon Peres cited the number of Gazan women who walked in the streets without their head-dress as proof of the success of the peace process.

Since its establishment nearly six years ago, the PA has been encouraging lax lifestyles among Palestinians. Three years ago, the PA, in cooperation with Israeli and Austrian firms, built a large casino in Jericho, which observers say has ruined the lives of many Israeli and Palestinians alike.

Last week, the mayor of Um al-Fahem, Sheikh Ra’ed Salah, said in an interview with the Hebron-based Hebron Times, published on 9 August, that scores of Arab businessmen lost all their money and were forced to turn to charity groups in his town for food.

Open letter to the people of Gaza

Editor: Following is a letter to the Palestinian People forwarded by the family of the Late Dr. David C. Dorr concerning his life and service and his appreciation for the friendship, love and hospitality found among the people of Gaza. On behalf of the Palestinian People, The Palestine Times would like to convey its deep condolences to the family of Dr. Dorr, who dedicated several years of his life to serving Palestinians in Gaza.

Dr. David C. Dorr, who worked as a surgeon at the Baptist Hospital in Gaza during the period 1959-1968, died on Father’s Day, 18 June 2000 surrounded by his family. One of his dying wishes was to extend to the many friends, acquaintances and in general to the people of the Gaza Strip his thanks and appreciation for the warmth, kindness and hospitality extended to him and his family during his time among them.

There were many Palestinian children born through the skill of his hands and many men and women alive today because of his surgical gifts given to serve mankind. Even those who knew and worked with him may not have known the road that brought him to Palestine.

David Dorr was born in Washington, DC to Colonel Philip Dorr and his wife Ruby.

Upon graduation from high school, David entered Mars Hill College and then in August of 1943 enlisted in the United States Air Corps. He served as a radar operator on a B-29. For 30 missions over Japan and helping his teammates under fire, he was awarded the Distinguished Flying Cross and the Air Medal with five oak leaf clusters.

In a diary, which he kept during this time, he tells of an unusual life-changing experience. It was on a visit to the Air Corps cemetery that he saw some crewmen taking a picture of a recently dug grave. “We’re taking a picture of our friend’s grave for his mother,” they said. David was deeply moved. He thought of the son that would not return home and the mother that would have only a picture of a marker in a military cemetery to remind her of him.

“If I am fortunate enough to return home, “ he writes in his diary, “I must do something worthwhile with my life.”

He did not forget this resolve. When he returned to the States, he entered Johns Hopkins University in Baltimore, Maryland, and then was accepted in the Johns Hopkins Medical School.

At this time, he married Roberta Kells who had similar aspirations of service.

He noted and was interested that Southern Baptists were opening hospitals in various countries. David volunteered to go and serve where his skills were most needed as soon as he was finished with his internship and residency.

However, tragedy struck. It was at the end of his second year of residency that doctors discovered he had Hodgkin’s disease. The little family with two small boys and a new daughter were devastated. All thoughts of Christian service had to be canceled. He was advised to stay on and complete the four-year surgical residency while under medical observation. There was no treatment for Hodgkin’s at that time.

Miraculously, through prayer and trusting obedience to God, the Hodgkin’s disease disappeared. Just as the residency programme came to an end and he was ready to go into practice, he learned that Baptists had taken over a surgical hospital on the Gaza Strip and needed surgeons. He packed up his family and moved to the Gaza Strip where he spent the next 11 years working among Palestinian refugees. The years spent in Gaza, including the experiences shared in the 1967 war, were some of the most rewarding years of his life.

Following his time in Gaza, he transferred to a new hospital that had just opened in the Arabian Peninsula.

In 1975, when Roberta developed a serious back problem that resulted in surgery, the family had to leave Yemen for the United States. They did not return to the Washington, DC area but rather settled in East Tennessee. David served in many hospitals in East Tennessee and finally retired in 1988 owing to the complications of Parkinson’s disease.

So it is with great warmth and thanks that his family conveys these sentiments to the people of Gaza whom he dearly loved and cared so much about.

God Bless you,

Philip Dorr,

Hamas delegation visits
Dr. Rantisi in PA prison

Gaza - A delegation of the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, has visited on Monday, 28 August, Dr. Abdul Aziz Rantisi, Hamas prominent leader in Gaza who has been detained by the Palestinian Authority police since the end of July for delivering press statements criticizing the Palestinian political situation and the Camp David talks.

Sheikh Ismail Hanneya, director of the office of Hamas founder Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, said the visit to Dr. Rantisi was made in coordination with PA police commander Ghazi Jibali and its aim was to ensure he was in a safe condition behind bars. He added that Rantisi enjoyed a state of high morale and good health. Hanneya said that Rantisi spent his time reading the holy Qur’an and a number of political and other books.

The delegation conveyed greetings of Sheikh Yassin and Hamas members to Rantisi along with their prayers for his release, Hanneya said, adding that Rantisi reciprocated the greetings and affirmed that victory came with patience. Hanneya affirmed that senior PA officials had assured Hamas that the case of Dr. Rantisi would soon be resolved, especially since he did not commit any legal or non-legal violation and that his statements fell in line with freedom of expression.

The Hamas Movement stressed the importance of releasing Dr. Rantisi and all remaining detainees in PA jails, he said, adding that an agreement was reached with Jibali allowing family members of Dr. Rantisi to visit him especially since none of them have been allowed to visit him from the time of his arrest.

Sheikh Yassin: Israel will
disappear in a few decades

Occupied Jerusalem - Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin has predicted that the Zionist regime (Israel) will cease to exist in a few decades. Speaking on a Sharja satellite TV programme on 21 August, Sheikh Yassin said the disappearance of Israel was a religious and historical eventuality.

“The Qur’an says that very clearly in Surat Isra’a…I have no doubt that this will happen in a few decades, certainly this century,” said Yassin during a special show marking the passage of 31 years since an Australian Zionist terrorist set fire to al-Masjidul Aqsa.

The Palestinian Islamist leader pointed out that he was optimistic that Arabs and Muslims were increasingly aware of the grave dangers surrounding the Aqsa Mosque, Islam’s third holiest site.

“The Zionists want to destroy the Mosque in order to build their mythological temple…this should be clear to us all.” Yassin stressed that the path of resistance and Jihad was the only means to liberate Palestine, saying that this method proved its validity in South Lebanon where a small band of dedicated and faithful men drove the mighty Zionist army out of their country.

He also called on Arab regimes to wake up, because the Zionists aim to swallow the entire Arab world, not just Palestine. “There is going to be a larger confrontation, and we’d better be prepared for it now.”

The Mufti of Jerusalem and the whole of Palestine, Ikrema Sabri spoke during the same show, saying that Zionist attacks on Jerusalem’s Haram al-Sharif increased in frequency in the last few years.

He pointed out that the recent decision by the Zionist regime’s high rabbinate to discuss the prospects of building a synagogue within the confines of the Haram al-Sharif signalled a serious shift in Zionist designs against Islam’s third holiest shrine.

Meanwhile, thousands of Islamists continued to march in the streets of the West Bank, Gaza and Jordan as well as other parts of the Arab-Muslim world, warning against Zionist designs against the Aqsa Mosque and al-Quds al-Sharif in general.

In Nablus, the largest town in the West Bank, thousands of Hamas supporters took to the streets following the Friday congregational prayers on 25 August, shouting Islamic slogans and demanding a stronger Arab-Muslim stance on Jerusalem. The estimated 5,000 demonstrators carried placards reading “Yes to our Aqsa, no to their temple.”

Hamas spokesman in Nablus, Jamal Salim, warned Muslims all over the world that Islam’s third holiest shrine was facing “real dangers” saying the “Jews are waiting for the opportune moment to demolish al-Aqsa.”

“Their frequent encroachments are feelers aimed at testing our resolve...we must demonstrate to them by deeds and words that this is the ultimate red line.”

A stronger warning came from Sheikh Hamed al-Beitawi, the head of the League of Palestine Muslim Ulema, the Rabitat Ulema Falastin. He said Israel and the Jews would regret the day they were born if they decided to tamper with al-Masjidul Aqsa.

“The destruction of al-Masjidul Aqsa would ultimately lead to the destruction of Israel,” he said.

The demonstrators called on Arabs and Muslims everywhere to take to the streets and to hold protests to demonstrate their concern for Jerusalem, the first Qibla of Islam and the third holiest Islamic shrine.

Before departing, the huge multitude declared in unison their commitment to al-Masjidul Aqsa, swearing by God Almighty to defend and protect the Mosque with their blood and souls.

Several large demonstrations had earlier taken place in several towns in the West Bank where pledges were made to defend al-Aqsa with blood and souls.

Concomitant demonstrations by Jordanian Islamists took place in Amman, Irbid and Zarqa where members of the Islamic Action Front and the Muslim Brotherhood took to the streets after Friday prayers, warning the Islamic Umma against the dangers surrounding Jerusalem and al-Masjidul Aqsa.

On 25 August, as many as 10,000 Jordanian Islamists marched in Amman, warning the Palestinian Authority against compromising Muslim rights in Jerusalem. Several Islamic speakers addressed the protesters, including Abdul Majid Ethneibat, Director-General of the Islamic Action Front, and Abdul Latif Arabiyat, the former speaker of the Jordanian parliament.

Arabiyat warned against normalization with the Zionist regime, saying “normalization means lending legitimacy to the Zionist occupation of Jerusalem and al-Masjidul Aqsa.”

It is expected that other protests will take place in the next few weeks and months despite American and Zionist pressure on the obsequious Jordanian government to suppress anti-Zionist protests, particularly those organized by the Islamists.

Chronology of Jewish attacks and conspiracies

Since the seizure of Al-Quds al-Sharif (holy Jerusalem) in 1967, Jewish groups, often through direct or indirect connivance with the government of Israel, have been attempting relentlessly to destroy al-Masjidul Aqsa (Aqsa Mosque), using a variety of vicious means and tactics. These range from attempting to destroy the Mosque using a helicopter gunship to digging a tunnel beneath the Mosque, which happened in the summer of 1996.

The most serious Jewish crime so far against the Mosque took place on 21 August 1969, when an Australian Christian tourist named Dennis Rohan attempted to set al-Aqsa Mosque on fire and in the process destroyed the beautiful and priceless wood and ivory pulpit sent from Aleppo by the Muslim ruler Saladin, as well as damaging the Mosque’s ceiling. Although the Israeli government labelled this an isolated attack by a deranged individual, in fact the arson attack prompted the United Nations Security Council to condemn Israel’s failure to curb terrorist attacks on Islam’s third holiest shrine.

The fact that the UN felt it necessary to take this step demonstrates that this action was a deliberate act of aggression carried out with the unmistakable acquiescence of the Israeli government.

But this is not the only act of Israeli aggression against the sacred Mosque, which is the third holiest shrine in Islam after the sacred mosque in Mecca and the Prophet’s Mosque in Medina.

The following are some of the crimes and ill-designs carried out by Jewish terrorists against the Aqsa Mosque, the Dome of the Rock and other Muslim holy places in Jerusalem:

7 June 1967: The occupation authorities confiscated the keys of the Western Gate, also known as Bab El-Magharba, immediately after Israeli troops seized the town from the fleeing Jordanians.

9 June 1967: The congregational Friday prayer was not held on orders from the occupation authorities. That was the first time the Juma’a prayer was not held since the liberation of Jerusalem from the hands of the Crusades in 1167 AD. On 19 October 1990, the Juma’a prayer was delayed for two hours because the occupation authorities denied Muslim worshippers entry to the Haram compound.

August 1967: Chief Chaplain of the Armed Forces, Shlomo Goren (later Israel’s Chief Rabbi) lead 50 armed extremists onto the grounds of Haram al-Sharif. “It is a holy commandment,” Goren said, “for Jews to go to the Muslim grounds, which Jews call the Temple Mount.” Writing in an Israeli publication, Eti Ronel reported, “Many rabbis, including members of the Council of the Chief Rabbinate, support..Jewish sovereignty” over Haram al-Sharif.

21 August 1969: David Rohan, an Australian Christian tourist, set al-Aqsa Mosque on fire destroying the beautiful and priceless wood and ivory pulpit sent from Aleppo by the Muslim ruler Saladin, and also damaging the Mosque’s ceiling. This arson attack prompted the United Nations Security Council to condemn Israel’s failure to curb terrorist attacks on Islam’s third holiest shrine.

16 November 1969: The Israeli occupation authorities seized the Fakhriyya Corner on the south-western side of the Haram Al-Sharif.

19 December 1969: Four months later, a group of militant Jews storm their way into Haram al-Sahrif in order, they claim, “to conduct Hanukkah prayers.”

In the 1970s, members of the radical Gush Eminum movement began holding underground secret meetings where they plotted to blow up the Dome of the Rock. They believed this action would compel God to send the long-awaited Messiah.

14 August 1970: The Gershon Solomon group, an ultra-fanatic group dedicated to the so-called rebuilding of the Temple of Solomon on the site of Al-Aqsa Mosque after it is demolished, forcibly entered the premises of the Haram, but were repulsed by Muslims. The confrontation resulted in tens of worshipers being injured by Israeli troop gunfire.

3 March 1971: Gershon Solomon leads Temple Mount Faithful followers onto Haram al-Sharif. After struggling with Palestinian guards, they are expelled. Three years later (3 March 1974) Solomon again, with followers, storms the Mosque. Again (14 July 1978) Solomon leads militant Jews onto the Islamic holy grounds. Palestinians stage protests. Israeli troops hurl tear gas to quell the rioting.

19 April 1980: A group of Jewish Rabbis and sages held a semi-secret conference devoted to exploring ways and means “to liberate the Temple Mount from Muslim hands.”

10 August 1980: Three hundred Gush Emunim fanatics, heavily armed, overcome Palestinian police and storm the grounds, but are later dispersed. A month later (15 September 1980) armed Gush Emunim settlers associated with Stanley Goldfoot and the Temple Mount Faithful again force their way onto the Mosque grounds. After scuffling with police they are evicted.

28 August 1980: The Israeli occupation authorities dig a tunnel right underneath the Mosque.

30 March 1982: Numerous letters were sent to Muslim Waqf authorities urging them to abandon the Temple Mount and warning them of the dire consequence of their “usurpation of our Temple.” The letters were written in Hebrew, English, French, Spanish and Polish.

11 April 1982: Alan Goodman, an Israeli citizen with a U.S. passport, marches into al-Aqsa with an M-16 rifle and opens fire on worshippers, killing 2 Palestinians and wounding close to 60 others. In November 1997, the Israeli government releases Goodman. Unrepentant, Goodman boasts, “I fulfilled my mission.” Since Goodman’s murder spree, more than 20 religious fanatics (Meir Kahane among them) have been caught trying to blow up al-Aqsa Mosque.

20 May 1982: Several Zionist organizations sent death threats to Waqf officials.

25 July 1982: Yoel Lerner, a member of the militant Meir Kahane Kach movement, storms the Mosque grounds with plans to dynamite and destroy the Dome of the Rock.

10 March 1983: Armed Gush Emunim fanatics climb the walls onto Haram al-Sharif attempting to overcome security guards and take the Mosque by storm. They have in their possession large quantities of explosives, automatic rifles and pistols. Twenty-nine are charged and held for trial.

26 March 1983: The main entrance to Jerusalem’s Waqf department collapsed as a result of Israeli excavations underneath it.

21 September 1983: An Israeli court acquits the 29 Jewish terrorists who six months earlier had laid siege on the Mosque.

27 January 1984: In the most ambitious plot to dynamite and destroy the Mosque, Jewish terrorists, armed with 250 pounds of explosives, including dozens of grenades, boxes of dynamite and 12 rounds of mortar, attempt to dynamite and destroy the Mosque. They are led by Rabbi Moshe Levinger, one of the most militant of Jewish extremists.

Also in 1984, Rabbi Israel Ariel founded the periodical “Tzfia” (Looking Ahead) to provide a public forum for discussing the rebuilding of the Third Temple. In 1986 he opened a museum in the Old City where visitors can see the vessels, musical instruments and priestly vestments being made for use in the Third Temple.

21 August 1985: The Israeli police permitted Jewish extremists to hold prayers within the confines of the Haram premises.

1994: The Israelis appoint Meir Davidson, a senior official of Ateret Cohanim, as municipal adviser of Palestinian properties. This signals the Israeli government will work closely with an organization whose aim is the destruction of the Mosque.

4 August 1986: A group of Rabbis issued a final ruling allowing Jews to pray at the Haram Al-Sharif and demanded the establishment of a synagogue in the area.

12 May 1988: Israeli soldiers opened fire on a peaceful Muslim march at the Haram, killing and wounding about 100 Palestinians.

8 October 1990: A group of Gush members broke into Al-Aqsa with the intention of laying a foundation for the Third Temple on the Haram yard. When Muslims tried to prevent the intruders, the army attacked the worshippers, killing 21 Muslims and injuring close to 150 others. In the meantime, the Israeli government continued a programme of digging and excavating under Al-Aqsa in an attempt to destroy the Mosque in a way that would make it look like an accident.

25 July 1995: The Israeli High Court of Justice issued a ruling, allowing Jews to pray at the “Temple Mount.” The decision sparked widespread protests among Muslims.

September 1996: Ateret Cohanim, funded largely through tax-exempt dollars donated by rich American Jews, including Miami millionaire Irving Moskowitz, opened a tunnel (excavated in secret night-time operations) that runs the length of the Al-Aqsa complex. The controversial tunnel sparked intense fighting which claimed the lives of 60 Palestinians and 15 Israeli soldiers. Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu proudly visited the tunnel, as have fundamentalist Christian leaders.

18 October 1998: Gershon Solomon, with followers waving Israeli flags and blowing rams’ horns, mount a ramp to the Mosque grounds. “The time has come to rebuild the Jewish Temple,” said Solomon. To underscore this point, Solomon parked a flatbed truck carrying a 4-ton marble “cornerstone” for that new temple near an Old City gate.

10 August 2000: Hundreds of East Jerusalem’s Muslims repulsed several attempts by extremist Jews to forcibly enter the Haram al-Sharif, the plaza housing the Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock. Scores of extremists led by Gershon Solomon, head of the so-called Temple Mount Faithful, attempted twice to force their way onto the Haram al-Sharif but were repulsed by Muslims who converged to the site, heeding a call to that effect from Muslim Wakf officials. Wakf officials reportedly warned Israeli police officials that “lots of blood would be spilt” if the extremists were allowed into the Haram al-Sharif.
