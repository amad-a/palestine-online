Zionist police gang up on peaceful demonstrators in Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

Underscoring Jewish racist attitudes toward non-Jews, Israeli police attacked and violently suppressed a peaceful demonstration by thousands of so-called Israeli- Arabs (Palestinians who hold the Israeli citizenship) in Jerusalem on 24 November, injuring scores of protesters.
The protesters, who included local councils’ heads, Knesset members and civic leaders as well as ordinary citizens arrived in buses in Jerusalem from all across Palestinian towns occupied by Zionist gangs in 1948. And their goal was to protest the financial plight of their towns and villages and official Israeli economic discrimination against the 1200,000-strong Arab community in Israel.
However, as the estimated three thousand protesters sought to make their voice heard, by chanting slogans against government policy, the police attacked them savagely, using batons and other riot gear, injuring as many as twenty demonstrators.
Among the injured were Muhammad Zeidan, Chairman of the Arab leadership’s monitoring committee, which is comprised of council heads, MPs, and leading figures in Israel’s Palestinian community.
Moreover, Knesset Member Ahmed Teibi was hospitalized after police reportedly beat him.
However, in another telling development, Teibi was attacked at his hospital bed by several thugs from the terrorist group, known as Kach, who sought to beat and humiliate him.
According to Teibi, Police arrived at the hospital on three hours after the hoodlums had arrived and made their point.
The situation opposite the Prime Minister’s office, where the demonstrators gathered, became particularly nasty around 11:00 am, as policemen equipped with batons and truncheons abruptly and without any warning attacked demonstrators head-on, injuring Teibi, and two other Arab Knesset member, Muhammad Baraka and Azmi Beshara.
One demonstrator suffered a broken hand, while another fainted and was transferred to hospital.
“The police brutally beat the demonstrators, council heads, women and Knesset members. I was in the front row and I was punched and took several blows in the stomach and on the shoulders and arms from batons,” Teibi told the Israeli state-run radio on 25 November.
Teibi accused the police of deliberately and premeditatedly attacking the peaceful demonstrators.
“They did it with vindictiveness and hatred as if they were seeking to settle accounts with us,” adding that “They wouldn’t behave in this way if the demonstrators were Jews.”
Abdul Malik Dahamshe, another Arab Knesset member, also criticized Police brutality toward “Arab citizens of Israel.”
“When the police faces Arab demonstrators, it like facing enemy forces…but this will not lessen our determination to challenge this enduring and institutionalized racist discrimination against us.”
Talab al Saneh, who represent the Arabs of the southern region of Palestine, the Negev, said the police were behaving in a disgraceful way.
“Instead of sending officials to listen to us and hear our demands, they sent their crack police to beat us ….they think that the police repression is the solution, but they are wrong, there are real problems to be solved.”
Later on, the United Arab list and Democratic Front for Peace and Equality, the two main political Arab parties in Israel, issued a joint statement, denouncing police brutality.
The statement said the Zionist police acted in a brutal and violent manner, calling the repression of the demonstration a “disgrace to Israeli democracy.”
Meanwhile, the Arab sector in Israel observed a general strike on 24 November during which All-municipal services came to a halt.
Earlier, several Arab leaders representing various political orientations had threatened to dispatch delegates to Arab capitals in an effort to recruit financial assistance to their financially broken towns and villages.
The root cause of this enduring crisis, which engulfs the entirety of the Arab sector, is the official Israeli discrimination against Arab in the Jewish state.
According Professor Majid Haja Yahya, Professor of Sociology at Haifa University, this discrimination didn’t start yesterday and was not a passing phenomenon.
“Discrimination against the Arab community in Israel is a build-in phenomenon that goes back to the establishment of Israel itself over a half century ago,” he said.
He pointed out that while Arabs in the Jewish state makes up to 22% of the population, they only receive less than five per cent of the financial resources allocated to local council in the Jewish state.

Final status talks open amid sharp differences

Occupied Jerusalem – PT

Israel and the Palestinian Authority began the so-called final status talks earlier this month amid sharp differences over the most fundamental and sensitive issues of the Arab-Israeli conflict—Jerusalem, the refugees and Jewish settlements.

The two-hour session of formal talks, which took place in Ramallah on the West Bank on 10 November, was aimed at trying to map out an agenda for hard negotiations the two sides hope to wrap up in ten months.

However, while the Ramallah talks were described by both parties as “practical and business-like”, the policy statements delivered by the heads of the two delegations, Yasser Abed Rabbou and Odid Eran, revealed and underscored the clearly wide gap between the two sides’ perceptions and stances on virtually all outstanding contentious issues.

In his speech, the text of which was later circulated to reporters, Abed Rabbou demanded full Israeli withdrawal to the 4 June 1967 borders, calling this “an absolute requirement for peace.”

“Without the return by Israel of all of the land acquired by war in 1967, including Jerusalem, our efforts will amount to no more than deferral of crises.”

Furthermore, Abed Rabbou pointed out that in order for peace to take root and prevail, a prompt end should be put to Jewish settlement expansion.

More to the point, Abed Rabbou reminded the Israeli delegation that the refugees’ enduring cause was a paramount matter for the Palestinians.

“The Arab-Israeli conflict,” he said “did not start in 1967,” adding that the UN Security Council resolutions regarding refugees ought to be carried out, or else there would be no peace in the region.

For his part, the head of the Israeli delegation, Odid Eran, reiterated the traditional Israeli position on the final status issues.

Israel, he said, would not revert to the 1967 borders; Jerusalem would remain “Israel’s capital”; and the refugees would have to be resettled in host countries where they are now residing.

Both Eran and Abed Rabbou wrangled sharply but politely over the meaning of resolutions 242 and 338.

Abed Rabbou indicated that the resolutions were in effect the bedrock foundation of the peace process, while Eran sought to either downplay or dilute their relevance.

Earlier, Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak remarked that the two resolutions did not apply to the West Bank, Gaza and East Jerusalem, arguing that the resolutions applied only to occupied territory belonging to sovereign countries such as Egypt and Syria.

The statement, later echoed by several Israeli officials and repeated by Barak himself in Paris on 8 November, drew angry reactions from Palestinian leaders.

Yasser Abed Rabbou described the remarks as “a grave distortion of the very concept of the peace process.”

The former Chief Palestinian negotiator, Sa’eb Ureikat, said the statements amounted to “a grave and scandalous mendacity.”

“If [resolutions] 242 and 338 do not apply to the West Bank, then what is the reference for the peace process?” asked Ureikat.

Even PA Chairman Yasser Arafat, known for his generally circumspect public reactions to public statements by Israeli officials, felt provoked by Barak’s remarks.

“I want to remind Barak that when we sat down together at Madrid (peace conference in 1991), we did so on the basis of United Nations resolutions 242 and 338 and the principle of land-for-peace,” Arafat told journalists on 8 November in Paris where he was attending the Socialist Internationale conference.

For his part, Eran sought to re-present Barak’s statements on the applicability of 242 and 338 concerning the occupied Palestinian territories in a more diplomatic and euphemistic manner.

He said Israel recognized that the two resolutions constituted the ultimate terms of reference of the peace process.

However, he added that “it is no secret that the two sides hold different interpretations of the meaning of the two resolutions.”

Eran’s statement was in fact a euphemistic reiteration of Barak’s set of “NOs”, which include no return to the 1967 borders, Jerusalem remaining Israel’s capital, and the bulk of settlements remaining under Israeli sovereignty.

Talks unlikely to produce substantial results

Having illustrated the wide—some say irreconcilable—gaps in their respective position, the two delegations agreed to meet again on Thursday, 11 November.

However, given the enormous distance between their two respective stances and Israel’s recalcitrant refusal to meet the very minimum of Palestinian expectations, much less aspirations, it seems unlikely that the talks will produce substantial results under the current circumstances.

This, observers believe, may eventually lead to the opening of an alternative secret track involving Barak and Arafat.

Needless to say, however, this kind of track is dreaded by most Palestinians, who never recovered from the jolt of Oslo. However, it is amply clear that Israel, in particular Mr. Barak and the Israeli intelligence community, prefer to have secret talks with Yasser Arafat.

The rationale behind this strategy is that in secret talks, Arafat could be cajoled into giving much more concessions in secret than in public. Indeed, the secret channel seems already to have started somewhere in Europe, according to the London-based Saudi daily, al-Shark al-Awsat. It is certain that more will be revealed in days and weeks to come.

Redeployment: the saga continues

Occupied Jerusalem - The hand-over by Israel of 5% of the West Bank to the Palestinian Authority, as stipulated in the revised Wye Memorandum agreement signed in Sharm el-Sheikh two months ago, has been postponed pending the Palestinian leadership’s consent to the withdrawal maps.

So far, several rounds of high-level talks, including a previously unannounced late-night meeting between Arafat and Barak in Tel Aviv on 14 November, along with the so-called “bridging efforts” of U.S. envoy Dennis Ross, have failed to get the process going.

The PA had hoped that Ross, who arrived in Israel on 15 November, would convince Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak to revise the maps in a way that would allow for more Palestinian population centres to come under PA civilian control.

However, far from convincing—or even seeking to convince—the Israelis to revise the maps, Ross actually made remarks acquiescing to the Israeli position.

That position, as stated several times by Foreign Minister David Levy, dictates that “Israel alone has the exclusive right to decide the location of land to be transferred to the Palestinians.” In other words, any Israeli redeployment in the West Bank does not require Palestinian approval.

The PA rejected this interpretation, saying that the Israeli attitude amounted to “a take-it-or-leave-it offer rather than a bilateral agreement between two sides.”

“Show me one article signed since the declaration of principles of Oslo and up until the Sharm el-Sheikh memorandum which specifies that Israel make these decisions unilaterally,” protested Palestinian negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat.

He further pointed out that while negotiating the Sharm el-Sheikh agreement, the two sides had agreed that the maps would be subject to both parties’ consideration, since “otherwise, this would be dictation not negotiation.”

Palestinian objection to the maps (which does not amount to a rejection) is not unreasonable. Three per cent of the West Bank territory slated to become Area (B), where the PA exercises civilian responsibilities while Israel maintains overall security authority, has actually turned out to be barren desert land and largely unpopulated.

Likewise, the remaining 2% (of the 5% to be covered in the current redeployment) earmarked to become Area (A), where the PA maintains civilian and security responsibilities, also turned out to be isolated pockets of territory with little or no integration into the autonomous enclaves.

Redeployment maps insulting to Palestinians

The Israeli redeployment maps, which had earlier been endorsed by the Knesset and the Israeli Cabinet, proved embarrassing for some Palestinian officials.

Palestinian Legislative Council speaker Ahmed Qrei lambasted the maps as “an insult to our sense of dignity.”

“Our people are not going to feel a change; what can we tell them?” he complained.

One Palestinian commentator, quoted by the Israeli English daily, the Jerusalem Post, on 14 November, spoke of Israel wanting the PA to safeguard the well-being of snakes and scorpions, as the areas suggested for transfer to the PA are virtually unpopulated.

Notwithstanding, Barak remained adamant in the face of Palestinian protestations.

“The maps have already been decided upon and have been approved by both the Cabinet and the Knesset. The transfer of land will take place according to them,” Barak said with a whiff of insolence during a tour of the Negev on 15 November.

Moreover, some Israeli government officials accused Arafat of deliberately stalling the talks, calling his refusal to accept the redeployment maps as “just another ploy to get the U.S. more involved in the details of the talks.”

This may well be true. Arafat, after all, views the United States as the main (if not the sole) guarantor against the imposition by Israel of its will on the PA under the rubric and in the context of “direct, bilateral negotiations.”

Furthermore, it is increasingly clear that while the current deadlock is considered an interim-phase issue, its relevance to the final status talks is unmistakable. The two sides, particularly the Israelis, seem to be quite keen about this dimension.

Psychologically, Israel does not want to appear to back off in the face of Palestinian objections, even though a step as such would be perfectly innocuous for Israel, since the transfer of the bulk of the remaining Palestinian villages and hamlets (now under Israeli control) to PA control would ostensibly strengthen, not weaken, the Israeli bargaining position in the final status talks.

Indeed, on many occasions, Israeli officials from both the Likud and Labor parties have boasted that Israel was letting the Palestinians go free since up to 90% of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza Strip were ruled by the PA, not by Israel (the claim is, of course, erroneous since the Israeli army continues to operate freely throughout the West Bank and Gaza and control roads linking various Palestinian population centres, including those under exclusive PA administration).

Some observers believe the Israeli tactic is to get tough with Arafat on the remaining unresolved interim issues (Ureikat says there are 34 of them) in order to lower his expectations of the final status talks. In other words, Israel would like to retain as many cards as possible from the interim phase in order to use them as bargaining chips to extract concessions from the PA during the final status talks. Thus, the concept of “linkage” (linking the implementation of the interim phase with the final status talks), which had been proposed by Barak and rejected by the PA, would be realized, de facto, at least.

For its part, the PA strategy is exactly the opposite and is focused on gaining as much land as possible before hard negotiations on fundamental issues begin. This strategy, however, does not seem to be working effectively, with only an estimated 5% of the West Bank now under full PA administration and another 12% under joint PA-Israeli control.

Barak’s predecessor, Benyamin Netanyahu, had insisted that Israel alone would decide the scope of Oslo’s third and final phase of redeployment. This, he said, would not exceed 1% of the West Bank, in contrast to the PA-declared position that the said phase would cover the entirety of the West Bank minus East Jerusalem, the settlements and Israeli army military positions.

Barak’s accommodating approach to the settlers, as exemplified in the recent rendering of legitimacy to the bulk of settlements and settlement outposts erected during Netanyahu’s term of office, more than testifies that the differences between him and his predecessor are over shape rather than substance.

Meanwhile, in parallel to the interim-phase talks, Israel and the PA have so far held five “exploratory” rounds of the so-called final status talks.

The last of these rounds took place in Ramallah on 22 November, and the Israeli representative, Odid Eran, who also heads the Israeli team to the interim-phase talks (which suggests that Israel considers the final status talks a mere continuation of the interim phase), presented the Israeli views on the final status issues.

The PA representative, Yasser Abed Rabbou, will present the Palestinian views in the next round of talks, expected to take place during the last week of November.

According to observers, while the two sides try to conduct the talks in a businesslike atmosphere, especially in the presence of the media, it is clear from their statements that they not only talk at cross purposes but think at cross purposes as well.

And given the fundamental differences between the two sides, it is unlikely that the Eran-Abed Rabbou talks will culminate in a breakthrough in the foreseeable future, if at all.

Hamas distances itself from bombing

Occupied Jerusalem - The pipe bomb blast in the Israeli town of Netanya on 7 November, which slightly hurt around 20 people, continued to puzzle observers as to the identity of the perpetrators. Indeed, not only has no Palestinian organization taken credit for the attack, but some Palestinian officials, including Yasser Arafat, suggested the incident might have been perpetrated by rival gangs of the Israeli-Russian mafia.

However, Israeli political leaders and security officials immediately pinned the blame for the attack on Hamas or the Islamic Jihad.

Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak issued a strongly worded statement, vowing to deal a knockout blow to terrorists. He argued that the bombing was aimed at stalling the peace process, having been carried out on the eve of the resumption of the final status talks between Israel and the Palestinian Authority in Ramallah on 8 November. He stressed that he did not intend to slow down the pace of the peace process because of the incident.

However, Barak and other Israeli officials did call on the PA to “act effectively to prevent terrorists from harming the chances of progress in the peace process.”

“Combating terror was a centerpiece of the Oslo Accords,” said Israeli deputy-defense Minister, Ephraim Sneh, adding in an almost threatening tone that the PA “had better understand this.”

Nonetheless, Israeli officials did not really chastise the PA for the attack, and instead stressed that terrorism was the common enemy of Israel and the PA.

In fact, on the same day of the attack, Sneh admitted that 1999 was the best year for Israeli-Palestinian security coordination since the establishment of the PA in 1993.

He noted that the PA was interning hundreds of Palestinians without charge or trial in deference to Israeli security interests.

Initially, PA reactions to the incident were strong and quick. One PA official, Al-Tayeb Abdul Rahim, described the blast as “an act of terror which we condemn without reservations.”

He called on the Israeli security authorities to extend to their Palestinian counterparts all relevant information about the perpetrators.

He accused “elements within Hizbullah, Hamas, and the Islamic Jihad,” who he said were acting at Iran’s beck and call to seek out a way to sabotage the peace process and consequently “impede the liberation of additional parts of our homeland.”

He alleged that three groups were, under Iranian tutelage, plotting to launch a new spate of bombings for the purpose of aborting the final status talks, saying the PA had “solid information” to that effect.

In any case, the charges leveled by Abdul Rahim don’t seem to be genuine.

In fact, Hamas and Islamic Jihad sources described the statement as an “hallucination” and “cheap misinformation designed to appease and please the Zionists.”

Earlier this month, the Israeli Hebrew press disclosed that this report about a secret meeting of Hamas, Islamic Jihad and Hizbullah officials in Tehran, which was first published in the Paris-based journal al-Watan Al-Arabi, actually originated in the “psychological warfare unit” in the Israeli army and had actually been completely concocted.

Apart from that, the PA has not rounded up suspects as it routinely does in similar circumstances, perhaps because of certain suspicions that the incident was not a “convincing terrorist act” as one Palestinian official put it.

Indeed, Arafat’s statements in Paris that the incident “was likely an act of the Israeli mafia” contradicted statements made by Al-Tayeb Abdul Rahim, and underscored the lack of coordination in PA reactions to events in Israel.

Hamas leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin distanced his Movement from the Netanya blast, saying Hamas was no longer targeting non-combatant civilians.

Yassin said he was sure that the military wing of Hamas, the Izzidin al-Qassam Brigades, did not carry out the bombing, saying that “our fighters do not attack civilians.”

Yassin reiterated Hamas’ decision, which he described as final, not to attack civilians and non-combatants except when Palestinian civilians are targeted by the Israeli occupation army. Hamas, he said, took a solemn decision to that effect, adding that he challenged the Israeli occupation authorities to reciprocate.

“We decided to take non-combatant civilians out of the dictionary of the conflict,” he said.

Yassin’s recurrent emphasis to that effect suggests that he personally pushed for the decision, which was first announced in a leaflet circulated last month and signed by Hamas’ military wing.

The motive behind the decision, it seems, was both moral and political.

Hamas, Yassin argued on several occasions, ought to return to its Islamic purity which entails that it refrain from attacking women and children as, indeed, commanded by Islam.

Earlier, Yassin discounted a leaflet also signed by the Izzidin al-Qassam group vowing to resume attacks on Israeli targets in light of the unabated expansion of Jewish settlements.

However, Islamist sources said later that “they could neither confirm nor deny the authenticity of the leaflets since there is no direct contact between the political and military wings of Hamas.”

Muslims lay cornerstone for Mosque in
Nazareth amid Christian objections

Occupied Jerusalem - Amid fierce Vatican opposition, Muslims in Israel’s largest Arab town, Nazareth, today laid the cornerstone for a large mosque a few hundred yards opposite the Church of the Annunciation.

The mosque, to be called “Masjid Shehabuddin” (the Mosque of Shehabuddin), is to be built on Muslim Waqf (Muslim trust) land located in the vicinity of the historical Basilica.

Attending the ceremony were Muslim leaders from the one-million strong Arab community in Israel, including Knesset members, religious figures and a multitude of ordinary citizens from Nazareth and surrounding Arab communities.

However, the mayor of Nazareth, Ramez Jarayseh, as well as Christian religious and political leaders chose to stay away to show their displeasure at the Israeli decision to build the mosque in the vicinity of the Church.

Jarayseh, a Christian communist, has relentlessly sought to prevent the building of the mosque, arguing that the land in question belonged to the Municipal Council.

However, Jarayseh’s arch political rival, Salman Abu Ahmed, disputed this, arguing that Muslims produced authentic and irrefutable evidence supporting their claim that the land was Muslim Waqf land dating back to the Muslim hero Saladin over eight centuries ago.

Abu Ahmed, head of the United Nazareth List, which championed the cause of the mosque, criticized Christian objections and sensitivity to the building of a mosque.

“This (the mosque) is a house of God and that (the Church) is a house of God, and there is no reason in the world that two houses of God cannot stand side by side.”

Abu Ahmed argued that the establishment of the mosque on Waqf land near the Church should be a cause for unity not division between Muslims and Christians not only in Nazareth but also in the whole of Palestine.

“Churches and mosques stood in the past, stand today, and will continue to stand side by side…this should be a cause for harmony and brotherhood not disharmony and discord.”

He denied that Muslims in Nazareth had come under pressure to delay or cancel the cornerstone-laying.

“No pressure was put on us. Some remarks were made because it was thought that there was tension in Nazareth, even though this is not the case and I can reassure everybody about this,” he said, adding that “all the residents of Nazareth accepted the decision of the seven-member forum of the Arab leadership’s Monitoring Committee and the Ministerial Committee.”

Other Islamic leaders in Nazareth and the rest of Palestine said they had rejected calls by the Palestinian Authority to defer the ceremony because the intention of the PA was apparently to ultimately bring about the cancellation of the mosque’s construction.

Another Muslim leader in Nazareth marveled at the controversy surrounding the mosque.

“We are building a mosque here, not a casino or a theatre or a place of corruption,” said Ahmed al-Zu’abi, head of the Waqf in Nazareth.

Earlier, Zu’abi rebuffed a call from Yasser Arafat to postpone the building of the mosque.

“Arafat has no right to tell us what to do; let him first liberate Al-Aqsa Mosque.”

Christian leaders protest

Protesting the building of the mosque, Christian leaders in Israel and the PA autonomous enclaves decided to close their churches in the face of tourists and pilgrimage for two days, 23 and 24 November.

Latin Patriarch Michel Sabbah, head of the Roman Catholic Church in the Holy Land, pointed out that the closure of the churches for two days came to symbolize Christian displeasure and dissatisfaction over a whole host of issues.

“We were obliged to close our churches to make our voice heard.”

Another Roman Catholic leader, Elias Odeh, of the Church of the Annunciation itself, also voiced the same vague grievances.

“The feeling of Christians is that there is no future. There is no security, and there is no law.”

The decision to build the mosque has angered the Vatican and cast a cloud over prospects for a pilgrimage by Pope John Paul to Nazareth in March.

On 23 November, the Vatican said that by allowing the building of a mosque on a disputed site near the Basilica of the Annunciation in Nazareth, the government is fomenting tension between Christians and Muslims.

The denunciation came in the form of a statement by Joaquin Navarro-Valls, director of the Vatican Press Office.

“I believe the political authorities in this case have a great responsibility, because instead of favouring unity, they create the foundation to foment division,” said Navarro-Valls.

Vatican charges were strongly denied by Israel.

Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy described the charges as “dangerous”, saying the decision to build the mosque was a compromise accepted by all parties.

It is not clear what prompted Christians to oppose the building of the mosque in the area.

Local sources in Nazareth opined that Christian leaders were worried that the presence of an Islamic edifice next to a Church would undoubtedly undermine the latter’s dominating presence.

One Muslim source in Nazareth, who asked for anonymity, said he believed the real reason was Christian worries that the mosque would attract some of the lustre of the Basilica and might serve as “a propagation centre” for the Islamic faith in an area which many Christians consider a Christian bastion.

“They want Nazareth to be exclusively theirs just as Mecca and Medina are exclusively Islamic,” said the local Muslim source.

Sharansky promises to stop “ethnic cleansing” in East Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem- The Israeli interior minister, Natan Sharansky, said he would order an end to the policy of confiscating residency rights from Palestinians in East Jerusalem.

In an interview with the Jerusalem-based daily, al Quds, Sharansky said the new policy would go into effect very soon.

However, Palestinian sources in Jerusalem discounted Sharansky’s remarks, describing them as “for domestic consumption.”

“We want action, not words,” said an Arab source.

So far, Israel withdrew residency rights from as many as 20,000 East Jerusalem Arabs.

However, the Israeli policy had a boomerang effect in the form of a flood of Palestinians returning to Jerusalem, which may have prompted the Israeli government to reconsider the policy of withdrawing residency rights from Arabs.

Meanwhile, an Israeli writer revealed that the Israeli successive governments adopted a racist policy toward Palestinians in East Jerusalem aimed at curtailing Arab demographic growth.

In his book published last month, Emir Hechen, a former secretary of Jerusalem municipal council for ten years, said that all Israeli governments adopted a policy towards Arabs in the Eastern part of town whereby Arabs wouldn’t exceed 20% of the over all population.

Hechen said several tactics were employed, including home demolition, refusal to grant Arabs building licenses, confiscation of residency rights from East Jerusalem’s Arabs and encouraging Arabs to leave the town.

Hechen describes the enduring policy as “bluntly racist” but “politically expedient.”

Israel still practices ethnic cleansing against Palestinians in East Jerusalem.

Islamists make unprecedented gains
in Catholic University election

Occupied Jerusalem- An Islamist coalition of Hamas and the Islamic Jihad made unprecedented gains at the University of Bethlehem, the only Catholic University in Palestine.

Hamas and the Islamic Jihad received 8 seats of the 31-seat student general assembly during the student election, which took on 24 November.

Last year Hamas and the Islamic Jihad received only ten per cent of the student vote.

A coalition of the Fatah and the Palestine People’s Party received 13 seats while the leftist coalition of the Democratic and Popular fronts for the liberation of Palestine received 10 seats.

This means that neither Fatah nor the left would be able to form the student council alone without entering into a coalition with the Islamic block.

Over sixty per cent of the student body at the Catholic university are Christians, as the Vatican-run college adopts admission criteria based on sectarian considerations.

Hamas and the Islamic Jihad control all student councils in the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

Arafat understands trade ties between Indonesia and Israel

Amman- Palestinian Authority Chairman Yaser Arafat reportedly voiced “understanding” and “support” for the Indonesian President Abdurahman Wahid’s intention to start “trade ties” with Israel.

Arafat reportedly told Wahid, whom he met in Amman last month, that he was not against establishing commercial relations with the Jewish state.

For his part, Wahid claimed that commercial ties with Israel would serve the Palestinian Authority.
He didn’t say how would that serve Palestinian interests.
Wahid later told the Israeli radio that Indonesia would open a diplomatic mission in Gaza and later another mission in Israel.

Wahid told the Hebrew service of “Cal Yisrael”, the Israeli state- run radio, that Israel and Indonesia were maintaining a certain security relations dating back to the Suharto regime.

Last month, thousands of Indonesian students took to the streets to protest their government intention to establish economic ties with Israel.

Israel still occupies Jerusalem, where the Aksa Mosque is located, which is considered one of Islam’s holiest shrines.

Fatah leader: Hamas is better organized than Fatah

Occupied Jerusalem - Fatah, the political backbone of Yasser Arafat’s self-rule authority, received a painful blow when supporters of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and its junior sister the Islamic Jihad beat Arafat’s followers and the leftist camp combined in the recent college elections.

Hamas and the Islamic Jihad won 42 seats of the 81-seat general assembly, while Fatah won only 34 seats, one seat less than the number of seats won by the movement last year. The leftist camp won only five seats.

Fatah Secretary-General in Nablus attributed Hamas’ victory to political and organizational factors.

The peace process, he said, was not very attractive for the Palestinians and the overall Palestinian situation is not very encouraging.

He said Hamas was better organized at the universities and its supporters were highly motivated and in a state of high morale.

He claimed the PA was not very generous with Arafat’s supporters at al-Najah University. He said, “We received only $10,000 from Abu Ammar.”

The PA security agencies exhibited a strong show of force outside the university, apparently to intimidate students and bully them into voting for Fatah.

Obviously, the effect of the show of force was quite negative for Fatah and the PA.

Hamas dismisses Israeli report of
“chemical attack” as wild fantasy

Occupied Jerusalem - The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) dismissed an Israeli report that Israel thwarted a plan by Hamas to launch a chemical attack on Israeli targets as a “wild fantasy.”

An Islamist spokesman in Ramallah described the report as “concocted by the Israeli intelligence.”

Report: Israeli troops killed 355 Palestinians since Oslo

Occupied Jerusalem - A report publish by the Foreign Relations Department of the PLO in Ramallah indicated that 355 Palestinians, including 34 children, have been killed by Israeli soldiers and settlers since 1949.

The report also revealed that 810,000 Palestinians were imprisoned at least once between 1967 and 1994, including 18,972 Palestinians receiving prolonged “administrative detention.”

The report showed that since the current Israeli premier over four months ago, more than 2,626 settlement units have been or are in the process of being built.

Moreover, the report showed that last month alone the Israeli occupation authorities confiscated 2,079 dunums and cordoned off 80,210 dunums of Palestinian land in the West Bank, as well as rooted out 700 trees.

The report also pointed out that Israeli repression of Palestinians has not changed substantially compared to the pre-Oslo period, adding that in many respects it increased in quality rather than decreased.

Arafat appoints convicted murderer as General Prosecutor

Occupied Jerusalem - Palestinian Authority leader Yasser Arafat decided on 7 November to appoint Mr. Abdel Latif Abdel Fattah as a General Prosecutor in the Palestinian Attorney-General’s office in the West Bank.

According to the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, Mr. Abdel Fattah was convicted in 1998 by the Military Court in Jericho of mistreatment and carelessness, which led to the death of Waleed Alqawasmeh in the same year.

Abdel Fattah was sentenced to serve seven years in prison for his crime.

It was Arafat himself who ordered the investigation committee, which later brought Abdel Fattah for trial.

Now, one year later, Arafat appointed him to a high-rank position in the General Prosecution system.
