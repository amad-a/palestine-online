Hamas reacts angrily to deportation of leaders from Jordan

Hebron- From Khalid Amayreh

The Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, reacted angrily to the deportation from Jordan on 20 November of four of its Amman-based leaders to Qatar, describing the Jordanian measure as “harsh and provocative.”

Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin termed as “illogical and abnormal” the “deportation by a government of its own citizens,” adding that the deportation flagrantly contravened the Jordanian constitution.

Hamas’ Information Office in Gaza released a written statement to the press, denouncing “the hostile and unprecedented act against Jordanian citizens by a Jordanian government.”

The statement drew an analogy between the draconian Jordanian measure against the four Islamist leaders (Khalid Masha’al, chief of Hamas political bureau; Ibrahim Ghousheh, Hamas official spokesman; and Ezzat Rasheq and Sami Khater members of the political bureau) and the deportation of Palestinians from the West Bank and Gaza by the Israeli occupation authorities.

“This vile act reminds us of the notorious actions of the Israeli occupation authorities, only this time the deportation and expulsion is committed by an Arab government.”

Ismael Haniyya, a close confidant of Yassin, accused the Jordanian government of acting on Israel’s behalf.

“This is certainly not a sovereign Jordanian decision; no sovereign State resorts to deporting its own citizens to appease another country.”

The deportation of the four Islamists drew indignant reactions from Palestinian Islamists, intellectuals and human rights activists, as well as from ordinary citizens.

“This is a reflection of the dire state of affairs the Arab world has reached…what more can one say about a government deporting its own citizens in order to please a foreign country,” said one Islamist leader in the Ramallah area.

Nayef Rajoub of the Grand Mosque in Dura said the harsh measure against the four Islamists was solely aimed at appeasing Israel and the United States.

“There is only one explanation of what happened, and that is the government of Jordan is not answerable to its own people but to some other entities which we know all too well.”

In Qatar itself, Khalid Misha’al dismissed the Jordanian description of the deportation as “only a temporary transfer” as “rubbish coming out of a rubbish mentality.”

“What would you call blindfolding an innocent citizen, handcuffing him, forcing him into an airplane, and sending him to a foreign country?” retorted Misha’al.

He described the manner in which the deportation was carried out as “vindictive, humiliating and full of malice.”

One Qatari sympathizer remarked on the mistreatment of the four Islamists at the hands of the Jordanian Interior Ministry officials saying, “I’m sure if they were Jews from Tel Aviv, they would not have been treated as such.”

For its part, the PA refused to comment on events in Amman, saying in a terse statement carried by the PA news agency, Wafa, that “what happened was an internal Jordanian affair” and that “we don’t interfere in the internal affairs of a brotherly country.”

It was widely believed that the Jordanian crackdown on Hamas was carried out, at least in part, under incessant pressure from the PA, which repeatedly accused Hamas external leadership of directing acts of violence against Israel for the purpose of corroding the peace process.

However, the PA Minister of Communications, Imad al-Faluji, called on Hamas leaders to transfer its politburo into Gaza “under the umbrella of the Palestinian (National) Authority.”

When asked if PA Chairman Yasser Arafat would welcome a step as such, Faluji answered in the affirmative.

The same offer was made later by PA Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub, who said in an interview with the Israeli English daily, the Jerusalem Post, on 24 November that the PA was willing to offer Hamas “sanctuary”, provided that the Islamist movement accept PA rule and renounce violence.

It is unlikely that Hamas would heed Faluji’s and Rajoub’s advises, since putting all its eggs in the Gaza basket would place the movement at the mercy of Yasser Arafat’s security agencies.

The Palestinian Authority, PA sources intimated, had been instrumental in pressuring Jordan to expel the four Islamist leaders from Amman.

PA Chairman Arafat traveled to Jordan on 24 November and reportedly thanked Jordanian officials, including Prime Minister Abdel Rauf Rawabdeh, for deporting the Islamist leaders to Qatar.

Earlier, Arafat parroted Jordanian claims that what happened was not “deportation” but “forced travel” aimed at resolving a crisis.

“The decision was made to end the crisis; it wasn’t a deportation,” said Arafat in statements carried by news agencies on 24 November.

In Amman, the Muslim Brotherhood and the Islamic Action front, Jordan’s largest political party, denounced the expulsions and warned that they will exacerbate the crisis between the Islamists and the Jordanian government.

In a statement released by the Islamic Action Front on 23 November, the Islamists accused the government of moving against Hamas in a response to U.S. and Zionist demands imposed on Jordan at a phase when the Palestinian cause is facing liquidation.

Meanwhile, it expected that a group of Jordanian lawyers would challenge the government decision before the highest Jordanian court. Saleh al-Armouti, who represents the four Islamic leaders, contented that the decision to deport his clients was “unconstitutional 100%.”

“There is a clear clause in the Jordanian constitution outlawing the deportation of Jordanian citizens from their own country,” said Armouti.

“The government committed a manifest criminal act,” he added.

Everybody Knows the Good Guys Lost

By Ruth Anderson

Everybody knows the good guys lost. Everybody knows the fight was fixed. Everybody knows the deal is rotten. Everybody knows the plague is coming and it is moving fast.

My first name is Khaled. I have no weapons to fight with except my belief in God and my commitment to liberating the country of my origin, Palestine. Yet, people fear me. Perhaps this is because they fear the truth and they fear those who would sacrifice their lives for what they believe in.

I have been jailed for what I believe in. I was almost killed for what I believe in. And now, I am a deportee. Those of us who were deported were handcuffed and blindfolded. This is unheard of. Yet, I will not give up until death takes me. The organization I belong to is Hamas. In 1993, Hamas was legalized in Jordan by the government. It has never been illegal nor has it ever done anything against the Jordanian government.

I realize that it is because of pressure from the Israelis, the Palestinian Authority and Washington that the Jordanian government was coerced into expelling me and some of my compatriots. These various forces want to ensure that the peace process, no matter how much it further depletes the remnants of Palestine, be safeguarded.

I am not against peace. I am neither a warlord nor a warmonger. I have nothing to gain from war. I wish no one harm, but at the same time, I cannot bear to see what is left of Palestine bargained off by those who usurped it in the first place.

What bothers me, too, is that I am a Jordanian citizen. How, then, can I be deported from the country that gave me citizenship in the first place? Surely no country in the world deports its citizens for peacefully practicing what they believe in. I do not threaten the security of the Jordanian government nor the security of any other government. And, I have every right to be concerned about the bargaining off of the land of my forefathers.

My deportation has serious implications to the millions of Palestinians who were given Jordanian citizenship. More than 70% of Jordan is made up of Palestinians. If my fellow sufferers and I can be expelled, then Jordan cannot really be considered home to the rest of the Palestinian population who hold Jordanian passports. Imagine what a crisis it would be to the stability of the government if all the Palestinians just up and left Jordan. There would be very few people remaining, leaving Amman a virtual ghost town. A true and just peace must ensure that all are entitled to equal social and political rights, human dignity and to live on their land without fear of harm. True peace would guarantee that little Palestinian children are free to play in front of their homes without fear of being struck and/or killed by Israeli “stray” bullets. True peace would mean an end to the “Double Occupation” that plagues all Palestinians who still live in Palestine. True peace would also mean that PA jails that contain Hamas members be emptied.

But then, everybody knows the good guys lost. Everybody knows the fight was fixed. My fellow expellees and I are heroes in the seaweed. And remember, history is written by those who hang heroes.

Everybody knows the deal is rotten. Everybody knows the plague is coming and it is moving fast.

The pretext given for my leaving Jordan by the Jordanian government is that I and my companions were not being deported, but rather we were having our sentences suspended by traveling to Qatar. At this time, I do not know what will happen to me and my fellow deportees. We have been separated from our homes and the ones we love. Some of us have been badly beaten, and all of us have undergone “convincing” words to “encourage” us to change our minds about our commitment to what we believe in. So what if a little bit more of Palestine is sold every day? So what if Palestinian children die in the streets in their occupied land, simply because they are Palestinians? So what if the streets of Palestine are covered with the blood of martyrs who died for liberty and freedom? And so what if more and more Jewish settlements are being built on what is left of Palestinian land? It’s no big deal anyway. No big deal to anyone but those Palestinians who cherish liberty and love their homeland.

Dear Secretary-General:

RE: The unfinished business of the refugees

As you prepare to lead the United Nations into the twenty-first century, one issue must, inevitably, remain at the top of your agenda. It is the question of Palestine and the final restoration of its people’s human rights. This is perfectly understandable as no other international issue has engaged as much time and resources of your Organization. Because it remains largely an unresolved problem, we are obliged to proffer some approaches for your wise consideration.

You may recall that this December marks the fifty-first anniversary of General Assembly resolution 194, which calls for the return of the Palestinian refugees to their homes. It is, likewise, the twenty-ninth anniversary of resolution 2535 B (XXIV), which recognizes “that the problem of the Palestinian Arab refugees has arisen from the denial of their inalienable rights under the Charter of the United Nations and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.” Israel’s continued refusal to comply with these international rulings must naturally be a source of grave concern and unease for you. The failure of UN appeals, censures, resolutions and condemnations to alter the policies of the Jewish State means that 5 million Palestinians will, regretfully, enter the new millennium facing the threat of permanent exile, primarily because they are Arabs.

From your favoured position as head of the UN, we believe that you are uniquely placed, perhaps better than any other world leader, to appreciate the magnitude of the Palestine problem and the depth of its people’s sentiments toward your Organization. Their demands are firmly rooted in the same principles of “justice and international law” that are enshrined in Article 1 of the United Nations Charter. Like all the other peoples who were detached from the Ottoman Empire in 1917, the Palestinians were recognized as an “independent nation” by Article 22 of the Covenant of the League of Nations. Although they were never divested of sovereignty over their land in the period leading up to 1947, the UN took advantage of their weakness and granted 57% of their land to a Jewish immigrant minority that constituted no more than 32% of the population. The failure of all subsequent peace efforts to resolve the Palestine problem was essentially owing to the non-recognition of this initial abuse of power and excess of jurisdiction.

With key world figures now endeavouring to modernize their States in order to meet the challenges of the next century, you could not have found a more auspicious occasion than the year 2000 to restore credibility, confidence and hope in the UN. The failure of more than 600 Security Council and General Assembly resolutions (now compiled into four volumes by the Institute of Palestine Studies) to secure their inalienable rights has left the vast majority of Palestinians embittered and disillusioned. Under the circumstances, the UN must, sooner rather than later, make one of two choices: (a) It can, on the one hand, be a willing accomplice to the imposition of a “Pax Hebraica” upon the Palestinian people in the the year 2000 in which they receive less than 20% of their ancestral land, while two thirds of their kith and kin remain permanent exiles, or (b) alternatively, it can scrupulously translate into reality the ideal outlined in its Charter “to develop friendly relations among nations based on respect for the principle of equal rights and self-determination of peoples.” Whichever it chooses, the question of Palestine will, by virtue of its sheer longevity and complexity, almost certainly be the litmus test of the UN’s success or failure.

There are means of enforcement

Assuredly, any meaningful attempt on your part to ensure Israeli compliance with UN resolutions would result in a veritable diplomatic war with the powers that be in Washington. Thanks to the efforts of Henry Kissinger, they have, since 1975, placed their UN vote entirely at the disposal of Israel in accord with the Memorandum of Agreement which he arranged between the United States Government and Israel in connection with the Egyptian-Israeli Agreement of 1 September 1975. Article 8 of that Memorandum reads: “The United States Government will vote against any Security Council resolution which in its judgement affects or alters adversely the Agreement.”

That being their stated intent, every successive American administration has, in practice, voted against UN resolutions which sought to end Israel’s status as the belligerent occupier of territories it acquired by force. The UN’s complete resignation to this situation has, naturally, sent the wrong signals to millions of dispossessed and disenfranchised people around the world. They have concluded that there are no clauses or mechanisms in the UN Charter that enable it to enforce its decisions. And, that the General Assembly is powerless to act when a member or members of the Security Council abuse their power and jeopardize world peace. Though largely excusable, we believe this perception is fundamentally wrong.

Contrary to the impression conveyed by Washington that it has been endowed with absolute rights and privileges within the UN, there are certain principles that can prevent this unilateral subversion of the UN. Here again we refer to the Charter’s Article 1, which states that the purpose of the UN is to “take collective measures” for the prevention and removal of threats to peace and the suppression of acts of aggression. This principle, you would recall, was eminently effected in the famous “Uniting for Peace” resolution (377 A (V) of 3 November 1950. “That if the Security Council, because of lack of unanimity of the permanent members, fails to exercise its primary responsibility for the maintenance of international peace and security….the General Assembly shall consider the matter immediately with a view to making appropriate recommendations to members for collective measures, including in the case of a breach of the peace or act of aggression the use of armed force, when necessary, to maintain or restore international peace and security.”

Secretary General, in the light of the above, you would agree that there are no “indispensable” members of United Nations, as some would like us to believe. Yet, with regard to the Palestinian Arab refugees, “the enjoyment of their right to return to their homes and property … is indispensable for the achievement of a just settlement of the refugee problem…” (resolution 3089 D, 7 December 1973). Without attempting to predetermine your response to this letter we, somehow, assume that because of your many years within the Organization, you would soon come to the same conclusion as Dr. John H. Davis, the former Commissioner General of the United Nations Relief and Works Agency (UNRWA). That “in the end, one must even be prepared to impose corrective measures on Israel against her will.”1

Israel’s continued violation of international law is not just a manifestation of its militaristic ambitions. It is also the reflection of an outdated doctrine that seeks to accrue to itself privilege, favour and exclusivity. Thus, as long as its policies are guided by such beliefs, the threat and actual use of UN force must become unavoidable and necessary. Surely, if nothing else was learned from the past 51 years, it is the fact that the Jewish State has never relinquished Arab land or restored any of their usurped rights except after being confronted with force. Backed by the British and French veto of resolution 999, it refused in 1956 to relinquish Egyptian territory. Then, it was only after the General Assembly established the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) to impose its will did the Jewish State comply.

Taking care of business

As is the common tendency today among government officials and international civil servants, you may also ask whether or not the UN is able to now turn back the clock of time 51 years to ensure the implementation of resolution 194? The answer is yes. It can and must; just as it turned back the clock 3,000 years in 1947 to create a Jewish State in Palestine. In many respects the UN is equally culpable as the Zionists for creating the refugee problem. This was accomplished by its execution of three fateful decisions, all prejudiced against Palestinian national rights and aspirations. The first step was the partition and exodus that it provoked. The second was not to include the Palestinians under the mandate of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) but instead create for them an enfeebled and powerless UNRWA. And thirdly, the decision taken in 1951 to exclude the refugees assisted by UNRWA from the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees, thereby denying them the special protection provided for in the Convention as well as the international protection given by UNHCR.

Admittedly, our letter would be incomplete if it failed to register a note of concern about the United Nations Conciliation Commission for Palestine (UNCCP). Appointed in 1948 by the Assembly under resolution 194, the record of this august body consisting of France, the United States and Turkey has been typically one of neglect and unfinished business. Charged with the responsibility to facilitate the repatriation, resettlement and economic and social rehabilitation of the refugees and the payment of compensation, the Commission, it appears, has long ran out of what it takes to discharge its obligations. We, therefore, note with bemusement that while it has not undertaken any significant initiative to resolve the refugee problem since 1964, the General Assembly never formally abolished the Conciliation Commission.

That the UN should, wittingly or unwittingly, allow the abuse of its good offices to undermine international law, prolong human suffering and threaten world peace is totally inexcusable. Surely, time has changed. Whereas yesterday’s leaders claimed that that there is no place for morality in international politics, today’s men have recognized the need for an ethical dimension in international relations. For this reason, and many more, we shall remain optimistic about the future, hoping that we would not have to wait much longer before men of true honour and courage would arise to defend the law both in letter and spirit.

Undoubtedly, Israel’s aim to impose permanent exile on the Palestinian refugees is the surest way to prevent their exercise of sovereignty over their land. Nothing on the horizon suggests that their ultimate return would be realized under your stewardship. While history would be oft-forgiving of those who tried and failed, it surely would neither forgive nor forget those who had the means and did not try. It would, moreover, never accept their claims that time and the prevalence of Israeli occupation were too overwhelming. For in the same manner that the prolonged occupation of Poland from 1795 to 1919 did not annul the rights of its people to national independence, sovereignty and return, so too the Palestinians expect the full restoration of their inalienable rights, no matter how long the Israeli occupation.

Yours sincerely,

Dr. Daud Abdullah

Senior Researcher, The Palestinian Return Centre, London

Terrorism: Deceptive Definition with Simplistic Terminology

By Ramzy Baroud

A suicide mission in the heart of Jerusalem conducted by a young Palestinian man is defined as an act of terrorism. The almost daily Israeli bombardments of South Lebanon are seen as justifiable Israeli acts of retaliation to combat terrorism. Why?

Unfortunately, such a question is no longer lingering in the Arab media and mind as it once was and should have remained. In fact, in order for many Arabs to distance themselves from any act that can be described by Israel and the United States as “terrorism”, they appear more furious and hostile to this “violent” approach than Israel itself does. In the meantime, Israel nowadays is hardly held accountable for its part in the ceaseless violence as if its repetition has become a daily ritual which the residents of South Lebanon depend on for a good night’s sleep. It’s absurd, odd and maddening. I personally hold no expectation on the United States for its unfair and bias stand, which hardly fails to side with Israel. The United States’ close relations to the Middle East are motivated by two factors: its support of Israel and its need for the region’s abundant resources. Therefore, blaming the U.S. based on morality is merely a waste of time.

The real worrisome notion is the fact that Israel’s mental manipulation is driving many Arabs and Muslims to see the world through Israel’s eyes. While many are engaged in a never-ending debate over whether suicide bombings are morally permissible or not, Israel’s terrorism is overlooked and no longer confronted as the real source of violence. Israel is lucky enough to have its influence in the Western media, which cleverly conceals its faults. The terrorist bombardment of civilians in South Lebanon is therefore an act of retaliation; real bullets become rubber bullets; the killing of unarmed Palestinians becomes an act of self-defense; even the direct genocide of Palestinian civilians by an Israeli Jewish terrorist, according to twisted Israeli definitions, becomes an unfortunate act committed by a mentally disabled person.

The violent behaviour of a Palestinian who was born Stateless, poor, and oppressed physically and mentally originates from an entirely different circumstance than an Israeli Jew who spent some or most of his life in Brooklyn, Moscow or elsewhere. Even if the question narrows down to rejecting terrorism in all of its forms and mechanisms, regardless of its origin and motivations, shouldn’t the term terrorism be associated with both Israelis and Palestinians?

Israel’s terrorism is of a much larger magnitude than terrorism promoted by a small group of individuals, for Israel’s terrorism is the result of a State-approved agenda. In fact, Israeli terrorism is dealt with openly during campaign elections and public debates, although they refer to it by different names. When an Israeli candidate promotes himself in order to gather support among voters, he promises to crack down on Lebanese terrorists (translation: “I promise to randomly bombard South Lebanon”).

It is indeed frightening to know that Israel’s terminology is finally doing its magic. When the radio declares that three Lebanese civilians were killed and eight were injured in an Israeli air raid, no one expects that such killings will affect the continuation of the peace process or Israel’s image around the world. If the same killings accrued following a suicide bombing attack—while they in fact resemble a desperate cry for help—most of us hold our breath, waiting for an Israeli reaction. Intelligent political analysis and commentary fills the newspapers on whether such a “hideous act” will jeopardize the peace process. And when Israeli leaders declare their willingness to resume the peace process regardless of terrorism, the world stands, bows and applauds the courageous Israeli leaders for their warm hearts and their endurance.

Does anyone know Suad Fouan, a Lebanese woman from al-Houlah village in occupied South Lebanon? Suad was wounded after an Israeli missile fell on her house in early November. The Israeli missile also injured seven of Suad’s neighbours. Thousands were not as lucky as Suad and her neighbours; they died after hi-tech Israeli weapons bombarded their humble homes in the dark. And the world refuses to see or acknowledge such cruelty, inhumanity and blatant terrorism.

Pleasing the United States by defaming Arab terrorism is consequently affecting our ability to point at Israel’s ongoing campaign of terror in South Lebanon and the occupied Palestinian territories. It’s important to remind ourselves of who is the cause of ongoing Middle East violence. If we overlook such a fact, Israel’s deceptive definition of the term “terrorism” will make the Israeli account the only side of the coin that the world shall ever see.

Everything Is Different

By Flora Nicoletta*

Gaza City - There is in Gaza a dozen representative offices—in case of a rosy future they will be called embassies—and three cultural centres. After working in Algeria and Kuwait, Georges Bertrand headed since 15 August 1995 the French Cultural Centre (FCC) of Gaza, which was established in 1989 and inaugurated in 1994. Located in Er-Rimal, the most elegant borough of the capital of the Strip, on Victor Hugo Street, the Centre stands amidst a green and bloom-ridden garden complete with cypress, flame and palm trees, a sleeping cat and a hibiscus, like the one imagined in a book of tales a long time ago. Under a roof of palm branches are the kiosk and five tables belonging to the cafeteria. Georges Bertrand’s mission in Gaza ended in July 1999, and he gave us the following interview before his departure.

“In Gaza, everything is different. My post is very tiring because I’m in charge of several duties. I’m the director of the Centre and at the same time I represent the consul-general. For instance, tonight there is a reception and the consul is busy in Jerusalem, so he asked me to go. I represent the consulate to the organizations here, Palestinian as well as international. And as soon as there are some problems, the people come and look for me—problems with a bank, problems with a prisoner. I’m a little bit like an ambassador and I’m invited to everything which is French or Francophile. I represent France, this is sure. For the people, I represent France because I’m always on the spot.

“According to the Oslo accords, the flag should be at least 8 metres from the entrance in order not to be seen from the street because we are not an official diplomatic representation. Therefore, it’s prohibited to raise the flag at the entrance. It’s the same rule for all the representative offices, but the Chinese have put their flag on the roof and lit it up with floodlights...

“I work ten hours a day. I also work during the night at home; I prepare everything on the computer for the day after. I work every day, only Friday is a holiday. I work around 70 hours a week but I don’t complain; I’m not obliged to do this. My fatigue gives me something: I see results. It’s not like working in an office in Jerusalem; here I can see the fruits of my hard work. We want to be a real cultural centre with plenty of activities, to do many things for the population and this means, naturally, a lot of work. In addition, we are not a centre like the others because at the same time we also have a consular antenna.

“On top of that, it’s a very difficult post because of the Erez border crossing. To organize anything in Gaza is very complicated because everything has to come from the other side. For a concert, we need a piano and we have to rent it and to bring it here. Everything we do is tiring, but of course it’s very enriching from a human point of view in the sense that this centre was vegetating four years ago, practically nobody was coming, and today it is one of the most active centres in the Palestinian territories.

“In Gaza, there are the British Council and the German Centre, which is an antenna of the Goëthe Institut of Tel Aviv. They hold one cultural activity per year and that’s all. They open for a few hours a day. We are open from morning till evening for the courses as well as for the cultural activities.

“I spent almost one year asking for the consular antenna and I got it. The first year I was here, I asked for it because most of the local population cannot leave the Strip; consequently, the only way for them to ask for a visa was through the FCC. On the other hand, Gaza is the headquarters of the executive branch of the Palestinian Authority; there are many organizations and Arafat lives here. Plenty of people need visas in order to accompany him or for other reasons and all the requests are made here. Thanks to the Schengen Convention, when they get a French visa, they can travel to several European countries.

“In June 1996, the consular antenna was established. Everything is exceptional here, everything is exceptional in Gaza and in this region. It’s the sole FCC in the world with this kind of duty, and, of the three cultural centres in Gaza, we are the only one which functions in this way. For the Goëthe Institut, there is a guy who comes from Tel Aviv every week; he comes to bring the visas and returns. Instead, in our centre, we have someone who is paid and works every day on the spot, except on Sunday. But the decisions for giving the visas are taken in Jerusalem because it cannot be the task of a local.

“Suppose a Palestinian wants to go to Italy; there are some travel agencies that can help him to obtain the visa in Jerusalem, but it’s very expensive. These agencies have people in Jerusalem with the applications. One comes here, makes him fill in the applications and returns back with the dollars.

“We have two kinds of activities in the centre: linguistic and cultural. We give courses to children and adults and the children are numerous. We have courses at all levels, seven levels, in addition to courses of translation and conversation. And we are obliged to refuse people. We also give courses by contract with ministries and organizations outside the Centre. And we train teachers at the Faculty of Education in coordination with a French university. We also teach Arabic to foreigners.

“We have a library and the “Cine Club” once every week—the only cinema in Gaza. We organize concerts, and we have a theatrical company. And now I would like to speak about the cafeteria. Our Centre has become too small, the premises are not large enough now and more and more people are coming. So we opened a cafeteria in April 1997 in the garden. At first, only the males were going to the cafeteria and the females stayed far away. At a later time, the females went to the cafeteria but did not sit at the same tables with the males. Now you can see all the people sitting together. There was fear at the beginning. A woman said to me, ‘At least now I can sit with a youth and laugh with him without the people saying, She’s unfaithful to her husband.’ I’m very happy for this.

“It should be clear that the cafeteria is not a place for romantic rendezvous. It’s a place where people of both sexes mix as well as people of all nationalities. For me, this is very important. I don’t want a cafeteria where all the Arabs sit together at the same table and all the expatriates at another one. It’s a place for exchange at all levels. Even the consul-general, delegations, come to the cafeteria when they have appointments in Gaza and some spare time.

“The weather is usually hot here and it does not rain too much, therefore we thought: why not the cafeteria in the garden? But we did not want it to be seen from the street; we wanted to create a place a little bit secret, intimate. So the people will feel comfortable. It represents for us an additional room, secret, it’s a cafeteria and a meeting room at the same time.

“One of the most extraordinary events we ever organized [on 2 March 1998] was the recital of Sapho [a Jewish-born Moroccan singer]. It was really extraordinary. The problem was that all her material was blocked at Erez with her musicians. A ton and a half of material... Everything was blocked at Erez, including our cooperators. We had rented the materials in Israel. It took six hours of negotiations between the Palestinian, Israeli and French sides in order to get things moving again and eventually Sapho sang in Gaza. An extraordinary success. There were Madame Arafat, whole ministries, masses of people in attendance.

“The most terrible time during these four years was during the famous clashes of 26 September 1996 [following the opening of an archaeological tunnel by the Israelis for tourists in the Old City of Jerusalem that claimed the lives of 60 Palestinians and 15 Israelis all in the Occupied Territories]. That day was a Thursday. At that time, France decided to donate urgent humanitarian aid. France, and also Italy, sent it by airplane to Cairo, and when the humanitarian aid reached the Rafah border crossing, the Israelis prevented its entrance into the Strip; it could not pass urgently. Therefore, the medical aid had to be sent back to Cairo and from there to Tel Aviv and then to Jerusalem.

“We were waiting in Gaza. What happened is that the Israelis at Erez authorized CNN crews to enter the Strip but not the humanitarian aid. The only way to pass was with consular vehicles with CC number-plates. I went to Jerusalem on Sunday, 29 September. All the staff of the Consulate packed the medical aid in cardboard boxes and they were loaded into vehicles. We drove in a convoy as far as the Strip. All the consular cars had been requisitioned, together with UN cars, UN trucks and UNRWA vehicles. Eventually, we reached Esh-Shifa hospital [in Gaza City] with all the aid and the Minister of Health, Riyad Ez-Zanoun, was waiting for us and he congratulated France for its deed.

“At the same time, I had to take care of around 40 French nationals who were in Gaza: cooperators, teachers, NGO staff and some French women married to Palestinians. The other two cultural centres had closed their doors. We stayed open on Thursday and Friday [while bloody clashes were still going on]. But after 15 days, I collapsed a little bit, and then I completely collapsed 15 days later. It was the first time in my life I had seen something like that: a sort of massacre within arm’s reach. I had never seen that...

“The following month, during the visit of President Jacques Chirac, the Israelis cut off all the mobile telephones. We couldn’t communicate with each other for two or three hours, the time of Chirac’s visit in Gaza, the time to disrupt everything...

“Another event I would like to mention took place on Friday, 13 October 1995, with the coming of 800 [French] pilgrims to Gaza accompanied by Abbot Pierre and Bernard Kouchner, the then-French Minister of Health. The tour was organized by a travel agency in Jerusalem, and we organized the part in Gaza, which lasted for one day. Palestinian Honour

The Ethic of Resentment:

A Nietzschean Analysis of Islam and the West*

Mohommed A. Muqtedar Khan

( 2-2)

The Ethic of Nobility

The contention that the ethic of resentment underpins Western constructions of Islam must remain unsubstantiated until it can be demonstrated that indeed Islam is based on an ethic of nobility, as Nietzsche claims, and that there are identifiable reasons for Christian and Western resentment and fear of Islam. The Islamic civilization and its discourses have basically two major components. One is the glorification and celebration of itself as the final word of God and the realization of His will on Earth.21 The second is the internal dialectic of self overcoming itself. The first discursive quality can be understood as something common to all major civilizations that have not consciously manufactured an “other” to function as a yardstick to define self. It is the second, historical quality of Islam that suggests to me that it is based on the ethic of nobility. This devotion to the Oneness and Will of the Ultimate also explains the cyclical tendency to “purify self” through various revivalist movements and also gives insights into the historical roots of the current global resurgence of Islam.

Revivalism is not a new phenomenon in the history of Islam. There is an intrinsic dialectic within Islam, which is exclusive and unique. This dialectic is between Islam and the un-Islam, between haqq (truth) and batil (falsehood). Thus each time social developments move away from the basic teachings of Islam within the Muslim society, there comes a mujaddid (reviver)22 who starts a process of revival — purification — of Islam. Recurring movements of resurgence are a dialectic intrinsic to Islam in order to guarantee its continuity. In other words, Islam is itself a process of tajdid (renewal). Indeed as Esposito identifies, tajdid and islah (reform) are the fundamental components of Islam’s world view and history.23 The Prophet Muhammed (peace and blessings of Allah be upon him) was the first mujaddid of Islam who restored and purified the din al Ibrahim (the religion of Abraham). Indeed it can be stated that revivalism is a fundamentally reconstitutive element of Islam.24 This self renewal can be seen also in the light of Nietzsche’s ethic of nobility. It is indeed an in-built process which lets history evolve through a constant effort to overcome self. Islam becomes Islam again and again in history through a process of overcoming itself. It does not need an “other” in order to overcome and assert itself. This is without doubt the source of the strength and vitality of Islam, and this makes it so difficult to make it disappear. The return of Islam in Turkey after four decades of totalitarian imposition of secularism is one of the many contemporary manifestations of Islam’s rejuvenative character, its ability to overcome itself and emerge in a renewed and reformed self.25

Roots of Christian and Western Resentment

The rise of Islam, both in numbers of adherents and territorial expansion, in conjunction with its claim that it is the final and the unadulterated revelation of God, threatened Christian claims to the same. The phenomenal expansion of Islam compelled Christian theologians and thinkers to discredit it. Western scholars, philosophers as well as theologians, refused to treat Islam on its own terms and continued to brand it as a Christian heresy and the Prophet of Islam as an evil and even insane heretic. This was further compounded by the failure of Christian missionaries to convert Muslims to Christianity while the opposite seemed to happen at an alarming rate — from India in the East to Spain in the West. Moreover, while Jews and Christians heaped abuse on Islam and its prophet, Islam itself embraced Moses and Jesus as prophets of Islam worthy of respect and reverence. As Daniel Pipes argues, the Church’s resentment of Islam led to a polemic strategy designed to discredit Islam’s claim to true revelation by denying the prophethood of Muhammad. The advent of the Crusades transformed this theological resentment into a civilizational one after the Christians’ defeat.26 Islam’s parallel claims and its relatively greater success in realizing these claims were the primary source of Christian resentment against Islam during the Medieval period in Europe and the coeval Classical period in the Islamic World. This resentment led to the development of Orientalism and the construction of the quintessential “other,” namely, Islam.

One can understand the resentment against a powerful other. The persistence of the ethic of resentment against a much diminisshed and even marginalized Islam, however, is both unhealthy and irrational. While resentment in the first case led to a creative response and competition that has undoubtedly contributed to the reform, enlightenment, and incredible development of the modern West, the persistence of that sentiment only weakens it. The current search for another “arch evil enemy” is rapidly refocusing on Islam. While there are some reasons for the present resentment, Islam does not present a sufficient challenge or threat to the global hegemony of the West and its new values to warrant relaunching the crusades — this time not in the name of Christianity but of Western Civilization. The contemporary resurgence of Islam does not really threaten Western societies or Western democracies.27 But it does definitely increase the cost of Western domination and hegemony over Muslim lands.28 This state affairs, while complicating post-Cold-War redefinition of global politics, does not merit the bogey-man image that Islam held prior to the emergence of the modern world.

The balance of power in premodern times was distinctly in favor of Islam, which may simplisticly explain the ethic of resentment dominating Christian sensibilities and attitudes toward Islam. Since the balance of power today is so overwhelmingly in favor of the West, what then is the reason for the perpetuation of resentment? The answer lies perhaps in De Tocqueville’s argument that the love of equality in Western society is invariably accompanied by the sentiment of envy and resentment.29 De Tocqueville argues that a sense of resentment and even intolerance of difference and distinction underpins the love of equality, often understood as homogenizing.

He asserts that the love of equality, which is basic to democratic societies, often manifests a terrible intolerance of the “other,” very similar to the Nietzschean depiction of slave morality and of whatever chooses to stand separate from the majority.30 Following his argument that the source of this democratic sentiment is essentially Christian ethics, it is not difficult to understand whence the concurrent intolerance and resentment of the “other” also comes.31 Thus in the light of De Tocqueville’s insights, one may venture to advance the argument, carefully and guardedly, that perhaps the democratic instinct is the purveyor of the ethic of resentment and that pursuit of global equality and homegeneity is producing an extreme intolerance toward the modern resurgence of Islam, which has already been labelled, a priori, as undemocratic. While the discussion about the relation between the positioning of the West as democratic and Islam as aristocratic/authoritarian and the ethic of resentment is interesting and also germane to this paper, it unfortunately is beyond its scope.

Conclusion

The argument that Orientalism essentially created an inferior and antithetical Orient to function as the “other” for the purposes of self-definition lends credence and also derives support from Nietzschean contentions that the modern world has emerged from the Judeo-Christian ethic of resentment. My arguments draw upon the post-Cold War hunt for another “other,” and its targeting of Islam, to suggest that the ethic of resentment that determined Christian perceptions of Islam continues to function in Western perceptions both of itself and of Islam.

While there were justifications for Christianity to fear Islam, there no longer are sufficient reasons for the West to harbor a similar fear of Islam. Medieval Christendom was an underdog. The present West is by far the preponderant and even dominant power. Indeed this preponderance of power in conjunction with the consequences of colonization of Islamic lands may have even changed the nature of Islamic morality — from master to slave.

If one were to believe the analysis of Bernard Lewis and Fukuyama, who argue that Islam has a lot to be resentful of in its relations with the West and even to hate the West,32 then modern Islamic resurgence is indeed a fundamental departure from traditional revivals. It is not based on tajdid (renewal) and islah (reform) but stems merely from the resentment of a disadvantaged slave, as Fukuyama would have us believe. If this is true, then the West has nothing to fear from Islam. The embracing of the ethic of resentment will do to Islam what it did to Christianity — secularize and democratize it. The days of the other are over, if the other is on the way to embrace the ways of oneself.

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

By Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 3

Jewish Fundamentalism Within Jewish Society

The religious-nationalist Jewish compromises with modernity regarding women are exceedingly complicated in many ways. The Halacha forbids Jewish males to listen to women singing whether in a choir or solo regardless of what is sung. This is stated directly in the halachic ruling that a voice of a woman is adultery. This is interpreted by later halachic rulings stipulating that the word “voice” here means a woman’s singing not speaking. This rule, originating in the Talmud, occurs in all codes of law. A Jewish male who willingly listens to a woman’s singing commits a sin equivalent either to adultery or fornication. The great majority of NRP faithful members, nevertheless, listen to women singing and thus commit “adultery” routinely. Some of the most strict NRP members, especially among the religious settlers in the West Bank, have not only puzzled over this problem but at times have tried to solve the problem of how to adjust by developing creative approaches. In the early 1 990s some of the settlers founded a new radio station, Arutz, or Channel, 7. For their station to become successful and to appeal as broadly as possible to Israeli Jews, the settlers understood that the songs of the fashionable singers of the day, some of whom were women, would have to be broadcast. The rabbinical censor, however, has refused to allow a breach of the Halacha whereby male listeners would hear female singers and thus commit “adultery.” After further consultation with the censor, the settlers devised an acceptable solution that is still being employed. Men sing the songs, made popular by women; the male voices are then electronically changed to the female pitch and are broadcast accordingly over Arutz 7. A part of the traditional public is satisfied by this expedient; and the learned NRP rabbis insist that no adultery is committed when men listen to the songs being sung. The Haredim obviously have rejected and condemned this accommodation and to date have refused to listen to Arutz 7. Even more importantly, the Haredim, after increasing somewhat their political power in the 1988 elections, were able to impose their position in this regard upon the whole state by forcing a change in the opening of the new Knesset session. The opening ceremony previously began with the singing of “Hatikva,” the Israeli national anthem, by a mixed mal-female choir. After the 1988 election, in deference to Haredi sensitivities, a male singer replaced the mixed choir. After the 1992 election, won by Labor, an all-male choir of the Military Rabbinate sang “Hatikva.”

How can the Haredim, who altogether constitute only a small percentage of Israel’s Jewish population, at times, either alone or even with the help of the NRP, impose their will upon the rest of society? The facile explanation is that both the Labor and Likud parties kowtow to the Haredim for political support. This explanation is insufficient. The kowtowing continued between 1984 and 1990 during the time that Labor and Likud had formed a coalition. Curring favor from the Haredim for alignment purposes was then politically unnecessary. The offered explanation, furthermore, does not adequately take into account the special affinity of all the religious parties, perceived since 1980 as fundamentalist, to Likud and other secular tight-wing parties. This affinity, especially between Likud and the Haredi religious parties, based upon a shared world outlook, is at the crux of Israeli politics. (This affinity is analogous to that existing between Christian and Muslim fundamentalists and their secular right parties.) The relatively simple case of the NRP illustrates this well. The NRP recognizes, although does not always follow, the same halachic authorities as do the Haredi parties. The NRP also adheres to the same ideals relating to the Jewish past and, more importantly, to the future when Israel’s triumph over the non-Jews will allegedly be secure. The differences between the NRP and the Haredim stem from the NRP’s belief that redemption has begun and will soon be completed by the irnminent coming of the Messiah. The Haredim do not share this belief. The NRP believes that special circumstances at the beginning of redemption justify temporary departures from the ideal that could help advance the process of redemption. NRP support in some situations for military service for talmudic scholars is a relevant example here. These deviant NRP ideas have been undermined since the 1970s by the expanding Haredi influence upon increasing numbers of NRP followers who have resisted departures from strict talmudic norms and have favored Haredi positions. This process has been counter-balanced to some extent by the growth in prestige of the NRP settlers who are esteemed as pioneers Minister Rabin by a messianist may have momentarily increased Haredi prestige.

The religious influence upon the Israeli right-wing of Israel B is attributable both to its militaristic character and its widely shared world outlook. Secular and militaristic right-wing, Israeli Jews hold political views and engage in rhetoric similar to that of religious Jews. For most Likud followers, “Jewish blood” is the reason why Jews are in a different category than non-Jews, including, of course, even those non-Jews who are Israeli citizens and who serve in the Israeli army. For religious Jews, the blood of non-Jews has no intrinsic value; for Likud, it has limited value. Menachem Begin’s masterful use of such rhetoric about Gentiles brought him votes and popularity and thus constitutes a case in point. Religious influence is evident in the right’s general reverence for the Jewish past and its insistence that Jews have an historic right to an expanded Israel extending beyond its present borders. More than other secular Israelis, members of the Israeli right insist upon Jewish uniqueness. During many centuries of their existence, the great majority of Jews were similar in some ways to the present-day Haredim. Thus, those Jews who today revere the Jewish past as evidence of Jewish uniqueness respect to some extent religious Jews as perpetuators of that past. An essential part of the right’s emphasis upon uniqueness is its hatred of the concept of “normality,” that is, that Jews are similar to other people and have the same desire for stability as do other nations. Some cultural affinities between secular and religious Jews of the Israeli right are not primarily ideological. Many Likud supporters, whether Sephardic or Askikenazi in origin, are traditionalists; they view rabbis as glamorous figures and are affected by childhood memories of the patriarch4 family in which education was dominated by the grandfather and the women “knew their place.” Although most pronounced in those of the religious vanguard, such considerations also affect secular Jews of the right. The suspicion is long-standing that members of the national camps [that is, the secular right] use power-mad rhetoric to cover their subliminal existential fear of the entire world. The Glory of Ramadan

By E. Yaghi

Ramadan is the (month)

In which was sent down

The Qur’an, as a Guide

To mankind, also clear (Signs)

For guidance and judgment

(Between right and wrong).

So, every one of you

Who is present (at his home)

During that month

Should spend it in fasting,

But if anyone is ill,

Or on a journey,

The prescribed period

(Should be made up)

By days later.

God intends every facility

For you: He does not want

To put you in difficulties.

(He wants you) to complete

The prescribed period,

And to glorify Him

In that He has guided you;

And perchance ye shall be grateful.

(Surat al-Baqara, chapter II:verse 185).

Ramadan falls in the ninth month of the Islamic year. It is a month of fasting and piety ordained on all adult Muslims. Ramadan was designated because it is the month in which Prophet Mohammed (peace and blessings be upon him) received the first revelations of the Holy Qur’an.

The fast of each day in Ramadan begins with dawn and ends immediately when the sun sets. During the hours of fasting, people must refrain from food, drink, smoking and conjugal relations. The month of Ramadan is a month of contemplation and worship. Sawm, or fasting, is the fourth pillar of Islam and another act of Ibadah (worshipping God).

Fasting helps develop self-control and helps us to overcome our human weaknesses. It is a training programme that refreshes us and enables us to carry out some of our duties towards God, who is our Creator and Sustainer.

Fasting gives us the feeling of knowing what it is like to be hungry and thirsty. We experience firsthand what it is like to have an empty stomach. This experience develops empathy for the poor and hungry and enhances social responsibility. It also teaches us discipline and to control our love of comfort.

Fasting helps us to be truly obedient to God’s commands. The month of Ramadan, then, is a month of forgiveness, mercy and a means of avoiding the punishment of Hell. Fasting is the one act that is performed solely for God’s sake. All other acts are performed for our own sakes. Furthermore, those faithful who fast will enter Paradise through a special gate called Al-Rayyan.

Fasting helps us to control our passions and enables us to become people of good deeds and intentions. In fasting, we blend the brotherhood of man with individual self-discipline.

And when we are hungry, our sense of gratitude towards God is awakened, and it is then that we can rightly measure and truly appreciate the countless blessings of our Lord once we are deprived of them.

Fasting has an immense impact on society, for all Muslims, irrespective of their status in life, must observe the obligatory fasting during the same month. This brings to light the essential equality of men and should, too, create in them sentiments of love and brotherhood.

Fasting sharpens our will-power and helps make us less enslaved to our bodily passions. It is in fasting that the spirit has control over the body and where man has full control over his body without being a slave to his physical needs or desires.

The purpose of fasting in other religions is somewhat partial. It is either for spiritual goals, physical needs, or cultivating the intellect, but never for all three combined. However, in Islam, fasting is for all these gains and for many other purposes, including social and economic, moral and humanitarian, private and public, personal and common, and inner and outer. And, since the Islamic calendar is a lunar one, the month of Ramadan, even though it is the ninth month in the Islamic calendar, occurs during different seasons of the year. Thus, the one who fasts experiences the spiritual flavour of Ramadan at variant seasons and variant climates. Sometimes Ramadan falls during long and hot summer days, sometimes during short and cold winter days, sometimes during cool and beautiful spring or fall days. Thus, fasting entails an unfailing effort on the part of the Muslim believer to be ready, dynamic and adaptable.

Wherever you are, whoever you are, may you have a spiritually rich and ever-after rewarding Ramadan. Ramadan Mubarak!

The Wicked Sister

By E. Yaghi

Once upon a time, there lived in Paradise Lost a man by the name of Alfred. Alfred was a hard-working young man who tilled his land with the love of his heart and the toil of his hands. Each day he would go out to the fields and dig his sweet earth in order to provide for his belligerent wife. Yet, no matter how hard he worked or how many crops he sold, he just never seemed to please her.

Each evening, as he trudged home from his fields where grew his dreams and all the wildflowers anyone could ever imagine, he would say to himself, “Oh, I hope that my Rose will be happy! One day, I am going to buy for her the most beautiful dress and the finest shawl just so that I can see for once her eyes shine with joy and hear her tell me how much she appreciates me!”

Alfred’s way home was scented with the aroma of pine trees, green grass and honey nectar. Bees happily buzzed over his head, nightingales filled the surrounding forests with song, and furry little animals scampered out of his way as his boots stomped away those acres before he reached his small and humble cottage. But to him, his cottage was his palace and in his dreams, the walls of his cottage were made of sunflowers so bright they shone like gold.

His stomach growled and he panted with thirst. He could hardly wait to eat, to drink and to fill his stomach with something warm and satisfying. Soon, he reached the door of his humble cottage and thrust it open with his right hand. Inside he was met by his Rose’s frown. He almost felt like going back out into the fields again, but his hunger and fatigue persuaded him not to.

“Rose, I’m home! What’s for dinner? I’m so hungry I could eat a horse!”

“We’re having broth and bones tonight. That’s all I had to cook.”

“But, Rose! I bought you more than a kilo of lamb to cook for supper tonight. What happened to it all?”

He noticed her frown growing wider and deeper as she said in the most irritated voice he knew she had, “It’s your sister!”

“My sister?” he queried. “What has my sister got to do with the meat I bought for dinner?”

“She ate it all!” His wife almost shouted to him.

“But she lives far away from us; how could you even say such a thing?”

“Distance never stopped her from coming and eating our food before now, did it?”

What could poor Alfred say? His sister had come, according to his wife, and eaten all their meat, leaving nothing but bones. He was so hungry and had imagined gulping down a succulent meal as he plowed his fields earlier in the day. Now, he would have to try to fill his stomach with mere broth and bones. He loved his sister, but this was not the first time that she had come while he was in the fields and eaten all the good food. Well, he would have to see about that. Now he was tired, and even the broth and bones would not taste so bad.

Next day, Alfred returned to his fields as always. But on the way home, he stopped by a village store to buy some more meat for dinner the following evening. Surely, his sister would not come back again so soon and he could enjoy a good meal. When he reached home, he told his scowling wife, “Here is some more meat. Please cook it for us tomorrow.”

He almost stumbled and fell when she wrenched the meat from his hand and hastily threw it in a pot to cook for the morrow. He quickly ate and almost as soon as he hit his bed, he was fast asleep.

The next day, he set off for his fields with a quick and almost happy step. Tonight, for dinner, he would eat a good meal. He had not had a good, rewarding, filling meal in a long time it seemed. He worked away the hours and as the sun set, he whistled his way home, taking in all the glory of the soft hues of the sinking summer sun. Tonight just seemed especially beautiful. He could hear every bird sing, could almost hear the grass growing, too, and this evening, the wildflowers and pine trees emitted a special aroma. Indeed, he was in his Paradise Lost.

As soon as he got home, he burst through the door and hollered to his wife, “Rose, I’m home. Did you finish cooking? I’m starving!”

He could see the sullen expression on her face but he did not believe his ears when she replied in her usual snide way, “I am cooking soup tonight.”

“What?” He must not be hearing right. He must be too tired. “What are you saying?”

“You heard me!” she answered. “I said we are having soup tonight.”

He could feel his heart fall with a thud and knew that his face reflected his disappointment. And he almost screamed out his next response to his wife. “Come on now! Don’t try to tell me my sister came and ate all the meat again!”

“That is exactly what happened,” she dryly answered with no expression on her eternally sour, dour face. “Your sister came and ate all the meat as if there was no one else in the whole world but her. I was horrified to see what a glutton she made of herself!”

He had to do something about this whole affair. Perhaps it was his greedy sister that was causing his wife to be so grouchy and grumpy all the time. He found it hard to believe, but for some time, ever since his wife began to complain about his sister, she had turned into a grouchier, grumpier person that she had ever been before. He could not believe the evil thoughts that started growing in his mind and heart. Evil thoughts and intentions for his sister, the sister that he once had loved more than anything in the world until he married. But that seemed like an eternity ago, and this was now. His sister did not appreciate how hard he worked and how hungry he was every evening when he went home from the fields. She had become greedy and selfish and hogged down his food. What would she do next? He had to protect his rights and even his health.

So, every day when Alfred went to the fields, he plotted ways to stop his sister from taking advantage of his wife’s good hospitality. And finally, one night, on his return home, he found his sister waiting to see him. When he entered his small and once cherished cottage, he saw her face light up and she ran to him, hugging him, saying, “Oh dearest brother! It has been so long since we have seen each other!”

“Yes, too long.” Was all he could muster up. And then, he added, “Why don’t you stay the night? We have an extra room where you can sleep. You know, the room we are saving for the baby we are still hoping to have.”

“Yes, brother. Thank you for your kindness. Don’t worry, one day you will have that baby you so want, God willing!”

His wife sat with his sister and they talked about small things, though he noticed his wife begrudgingly making whatever conversation she managed. But really, he didn’t blame her. His sister was trying to ruin their marriage and take all they had for her own selfish purposes! He went outside and began to dig for scorpions and hunt for snakes. Much later, he filled several cans with scorpions and other cans with snakes. He slipped past his wife and sister and placed the cans in his sister’s room, knowing that she would notice them and open them and then he would be rid of her forever!

He said good night and went off to bed. His sister soon went to her room and his wife joined him. Meanwhile, when his sister was alone, she noticed the cans sitting all around the room. “What could be here in these cans, and are they for me?” she wondered.

She opened the first can and it was full of gold coins. She opened a second can and it was full of diamond necklaces. She opened yet another can and it was full of gold bracelets. All she could say was, “That is good. I will shut them and keep them for my brother, for he deserves the best always and, after all, he is the one who works so hard. I am happy that he seems to be doing so well. He can now afford to build a bigger house, get some hired help and live an easier life.”

With these thoughts and a smile on her face, she fell asleep. Next morning, expecting to see her dead, Alfred slowly opened her door. He found her dressed and looking clean and fresh. His eyes almost popped out of his head, but he managed to say, “UMMMMMMM, well, did you sleep well last night, sister?”

“Oh yes, dearest brother. I slept better than I have for a long, long time. Here, my brother, please take these cans and put them in a safe place. They are yours.”

He retreated a few steps backwards and said in an almost inaudible voice, “No, these cans are for you. You must take them.”

She moved closer to him, holding out one of the cans. “I cannot take your gold and diamonds! I want you to have them. You deserve all the best in life!”

Gold? Diamonds? What was she talking about? He looked into the can she was holding and sure enough, gold coins almost too bright to look at, filled the can to the brim. He blinked several times, pinched himself even, thinking he must be dreaming. But he was awake, more awake than he had been in ages.

His sister took another can and in delight spoke, “Look, dearest brother, this can has diamond necklaces! How could I ever accept such gifts from you?”

Then Alfred knew that his sister was innocent. For, had she been guilty, the snakes and scorpions would have killed her and had she been greedy as his wife constantly accused her of being, she would not have refused gifts of gold and diamonds. Now he knew his wife had been lying all along. So he ashamedly told his sister all that had happened and how he had actually filled the cans with snakes and scorpions to kill his only sister.

She was sad and very quiet. For a long time, she said nothing, even though her brother cried and apologized to her. He could see all the hurt on her face and in her eyes. But all she said was, “I am so sorry for you, my dear brother. You must have been living in such misery that it has clouded your thinking. I have never been to your house in over a year and, of course, would not ever think of eating all your meat or your food. Surely, you must remember me for not ever being selfish or taking advantage of anyone’s hospitality, especially yours. I think your wife has been feeding the meat you bring to a secret person who she just might be having an affair with.”

His sister was right. Alfred discovered that his wife did have a secret male friend that she used to feed all the good meat to. So he did the most honourable thing he could think of doing. He divorced his Rose, for her thorns were too sharp and had pricked him one too many times. He later remarried and now has an enlarged cottage full of young ones and a heart full of happiness. He considers himself to be the richest man in the world and when his sister comes for a visit, she is always welcome!

As for the cans filled with gold and diamonds, Alfred insisted that he and his sister divide the treasure in half. Though he gained a fortune and so did his sister, they gained so much more when they re-gained one another and renewed their faith in God.
