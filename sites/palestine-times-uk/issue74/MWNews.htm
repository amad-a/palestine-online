Jordanian soldier sentenced to life imprisonment for killing Israelis

 

Amman - PT

A Jordanian military court on 19 July sentenced Ahmad Dakamseh to life imprisonment with hard labour on the charge of killing seven Israeli girls who were shot down on 13 March while visiting Baqoura, a tourist spot north of Jordan on the border.

The court found Dakamseh, a 26-year-old soldier, not guilty of premeditated murder but he was convicted of committing intentional murder. The court also ordered to demote his rank of corporal to private and to discharge him from the military service. Dakamseh, who was serving at a border post near Baqoura, was insulted by the Israeli teenaged girls while he was performing prayers. When he finished, he took up his gun and began shooting in the direction of the girls. Medical reports showed that he was mentally disturbed.

A statement was issued by the defence lawyer expressing his shock over the verdict. He said that he expected the sentence to be eased because the murder was not a premeditated crime and that his client was not aware of what he was doing. He also said that the girls were students at a higher Jewish religious school and they mocked the Jordanian soldier as he prayed.

The former Prime Minister Ahmad Obeidat, who was also a member of Dakamseh's defence team, expressed his dissatisfaction with the sentence saying he expected it to be between 10 and 15 years because the crime was not premeditated. "In the case of a crime affecting more than one victim, life imprisonment is the usual sentence, but the court applied this without taking into consideration the mental state of the accused," he said. Dakamseh escaped the death penalty when the court ruled that the murder of the girls visiting the border tourist spot known as "peace island" on 13 March was not premeditated or politically motivated.

The court said his shooting spree could be explained by "personality disorders," "extreme fatigue after a 22-hour guard shift," and "sexual frustration, because his wife had recently given birth."

Described as the most serious decision in its history,

Jordan's Muslim Brotherhood decides to boycott elections

 

Amman - Special for PT

A leading Islamic group in Jordan decided to boycott the parliamentary elections scheduled to be held next November.

A statement issued by the Muslim Brotherhood, the main Islamic and Opposition stream in Jordan, attributed its decision, which was described by analysts and observers as the most serious political decision in the MB's 50-year history, to a number of accumulated abuses of human rights and public freedoms as well as to the arbitrary decisions made by the successive governments which caused the democratic process in the country to deteriorate.

"The MB participated in the 1993 elections despite the dissolution of parliament before the end of its legal mandate and the issuance of the temporary law of "One Man One Vote" in 1993 which, according to many local, Arab and international leaders of the public opinion and public work, aimed to diminish and contain the Islamic Movement. This law has torn the social fabric and paralysed the popular political forces from developing the political and social life of Jordan. It has also aborted the possibility of any real exchange of power or influence on the political and legislative decisions," the statement said, adding that the law led to the coming of a parliament that did not represent the people's conscience or the different social categories. "It was a parliament run by the government and through which it succeeded to pass all legislations, laws and decisions the government wanted," the statement argued.

Explaining why the Muslim Brotherhood did not boycott the 1993 elections although they were held under the same law and political circumstances, it pointed out that this was to give the government a chance to reconsider its policies. "If the MB refrained from taking the same decision in previous important political occasions, this was for the purpose of giving a chance for reform and with the hope to activate its role in parliament as well as to fulfill important achievements to confront the reconciliation agreements with the Israelis and their consequences. The MB was also hoping to stop the deterioration of democracy. However, the accumulated events and pressures continued because of the progressive regression in various areas which obliged the Movement to stop and reconsider the situation."

Commenting on the situation of the public freedoms and human rights in Jordan, the Muslim Brotherhood noticed that it was deteriorating. "There was a marked increase in arbitrary and unjustified arrests, especially those members of the MB and those who sympathized with the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas). Some of those political prisoners were subject to different types of unprecedented physical and psychological torture in this country. A large number of citizens were arrested following the increase of bread prices and many others were pursued on charges of slander and received severe sentences. The government prevented the majority of the peaceful activities of the parties in which they wanted to express their stands. The Opposition parties were dealt with as if they were illegal parties. Several measures taken by the government made these parties live in fear and obsession over an expected collapse of democracy," the statement pointed out.

The Islamic Movement attacked the Jordanian-Israeli peace treaty saying it endangers Jordan's existence, identity, sovereignty, independence and power. "The treaty also confirms the falsity of the promises of the so-called peace. The citizens are now rejecting it more than any time before. They also understand its dangers and fear the future more than any other time," the statement added.

"The Muslim Brotherhood are aware of the critical state of the local, regional and international politics and understand their responsibility towards the country and the nation's causes, especially at this crucial stage, and they believe that their decision of boycotting the 1997 parliamentary elections is necessary to establish democracy and protect the homeland just as their decision to participate in the previous elections was to achieve the same goal."

However, the MB specified a number of points it said would rectify the present situation:

1. Making constitutional reforms that establish the separation between authorities and give the legislative authority its full right and role in legislation, supervision and accountability.

2. Cancelling the "One Man One Vote" law and replacing it with a civilized law that can achieve impartiality and justice in order to allow the citizens to elect the deputies who represent them honestly.

3. Cancelling the temporary press law in order to maintain the freedom of expression and the democracy of the press and media.

4. Ceasing all arbitrary measures against the parties and other establishments of the civil society and allowing the activities of the peaceful Opposition.

5. Treating the different economic situations through serious action by resisting all forms and instruments of corruption and rejecting the implementation of dictation by the International Monetary Fund and the policies of the New World Order.

6. Releasing freedoms and ceasing the abuse and violations against these freedoms in a way that fosters the popular partnership in decision-making and in forging the trends that affect the present and future of the people.

7. Ceasing the normalization of relations with the Zionist State and closing the door to prevent its penetration into our society.

The Muslim Brotherhood has 15 members in the present Jordanian Lower House of Parliament who won the 1993 elections under the Islamic Action Front (IAF) compared to 23 deputies in 1989. The Muslim Brotherhood established the IAF in 1993 following the issuance of the parties law to be its political party although it attracted a number of Islamists other than the members of the MB.

Meanwhile (at the time of writing this report), four Jordanian opposition parties decided to follow the MB in boycotting the 1997 parliamentary elections. The professional associations had also decided to follow suit. Mr. Emad Abu Diyya, a member of the MB's executive committee, predicted that the other opposition parties will follow. "The people are fed up; they are tired. The economic and political situation is making everyday life harder and harder for the average citizen," he said.

The strategic goals of the U.S. assistance to Egypt

By Abdel Karim Hammoudeh

The U.S.-Egyptian relations have been strongly shaken recently after a recommendation made last June by the foreign operations subcommittee of the congressional appropriations committee deleting Egypt from the list of the foreign countries that receive American military and financial assistance. Egypt was due to receive $2.1 billion of this assistance during 1998. Moreover, the committee's chairman Senator Mitch McConnell declared a decision to deduct $205 million from the assistance allocated to Egypt and pay the amount to Jordan instead.

The timing of the U.S. committee's decision carried political implications because it came at a time when the peace process stalled in the Middle East. It also came amidst American and Israeli accusations against Egypt that it was encouraging the Palestinians and the other Arab parties to face the Israeli obstinacy. Although, the decisions and recommendations made by the congressional committee are not binding to the Congress or to the U.S. Administration, the committee's decision had a symbolic significance. Observers say that if the U.S. Administration wanted to achieve certain objectives from the assistance to Egypt, the hinting to cease it could be a kind of pressure which eventually serves Israel's interests. According to American and Arab diplomatic sources, no country is eligible for the U.S. assistance nor should it consider this assistance an unquestionable right. The continuation of this assistance depends on the response of that country to Washington's desires and interests. The congressional committee justified its decision concerning the assistance to Egypt because of Egypt's efforts to guard and protect Libya which, according to the committee, made Cairo a state that defends terrorism. It "accused" Egypt of building close relations with Libya, refusing to attend the Middle East summit which President Clinton was invited to for last September, suspending the multilateral peace talks, impeding talks between the PLO and the Israeli government over Hebron, and leading efforts to revive the boycott of Arab commercial relations with Israel.

U.S. assistance to Egypt

The U.S. Administration began rendering assistance to Egypt in 1975 after the restoration of the relations between the two countries. That year, the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) opened an office in Cairo to give assistance to Egypt. In 1978, following the signing of the Camp David Accords between Egypt and Israel, the financial assistance was sharply increased and covered different aspects of economic activities, infrastructure and birth control projects, environment protection programmes, and the encouragement of developing democracy.

A report issued by the U.S. Congress revealed that Egypt has received total assistance of $18.8 billion from the United States between 1975 and 1994, of which $15 billion were donations and $3.8 billion were easy loans. Sources pointed out that the $2.1 billion of American assistance to Egypt is divided into $1.3 billion in military assistance and $800 million for development programmes. An American researcher pointed out that Egypt's military purchases from the United States during the last 20 years have achieved a number of benefits to the U.S. industry:

- A total of $8.261 billion as profits and wages of workers in the American military industry besides offering about 136,600 jobs every year.

- A direct impact on production and investment which were estimated at $7.9 billion which in turn offered 102,000 job opportunities.

- Local and federal taxes and charges estimated at $4.098 billion.

Egypt's Position

Many Egyptian economists stress that the importance of the U.S. assistance to Egypt is weakening because of inflation and the rates of economic growth in Egypt. They add that the U.S. assistance now forms only 3% of Egypt's national income which reached 268 billion Egyptian pounds (about $75 billion) in the annual budget 1997/98 while it formed 12% in the 1979 budget. The economists pointed out that most of the assistance is spent on American commodities that are not needed by Egypt and on high payments and wages for American consultants. Therefore, the ceasing of the assistance will not have a negative impact on the Egyptian economy. They added that 70% of U.S. assistance goes back to the U.S. treasury.

The Egyptian government began considering measures to face all possibilities and consequences of a future U.S. decision to cease the assistance. A committee was set up for this purpose which recommended a reduction in the government subsidization of commodities and services, the government expenditures, and the allocations for activities that use the U.S. assistance funds.

Although the congressional appropriations committee, headed by Senator Ted Stevens, refused the recommendation by the foreign operations subcommittee and hence kept Egypt on the list of foreign aids for 1998 with the same level of assistance ($2.116 billion), the crisis caused by the subcommittee's recommendation had many reactions inside Egypt. It is a precedent that could be repeated in future to pressure and blackmail Egypt politically and economically. These pressures are not exerted to serve the U.S. interests but to appease the Zionist Lobby in Washington.

Turkey on Erbakan's road... in spite of Attaturk

By Nabil Chbib

Turkey today is different than what it was before the coming to power of Necmettin Erbakan and his Rafah Party a year ago. The assessment of Erbakan's supporters could be different than that of his rivals regarding the 11 months he spent in power. But they have no way to differ over or deny the following political facts:

- The secularists in and out of Turkey were accusing the Islamists of making use of the democratic 'game' in order to come into power and then got rid of the other parties. This was not the case. Rafah was the only party that defended democracy while the other parties ran in the opposite direction.

- Islamists were also accused of not being able to run a modern state and of taking the country back to the 'European' Middle Ages. But the last 12 months were the only period in the history of modern Turkey in which its economy flourished. Many significant achievements were made during that period, such as solving the problem of electrical power needs through agreements with neighbouring countries, breaking the monopoly of the Western arms factories by the establishment of joint factories with Islamic nations, and increasing the share prices in the Turkish main stock exchange in Istanbul, which doubled compared with its prices on the first day Mr. Erbakan took power.

- All decisions made by the army leaders regarding the dismissal of the Islamist soldiers and even the foreign policy were implemented by the government without any objection. But Mr. Erbakan did not yield to this 'tradition' revealing the real face of the secular military which is supported by the West.

- Mr. Erbakan had two choices regarding the future government of Turkey: to return to its religious, cultural and historic origins and play a major role in the Islamic world to which it belongs, or to stay where Attaturk put it, at Europe's doorstep where the secularists can only hope to enter. These secularists serve the West by any and all means. They also serve Israel despite the evident U.S.-Israeli aggression on the region which endangers the interests of Turkey as well as the surrounding countries. Despite their efforts, they are still unable to establish a foothold in Europe.

There is one point that should not be ignored: Mr. Erbakan's achievements during his one year in government were the fruits of his efforts over the past 20 years. The cheating, deception and pressure by the secular powers, both the right and left wings, in order to force Mr. Erbakan to hand over the power, should not be viewed as a victory for the secularists over the Islamist party in Turkey. Many points to the contrary can be easily observed:

- All right and left parties in the secular camp found themselves forced to meet in order to achieve a certain goal in the short run which was to get rid of the party (Rafah) that occupied the first rank in the last parliamentary elections.

- There is no other objective that can unite the present coalition government parties led by Prime Minister Masout Yelmaz. They do not have identical views regarding certain important issues such as fixing a date for early elections, the economic policies, or the Kurdish question.

- Yelmaz came to power by a military order. This is enough to make people reject such a false form of democracy and to make Rafah more popular. The public opinion polls indicate that Rafah will easily win 30% of the parliamentary seats in the coming elections and some expected it will have an absolute majority!

- The major, if not the only, mission assigned for the new government is to carry out the military scheme which aims to uproot the Islamic trend in Turkey. This means the closing of the religious "Imams" schools and institutes, which were originally established by the secularists to control the religious trend. But later these schools became one of the most important information channels for the Islamists. The scheme also targets the universities which their students rebelled against Attaturk's secularism.

It is a kind of political stupidity that one expects this scheme to succeed. Attaturk's Western-made secularism has possessed all means in the last 70 years to impose its perspectives, methods and ailing ideas on the Turkish people in circumstances more severe than the present ones. But the result was that the generations which abide by their religion and civilization are the people who were born in the golden times of secularism. Therefore, how will the next generation be in Turkey?

The international powers may support the secularists in Turkey with all possible means, but, as usual, it is a conditional and short-term support because the decision makers in the West are convinced that Turkey is an Islamic country. Therefore they will never contribute to a real economic or technological development in Turkey because, sooner or later, these achievements will be in the hands of the Turks who are loyal to their country and to its religion, Islam.
