Press Cuttings

The Financial Times, 15 September 1999

Negotiations on a final settlement of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict got off to a shaky start yesterday.

..However, Israel was preparing at least a dozen committees to deal with the issues at the core of the conflict. These are the future status of Jerusalem, the 3.5m Palestinian refugees, Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, Israel's borders and water.

On Jerusalem, Mr Barak said this week the unity and sovereignty over the city was not in question. Israel, he added, intended to "strengthen her sovereignty in Jerusalem".. However, there are at least five models on the negotiating table, including an Israeli proposal to expand Jerusalem's municipal boundaries. This could allow Israel to suggest Abu Dis, a Palestinian village on the outskirts of east Jerusalem, as a capital for Palestine.

The refugee issue is no less complex. Mr Barak has said there can be no right of return to Israel for those Palestinians who fled the 1948 war. Mr Arafat has declared the opposite.

The Times, 16 September 1999

Binyamin Netanyahu, the former Israeli Prime Minister, and his wife Sara were questioned by police for several hours yesterday after media allegations that they took part in a fraud involving more than 70,000 of state funds.

The couple arrived at the Bet Yam fraud squad offices in Tel Aviv for questioning about an alleged scam involving renovations, house cleaning and other chores at their private home by an employee who submitted an enormous claim to the Government.

The Financial Times, 21 September 1999

Prominent Palestinian intellectuals want Yassir Arafat to hold a referendum before the Palestinian leader signs a final peace settlement with Israel.

The call for a referendum, made at a weekend conference on final settlement issues in the West Bank city of Ramallah, signals growing concern and frustration among liberal Palestinian intellectuals.

They fear their negotiators will make too many compromises over the final status issues and that, as in previous negotiations, Palestinian leaders will consult neither intellectuals nor the public.

Ha'aretz, 21 September 1999

So far this year, donor countries have given the Palestinian Authority only $174 million out of a promised $740 million, said a PA minister on Sunday.

According to Mohamad Shtaye, director of the Palestinian Economic Council for Development and Reconstruction, which carries out many of the infrastructure development plans, the amount is even smaller: between $85 and $90 million. Shtaye foresees that the low level of contributions will impact on the Palestinian Authority's investment plans for the upcoming years as well as private sector activities.

The Times, 23 September 1999

Ehud Barak, the Israeli Prime Minister, yesterday applauded the arrest in Jordan of three prominent members of Hamas, the militant Islamic group believed to have been plotting attacks against the Jewish state.

Mr Barak, who is on a visit to Berlin, said: "The very attitude of King Abdullah [of Jordan] to Hamas is an example of security awareness, an anti-terror approach and of courageous standing that puts a limit on the operational latitude of an extremely dangerous organisation."

The New York Times, 23 September 1999

The political and security threat posed by the militant Islamic movement Hamas got renewed attention Wednesday when Israeli officials charged that two bombs that exploded prematurely this month had been intended for passenger buses, and when Jordan arrested three leaders of Hamas at the Amman airport.

The Israeli Ministry of Public Security said four Hamas plotters, all Israeli Arabs, had planned to bomb two Jerusalem-bound buses on Sept. 5 after being recruited and trained in the West Bank.

Ha'aretz, 24 September 1999

When the talks on the framework agreement with the Palestinians begin in the near future, we will see the Palestinian side demanding that Israel accept responsibility in principle for the creation of the Palestinian refugee problem during the War of Independence in 1948..

Israel's representatives to the talks must therefore exercise extreme caution concerning each and every letter in the wording of documents expressing regret or recognition of the rights of the refugees.
