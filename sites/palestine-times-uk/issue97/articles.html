Palestinian Anger By Remote Control

By Mahmoud al Khatib

The “Day of Anger”, which was organized last month by the Palestinian Authority in protest against the Zionist settlement, was a failure. The Palestinian Street did not react to it enthusiastically, and the demonstrations were scattered in a small number of villages. There were no confrontations with the Occupation troops in the way the PA wanted. In short, the Palestinian protests were not in proportion with the Zionist crimes for which the “anger” was ignited.

Arafat’s Authority committed a mistake when it dealt with the Palestinian People as a “robot” that can be moved and stirred with a remote control. For the anger and exasperation, which are spontaneous human feelings, against the Jewish settlement policies cannot be ignited in the morning and cooled off in the evening through a decision signed by the PA Chairman.

The failure of the PA’s “Day of Anger” was discussed during the meeting of the Revolutionary Council of Fateh, the main PLO faction. Members of the Council considered this failure as a popular stand against the PA, not the Jewish settlement itself. The Palestinians are no longer trusting an authority that wants to make use of their national sentiments to serve its narrow interests in talks with the Zionist government. As some Palestinians put it, “The masses cannot cooperate and respond to a corrupt authority, which colludes with Israel at night and asks its people to take to the streets protesting against Israel in the morning!”

The Jewish settlement policy has continued and escalated since the first day of the occupation of the Palestinian land. It has not stopped with the signing of the Oslo accords. On the contrary, the settlement activities reached their peak after signing the Wye River agreement last October. Sources say that more than 20 Jewish settlements have been built in the West Bank since that date. The Oslo accords and the five-year transitional period were understood by the Israelis as an invaluable chance to swallow whatever they want from the Palestinian land in order to create new facts on the ground before the final status negotiations.

The Likud and the Labour are playing integrated powers in this regard. The construction of settlements and the confiscation of Palestinian lands have not been halted with the changing of the Zionist governments. In fact, Benyamin Netanyahu (Bibi) came to complete settlements that Rabin and Peres started. Barak has also announced that he would not stop the settlement projects started by Netanyahu, although he is not going to approve the construction of new settlements in the West Bank. Before Barak takes power, Netanyahu’s government allocated an additional budget of $12 million for different settlement projects in the West Bank.

Barak is not so different than the arrogant Netanyahu. He warned Arafat after the “Day of Anger” that he would not implement the Wye River agreement if the Palestinians did not keep silent. Barak’s attitude goes in harmony with his declared platform. It is also no different than the guidelines of the coming coalition government, which, according to Israeli reports, are based on several principles:

- The peace agreements with the Palestinians and other Arab parties should be based on the vital security interests of the Jewish State;

- The acceleration of the peace process towards the final agreement with the Palestinians, which will be subject to a referendum. This means that the Israelis will force the PA to overstep the Wye River agreement, which was not implemented by Netanyahu’s government, and to immediately start the final status talks although the Zionist occupation has not completed the redeployment of its troops in the West Bank according to the timetable agreed upon in the Wye River agreement;

- Jerusalem will remain a “united and eternal” capital of the Jewish State;

- No new settlements will be built until the two parties reach an agreement on the final status of the West Bank and Gaza. Also, the present situation of the settlements will not be changed. The Israeli government “will meet the demands of the existing settlements,” which means that Barak will continue supporting the present settlement activities.

The Palestinian Authority continues to receive slaps in the face without showing any sign that it has begun to understand the Israeli mentality. Contrary to the result of last May elections, Barak gained only 48% of the Jewish votes against 52% obtained by Netanyahu. This means that Barak won thanks to the votes of the Arabs who live in the 1948-occupied Palestine. Reports say that 95% of Arab votes went to Barak (about a half million votes). The great lesson that should be absorbed by the PA is that the Israeli community has been tending towards extremism and the Right.

Despite that, the PA’s joy with Barak’s coming was suppressed from the beginning when Barak made a number of statements regarding the peace process with the PA, which showed that he was not anyone but “Bibi’s twin”! This put the PA in a critical position. On the one hand, Barak was a disappointment although the PA marketed him among the Arab electors urging them to vote for him. On the other hand, the PA has become aware of the fact that it is unable to gather the Palestinians round it and to restore its popularity among its people, which was proven at the Day of Anger!

Abu Marzuk: Arafat should declare the failure of Oslo

Occupied Jerusalem - Many observers consider Musa Abu Marzuk one of the most shrewdest and wisest Palestinian Islamist politicians. Last month, the Palestinian journalist Tawfik Abu Bakr interviewed Abu Marzuk in Amman. The following are excerpts from the interview.

Question: What is your evaluation of the political transformation in Israel which brought Barak to the helm of power in the Jewish State?
Answer: I believe there is a great deal of exaggeration about the significance of the outcome of the Israeli election. We know that the two biggest parties in the Zionist regime have been alternating power over the last 20 years. Before 1977, the Labor party ruled Israel unceasingly since 1948. So there is nothing unusual about the Labor Party coming to power again. Then, there is not a difference of substance between Ehud Barak and Netanyahu, and when we thoroughly examine Barak's ideas, we find out that they are very much similar to those of Netanyahu. These media may sometimes project his ideas differently, but in reality there is no inherent or intrinsic difference between Barak and Netanyahu.

Q: What do you think brought about the downfall of Netanyahu?
A: What brought down Netanyahu was the fragility of the Netanyahu coalition government and the nature of political coalitions in the Israeli arena, which has too many parties. There is another factor, though, and that is the United States, which contributed to the downfall of Netanyahu.

Q: How did this come about?
A: The U.S. helped the Labor Party disintegrate the Likud Party by convincing David Levy first, and then Yitshak Mordechai and other former Likudists, who later formed the Centre Party.

Q: Do you see a way out of the Oslo impasse?
A: The way out is by declaring the failure of Oslo; it is by appearing before the people and announcing that "I signed the Oslo Accords for the interest of my people, and for the interest of my people I abandon these accords." Then the Palestinian people would be reunited.

Q: But the termination of the Oslo Accords would only makes us revert to the pre-Oslo situation and would result in dismantling PA institutions and drive around a quarter of a million Palestinians out of Palestine.
A: Things are not that simple. Who could oust 150,000 or more who returned to their homes? If they are truly freedom-fighters, let them wield their guns and resume armed struggle anew.

Q: They can't be ousted in one day, but there are many ways they can be harassed and forced into leaving.
A: If those returnees are not selfish hangers-on looking after their narrow interests, let them become true strugglers, then Oslo would become an Israeli demand.

Q: What about the currents in Hamas now?
A: There is a trend in Hamas to be more moderate and pragmatic, and this is represented by the home-front leaders who are suffering the Israeli occupation. This trend found expression in Hamas participation in the Central Council meetings in Gaza in April.

Q: What else?
A: Hamas has not carried out any military operations for some time now, and this, I suppose, contributed to the downfall of Netanyahu.

Q: Is this attributed to a new way of thinking in Hamas or to PA pressure?
A: Hamas has not frozen military action, and armed struggle is still Hamas' way, a way that is not accepted either regionally or internationally. Hamas hasn't changed, and the moment Hamas abandons armed struggle, it will not be Hamas; it will be some other movement.

Q: Will Hamas join PLO institutions?
A: What institutions? The PLO doesn't have true institutions warranting serious discussion of the matter. Where is the role of the National Council (PNC) or the Central Council (PCC) or the Palestinian National Fund (PNF)? All these bodies are now in the service of the authority. Even Fatah itself has been turned into a tool in the service of the PA. Unfortunately, these bodies and so-called institutions don't meet unless Israel and the United States call for such meetings. No serious nationalist in Hamas or other organizations take part in these bodies which noted Palestinian figures have abandoned. Hamas adopts armed resistance against the Zionist occupation, and this is incompatible with the stance of the PLO.

Q: What did the PA do to Hamas?
A: If the PA were to stop pressuring and persecuting Hamas, it would come to an end tomorrow. We all know that the mission of the PA is to protect the "agreements."

Propping up the Basic Law in London’s Underground

By Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Obsolete, defunct, null and void; this is how Israel’s propaganda machine describes UN resolution 181 of 1947. With general elections now decided, the Jewish State is intensifying its media blitz on Western cities. From Los Angeles through Chicago to London, the message is the same: There must be no return to the 1947 resolution. Faced with the challenge of securing a final settlement with the Palestinians before the new millennium, Israelis of every political persuasion remain virtually haunted by the past. As the race against time gathers momentum, there are, evidently, only two options left. Either the settler colony rectifies the wrongdoings of 1947, or confronts an unknown future with the burden it incurred from dispossessing the Palestinians.

Judging from the tone and content of the current media offensive it is clear that the Israelis will choose the latter option. Those who wishfully thought that Prime Minister-elect Ehud Barak would bring a new approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict were given a shocking indication of what lies ahead. Speaking in the characteristic language of disdain, threats and belligerence, he issued within hours of his election his famous “Four Nos”: No to the division of Jerusalem; no to a withdrawal to the boundaries of 1967; no to the dismantling of the settlements; and no to the creation of a Palestinian army.

Although all four conditions negate established Palestinian national objectives, the first is, undoubtedly, the only one upon which the future peace and stability of the entire Middle East rests. No other issue apart from that of the refugees can evoke as much passion both locally and regionally as that of Jerusalem. Palestinian resistance to Netanyahu’s provocations during the 1996 tunnel affair and the 1999 Orient House crisis are both pertinent reminders of how critically important this city is. Israel’s ongoing campaign against the validity of resolution 181 must therefore be seen not simply as an attempt to determine the nature and size of the future Palestinian State but also as one to legitimize its occupation of Jerusalem.

The Basic Law
In hindsight, the Partition Resolution [181] made absolutely no statutory provisions for Israeli sovereignty over west Jerusalem. This, quite sadly, is a fact that is overlooked much too often by opinion and policy makers alike. The failure of the 1948 and 1967 annexations of west and east Jerusalem respectively to gain legitimacy has always been a source of unease for the Jewish State. Hence, it resorted to the familiar practice of creating its own legitimacy by enacting in July 1980 the Basic Law, which presumed that a “complete and united Jerusalem is the capital of Israel…” In effect, this measure was no different from the Law of Return (1950) that was enforced to facilitate Jewish immigration and simultaneously prevent the return of the Palestinian refugees. Neither was it any different from the Law of Absentee Property (1950), which provided legal cover for the expropriation of millions of acres of Palestinian land not provided for by resolution 181.

One of the popular arguments put forward by the Israelis against the Partition Resolution is that it was passed by the General Assembly (and not by the Security Council), and is, therefore, not binding. They pretend to forget that it was the General Assembly, which “Acting in discharge of its functions under Article 4 of the Charter and rule 125 of its rules of procedure” had decided “to admit Israel to membership in the United Nations” (Resolution 273 (III) of 11 May 1949). They fail to realize, moreover, that if the principles of justice were applied in this matter without fear or favour, Israel’s eligibility in the organization would become entirely untenable because of its refusal to honour two of the conditions under which it was granted membership. The first of these conditions which stems from the UN Charter specifies that Member States must publicly define their borders. In the second instance, Israel also gave an undertaking to the UN to allow the Palestinian refugees to return to their homes. Half a century later, the Jewish State continues to defy the international community by refusing both to define its borders and allow the refugees to return to their homes.

Given their ambiguous favouring of Security Council resolutions over those of the General Assembly, it must be pointed out that it was the former that adopted resolution 478 in 1980 censuring “in the strongest terms the enactment by Israel of the ‘Basic Law’ on Jerusalem and the refusal to comply with relevant Security Council resolutions.” While there is not a single UN resolution supporting the Israeli argument that resolution 181 is invalid, the Security Council has actually declared that the Basic Law and all such measures adopted by Israel to change the geographic, demographic and historical character and status of “the Holy City of Jerusalem are null and void and must be rescinded” (resolution 476). Instead of complying with the will of the Council, the Israelis have persisted in their campaign to mislead public opinion on the issue of Jerusalem. Throughout the past six months they found no better place to advance this cause than the busy stations of the London Underground.

In the Underground
As expected, the decision by the Israeli Tourist Board to post billboards in the London Underground depicting the Aqsa Mosque in east Jerusalem and the Dome of the Rock did provoke widespread public indignation. More than 20,000 letters of protest were sent to the relevant authorities demanding their immediate withdrawal. The first poster that appeared in January 1999 bore the caption: “In the land where the millennium began…the Dome has already been built.” Local Muslims as well as Christians viewed the stunt not only as an affront to their religious sensitivities but also as an assault on Britain’s sovereignty, particularly because it has always maintained that east Jerusalem is occupied territory.

While the official response to the letter campaign claimed that there was no deliberate attempt to make a political statement concerning the legal status of east Jerusalem, it did accede that a play was made on the Millennium Dome in London to lure holiday makers to Israel. Despite the removal of the posters in February and written assurances from the authorities that they will not be displayed again at any point in the future, the Israeli Tourist Board somehow managed to restore them six months later, albeit with a modified photograph and caption, the latest being: “Can you really experience the millennium in SE 10?” With the word “Israel” tucked into a corner of the poster, the tacit and deceptive suggestion is that a visit to the Dome of the Rock will be a more rewarding experience than going to the London Millennium Dome.

From weakness to strength
In spite of its cunning and ferocity, Israel’s latest media offensive has been wholly counter-productive. By using one of Europe’s busiest thoroughfares to preempt the final status of Jerusalem, it managed to win few friends and alienate many. Time and circumstances have played a crucial role in this. Unlike the decades preceding 1967, public awareness about Jerusalem has changed considerably. As the Israeli-Palestinian conflict enters its second century, people are no longer beguiled by empty slogans about anti-Semitism and Arab or Islamic terrorism. They have learnt, in fact, that national histories are not simply about personalities, no matter how media-friendly or articulate they may appear. And that what matters most today is the ability of States to honour their treaty obligations, respect human rights and aspirations, and uphold the principles of equality and justice. Hence they continue to ask, what is the value of peace to the Palestinians if it censors their freedom, usurps their land and denies their humanity?!

Gone were the days when Israel could contravene Palestinian national rights on the pretext that all Arabs are undemocratic and Israel is the sole defender of democracy in the region. Surely it is common knowledge that Israel is the only “democracy” in the world that legalizes torture and enforces apartheid policies against its subject people. It demolishes their homes, confiscates their identity documents and evicts them from their homes not because it is right but only because it has the power to do so. As a result, the isolation that has befallen the Jewish State has nothing to do with the personalities of its leaders. It is rather the consequence of its oppressive policies against the Palestinians.

For all intents and purposes, the presence of the Jerusalem posters in the London Underground is a natural extension of these policies. Their collective and unquestioned illegitimacy is confirmed in UN Security Council resolution 476. “That all legislative and administrative measures and actions taken by Israel, the occupying power, which purport to alter the character and status of the Holy City of Jerusalem, have no legal validity and constitute a flagrant violation of the Fourth Geneva Convention.” No amount of media spin will alter this fact. Not even the miraculous mantra of anti-Semitism, which in the past opened the doors to Palestine, can change the legal status of Jerusalem. True, in the present circumstances Israel may succeed in imposing its political and military authority over the city. This, nevertheless, must rightly be seen as a deferral of the issue until such time that the balance of power changes.

A final word of caution must be noted. Far too often Arabs and non-Arabs alike have tended to exaggerate the power of Israel. Recent events in the region have, however, shattered the myth of its invincibility. The example of southern Lebanon is a classic example. There it was defeated by a small but organized liberation army that had none of the weapons or technology of its enemy. In the occupied West Bank and Gaza Strip, it came face to face with similar humiliation before it was rescued by the 1993 Oslo accords. Today, Israel relies largely upon the CIA to maintain “law and order” in the territories it illegally occupies. Judging from these two examples, it is clear that Israel got to the position where it is today not so much because of its strength but rather because of the weakness of its adversaries. To reverse this situation the words of the veteran Palestinian leader Haidar Abdul Shafi remain as relevant now as they were when he proclaimed them in July 1993: “I tell you plainly that the negotiations are not worth fighting about. The critical issue is transforming our society. Only once we achieve this will we be in a position of strength..”

*The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre, London, and editor of its Return Review.

A Tale of Six Villages

By Ramzy Baroud

Two large Palestinian delegations protested Israeli ruthlessness on the mornings of Friday and Saturday, 11 and 12 June 1999.

One delegation represented three Palestinian villages that were destroyed in the war of 1967. The other delegation represented three other Palestinian villages facing destruction at the present time by Israel and its Jewish settlers.

Thirty-two years ago, while the war between Israel and the Arabs was still in effect, Israeli army occupation forces launched a campaign of terror against Palestinians residing in the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

Part of the campaign included destroying the three peaceful villages of Imwas, Yalu and Beit Nuba, located halfway between Jerusalem and Tel Aviv.

The three villages were entirely destroyed, as if they never existed. Those who remained and fought were savagely murdered. Those who fled the once-happy place had to live with the anguish and loss like millions of Palestinians refugees scattered in almost every corner of the world.

But, the villagers, like most Palestinians, never abandoned their dream of returning to their homes.

Knowing that the only thing Israel spared from the ruins of these three villages was the old graveyard where many of the fallen martyrs are still buried, the refugees have tried to reach the graves of their elders for many years.

However, the permanent Israeli military checkpoint on the road between the villages and the West Bank town of Ramallah has always shattered the hopes of the locals who attempted to visit the legendary site.

On Friday 11 June, the only successful Palestinian delegation finally made it to the destroyed villages.

Scores of Palestinians gathered beside the abandoned graveyard. They read Qur’an for the souls of their loved ones. Around noon they prayed the Friday prayer in the open, facing Mecca and the place that once was their olive groves.

According to Ehud Barak’s peace promises, Israel will not pull back to the borders of the 1967 war. Such a threat, if carried out, means that the three villages located in the western section of the West Bank will remain under Israeli control and will not be inhabited again by its anxiously awaiting owners.

The people of the three villages are worried that they will fall victims of “peace” the same way their fathers and mothers fell victims of war.

The next day, a delegation from the villages of Azun Itmeh, Beit Amin, and Sinyria, located near the Palestinian town of Qalqiliah, protested Jewish settlers' confiscation of their land under the protection of Israeli forces.

The three threatened villages are located between two ever-expanding Israeli settlements: Oranit and Tishari Tiqfa.

As a result, the two Israeli settlements have been slowly expanding at the expense of the land of Palestinian farmers.

The decision to build a new neighbourhood as an addition to one of the two settlements (which was approved by the Israeli Interior Ministry two years ago) gave a green light to the Israeli army bulldozers to openly confiscate the land which belonged to the Palestinian residents of the area.

The irony is the destruction of the first-mentioned villages, occupied in 1967, took place in time of war. Nowadays, the destruction of the latter-mentioned villages and many more villages located in the West Bank and on the outskirts of Jerusalem is taking place at a time that the world celebrates the courage and heroism of the peacemakers in the Middle East.

After 32 years of occupation of the West Bank and Gaza, many things have indeed changed.

One main difference is the world’s definitions of peace, war and occupation have been extensively rewritten.

While Israeli occupation forces are still controlling most of the West Bank and Gaza, including their control over the borders while letting loose the herd of Jewish settlers to conduct their acts of piracy, the world is heaving a big sigh of relief to thank God, as peace has finally made it to the Middle East.

But with 32 years having passed, the inhabitants of these six villages, destroyed in 1967 and 1999, hold onto age-old principles that they have written in their hearts, rather than on an unworthy piece of paper.

It is these principles that have brought the people of Imwas, Yalu and Beit Nuba back together to pray beside the abandoned grave yard; it is these principles that have made the people of Azun Itmeh, Beit Amin and Sinyria stand with empty hands and faithful hearts in front of the Israeli bulldozers to defend their land.

It is a fundamental principle in truth that cannot easily be comprehended or understood. But for those Palestinians, and perhaps for all the victims of occupation and dispossession, it is the only way to seek justice.

All that Talk about Democracy

By Ray Hanania*

I remember as a young Diaspora Palestinian many years ago being inspired by the repeated references Palestinian revolutionaries made to the term "Democracy".

It was "Democracy" this. "Democracy" that. Remember the stated goal of the Revolution upon the liberation of our homeland: The creation of a "Democratic" non-sectarian State in Palestine where "Jews, Christians and Muslims" would be treated equally? Well, certainly, Palestine has not been liberated.

And, while we have been occupied for many years now, I wonder if we are not now, in fact, imprisoned.

Of course, it must be hard for people to live in a Democracy. At least, that's how it seems to us Diaspora Palestinians who shake our heads these days at all the news reports of silly but serious confrontations that are taking place as "Democracy" sweeps through the un-liberated lands of Palestine.

The repeated arrests of Palestinian journalists are not surprising headlines. When you realize that it's not the Israelis doing the arresting but members of the Palestinian's own secret police handcuffing other Palestinians for "free speech" violations, it gives me pause.

And just as we once decried the growing radicalism of the fanatic Israeli Zionist movement, and the growth of hardcore conservative Jewish extremism, we now see the same thing happening to us.

In Nazareth, for the first time, Palestinians can start worrying not about the Israelis confiscating our lands, but Muslims and Christians battling over the closeness of a Mosque to a Church.

My head shakes.

Or, in Bethlehem, where one telephone call from a "Christian" television viewer complaining about a "play" that didn't exactly cast Christianity in the "proper light" has the Palestinian police raiding and closing the offices of the independent TV station Al Roa.

Very sad, and my head shakes even more.

What is terrible in all this is that it seems from these and other reports that we Palestinians make great sufferers. In suffering at the hands of the Israeli occupiers, we were able to find solidarity with each other, Christians and Muslim Palestinians, Bethlehemites and Nazarenes. When we are not suffering at their hands, we seem to look for suffering and make each other suffer more. The PA needs to dust off some of the old writings of Arafat and other revolutionaries and remember the importance of "Democracy."

And Christians and Muslims back home have to stop thinking about themselves and start thinking of the Palestinian Arab community.

As a Palestinian, it is embarrassing to see our people do exactly those things that we once abhorred in others.

But, it's not just the PA. There are religious fanatics who insist on imposing upon society their rigid religious standards and their interpretations of both the Qur’an and the Bible.

No one is stopping a Priest or an Imam from lecturing about Christianity and Islam from the pulpit. But, it doesn't belong in government, and it doesn't belong in the news media.

The PA needs to re-open Al Roa TV and stop acting like they don't know what the term "Democracy" means.

There is an important lesson the PA has missed that many in the United States already know.

Here, the government allows their critics to speak freely, because the government knows that muzzling someone's opinion usually gets a lot more media attention than the opinion you are trying to silence.

And to those self-appointed religious vigilantes: the truth of a message is much more powerful when it has been challenged by that with which you do not agree.

In other words, criticism makes the intended target a much more powerful force.

*Ray Hanania is a Palestinian American writer and journalist. His columns are archived on the web at (www.hanania.com).

Jews and Muslims in the Post-Israel Era

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

It has been 15 centuries since the Muslims established themselves as a State in which followers of all three monotheistic religions coexisted peacefully and equitably. Until the beginning of the 19th century, the Islamic empire, whose terrain extended over three continents, provided an atmosphere of tolerance that, in contrast to the history of Europe and the Western world in general, prevented the progression of ideological and religious differences into physical conflict. Islam, whose values and principles governed the public and private conduct of individuals and groups, recognized the citizenship rights of Christians and Jews within the Islamic State. In accordance with these rights, their blood, honour, wealth, faith and shrines were sanctified. This recognition enabled the followers of these creeds to realize their potential capabilities and thus innovate and participate on equal footing with the Muslims in building the Arab-Islamic civilization. Muslims never used the term 'minorities' to describe fellow citizens who followed other religions. The Islamic State provided a safe haven for those oppressed in their own countries. Jews in particular suffered persecution and banishment at the hands of European Christians who blamed them for every single crisis or catastrophe incurred. It was only in Muslim lands that the Jews found peace, security and freedom.

The Muslims' perception of the Jews remained unchanged during the first 13 centuries of Islamic rule. They saw them as People of the Book who, together with the Christians, shared with the Muslims common values of faith and conduct that entitled them to citizenship rights in the Islamic State. Such perception began to change after the Zionist movement managed to embroil Jews in its colonial project with the aim of establishing a national home for the 'Jewish People' in Palestine. In the wake of the Second World War, and as a result of the persecution of the Jews in Nazi Germany, the scene was set for the accomplishment of the Zionist dream. The determination of the World Order to enable the Zionists to establish a Jewish State in Palestine turned Jews into enemy number one of both Arabs and Muslims. The Muslim hatred for the Jews was augmented by the fall of Arab Jews into the trap of Zionism.

Historic homes of Jewish communities, such as the Arab Maghreb, Egypt, Iraq and Yemen, witnessed a massive exodus of Jews who migrated to the recently established Zionist entity in Palestine. It turned out later that it was the Zionist movement's acts of terror that intimidated the Arab Jews into emigrating. It was then that the Arabs no longer discriminated between the Zionist invaders who came all the way from Poland, Russia, America, Western Europe or South Africa and the Jews who had been living with Arab Muslims and Christians, and who, for many centuries, shared the same history and civilization with them.

In spite of the undisguised secularist¾ even atheist¾ root of the Zionist project, some Arab and Muslim thinkers deemed it necessary, perhaps even useful, to focus on a purely religious explanation for the Zionist phenomenon.

Through a re-reading of history, aided by a re-interpretation of the sacred text, these thinkers sought to prove that Jews, by virtue of some inherited characteristics, have always been corrupt and ill intentioned. But the real help came from Christian anti-Jewish writings. The most influential document in this regard was the one entitled, “The Protocols of the Elders of Zion”, which concludes that Jews have hatched a global conspiracy aimed at imposing their control over the world and at subjugating all else to their influence so as to serve their own interests. The occupation of Palestine and the establishment of a Jewish State in it have been said by the believers in this theory to be a crucial part of the Jewish conspiracy.

Some Muslim writers have gone as far as interpreting the Qur'anic narrative vis-à-vis the Israelites and the Jews in light of what the Protocols had claimed. Hostility toward the Zionist project may have blurred the eyes of many Muslims from seeing the difference between the Qur'anic chastisement of bad conduct and ill-manners, which some Israelites and some Jews practised (and which Muslims and Christians have been warned from copying) and the Qur'anic injunction concerning the right of Jews, as well as Christians, to Covenant rights, the violation of which by Muslims is a sin in the eyes of God.

Undoubtedly, the Zionist project bears full responsibility for this shift in the Arab and Muslim perception of Jews and Judaism. After all, it was this Zionist project that embroiled Judaism in its intrigues so as to bestow religious legitimacy on itself and to gain the support of the world's Jewry. The myths of a 'Jewish nation', the 'Land of Promise' and the 'Chosen People of God' were revived in order to convince the Jews, most of whom had initially been opposed to Zionism, to adopt the Zionist solution to the Jewish problem in the West. The ultimate objective had been to persuade the Jews to sponsor the State of Israel, which had been given a theological dimension that transformed it in the Zionized Jewish conscience into 'the end of time Messiah'. The ideology was in the beginning condemned by Jewish religious leaders as an adulteration of Jewish faith that had been predominant until the beginning of the 20th century and which forbade Jewish migration into Palestine with the purpose of settling there permanently. Such an action was viewed by Jewish Orthodoxy as a violation that entailed the forcing of the will of God and that amounted to the sin of apostasy.

Many Arabs and Muslims still do not realize that anti-Zionist Jews, who do not recognize the legitimacy of the State of Israel, do still exist. In spite of the gradual decline in their numbers during the first seven decades of the 20th century, anti-Zionist Jews are now believed to be on the increase. There are indications that the trend of Jewish anti-Zionism is growing. This may, at least partly, be due to the increasing public consciousness of the racist and fascist nature of the State of Israel whose policy and actions contravene the sublime values which people of religion from all faiths respect and seek to protect.

In essence, the Zionist project is a Western colonial enterprise whose success depends on two main factors. The first factor is the determination of a powerful West to see this enterprise continue. The second factor is the weakness of the Arabs and the Muslims who have lost the ability to defend themselves. As for the first factor, so long as the Zionist project serves the purposes of the current World Order, and so long as the economic and military capabilities of this World Order permit it to prolong the life of Israel, no effort will be spared in sustaining Israel. However, no sane person would imagine that this situation would continue forever. The imperialist West is in retreat and its escalating domestic problems will soon preoccupy it and divert its attention from many foreign affairs that have so far been considered strategic interests. The Zionist entity lacks the ability to self-sustain and therefore cannot survive without the U.S. umbilical cord that supplies it with funds and weapons. National Commission On The Causes
And Prevention Of Terrorism

By Atif Harden*

Introduction
Terrorism poses a problem to American policy-makers, first because we must define it in order to zero in on its prevention, and, second, because the best prevention is a cure. It is more effective and cheaper to address causes proactively than to respond reactively to effects.

In the great national effort against terrorism, we should reject two false premises. First we should reject the self-fulfilling prophecy of the "clash of civilizations." Second, we should reject the McCarthy-like tactic, used by some unethical people, of asserting that Islam, Muslims, and American Muslim organizations endorse terrorism.

The American Muslim population and its organizations pose no violent threat to the United States and our way of life. Speaking as an American, and as Executive Director of the American Muslim Council, I am weary of hearing and seeing Islam and Muslims portrayed as foreign and different. We are cousins of the Jews and Christians. We worship the same God, follow the teachings of the same prophets, and believe in the same books. We are all followers of Abraham.

Islam is not new to our homeland. There is strong evidence that Muslims were trading with the Americas before Columbus "discovered" the New World. Many more Muslims came in the holds of slave ships. We literally helped build this nation. Our roots are deep in America, and as immigrants from the Middle East, North Africa, the Sub-Continent, Southeast Asia, and the Balkans arrive, they continue to enrich us.

The Muslim community in America is the model minority community in the United States. We are a value-driven community, which shares a faith that is pro-family, pro-education, anti-racist, pro-business, law abiding, and anti-crime. There are no Arab, Pakistani, Somalian, Senegalese, or Indonesian ghettos in the United States. Muslims living in disadvantaged communities are often the lights of these communities, promoting family, education, cleanliness, chastity self-sufficiency, and zero tolerance toward the drugs and the mental slavery that blight produces. We are one of the best-educated groups in the nation, and have one of the lowest crime rates. Because of our commitment to serve God and our country, Muslim doctors, lawyers, teachers, accountants, and businessmen are serving in inner-city and rural communities, to which no one else in the country is attracted.

Facts
We must get our facts straight before we make policy. Is terrorism a tenet of Islam? No! Are Muslims, or those who claim Islam as their motive, sometimes involved in terrorist acts? Yes. Are Muslims involved in the majority or at least in a significant number of domestic terrorist attacks? According to the FBI, no! Is the killing of innocent men, women, and children supportable by Islamic religious doctrine? No!

The Qur'an says: "Fight in the cause of Allah against those who fight against you, but do not exceed the limits. Allah does not like transgressors" (2:190).

And what are these limits? Some light is shed on them when we read the instructions of the Prophet Muhammad to his followers, and also from the first caliph, Abu Bakr.

The Prophet Muhammad said, "I recommend you to stand in awe of Allah, and be good to the Muslims who are with you. Fight in the name of Allah and in the cause of Allah. Do not betray, do not be excessive, do not kill a child or a woman or an old person, or a person who is secluded in a hermitage. Do not burn a date tree, nor cut the trees, nor demolish buildings" (Sahih Muslim).

The first caliph of Islam, Abu Bakr, said, "Do not betray or be treacherous or vindictive. Do not mutilate. Do not kill the children, the aged, or the women. Do not cut or burn palm trees or fruit trees. Do not slay a sheep, a cow, or a camel except for your food. And you will come across people who have confined themselves to worship in monasteries. Leave them alone to their devotions."

Plea
Given our long history, commitment, and contribution to our nation, we should try in our public policy statements concerning Islam and Muslims to avoid the mistakes of the past. We must resist those who want to blame and demonize an entire community for the crimes of a few. Let us not give credence to the bigotry of those who will say that "Islam is a terrorist religion" or "a11 Muslims are terrorists" or "American Muslim organizations are front groups for terrorists." We already know the danger and stupidity of mass stereotyping. We did not elect a Catholic president until this century, because people feared that a Catholic would be under the control of the Pope. Others believe that we cannot have an Italian president because he would be under the influence of the Mafia. And still others believe that Jews are a fifth column that can not be trusted and whose loyalty will never lie with the United States. Not only have all these fears proven to be unfounded, but it is patently ridiculous to stereotype entire groups in this manner. We let our fears get out of control in the past, and we made a terrible mistake with the Japanese American community by interning them during the Second World War. Our national shame and regret over this incident continues to haunt us. Let us not make this same mistake with Islam and the Muslim community.

Our Prophet taught us that when one of us is oppressed, we are all oppressed. So yes, Muslims feel the pain of our co-religionists and do support the efforts of our brothers and sisters to escape oppression in Bosnia, Kosovo, Kashmir, Palestine, Kenya, Uganda, Russia, India, China, and the Phillipines; just as Irish Americans supported Catholics in Ireland; just as Polish Americans supported Solidarity; just as American Jews support Israel; and just as African-Americans supported the liberation struggles in Africa and, most specifically, Nelson Mandela in South Africa. Muslims do nothing different than any other sub-group in our nation. We may support causes that are not always understood by the public, but we are never m favor of injustice whether committed by non-Muslims or Muslims. And we should not be persecuted for holding "incorrect" political and policy beliefs.

Policy
Under no circumstances, no matter how righteous the cause, can the taking of innocent human life be justified. The political style of early Muslims was one where participants aimed to persuade a wide audience to recognize the spiritual, moral, and political merit of their cause. They were principled about methods and were willing to compromise and to move patiently from one limited end to another.

Political violence is now becoming theatre where the aim of the political act is nothing more than the venting of outrage. The cowardly acts of terrorism can not drown out the ultimate righteousness of a cause. It is important that we realize that the significance or legitimacy of a cause is not diminished because of the actions of a few radicals. Every significant movement in this country has been plagued by radical excess: the Independence Movement, the Anti-Slavery Movement, the Labor Movement, the Civil Rights Movement, the Anti-War Movement, the Environmentalist Movement, the Animal Rights movements, and now even the Anti-Abortion Movement.

Terrorist acts occur when groups or individuals feel ignored, marginalized, and inadequate facing a superior force. People's tactics must be condemned, but their legitimate grievances must still be addressed.

Program
In order for us to fight this problem of terrorism effectively, we must look closely not only at the effects of this horrible problem, but also at its causes. Therefore, I would like to recommend that the President and/or Congress establish a National Commission on the Causes and Prevention of Terrorism. Congress has proposed a committee on counter-terrorism, but this will deal only with the effects of the problem, and will not address its root causes.

We have used this model quite effectively in the past, especially concerning matters related to violence in our society. We have had the Warren Commission and its report, which helped mute much of the agitation the country felt after the Kennedy Assassination. We have also had the Commission on the Causes and Prevention of Violence and the National Advisory Commission on Civil Disorders. In both cases, if we had heard the complaints and grievances earlier of those who testified, we probably would not have seen the subsequent violence.

Imagine how many lives would have been saved if Timothy MacVeigh could have told us what was troubling him and those like him. It is easy for people to condemn him and his like for the awful crimes they have committed, but we must still ask ourselves whether his complaints and grievances could have been addressed through our political system. Granted, some people are plain criminal, but what harm would it do to establish dialogue with political dissidents. Let us talk to the Puerto Rican groups, the Popular Liberation Army, MEK, Mexican Revolutionary Movement, American Front Skinheads, Phineas Priesthood, and radical anti-abortionists. Over the past decades, these are the groups that have been suspected of domestic terrorist acts. We know that in working with gangs not only must they be dealt with harshly if they commit crimes, but that toxic conditions which create their violence must be changed.

Let us reflect on our approach to radical anti-war groups (SDS) and the radical civil rights groups (SNCC) in the 1970's. First, we took the route of demonizing them, but this only played into their hands and empowered them. But then we started to dialogue with them and began to address some of their core concerns, like poverty, homelessness, and the de-escalation of war in Vietnam. Addressing their problems and formulating solutions took away their anger and frustration. Now, in fact, many of them are respected contributing members of the establishment they once abhorred.

We will not be able to solve all of the problems posed by the right and left-wing groups that employ terrorist tactics, but we can dialogue with them and present to them a system that is not intransigent, one-sided, and is perceived at least to be fair. We have outstanding examples of other commissions of this sort. Let us learn from the lessons of the past to build a better future.
