Four years after Oslo: Have the skeptics' views been vindicated?

By Khalid Amayreh

When PLO Chairman Yasser Arafat and the assassinated Israeli Prime Minister Yitshak Rabin signed the Declaration of Principles at the White House Lawn on 14 September 1993, much of the world went into rhapsody over what was described as "a historical breakthrough."

Even some newspapers of record such as the Washington Post and the New York Times were carried away by the false euphoria. The Post described the agreement as "a giant leap of faith for a sacred land," while the Times argued that it represented "a triumph of hope over history."

For his part, Yasser Arafat sought to sell the vague Accords to a skeptic Palestinian public as "the first step towards the establishment of a Palestinian state with Jerusalem as its capital." The Israelis, however, were markedly noncommittal, often arguing that they understood Oslo to mean "limited Palestinian autonomy whereby the ultimate sovereignty remains in Israel's hands."

Now, four years after that famous handshake at the White House, the Oslo Accords seem to be in a moribund state and kept nominally alive only by artificial means. The Israeli occupation of the Palestinians' land and life is now harsher and more tormenting than ever. Palestinian towns and villages in the West Bank and Gaza are effectively reduced to "large and crowded detention camps." A collective "claustrophobia of the soul" is gripping the Palestinians by the throat, as Palestinians are confined to their immediate places of residence.

In short, the life of an average Palestinian has become an enduring hell, so much so that many people, and their number is steadily increasing, are now almost nostalgic for the days of direct Israeli occupation. The dreams of freedom have evaporated, the euphoria gone, and violence is imposing itself again on the region as the ultimate arbiter.

Ironically, many Palestinians did laud the DOP initially because they thought it would eventually deliver them from the shackles of Israeli oppression and domination. However, with the passage of time, and seeing how the agreement was being implemented on the ground, a collective feeling of being cheated and deceived began to replace whatever enthusiasm the Palestinians ever had for the agreement. The Israeli grip on Palestinian land and life has simply been made tighter, the hope for freedom and liberation collapsed, the dream turned into a nightmare.

Four years ago, opposition to the Oslo Agreement was nearly specific to the intellectual sector and the more politicized segments of society, in addition to the Islamic movement. Now, however, the "rejectionists" have been joined by many ordinary citizens who have come to feel "betrayed and cheated" by a "peace" process that served to lower the ceiling of Palestinian aspirations and expectations.

"This peace is killing us," complained a jobless Hebron man who used to make a living selling second-hand items in the streets of East Jerusalem. "They (the Israelis) got all they want, and we got virtually nothing...getting a work permit or a VIP card for a Palestinian Authority official has become our most ambitious goal," he added with frustration and bitterness clearly noticed in the tone of his voice.

Dr. Aziz Hayder, Director of the Al-Quds University Regional Studies Centre, opined that the depressive mood stems mainly from the collapse of the high hopes and dreams which the PLO sought to build in the hearts and minds of the people as part of its efforts to sell the peace initiative to the Palestinian Main Street.

Hayder argues that not only the dreams have not come true, but the Palestinians have actually been exposed to a "new and more sinister form of occupation."

"In the eyes of the world, the Palestinians are now at least partially free; they have a president, a legislative council, an army and a state in the making. However, this is all pie in the sky since Israel continues to control the oxygen we breathe and the water we drink...even Yasser Arafat can't leave the Gaza Strip without a permit from Israel," said Abdul-Jawwad Maraka, an outspoken critic of the Oslo Accords

Maraka argued that through Oslo, Israel has been able to rid itself of the prospect of a bilateral state, while at the same time maintaining its grip on the Palestinians, more effectively and more efficiently than ever before.

Indeed, the Israelis (Labour and Likud) did everything to weaken Palestinians' faith in Oslo. The building of Jewish settlements on the West Bank has increased by 30% since Oslo, with tens of thousands of hectares of Arab land confiscated for settlement expansion. Moreover, as many as 530 Arab homes have been destroyed for a variety of concocted reasons. Around 2,000 more homes are slated to be demolished, mainly for the purpose of checking demographic Arab growth in East Jerusalem.

Furthermore, the Arab-Islamic identity of East Jerusalem has nearly been obliterated as Israeli occupation authorities stepped up the sinister policy of ethnic cleansing against Palestinians which usually manifests itself in two ways: withdrawal of residency rights from East-Jerusalemite Arabs and denying them building licenses.

In the meantime, the occupation authorities continue to deny both Muslims and Christians access to their respective holy places in East Jerusalem. This is happening at a time when Jews and Israelis can travel almost anywhere in the Arab world without any restrictions.

In the West Bank itself, Israel (thanks to Oslo) has effectively retained most, if not all, of the assets while throwing all the liabilities to Yasser Arafat. Thus, for example, when Palestinian towns are denied drinking water supplies for prolonged periods, as is often the case since the Israelis control the tap, the blame is pinned on Arafat. Indeed, Palestinians have complained on many occasions to the Chairman that "we are thirsty, Mr. President, and our children go to school in the morning without getting their hands and faces washed." But the president would only shrug in their faces, telling them rather disagreeably that "I have more paramount issues to deal with...go to Sa'eb Ereikat (minister of local governance)". But Ereikat has no answer, since the Oslo Accords allows Israel to have absolute control of water sources with predictable ramifications to Palestinians.

But Palestinian indignation is over much more than just water. The West Bank, the supposed ear-marked home for the would-be Palestinian state, has been replaced by a series of scattered Bantustans and an Israeli-dominated Jerusalem. Any visitor to the West Bank would see for himself how Palestinian population centres have become similar to isolated islands in the midst of a sea of Jewish settlements.

The Palestinian Authority, which was initially welcomed on the premise that it would accelerate Palestinian emancipation from Jewish bondage, has been a big disappointment, even a fiasco. The PA has also turned out to be a manifestly corrupt autocracy run by one man, Yasser Arafat, who holds all the reins, controls all the money and takes all the decisions.

In addition, the PA often performs at Israel's beck and call, in effect doing the Israeli army's dirty work in the West Bank. However, when and if the PA Chairman hesitates to heed Israeli dictates and demands, Netanyahu accuses him of "dragging his feet in fighting terror"; yet when Arafat does the dirty work, e.g. carrying out massive arrests among the opponents of the Israeli occupation, the Chairman succeeds only in creating the image of "collaborator" for himself, while getting virtually nothing from Israel. Needless to say, continuous Israeli bullying, coupled with Arafat's inherent powerlessness, has resulted in the Chairman becoming a vanquished supplicant for Palestinian rights instead of the "equal partner" he had once thought Oslo would make of him.

In fact, the overall outlook seems so bleak for Palestinian rights that some Palestinian intellectuals have come to consider the Oslo agreement as an Israeli-American conspiracy aimed at liquidating or completing the liquidation of the Palestinian issue. Professor Naseer Aruri, a Palestinian intellectual from Ramallah, contended in a recent article in the London-based bimonthly journal Middle East International that the Oslo Accords left "four million Palestinian refugees on the back burner; the would-be catalyst for Arab unity has become the worst example of segmentation...[and] the oppressed have become the oppressor, the revolutionary collaborator and the resister a quisling."

The recent conflagration of violence in Jerusalem is a living testimony to the failure of Oslo. Obviously, this means that Oslo has achieved neither justice for the Palestinians nor security for Israel. Worse still, time is running out and may have already run out for a peaceful settlement between Israelis and Palestinians.

Oslo was a brat as deformed as the oblique balance of power that brought it into existence. Hence, it was a bad start, and a bad start usually makes a bad end. In the current Palestinian context, this seems to need no further proof.

Locked Up Without Trial

By Allegra Pacheco

Abed al-Rahman al Ahmar, aged 30, has another six months in "administrative detention." A second generation Palestinian refugee from Deheishe camp near Bethlehem, al Ahmar is still a university student. Israel's continual use of administrative detention and interrogations have prolonged his studies, as they have for many other Palestinian political activists.

Administrative detention means imprisonment by military order without charges or trial. International law permits an occupier to use this measure for brief periods only, provided there are imperative reasons of security.

Consecutive Israeli governments have abused the measure to imprison, repeatedly and indefinitely, the members of the Palestinian opposition (i.e. those who oppose the Oslo accords as the means to achieve a just peace.) Israel resorts to this measure when it has no evidence or legally-enforceable charges against these people.

Bethlehem was still under occupation in November 1995 when the Israeli army arrested al Ahmar as he was returning home from the city's university. Since then the Israeli occupation army has redeployed from the town, the Palestinian Authority (PA) has stepped in, and the occupation of the city has ended--at least nominally. Under Article 77 of the Fourth Geneva Convention, the occupying force must release the detainees to the authorities of the liberated territories.

Yet on July 28 this year, Israel's General Security Services (GSS or the Shin Bet) issued al Ahmar his fourth consecutive administrative detention order, this time until February 1998. At the end of this six month period, al Ahmar will have spent two years and three months languishing in Ha-Sharon prison, Central Israel, without a charge or trial. The Shin Bet will then probably extend al Ahmar's detention for a fifth time.

Al Ahmar is not alone. More than half the 800 Palestinian administrative detainees are serving their second, third, fourth and even ninth extensions. Ahmed Qatamish, a writer, has been in detention for four-and-half years; his brother Ribhi, a lawyer, for more than three years. The list goes on.

B'Tselem (Israeli Information Centre for Human Rights in the Occupied Territories) concluded that, "the pervasive use of detention extensions raises the possibility of administrative detention for life. Detainees have spent up to four years in administrative detention without knowing why or when they will be released. This uncertainty has devastating effects on the detainees and on their families. There is no indication that the authorities intend to release any of the these long term detainees.

Until today no one has been able to force the Shin Bet to stop extending detentions. Appeals from public figures, scattered Knesset members, reports by "Amnesty International" and B'Tselem have not produced any results.

The Israeli High Court has had many opportunities to intervene, but it always refrains from challenging the Shin Bet's activities. Time after time the court takes refuge in legalisms to avoid judicial review. The Shin Bet's prolonged detentions violate fundamental principles of international and Israeli law, namely, prohibition against arbitrary detention, the right to a fair trial and the maxim of "innocent until proved guilty." Yet the Court continues to uphold long-term administrative detention out of deference to the Shin Net's classified "security" considerations.

Before he received his latest extension, al Ahmar tried a different approach.He petitioned the High Court before the Shin Bet issued its order instead of after. His petition requested that the court order the security apparatus to explain why it intended to extend his detention for the fourth time instead of either releasing him or bringing him to trial. AL Ahmar argued that because the Shin Bet had violated his liberties and freedom so severely, he had the right to demand that it justify its proposed extension. He could have appealed against the extension after the Shin Bet issued the order, but this would have been too late. The court would have heard the appeal not less than a month after the order was issued, defeating al Ahmar's aim of preventing even a day of unjustified imprisonment.

Moreover, al Ahmar argued that for anyone accused under Israeli law, a judicial review is required before the police can extend the period of interrogation. It is also required if the police wish to hold the accused in prison until the end of the legal proceedings.

Finally, al Ahmar pointed out that the Shin Bet had interrogated him during his detention and had still not brought charges against him. This indicated , he said, that it intended to keep him in prison indefinitely. It was up to the High Court, therefore, to examine the extension intensively before it went into effect in order to prevent a breach of justice and Israeli law.

The Israeli High Court, however, ignored all of Al Ahmar's arguments. Justices ruled that the court could not intervene in this case, although they acknowledged that his 21 months in detention were appalling by all other standards.

In his opinion, justice Barak wrote "The order concerning administrative detention set up an (appeals) procedure by which to act, and this procedure cannot be bypassed." In particular, Barak referred the petitioner to the Military Appeals committees. But al Ahmar had already argued that these were not impartial forums, and detainees had been boycotting them for more than a year. Leaning on a technicality, Barak therefore chose to defer to the unjust military order rather than change it.

By refusing to make the Shin Bet respond to al Ahmar's claims, justice Barak effectively save it from having to open its actions to outside scrutiny. Thus the High Court once again reneged on its obligation to review without prejudice the questionable actions of the secret security service, reinforcing its conviction that it can continue imposing "administrative detention for life" unhindered by judicial intervention.

The ruling undermined the court's legitimacy as a fair and impartial forum for those seeking redress against the government's actions. The High Court failed to live up to its self avowed dictum of "clear expression in the laws emanating from this court" and of "democratic values and principles of freedom, liberty and equality." Justice Barak's decision was a pointed reminder that High Court is not an independent judicial body when it comes to Palestinian human rights, but just another member of the Israeli security apparatus.

Time passes. Life goes on for all of us. But for al Ahmar and the 800 administrative detainees like him, life remains suspended indefinitely in the crowded, miserable conditions of the Ha-Sharon, Tel Mond, Damoun and Megiddo prisons. For some this means another year of university studies lost; for others, another year away from their children and loved ones. The state of Israel and its "liberal" High Court of Justice remain conspirators in this flagrant assault on freedom.

Israeli 'Deal of Thieves' to Settle in Ras al Amud

By Steve Sozby

The takeover of two Arab homes in the Ras Al-Amud neighborhood of East Jerusalem by 11 Israelis settlers on September 14 was hardly surprising to most Middle East observers. Five days before the July 30 suicide bombing in West Jerusalem, the Jerusalem licensing committee approved a plan for building a 32-unit housing complex for Jewish settlers in the centre of the 12,000 strong Palestinian neighborhood. The long-delayed approval was given to Miami real-estate tycoon and supporter of far-right Zionist groups and policies, Irving Moscowitz, who reportedly purchased the land in 1990 and has sought to build a settlement there as a political statement on Jewish sovereignty over all of Jerusalem. This is the same Moscowitz whose pressure to open the tunnel near Al-Aqsa Mosque in September 1996 sparked a mini-war in the territories that left nearly one hundred dead and over a thousand Palestinian civilians injured.

The Israeli Jerusalem municipality agreed in late July on a plan submitted by far-right Moledet party activist Benny Elon which called for the building of apartments in the Arab section of East Jerusalem between the village of Silwan and the Mt. of Olives. Previous efforts to push through this plan, in a somewhat different form, in the early 1990s were linked to building Arab housing as well in the area to help ease the severe housing crunch. In 1992, Interior Minister Eli Suissa, who was also head of the District Planning Commission, linked the building of Jewish housing in Ras Al-Amud to the building of 560 Arab housing units there as well. This plan was held up for years by Yitzak Rabin, when he was acting Interior Minister, and former interior ministers Haim Ramon and Uzi Baram. In his last city council meeting in 1993, Teddy Kollek announced that the plan was approved against his objections, which were contrary to his stated policy of not building Jewish housing in heavily populated Arab areas.

The new plan that was approved at the end of July initially caused somewhat of a political uproar in Israel. The Labor Party accused Netanyahu of pretending that he did not know of the Jerusalem Municipality's approval of Moscowitz's plans to build in Ras Al-Amud. "Netanyahu is playing a double game. He know about the building permits and is denying it. He preferred to let the report be published so that he could pretend to be against it," said Dalia Itzik, an Labor MK. Itzik said that the Primer Minister was again "raising the Jerusalem issue to the top of the international agenda, causing the Palestinians to react sharply against us and making the Palestinian look much better than we do - not to mention that his move endangers the unity of Jerusalem."

Jerusalem is, of course, anything but united. Even before the Likud took power in May, 1996 Israeli behavior in Jerusalem has brought the territories to the brink of an explosion. The confiscation of hundreds of Arab Jerusalem I.D.s, the demolition of "unlicensed" homes, continued construction of Jebal Abu Ghuneim, the expulsion of Arab Beduins for the expansion of the Ma'aleh Adumin settlement and the strict collective closure resulting from the suicide bombings have increased tension in the holy city. Ras Al-Amud may become the spark that again bring Israeli and Palestinian confrontations to a violent impasse.

It is not important anymore whether Netanyahu was playing a game with the proposed settlement of Ras Al-Amud. The fact that the settlers moved into the disputed Arab land while he is the Prime Minister is what matters. On September 14, Israeli settlers, with heavy police protection, took over two Arab homes in the area. Netanyahu has claimed that he is not responsible for the settlers taking such a provocative step at this time, but it is clear he is not so concerned about the consequences that he is willing to forcibly remove the settlers, especially without U.S. pressure.

The Clinton administration, as usual, is not willing to take the diplomatic steps necessary to make the Israelis behave themselves. Instead of reacting to the taking of homes and the proposed effort to create yet another Jewish settlement on Arab land captured in 1967, the U.S. sought to mediate a compromise that would get the "peace process" back on track. This compromise was in the form of replacing the 11 settlers with ten Jewish seminary students, who are armed and heavily protected by Israeli soldiers. "Our main concern that the nature of this neighborhood would not change over time appears to have been met and we have received assurances (from the Israeli government) that this will not be changed," said State Department spokesman James Rubin. He then went on to call for the PA to return to the negotiating table with the Israelis and to better fight "terrorism" in the region.

There should be no question in anyone's mind to the purpose and objectives of the settlers at Ras Al-Amud. One of the leaders, Baruch Marzel, a leader in Kach party, said that the entry of the Jews into the area meant "the neighborhood has begun. We don't need a permit to build if we already have a house." He then described the struggle in East Jerusalem to be one of sovereignty and to ensure that any future negotiations over the area would be undermined by the presence of Jews in Arab areas.

It has been 18 months since Netanyahu won a surprising victory in Israel. The effort to find a common ground between Israelis and Palestinians in the holy land has been rolled back during this time by policies of a Likud party that is more interested in having land than in having peace. As a result, there are now more and more Palestinian youths willing to sacrifice themselves in suicide attacks because they feel that there is no prospect for achieving their rights at the negotiating table. Considering the current state of diplomacy in the Middle East, who can argue with them?

Al-Nabi Rubin Before 1948

The village was located on the south bank of the Rubin River, 3 km away from the Mediterranean. For Palestinians al-Nabi Rubin had great significance because it was the site of a shrine for al-Nabi (the Prophet) Rubin, who was honored with an annual mawsim (pilgrimage season), during which Islamic and popular celebrations were held. In the Judaic tradition Rubin (or Reuben) was the firstborn son of Jacob and Leah and was known for his scrupulous honesty, in contrast with his half-brother, Esau; while Esau looted and plundered the property of others, Reuben would not even collect plants from a field unless he knew they were ownerless (Genesis 30:14). It was not uncommon for Palestinian Muslims to honor such Old Testament prophets by building shrines for them; there was a shrine for the prophet Moses, for example, south of Jericho, not far from the region in which he is said to have been buried. It is thought that the shrine for al-Nabi Rubin was built in the same place where a Canaanite temple once had stood, and that the mawsim itself was pagan in origin.

The mawsim lasted from July through September and was one of two prominent mawsims for Old Testament prophets in Palestine-the other being dedicated to al-Nabi Musa (the prophet Moses). People flocked to the shrine from Jaffa, Lydda, al-Ramla, and the region's villages. They sang religious as well as secular/popular songs; danced the dabka; held dhikr sessions (Islamic devotional exercises led by adherents of Sufi mysticism); watched horse races and magic shows; and listened to preachers or zajjals (popular poets). [Sirhan 1989:565-66] The participants camped in tents and were served refreshments at makeshift cafes and restaurants. They shopped for wares at booths that entrepreneurs set up for the occasion .

The village itself was inhabited by members of the Abu Sawayrih tribe, descendants of the al-Maliha Bedouin who had lived in the Sinai. The village's land area, most of which was covered by sand dunes, was the second largest in the district after that of Yibna, and was designated as an Islamic waqf (pious endowment). Some of the houses, which were scattered across the site without any discernible nucleus, were also built inside the fruit orchards. Shops as well as a movie theater were built in the neighborhood of the shrine. Water was provided by wells and springs. The population of al-Nabi Rubin was predominantly Muslim. An elementary school for boys was established in the village in 1946, with an enrollment of fifty-six students in the mid-1940s.

The villagers worked in agriculture and animal husbandry; they also catered to the visitors during the mawsim. They cultivated mainly grain, followed by citrus and other fruits, such as figs and grapes. In 1945 a total of 683 dunums

was devoted to citrus and bananas and 4,357 dunums were allocated to cereals; 184 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards. Eucalyptus and chinaberry trees grew along the river bank and covered large portions of village land. On the far southwest edge of the land lay Manarat Rubin ("The Lighthouse of Reuben"), which was built over the ruins of the old port of Yibna (the lamnitarum Portus of Roman times). Recent excavations there have located a squarish, beaten-earth rampart measuring 800 m on a side that dates to the second millennium B.C.

Occupation and Depopulation

Circumstantial evidence indicates that the village was occupied during the second stage of the Israeli army's Operation Barak (see al-Batani al-Gharbi, Gaza District). Israeli historian Benny Morris reports that the villagers were expelled on 1 June 1948, three days before the villagers of nearby Yibna were forced to leave their homes. The expulsions were in keeping with the practice of the Giv'ati Brigade under its commander Shim'on Avidan and in accordance with the Haganah's Plan Dalet.[M:xvii; see M:127]

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

In 1949, Zionists established Kibbutz Palmachim (122149) on the coastal lands of the village, south of the mouth of the Rubin River. They also established the settlement of Gan Soreq (127150) in 1950, 3.5 km west of the shrine, on village land.

The Village Today

The shrine of al-Nabi Rubin stands amid shrubs and other wild vegetation. A minaret that has three lancet-arched entrances stands at one end of it (see photo). A number of minor shrines built of large stones also remain. Near the shrine is a deserted, free-standing cement structure that consists of a single, box-shaped room.
