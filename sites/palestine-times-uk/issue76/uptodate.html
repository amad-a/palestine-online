Five Israeli Soldiers try Sadistic urges on a Palestinian worker

 

Occupied Jerusalem-PT(18 October):

Five Israeli soldiers yesterday attacked and nearly murdered a Palestinian laborer near an Israeli army roadblock in Jerusalem.

The worker, Ahmed Shaker Shakarneh, from the village of Nahaline near Bethlehem, said he regained consciousness at the Jerusalem's Hadasa hospital after sustaining serious head wounds resulting from what he called "sadistic torture" at the hands of five Israeli soldiers.

On Thursday 16 October, a Beitshemesh police officer entrusted the five border policemen to take Shakarneh to the closest Israeli army roadblock along the green line.

Instead, however, the five soldiers took the Palestinian laborer, who had been collecting medical herb from the mountainous terrain nearby, to a remote and uninhabited spot where they "started a party of beating and of torturing."

"They started beating with their fists and kicking me with their army boots until I nearly lost consciousness. Then they started hurling stones at my head from a short distance, causing a big bleeding," Shakarneh said.

"Then one of them opened up my mouth and put lots of dirt in it, while the other four were laughing hysterically."

Shakarneh said he then was able to walk to the main street, two hundred meters away, where he found an Arab driver drove him to hospital.

Palestinian sources stress that acts of savagery by Israeli soldiers against Palestinian laborers are daily happenings and that most of the time, they pass unnoticed by the world because they happen away from the media.

Hamas accuses Rabbis of misleading Ulama

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (15 October)

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) has accused Israel's High Rabbinate of misleading Muslim Ulama (religious scholars) by urging them to issue fatwas or religious edicts against Palestinian Islamists' human-bomb attacks on Israeli targets.

"The rabbis are being morally selective, they want our Ulama to condemn violence against Israelis without committing themselves to do likewise vis-a-vis Israeli-Jewish violence and terrorism against our civilians," said Hamas Gaza leader, Dr.Abdulaziz al Rantisi.

On Tuesday, Hamas mentor and spiritual leader, Sheikh Ahmed Yassin received Rabbi Menhame Froman of the settlement of Tekoa near Bethlehem who reportedly sought to hand him a letter from Chief Sephardi Rabbi Bakshi-Doron asking Yassin to denounce violence.

Yassin refused to accept the letter, saying its proper address is Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat.

The letter read "we have to do whatever we can to stop violence and the only way to do that is through dialogue. I am hopeful that Sheikh Yassin can solve this problem. I call on him as a believer in God."

The letter, however, made no mention of the Israeli occupation of Arab-Muslim land and of the daily torment meted out to the Palestinians by the Israeli army and settlers.

Yassin told Froman Hamas would agree to a cease-fire if Israel agreed to pull out of the occupied territories.

Rantisi, considered a hard-liner within Hamas, said he was disquieted by "the moral inconsistency of Jewish religious leaders."

"We never hear them condemn the barbaric acts of the Israeli army in the West Bank. Their silence is very frustrating and doesn't auger well for future contacts between them and us," he said.

Rantisi added that it would be very unlikely that Rabbi Bakshi-Doron or other rabbis would succeed in obtaining fatwas from the Sheikh of al Azhar repudiating Palestinian resistance against oppression and occupation.

"Jihad against oppression and injustice is well-established in the Qura'an, an nobody can undo this, and any fatwa to the contrary would plunge its issuer into hell-fire and would be utterly unacceptable to Muslims," he added.

Bakshi-Doron announced on Tuesday that the Sheikh of al Azhar, Sheikh Sayed Tantawi, had sent him a letter denouncing "religiously-motivated terror."

However, a spokesman for Tantawi was reported as saying on Wednesday that "the Sheikh's remarks were distorted and taken out of context."

Moreover, the spokesman said the Israeli ambassador to Egypt, Zvi Mazal, omitted "all remarks (made by Tantawi) pertaining to the Israeli occupation and its causative relation to all forms of Palestinian resistance."

Zionists destroy more Palestinian homes

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (14 October)

In a flagrant measure that flies in the face of the Palestinian Authority and the international community, the Israeli army destroyed two more Palestinian homes at the village of Beit Ummar near Hebron.

The two houses, belonging to Mahmud Abu Dayya and his son Khaled, were destroyed by the Israeli army at noon Tuesday at the pretext that they were built without proper licensing.

Palestinian eyewitnesses said the Israeli army declared the vicinity where the two homes are located a closed military zone before bulldozers began to pulverize them in the full view of crying women and children who have been made homeless thanks to the callous Israeli act.

Mahmud abu Dayya, distraught by the demolition of his home which sheltered his family of 9 children, said the Israelis "were behaving no different than the Nazis...only difference is the name," he said.

Asked why he had not obtained a building license, abu Dayya said "I tried to obtain a license a hundred times and every time I went to the Building and Zoning office I was told I couldn't get a license."

Palestinian sources at Beit Ummar also revealed that the Israeli army is planning to destroy the homes of as many as thirty Palestinian families for alleged licensing violations.

Palestinians and human rights activists contend that the Israeli policy of home-demolition is considered by far the most callous and evil manifestations of the Israeli occupation of Arab land.

Last week, Hamas mentor and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin cited "home-demolition" as one of the chief factors behind Palestinian Islamists' martyrdom operations against Jewish targets.

Yassin said Hamas would reconsider attacks on civilian Jewish targets if and when Israel desisted from assaulting Palestinian civilian targets, including home demolition.

Interestingly, US Secretary of State Madeleine Albright urged, though half-heartedly, Israel to "stop unilateral acts" a reference to, inter alia, demolishing Arab homes in the West Bank.

Incidentally, Palestinian and Israeli negotiators were due to meet Tuesday evening to discuss "confidence-building measures" between the two sides, including the subject of home demolitions.

Netanyahu refuses to discuss settlements with Arafat

Occupied Jerusalem- PT ( October)

Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu and PA Chairman Yasser Arafat met at the Beit-Hanun crossing point before dawn Wednesday in a renewed effort to invigorate the erstwhile stalled peace process on the Palestinian-Israeli track.

However, a reliable Palestinian source close to PA chairman Yasser Arafat intimated that Netanyahu refused to commit his government to any freeze on Jewish settlement building in the West Bank.

There was no prior word of the American-brokered meeting, reportedly at the insistence of U.S. Envoy Dennis Ross who hoped that by keeping the press away, Arafat and Netanyahu would be better able to overcome their differences.

Following the meeting, the first in eight months, Ross said the two leaders agreed to hold regular meetings to advance what he called "the peace making efforts."

However, the Palestinian source mentioned above described Ross optimistic remarks as "what he would like to see happen rather than what is happening."

"The meeting took place only to satisfy American demands. It took place without any adequate preparation...It seems the Americans think that by just talking, problems will be resolved," said the source which spoke on condition of anonymity.

Israeli sources pointed out that the bulk of the meeting was devoted to the "terrorism issue" with Netanyahu pressing Arafat "to do much more in the fight against terrorism."

Last the PA closed down sixteen charitable, social and educational institutions associated with Hamas in Gaza.

The measure gained American and Israeli approval and satisfaction but invited strong indignant reaction from Palestinian masses and the Legislative Council.

Observers here surmise that Netanyahu agreed to meet with Arafat purely for public relations considerations, given the brewing crisis stemming from the 25 September Mossad bungled operation to murder Hamas political bureau chief Khaled Masha'al in Amman.

"He (Netanyahu) just wants to deflect attention from the series of scandalous blunders he has made," said Labor Party Leader Ehud Barak, commenting on the meeting at Erez.

For its part, the Palestinian Authority described the meeting as "very important."

PA spokesperson, Nabil Abu Rudeineh, said the meeting will have proven very important (if) Netanyahu is sincere in implementing the agreements and (if) he stops unilateral measures, an obvious allusion to the construction of Jewish settlements in the Occupied Territories.

The key word in Abu Rudeineh statement is the "if," given the fact that talks between the two sides stopped abruptly in March after Israel started to build a new settlement at Jabal Abu Ghuneim on the outskirts of East Jerusalem.

Indeed, many pundits here argue that Arafat has made a significant concession to Netanyahu by agreeing on the resumption of talks at the joint-committee level and by agreeing to meet with Netanyahu while settlement-building is continuing unabatedly.

According to Israel TV, Arafat had demanded that Netanyahu agree to at least temporarily stop construction of Jewish colonies in the West Bank before the meeting was determined, but Netanyahu refused.

Meanwhile, Hamas leaders ended a brief honey-moon with Arafat in the aftermath of Hamas founder Sheikh Ahmed Yasin's release from Israeli detention and return to Gaza.

Dr. Abdul Aziz al Rantisi, a senior Hamas leader in Gaza, said the meeting at Erez was "futile just like previous encounters."

"There is no reason to believe that it will be any different." al Rantisi told Journalists in Gaza on Wednesday.

YASIN'S TRIUMPHANT RETURN TO GAZA

Gaza- PT (6 October)

Hamas founder and spiritual mentor, Sheikh Ahmed Yasin, received a hero welcome upon his arrival in Gaza city on Monday afternoon, 6 September, with thousands of supporters and well-wishers greeting the 61-year-old wheel-chair bound Islamist leader with "ahalan wasahlan" and "God is great."

Yasin was first accorded an official welcome at Palestinian Authority headquarters on the Gaza shore. Greeting him were Yasser Arafat's personal secretary al Tayyeb Abdul Rahim, Suha Arafat( standing in for her husband who had earlier visited the Sheikh in hospital in Amman), and top PA political and security officials along with high-ranking Hamas leaders in the Strip. Islamist leaders from the West Bank were not present due to the Israeli closure on Palestinian territories.

After the brief official reception, Yasin was taken to the al Yarmuk stadium where over 30,000 thousand people were awaiting his arrival since the morning. There, decorated flags and banners greeted Yasin as "our great leader." One huge green banner read "In the name of Hamas we welcome the Sheikh of the intifada."

Yasin addressed the nearly jubilant crowd, appealing for national unity and urging Palestinians to be strong and united.

"There is no difference between this and that or big or small. we are all one people united in the face of occupation and oppression," he said in an unflinching tone.

Yasin told the cheering multitude that "the future belongs to us because our cause is just and our people is alive and well and will continue to be alive and well, God willing."

Yasin sounded as defiant as ever when he said that "we must continue to defy our oppressor and foil their efforts to disintegrate us into shreds."

Earlier, Yasin, who is physically paralyzed but mentally alert, said Hamas was not contemplating a cease-fire or truce with Israel as long "the Israeli soldiers' boots are pressed against our necks and chests."

"Resistance," he said, " is a natural human right, I'll fight even my own brother if he takes over my home and arrogates my rights."

Despite his conciliatory tone toward the Palestinian Authority and its chairman Yasser Arafat, Yasin never the less strongly criticized the closure by the PA in Gaza last month of sixteen social, educational, and charitable institutions associated with the Islamist movement.

He said "I call on the authority not to succumb to American and Israeli pressure, and to desist from incarcerating our sons because this is not in our interests; it is inadmissible to close civil institutions just because the Americans and the Israelis demand that to be done."

Yasin was flown directly from Amman aboard a Jordanian Air Force helicopter following the conclusion of a hastily-made deal between Jordan King Hussein and Israel whereby Israel freed Yasin and scores of other Palestinian prisoners in return for releasing from Jordanian custody of the two Mossad agents who on 25 September attempted to assassinate in Amman the head of Hamas political bureau Khaled Masha'al.

The release of Sheikh Ahmed Yasin and twenty two other Palestinians from Israeli detention was undoubtedly a significant morale-booster for most Palestinians who had of late given to mounting frustration, given Benyamin Netanyahu's determined efforts to impose the fait accompli in the West Bank and American and international powerlessness to stop Israeli insolence. The freedom of Yasin and scores of other Palestinian detainees already released and slated to be released soon was after all earned almost at no cost for the Palestinians. "It was a gift of God," said Hamas leader Mahmud al Zahar.

Moreover, many ordinary Palestinians feel Yasin's words breathe inspiration and encouragement into the hearts and minds of thousands of Palestinians who otherwise have accustomed to seeing Yasser Arafat repeatedly give in to Israeli dictates and blackmail.

"Here we have a leader who sticks with his principles regardless of the balance of power...only a real man like Yasin can make a real peace with Israel," said a Hebron Islamist doctor upon seeing Yasin's helicopter land in Gaza.

Notwithstanding, the Palestinians' joyful reaction to Yasin's freedom and homecoming was fraught with some consternation at King Hussein for concluding a deal with Israel that enabled the Jewish state to smoothly retrieve "our big hunt," the two Mossad agents who had make the attempt on Khaled Masha'al.

Islamist sources argued that had the King insisted, he could have secured the release of hundreds of Palestinian detainees from Israeli jails in return for the "two Canadians."

They pointed out that many of the 22 prisoners released as a result of the deal were actually Palestinian laborers who were jailed for entering Israeli without valid entry permits.

Non the less, it doesn't' seem that the dismay is deep enough to overshadow the widespread satisfaction at Yasin's release and Khaled Masha'al's safety.

"We knew from the very inception that the presence of two Mossad agents in King's Hussein custody is not exactly like them being in the hands of Hamas...we didn't expect too much form Jordan, anyway," said a Hamas leader in Ramallah as news of the swap spread.

There is no doubt that the release of Sheikh Yasin will strengthen Hamas, even in real terms. Observers here believe that the steadfastness the Sheikh had put up (he refused all along to pledge to renounce 'terrorism' in return for his release), his dignified and high-profile release from Israeli detention, and the Mossad failed attempt on Khaled Masha'al in Amman, will all make the movement more appealing to thousands of erstwhile disenchanted Palestinian and Arab youths who are dead tired of official Arab impotence vis-a-vis Israel.

Moreover, Hamas, thanks to the latest developments, has been able to gain renewed and badly-needed legitimacy in the region at large.

"From now on, the Hamas factor can't be ignored," said Hamas Gaza leader Dr. Abdul Aziz Rantisi, now considered the most senior Islamist spokesman in the occupied territories after Yasin himself. "Sheikh Ahmed Yasin is now viewed as second among equals vis-a-vis Yasser Arafat," he asserted, rather satisfactorily.

But being "second among equals" carries with it not only promises of constructive cooperation but also undesirable prospects of friction and discord, especially in an immature political environment where the leader, e.g. Arafat, is not really held to serious political accountability.

A free Yasin can therefore be either a helping hand for Arafat or a real thorn in his side, depending on how the PA chairman deals with Benyamin Netanyahu's policy of "take or leave it" towards the Palestinians.

Israel admits responsibility for bungling attempt on Misha'al's life

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (5 October)

In the first official reaction to the foiled assassination attempt on Hamas leader Khaled Misha'al in Amman on 25 September, Israel has tacitly admitted responsibility for the terrorist act.

On Sunday, the Secretary of the Israeli government, Dany Naveh, said in a brief statement that "for the time-being the Israeli government will not comment on the Misha'al affair."

Naveh claimed Misha'al was "a central figure in Hamas and is responsible for the death of many innocent Israelis," suggesting that it was perfectly legitimate to seek to murder him.

Moreover, Naveh, considered the most hawkish and jingoistic of the Netanyahu's government, said it was imperative that the Israeli government protect its citizens against "the terrorists."

However, when asked by a journalist whether Israel bears official responsibility for the failed attack on Misha'al, Naveh said "no comment."

Meanwhile, the Israeli Hebrew daily, the Yedeot Ahranot, confirmed a Sunday Times' report that Netanyahu ordered the assassination of Khaled Misha'al despite fierce opposition from Mossad head Dany Yatom.

The usually reliable Hebrew daily quoted Israeli officials close to Netanyahu as saying that "the prime minister wanted to present the Israeli people with a gift on the eve of Rosh Hashana (the Hebrew new year which occurred on 2 October)"

"I wanted Khaled Misha'al dead, that is an order," Netanyahu reportedly said to Yatom who opposed the operation.

Another Israeli paper , the elitist Ha'aretz, described the Misha'al affair as the most "serious failure ever of the Mossad."

The paper's military commentator, Zeiv Sheff, wrote in the paper Sunday edition that "this operation is by large the biggest failure in Mossad's history."

Sheff pointed out that the Amman debacle caused incalculable damage to Israel's relations with Jordan and Canada, undermined Israel's "fight against terrorism," and dealt a serious blow to the reputation of the Mossad.

In the meanwhile, Israeli sources quoted Hamas officials in the West Bank as saying that the Islamist movement was demanding the release of scores of Hamas activists and guerrilla leaders in Israeli detention.

According to the sources, Hamas is demanding the release of Hasan Salameh, the Islamist guerrilla leader whom Israel sentenced earlier this year to 1170 years for his role in planning attacks on Israeli targets.

There has been no confirmation of this by Hamas.

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (1 October)

In a surprising development, the Israeli occupation authorities released from jail after midnight Tuesday Hamas founder and spiritual leader, Sheikh Ahmed Yasin.

Sheikh Yasin's release from the maximum-security Ramle prison took place at about 1:10 a.m. as a Jordanian helicopter with full medical gear dispatched by Jordan's King Hussein was awaiting the wheel-chair bound 65-year-old paralyzed Sheikh.

Yasin arrived at the King Hussein Medical Hospital around 1:30 a.m. where he was reportedly received by the Jordanian monarch, Prime Minister Majali and a number of Jordanian officials.

According to semi-official Israeli, Palestinian and Jordanian sources, the release of Yasin has culminated secret trilateral contacts and negotiations involving Jordan, Israel and the American administration.

The contacts, described as intensive, reportedly ensued the apprehension last week of the two Mossad agents who attempted to assassinate in Amman Hamas leader Khalid Misha'al, which reportedly caused great embarrassment for the Jordanian government, King Hussein personally, and the Israeli government which singed a peace treaty with Jordan three years ago.

The sources disclosed that three high-ranking Israeli officials (Areil Sharon, Yitshak Mordechai and Dany Naveh) arrived in Amman on Sunday and expeditiously met with Jordan's crown prince Hasan.

The Israeli delegation reportedly communicated "important information" from Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu to King Hussein concerning the two Mossad agents.

The sources said an agreement emerged whereby crown prince Hasan would fly to West Jerusalem on the following day to meet with Netanyahu in order to discuss the details of the "deal," which he reportedly did.

Both Israeli and Jordanian official sources refused to "confirm or deny" the reported visits, which could suggest that they did take place.

Indeed, King Hussein himself alluded to a "deal" on Tuesday afternoon when he said he hoped Israel would offer "a gesture of good will" toward the Palestinians.

He actually mentioned Sheikh Yasin by name, saying he hoped Israel would release "the glorious Sheikh Yasin" in order to improve the general political atmosphere in the area.

For his part, Sheikh Yasin himself telephoned his family in Gaza, telling them he would soon return home.

Earlier, King Hussein telephoned Hamas leaders in Gaza and told them that Yasin was free at last and that he was not deported and that he could return to Gaza once his medical examinations were complete.

The king's phone call was received well by the Islamists, who retreated from earlier statements in which they accused the Israeli government of "deporting" Sheikh Yasin, and the Jordanian government of "connivance with the Zionists."

The Islamist leader said that Jordan now has a strong bargaining position which could, if properly invested, bring about the release of thousands of Palestinian and Lebanese prisoners in Israeli jails.

In the meanwhile, it is expected that the release of Sheikh Yasin will enable Hamas to acquire renewed political legitimacy in Jordan and the Arab world, which some observers opine could lead to more political moderation on the part of Hamas.

Human Rights organizations censure Arafat

for closing Gaza institutions

Occupied Jerusalem-PT(29 September)

Human rights organizations have renewed their criticisms of Palestinian Authority chairman Yasser Arafat for closing down sixteen charitable and educational institutions in the Gaza Strip in submission to Israeli and American demands.

The Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group (PHRMG) has labeled the closure of the civilian Islamic institutions "a flagrant violation of human rights and a serious transgression on the pillars of civil society."

Basem Eid, Director-General of the PHRMG said in a telephone interview on Monday 29 September the closure of the Gaza institutions amounted to "a blind submission to Zionist dictates."

He added that the draconian measure was "totally unjust," opining that "it will increase rather than reduce violence."

Eid, who is gathering details on each of the closed institutions, said the only people who are now going be hurt by the closure are the poor and orphan who have no where else to turn to for daily subsistence.

"Some of these institutions used to provide the poor with daily food supplement, so I don't understand what purpose does it serve to starve the poor, the orphan and the underprivileged."

Eid ridiculed PA claims that the closure of the institutions was aimed at serving Palestinian interests.

"It is extremely hard to believe that depriving children from going to schools and closing kindergartens serves Palestinians' interests."

Furthermore, Eid pointed out that the closed institutions are located in the Gaza Strip where Arafat's authority is stronger, not in the West Bank where the latest spate of bombings were presumably planned and executed.

He said this suggests that the closure was politically motivated, and had nothing to do with any genuine security considerations.

On Sunday, the another human rights organization, al Damir (conscience) lambasted the PA for "succumbing to Israeli demands."

The organization issued a written statement describing the closure of the Gazan institutions as "a clear assault on justice and human rights."

On Friday, PA chairman Yasser Arafat reportedly told a group of Hebron civic leaders that he realized that the closure of the Gaza institutions was unjust.

However, Arafat added that "I have no choice in light of the intense pressures I am facing."
