The PA clamps down on free press

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem-

After signing the hapless Wye memorandum on 23 October, the Palestinian Authority (PA) stepped up its suppression of the media on the pretext of “honouring our international commitments” and “combating incitement”. PA President Yasser Arafat, who spoke almost euphorically about the “historic agreement” after he returned from Washington, warned that “we will not allow anybody or any group to sabotage this great national achievement.” PA Chief Ghazi al Jabali, an Arafat hanger-on reputed for ordering night raids on Islamist-run colleges and universities, stated that “Israel was our enemy before the signing at Wye Plantation. Now this has changed. Israel has become our peace partner and we shall not allow anyone to incite or instigate against our partner.” Jabali went on to describe the United States as “our friend”, warning that whoever sets the American flag on fire “is a traitor.”

The campaign against the press began in earnest even before the return of the Palestinian delegation from the United States. Thus on 23 October, agents of the Mukhabarat (General Intelligence [GI]) raided the home of Hamed al Beitawi, whisking the high-ranking judge of the Shariah court in Nablus from his home to solitary confinement in a prison on the town’s western outskirts. Al Beitawi’s “crime” had taken place on a satellite TV station called al-Jazira, based in Qatar, whereby he criticized the Wye agreement as “a sell-out, placing the Palestinian society under CIA supervision in the service of Israeli security whims and demands.”

More to the point, some PA officials even publicly lashed out at the al-Jazira television station for “allowing the opposition to besmirch the agreement and vilify the Palestinian Authority.”

The onslaught on the press continued unabated, with the detention and harassment a week later of 11 journalists and cameramen for “interviewing an objectionable person without first receiving official permission.” That person was Hamas leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin who had lambasted the PA for signing “this submissive document.” When all efforts to silence the wheel-chair bound founder of Hamas failed, the PA decided to place him under house arrest, severing his telephone line and barring him from receiving visitors and performing prayers at his neighbourhood mosque. The house arrest, said Ghazi Jabali, was aimed at “protecting the Palestinian people and Yassin himself from his own evils.” Yasser Arafat, however, as well as his obsequious secretary, Tayeb Abdul Rahim, argued on several occasions that Yassin’s arrest was for his own protection, hinting that Israeli agents were planning to kill him.

The mendacity became apparent on 23 December, when Arafat canceled the arrest as “a gesture of good will” on the advent of the holy month of Ramadan. The real reason, however, was not because of Ramadan nor benevolence on Arafat’s part. It was the Chairman’s displeasure with Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, who, by that time, had unilaterally suspended, some say terminated, the Wye agreement.

Ministry of Information remains silent

Throughout all of the above, the PA’s Ministry of Information, headed by Yasser Abed Rabbu (head of the small pro-Oslo Feda Party), remained largely silent. Its private whispers on behalf of the journalists fell on deaf ears in the security agencies and met with further rebuffs in the General Information Department (GID), a new security-oriented body which was set up to deal with journalists. The GID had issued a decree on 5 November requiring that all foreign journalists obtain a special permit before entering the self-rule areas. Strangely enough, the Ministry of Information denied that such a decision had ever been made.

The PA harassment of the press was flagrant from the start of Clinton’s visit through the Anglo-American bombing of Iraq (16-19 December). When demonstrations broke out, the PA did not want the world to witness the torching of American flags. Several officials unashamedly claimed that “the United States has become the Palestinians’ best friend.” In fact, one PA security official warned that “whoever sets the American flag on fire undermines Palestinian national interests.” Jabali stated “that only traitors would demonstrate against the United States.” On 18 December, he ordered his men to brutally attack a group of journalists covering an anti-American demonstration in downtown Gaza. According to Najib Abu al-Jabin, who works for the Associated Press, the police “attacked, beat and dragged us to the local lock-up where drug addicts and other common criminals were being kept.” Zakariya Talamas, who heads the Gaza chapter of the Palestinian journalists union, was also beaten up and briefly detained. He was quoted by the Gaza-based weekly al Risala as saying “I’m very sad to say that the law of the jungle prevails here.”

In addition to abusing and maltreating journalists, PA police also shut down several press offices, accusing the proprietors of “covering illegal demonstrations” and “endangering relations with a friendly country.” Abdul Salam Abu Askar, one of those whose office was closed, testified as follows: “Several secret police agents besieged my office after midnight and asked me if I had disseminated films of the protests. Before I could answer, they dragged me to the police station where I found eight other photojournalists whose cameras, film and pictures had all been confiscated.” Abu Askar went on to say, “There I met Colonel Talal Abu Zeid, head of the Investigation Department, who demanded that I refrain from disseminating any press materials undermining the PA’s image.”

At the same time, PA police on direct orders from GI Chief Tawfik Tirawi briefly shut down eight private television and radio stations operating in the West Bank for “over-covering anti-American demonstrations.” Included were “TV Bethlehem” and “TV al-Watan” in Ramallah, whose audiences exceed those of the official PA TV station. As for those stations which it allowed to remain open, the PA told them to confine their coverage to “local news” or “face the consequences”, an indirect order to exercise “self-censorship” on everything that it broadcasts on their screens.

Suppressed freedom of the press widespread

The fettering of press freedom was not confined to foreign correspondents stationed in the autonomous enclaves or Palestinian journalists and cameramen working for foreign-based news media. Even PA radio and television, which are virtual mouthpieces of the PA autocratic regime, bore some of the brunt. Following the signing of the Wye River agreement, the editors-in-chief of the three Palestinian dailies published in the occupied territories—al-Quds, al-Hayatul Jadida and al-Ayyam—received “instructions” from Yasser Arafat's office to “scale-down their criticisms of Israel and to use a milder language when referring to Jewish settlers.” This invitation to self-censorship prompted Hafez al Barghouthi, editor of the Ramalla-based al-Hayatul Jadida, to declare in a rather satirical manner that “he whose mouth is full of water can’t speak.” In another instance, the three papers refused to publish articles critical of the Wye memorandum, especially with regard to its security aspects, including the stipulated CIA supervision of Palestinian media and civilian institutions.

Some of the papers, however, sought to circumvent censorship by publishing articles with academic overtones, criticizing, though indirectly and politely, the Palestinian leadership’s “modest negotiating performance.” But even this half-hearted effort was the exception rather than the norm, and it often came “too little, too late.”

The PA suppression of press freedom does not stem entirely from Israeli pressure or CIA “recommendations.” Some of the severest forms of censorship are reserved for criticism of Yasser Arafat himself and his administrative blemishes and financial mismanagement. Arafat, it is well known, is effectively the sole leader of the PA. Some say he is the PA. He holds all the reins, he controls all the money and he takes all the decisions. However, despite his absolute power and authority, the Palestinian media are not allowed to direct any criticism to his person, either implicitly or explicitly. “The President is above criticism”, said Husam Khader, a maverick Fatah Legislative Council member, who repeatedly demanded that all PA officials (including Arafat) be accountable to the people, but to no avail.

Besides Arafat, there are a whole list of subjects considered off-limits to the media, including security collaboration between PA and Israel, Arafat’s wife (Suha), Arafat’s secret bank accounts and the extra-ordinary financial dealings involving Arafat’s financial advisor, Muhammed Rashid, and shadowy Israeli firms.

When some of these “red lines” are crossed in Legislative Council debates, PA intelligence and Preventive Security agents make sure that the dirty clothing does not find its way to the newspapers or to private television stations. (Last year the PA closed down an educational television station for screening a debate session of the Council critical of the autonomous Authority.)

Freedom prevails

Notwithstanding, in the age of the Internet and satellite televisions, there is no hiding dirty laundry. At most, the PA can try to intimidate journalists and foster an atmosphere of terror, but it is swimming against the current. The PA seems almost certainly set to lose the race—the free word will find a way.

The Third or the Fourth Way!

Analysis of Mr. Munir Shafiq’s Critique of Edward Said’s Third Way

By Dr. Musil J. Shihadeh*

One cannot deny that Mr. Said’s “new” approach of the “Third Way” will obviate any further armed resistance to Israeli desperate practices to keep its unlimited hegemony over the Palestinians’ national rights, as rightly pointed out by Mr. Munir Shafiq. Using the examples of Algiers and Vietnam would not cater to Mr. Said’s thesis since the negotiations here followed a very bloody path that convinced the oppressors that continuing with the ugly status quo could not continue against the determined masses to regain their freedom. Therefore one has to conclude that such a phase of negotiations could not be actuated until the oppressor realizes that it would be a practice in futility to continue his colonialist ambitions of keeping his hegemony over the determined oppressed. So the final negotiation is an inevitable conclusion for a successful armed resistance, and this is not the issue, as much as whether the oppressor has realized that it has no other alternative but to cede to the will of the determined masses. The present “peace” negotiations proves conclusively that the time is not right, as the Israeli oppressors are dictating their humiliating terms for the total Palestinian surrender.

It is a well-known historical fact that political struggle will not achieve any tangible proximate goals of its own unless it is backed by armed struggle. As a matter of fact, and especially in our case, political struggle would carry no weight unless it plays the role of the public relations forum for armed struggle. Just as the miserable status quo of negotiations has proven, Mr. Shafiq would easily solicit my approval that the “Third Way” proposals by Mr. Said is nothing more than an illusion that stems out of a desperate attempt to find a solution, any solution. At the same time, I cannot perceive Mr. Shafiq’s thesis as a perfect approach to the present dilemma when we realize that one cannot hope to achieve certain national objectives without the active help and participation of certain factions on the other side. One cannot ignore the fact that many Israelis have “switched” their position to support the national rights of the Palestinians as a result of determined armed struggle. Notwithstanding, there are many Israelis who did support, and still do, the Palestinian national rights out of moral and ideological considerations. Many of them even support armed struggle, while others tend to use it to justify their supporting views. It is for this reason one does not have to choose between supporting those ideas of Mr. E. Said or those of Mr. M. Shafiq, as the issue is not exactly black and white. Being relatively weaker militarily than the other side, we should welcome any help that could emerge from any faction of Israeli society, as long as such help would not impose any pre-conditions on our armed struggle, strategies and final objectives.

But before we even delve into the means, we still have to define our objectives. Different factions of the Palestinian leadership tend to have different objectives, leading to the perplexity of the path and the further confusion of our masses. There are those leaders who have become pragmatic to demand the establishment of a Palestinian State within the area of the West Bank with over 170 Jewish settlements littering the Palestinian landscape, while some are a bit extremist to demand the pre-1967 borders void of all Jewish settlements. There are, of course, those who are demanding the exact reversal to the situation before 1948, comprised of a Moslem government that gives certain rights to the Jewish and Christian minorities. There are the “utopians” who are proposing the earlier models of a secular democratic State for all Jews, Moslems and Christians, or one State with bi-national composites. Surprisingly, very few are entertaining the prospect of demanding the implementation of UN resolution 181/11 of 29 November 1947, known as the UN Partition Plan.

If total justice is to be achieved, then one has to envisage an armed struggle that could go on for hundreds of years without guarantee of success, and which might annihilate our people in the process. Theoretical justice, legality and morality would necessitate the eviction of all Jews whose incursion into Palestine from the beginning of the 20th century has resulted in the dispossession of our people. Pragmatism, provided it is not used as a euphemism for further concessions, could also be contemplated to get out of the vicious circle as a possible solution to the conflict. A solution based on the semi-legal UN Partition resolution 181 of 1947 would be an ideal path since it would legitimize our struggle in the eyes of the world, which has adopted such a resolution in the first place.

Needless to inform the reader that resolution 181gives us a lot more than what it ostensibly purports. According to this resolution, it is prohibited to practice eviction, or land and property expropriation by any authority against anybody Jewish or Palestinian. This means that no one would have to become a refugee or lose his land or property, which would not allow the Jews to possess more than 7% of the total area of Palestine. Is this pragmatic or would it ever be acceptable to the Zionists? Of course not, but it beats the allegation that a one-State solution is tantamount to the “destruction” of Israel, as we would be talking from a legal base established by the UN.

According to the Jerusalem Post, 23 October 1998, it was reported that over 400,000 Russian NON-JEWS have immigrated permanently to Israel abusing the Law of Return, aside from using the same Law to admit hundreds of thousands of Jews from all over the world. This should unveil the fact that even within Israel proper, there is enough room to allow a good number of the Palestinian refugees to return. If we were to implement the call for a one-State solution for all, then the status quo would definitely make such a return an impossible venture, as it would not cater to the basic human and civil rights of the Palestinians. Again, I am not advocating the goal of implementing resolution 181 as much as I am trying to study our possible options and perhaps seek what is a feasible solution. We have to stop seeking solutions that are based on theoretical speculations that assume premises of morality, justice, logic and peace, as these are terms that might mislead us. We should explore the available options with a pragmatic approach that can solve the conflict with the least damage to our national aspirations and with the least possible time, and without jeopardizing such dignified goals.

When such an objective is determined, then, and only then, can we go forward toward seeking the proper vehicle to reach our goal, since the Zionists realize as much as we do that the peace wagon would never arrive unless the Palestinians are on board.

The Judaization of the Holy Land:

United States Policy Towards Jerusalem

By Dr. Ahmad Yousef*

(1-4)

Executive Summary

Since 1948 when the Jews first began their occupation of Palestine, the world has looked with both sympathy and suspicion at the ensuing Arab/Israeli conflict. The Jews were once seen by the world as a desperate people who were fleeing for their lives from the brutalities of Hitler’s holocaust. Beyond this, much of the world has always suspected Zionist intentions. This suspicion is the result of the propaganda and the sometimes brutal methods that the Jews have employed against the indigenous Palestinian/Arab people of Palestine, who are the owners of the land upon which the Jews sought shelter from their suffering at the hands of the Western world. The Palestinian people have suffered as much as—if not more than—the Jews, who fled Europe in search of a Jewish homeland and who proceeded to unleash upon their Arab hosts the brutal reality of the Zionists’ dream for land. The United States, Israel’s primary patron, has stood guard against reason, accountability, justice and humanity to accommodate the Zionist dream.

This unquestionable and unconditional patronage is best witnessed by American submission on the issue of Jerusalem and the final status negotiations of the Oslo accords or the “peace process”.

From presidential election to presidential election, the official U.S. policy on Jerusalem has been that Jerusalem is an international city, a holy land that has significance to the three world religions—Christianity, Islam and Judaism—and that the final status must be settled through negotiations. With the election of Bill Clinton as president, this official policy has changed.

Prior to Clinton’s tenure in the White House, every American President from 1967 until 1994 viewed continued settlement activity in Jerusalem unfavourably. It was standard U.S. policy that settlements were illegal and a violation of the Fourth Geneva Convention. Settlements in Jerusalem were considered unilateral actions prejudicial to the determination of Jerusalem’s final status and an “obstacle to the success of the negotiations for a just and final peace between Israel and its neighbours.”

In 1979, the first Security Council resolution to specifically address the issue of settlement construction was resolution 446. The United States and Britain abstained from voting. On 20 July 1979, in resolution 452, the United Nations called for an end to Israeli settlement building and again the United States abstained, even though the U.S. representative to the United Nations condemned settlement building as inconsistent with international law and the Fourth Geneva Convention. In March 1980, the United States voted to pass UN Security Council resolution 465, which called on Israel to “dismantle existing settlements and in particular to cease, on an urgent basis, the establishment, construction and planning of settlements.” Later the American ambassador to the United Nations in reference to the vote said, “there are a number of factors of a practical character that make impractical the call... some projects are not so easily dismantled.”

We are now faced with a project that cannot easily be dismantled and an Israeli Government that is determined to make dismantlement impossible by continuing to build settlements for Jews only on occupied Palestinian land.

In 985 AC (before Christ), according to an article by Patrick Cockburn of the British newspaper The Independent, Arab geographer Muhammad Ibn Ahmad al-Maqdisi wrote that Jerusalem was a “golden basin full of scorpions”. Patrick Cockburn adds that it has not changed.

There is congruity in the process of Judaizing Jerusalem. In Jerusalem, Israeli authorities carry out their Judaization schemes and impose a status quo by force of arms, while totally disregarding the resolutions condemning such acts that have been consistently voted for by all of the countries of the world, except the United States. Israel’s friends and supporters in the U.S. Congress and in the media play out their roles in this synchronized dance of deception by stepping mindlessly to the melody of an arrogant and ethnocentric Israeli Jerusalem policy.

It is sad to see a once proud and respected American people dominated and misdirected by the prodigal child, Israel. Israel’s continued settlement building in East Jerusalem, at a time when the United States claims to be keen on the peace process, is an inexcusable act of disrespect toward one of its few international friends.

While Netanyahu’s Government was being condemned by the Arab world and the entire international community for non-compliance with the so-called peace process, U.S. Defense Secretary William Cohen, in a joint press conference held on 11 April 1997, told Israeli Defense Minister Mordechai that he was asking Congress for $1.8 billion dollars in grants to fund Israeli military programmes, which are supposed to guarantee that Israel retains its “qualitative” edge over all Arab countries in the region. During his lobbying efforts, Cohen justified granting Israel the money by describing Israel as “the oasis of democracy in this otherwise unstable region”, and as “the United States’ longtime ally”. Israel is the only so-called democracy in the world that uses race and ethnicity to distinguish among its citizens. Cohen further insulted the sensitivities of the world community by saying, “We (America) are firmly committed to its security and prosperity.”

Muslims and Arabs heard these U.S. statements, and, having witnessed America’s complacent actions, have begun to ask questions. One of these questions is why Arab countries are still moving toward normalizing relations with Israel? Another question being asked is what does it mean when the United States rigidly opposes every UN resolution that condemns Israel? On the public level, Washington expresses distaste for Israel’s settlement policies in East Jerusalem. Yet after repeated U.S. vetoes in the United Nations that seek to block any international consensus on chastising and forcing Israel to come into compliance with international law, can Washington expect that its claim to be an “honest broker” of peace in the Middle East will be accepted as credible? Should the Palestinians, who seem to have the sympathy of the international community at large, continue to accept Washington’s grossly biased “processes”? To do so means that we have accepted the same failed logic that forced the Palestinians to sit down alone with the Israelis, who clearly have the upper hand in every way and who believe that negotiating with the Palestinians means dictating to them. They negotiate peace while their bulldozers destroy the homes of the people who own the land, simply because they are not Jews. America, the superpower, then casts its looming shadow over the truth, demanding that the world remain silent and submit to this illusion and ignore the ugly reality.

This is the American role, though it has not been explained this way to the American people by the Zionist officials in the State Department or by Congressional supporters of Zionism.

To fully understand the U.S. position on Jerusalem, it is necessary to reflect on the history of the U.S. Jerusalem policy over time. There has always been a clear American bias toward Israel and acceptance of Israel’s strategic plan to Judaize the Holy City of Jerusalem. In his article, Jerusalem: In Search of Common Ground, Stephen S. Rosenfeld acknowledges that Israel has indeed been changing facts on the ground in Jerusalem as part of a strategy designed, in Mr. Rosenfeld’s words, to “deny long-resident Arabs their right to live in Israeli-annexed East Jerusalem.” Mr. Rosenfeld then identifies this strategy as “creating facts”. He explains it thus: “Of all the facts that Israel has created, none is at once more prized, but also more provocative, than the dominant position Israel has carved out since the 1967 war. Thus allowing it to take Arab East Jerusalem, annex it to Jewish West Jerusalem, greatly expand its boundaries and settle it thickly, too. The Israeli Government now treats the city as its exclusive, eternal, indivisible and non-negotiable capital, and denies Palestinian territorial and national claims to any part of it.”

Jerusalem’s Legal Status and American Policy
Since the mid-19th century, Zionist theorists have been asserting to Jews all over the world that one of the primary goals of Zionism is to establish Jerusalem as the capital of Israel. When the Palestinian dilemma was handed over to the United Nations in 1947, following the termination of the League of Nations Mandate, Jerusalem was already a major concern. According to the UN Partition Plan outlined in resolution 181, which was issued in November 1947, Jerusalem was to be internationalized because it was agreed that this was the best way to defend all of the religious interests present in the Holy City. When the1948 war broke out, Israeli forces pushed toward Jerusalem and were able to fulfill half of the Zionist dream by occupying 66.2% of Jerusalem and the surrounding areas. While the Old City and its holy sites remained in the hands of the Arabs, Israel announced that the land it occupied—West Jerusalem—would be its capital, and the eastern part was then annexed by Jordan. The international community did not recognize West Jerusalem as Israel’s capital. The international community also refused to recognize a similar Jordanian declaration on East Jerusalem issued in 1960. The United Nations continued to affirm the need to implement resolution 181 regarding the internationalization of Jerusalem. The United States refused to recognize Jerusalem as Israel’s capital and maintained its initial stand in support of internationalization. But the U.S. view on internationalization did not last long. The Truman administration soon opposed internationalization for domestic political reasons. The official justification was impracticality, since there could be no executive power able to impose internationali-zation. In 1953, Israel moved most of its Foreign Ministry from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem. Eisenhower saw this as a provocation that flew in the face of international consensus. Eisenhower promptly ordered his Embassy staff in Tel Aviv not to recognize the move. A year and a half later, the U.S. ambassador offered his credentials in Jerusalem, and that was the end of the Eisenhower policy. Officially, however, the U.S. remained in support of internationalization from 1948 to 1967, although the political rhetoric was chaotic and dependent on the mood of the consecutive presidents and their respective secretaries of State. Simply put, U.S. policy moved from openly supporting the UN resolution that called for an international Jerusalem until 1967, when Washington started working against it. That was the year Israel defeated the Arabs and occupied what was left of Jerusalem.

The Promised Land of Broken Promises

By Ramzy Baroud

And the debate continues... Is there such a thing as the “Promised Land”? If there is, then what are its borders, to whom was it promised and why? As most of us already know, Zionist philosophy has used the biblical statement in Genesis (15:18) in which God promised to give His seed the land between the Nile in Egypt to the Euphrates to support their political claims to the “Promised Land”.

Assuming that the Bible is a reliable source for political allegation, Zionist philosophy regarding the “Promised Land” fails to address other biblical statements made by “God” regarding the same issue. For instance, in Genesis 21:13, “God” names Ishmael as His seed. Agreeing to that fact makes Ishmael the father of Arabs. “God” makes it clear, then, that Arabs are part of the promise. In addition, Galatians 3:27-29 clearly implies that all the believers in Christ “automatically” are the seed of Abraham. After this long cycle of biblical promises, Ephesians 2:12 excludes the Jews from the promise, because those without the belief in Christ are strangers. If this set of promises confuses you, you are not alone. Many more are still confused, even if some do not admit it.

Instead of arguing for or against these biblical claims and statements (which will always isolate and anger certain groups), I have decided to take another route. I am going to assume that God has given his promise exclusively to the Jews. If this promise was delivered in a godly fashion, let it be. If not, we have to deal with two conclusions and choose that which we find fit. First, God has broken His promise, which in itself is hard to accept as a characteristic of God. Or secondly, God has never promised anything He could not fulfill to start with.

David’s unfulfilled promise
In the middle of this century, David was given a promise. He was told that his “Promised Land” was waiting for him to return. David was a German Jew who suffered the consequences of the political turmoil in Europe during that time. After suffering through much hardship for a few year, David’s dream seemed closer to reality that ever before. In no time, David arrived with his family, wife and three children at an agricultural settlement. He was told, “this is an empty desert and it’s our obligation ‘as Jews’ to make it fit to be called the Promised Land.” Two years after his arrival, David found himself holding a rifle and chasing Arabs out of their villages. In the beginning, David was not comfortable watching his friends shooting defenseless and unarmed Arab peasants. Later on, David decided to do whatever it took to clear the land from the “Enemies of God”. One day, David found himself killing an Arab child in the arms of his mother.

At the conclusion of the “cleansing”, the “Promised Land” was cleared of 418 Arab villages and towns, which were destroyed, and 13,000 Palestinian Arabs were massacred. Over a million Arabs were displaced around the world, denied their human rights and dignity. Now, everything looks different. No more poor Arab villages and suspicious faces. David is lying on his comfortable couch watching TV. A picture of one of David’s children stands on the shelf. His name is Dani, an army officer in Hebron. Perhaps he is chasing little Arab kids with a more advanced rifle than the one his father once had. Even though a much older David seems preoccupied with his favourite programme on Israeli TV, a certain picture will never leave his mind. It is a picture of an Arab woman, weeping and begging for his mercy to spare the life of her son. David never listened to her that day and killed the boy. David looks around him, and with shame he cries. Then he asks himself ‘why didn’t I go somewhere else, any place else besides the “Promised Land?”

The Long Journey to Qundar
Ishaq is an Ethiopian Jew. He lived in the Qundar region of Ethiopia most of his life. Through the years, Ishaq made many friends in the area where he lived. His friendships included Muslims and Christians. His best friend was Mohammed. Ishaq and Mohammed looked alike. Some people thought they were brothers. In the early 1980s, some blonde-haired, blue-eyed strangers were spotted in Ishaq’s small and isolated town. After frequent visits to the local leadership of the area, the strangers openly appealed to the people to “return home”. Even though Qundar was the only home Ishaq knew, he was driven to believe that he lived in exile, and the “Promised Land” (also known as Israel) was where he and his people belonged. Even though Ishaq was never a practicing Jew, long years of unemployment pushed him to seriously consider this new option. The youth of Qundar, who never stopped talking about the blondes and the beaches of the “Promised Land”, convinced Ishaq to search for the lost paradise.

The journey to “Israel” was full of danger and hardships. With the help of some organized groups, Ishaq found his way to the Red Sea through Sudan, then on to Israel where he arrived along with a large group of Ethiopian Jews also known as “Flasha”. A few months after his arrival, Ishaq’s family joined his quest to the “land of milk and honey”.

Many years have passed since Ishaq and his family settled in the low-income housing of one of the Israeli cities. So far, none of the promises made to Ishaq have been delivered. Since he arrived in Israel, he has never had a stable job or a reasonable income to make a living with dignity in an area where prices never go down. Once a month, Ishaq stands in a long line of immigrants waiting for the Government’s assistance check. The worst part in that embarrassing moment is when the cashier throws the check at him with disgust. Yet, Ishaq learned to live without complaining. As a responsible Israeli citizen, Ishaq donated his blood once every few months. He was told that if he does so, he might save the life of one Israeli soldier. A TV report was aired some time ago showing that the donated blood of the Flasha found itself in the big dumpster behind the blood bank. Some officials rushed to explain the conduct of the blood centres, saying that the danger of the AIDS virus from blacks is much greater than other races. Once again, with the same silent face, Ishaq shook his head with regret and said nothing.

For a while Ishaq had been lecturing his little children about his “Promised Land”. However, the “Promised Land” he constantly talks about is different from the one he was originally told about. Qundar is what Ishaq believes to be his “Promised Land” and one day, he tells his family, they’ll return, regardless of all the danger and hardships.

Graveyards for Jews only
It was difficult for Haeem and his family upon their arrival in Israel from the Soviet Union. Even though they tried so hard, they didn’t fit with Israeli society. Abraham, Haeem’s oldest son, missed being part of a group. Despite the fact that he knew little to nothing about the politics of the region, Abraham joined the Israeli forces in Occupied South Lebanon. Abraham’s parents were filled with joy once they learned that their son would take his place in defense of his land.

Last year, Abraham was killed in the fighting. Even though a great deal of sadness was felt by the “returned” family, a greater sense of pride was felt. Jewish History, Jewish Religion

The Weight of History

By Israel Shahak

Anti-Jewish Persecutions
During the whole period of classical Judaism, Jews were often subjected to persecutions- and this fact now serves as the main ‘argument’ of the apologists of the Jewish religion with its anti-Gentile laws and especially of zionism. Of course, the Nazi extermination of five to six million European Jews is supposed to be the crowning argument in that line. We must therefore consider this phenomenon and its contemporary aspect. This is particularly important in view of the fact that the descendants of the Jews of pre-1795 Poland (often called ‘east-European Jews’- as opposed to Jews from the German cultural domain of the early 19th century, including the present Austria, Bohemia and Moravia) now wield predominant political power in Israel as well as in the Jewish communities in the USA and other English-speaking countries; and, because of their particular past history, this mode of thinking is especially entrenched among them, much more than among other Jews.

We must, first, draw a sharp distinction between the persecutions of Jews during the classical period on the one hand, and the Nazi extermination on the other. The former were popular movements, coming from below; whereas the latter was inspired, organized and carried out from above: indeed, by state officials. Such acts as the Nazi state-organized extermination are relatively rare in human history, although other cases do exist (the extermination of the Tasmanians and several other colonial peoples, for example). Moreover, the Nazis intended to wipe out other peoples besides the Jews: Gypsies were exterminated like Jews, and the extermination of Slavs was well under way, with the systematic massacre of millions of civilians and prisoners of war. However, it is the recurrent persecution of Jews in so many countries during the classical period which is the model (and the excuse) for the zionist politicians in their persecutions of the Palestinians, as well as the argument used by apologists of Judaism in general; and it is this phenomenon which we consider now.

It must be pointed out that in all the worst anti-Jewish persecutions, that is, where Jews were killed, the ruling elite- the emperor and the pope, the kings, the higher aristocracy and the upper clergy, as well as the rich bourgeoisie in the autonomous cities- were always on the side of the Jews. The latter’s enemies belonged to the more oppressed and exploited classes and those close to them in daily life and interests, such as the friars of the mendicant orders. It is true that in most (but I thank not in all) cases members of the elite defended the Jews neither out of considerations of humanity nor because of sympathy to the Jews as such, but for the type of reason used generally by rulers in justification of their interests- the fact that the Jews were useful and profitable (to them), defence of ‘law and order’, hatred of the lower classes and fear that anti-Jewish riots might develop into general popular rebellion. Still, the fact remains that they did defend the Jews. For this reason all the massacres of Jews during the classical period were part of a peasant rebellion or other popular movements at times when the government was for some reason especially weak. This is true even in the partly exceptional case of Tsarist Russia. The Tsarist government, acting surreptitiously through its secret police, did promote pogroms; but it did so only when it was particularly weak (after the assassination of Alexander II in 1881, and in the period immediately before and after the 1905 revolution) and even then took care to contain the breakdown of ‘law and order’. During the time of its greatest strengthen- for example, under Nicholas I or in the latter part of the reign of Alexander III, when the opposition had been smashed- pogroms were not tolerated by the Tsarist regime, although legal discrimination against Jews was intensified.

The general rule can be observed in all the major massacres of Jews in Christian Europe. During the first crusade, it was not the proper armies of the knights, commanded by famous dukes and counts, which molested the Jews, but the spontaneous popular hosts composed almost exclusively of peasants and paupers in the wake of Peter the Hermit. In each city the bishop or the emperor’s representative opposed them and tried, often in vain, to protect the Jews. The anti-Jewish riots in England which accompanied the third crusade were part of a popular movement directed also against royal officials, and some rioters were punished by Richard I. The massacres of Jews during the outbreaks of the Black Death occurred against the strict orders of the pope, the emperor, the bishops and the German princes. In the free towns, for example in Strasbourg, they were usually preceded by a local revolution in which the oligarchic town council, which protected the Jews, was overthrown and replaced by a more popular one. The great 1391 massacres of Jews in Spain took place under a feeble regency government and at a time when the papacy, weakened by the Great Schism between competing popes, was unable to control the mendicant friars.

Perhaps the most outstanding example in the great massacre of Jews during the Chmielnicki revolt in the Ukraine (1648), which started as a mutiny of Cossack officers but soon turned into a widespread popular movement of the oppressed serfs: “The unprivileged, the subjects, the Ukrainians, the Orthodox [persecuted by the Polish Catholic church] were rising against their Catholic Polish masters, particularly against their masters’ bailiffs, clergy and Jews. The character of anti-Jewish persecutions underwent a radical change in modern times. With the advent of the modern state, the abolition of serfdom and the achievement of minimal individual rights, the special socio-economic function of the Jews necessarily disappears. Along with it disappear also the powers of the Jewish community over its members; individual Jews in growing numbers win the freedom to enter the general society of their countries. Naturally, this transition aroused a violent reaction both on the part of Jews (especially their rabbis) and of those elements in European society who opposed the open society and for whom the whole process of liberation of the individual was anathema.

In contrast, the Old Christian, and even more so Muslim opposition to classical Judaism was remarkably free from racism. International law: a Perspective on U.S. strikes on Iraq

In mid-December, the United States of America, aided and supported by the United Kingdom, launched strikes against Iraq in an operation called Desert Fox. There have been many episodes in world history since the United Nations came into being where the use of force was condemned and the nations involved subsequently denounced. In some circumstances, this resulted in forceful action; Iraq was driven out of Kuwait during the Gulf conflict. In other circumstances, this resulted in little more than international condemnation. It may be argued that there is little point to identifying and debating these issues, since it is well established that the law is only as effective as the power that seeks to enforce it.

If it were established that the United States had broken international conventions on the use of force, the question of who would enforce the law on the world’s last real superpower would still remain. However, international law still plays a significant role in the world today, and while it may be frustrating to know that at times the legal reasoning may only be theoretical and not useful to any practical extent, it is still necessary to ascertain international legal positions. For it is still hoped that one day, all nations will be subject to the same just standards.

The use of force is legally justified in two other situations: (1) Actions taken by the UN Security Council itself to maintain and/or restore international peace and/or security, and (2) Self-defense, as stipulated by Article 51 of the UN Charter, which states: “Nothing in the present Charter shall impair the inherent right of individual or collective self-defense if an armed attack occurs against a Member of the United Nations, until the Security Council has taken measures necessary to maintain international peace and security. Measures taken by Members in the exercise of this right of self-defense shall be immediately reported to the Security Council and shall not in any way affect the authority and responsibility of the Security Council under the present Charter to take at any time such action as it deems necessary in order to maintain or restore international peace and security.” It is advisable to note that international law recognizes only the right of the United Nations to exercise force under the first case, and thus only the United Nations Security Council can declare such actions. Other regional organizations, such as NATO, cannot. However, in the second case, no State is obliged to wait for the United Nations Security Council to pass a resolution in order to defend itself.

What are the possible U.S.-British arguments? The U.S.-British air strike can be argued in international law in a number of ways: (1) The U.S.-British alliance was implementing UN resolutions as per the first justification for using force; (2) The U.S. and the British were defending themselves from Iraq as per the first part of the second justification for using force; or (3) The U.S. and the British were defending the rest of the world from Iraq as per the second part of the second justification for using force.

The first claim is dealt with quite simply. In order for the United States and Britain to claim that it was acting on behalf of the United Nations to hold water, they must demonstrate that they had UN approval for its actions. The question to ask is: was it necessary for the UN Security Council to pass a resolution to grant Member States permission to attack Iraq? Yes. Has there been such a resolution in recent history? Yes. This resolution was passed in 1990, and authorized Member States to use “all necessary means to uphold and implement resolution 660 (1990) [this resolution dealt with Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait] and all subsequent relevant resolutions and to restore international peace and security in the area.” It remains to be seen whether or not the coalition that went into the Gulf overstepped the doctrines of proportionality in trying to evict Iraqi forces from Kuwait. However, with the eviction of the forces, the resolution was thus fulfilled and is now not relevant. In any event, this resolution was nullified by resolution 687, which declared that a cease-fire was in place: “upon official notification by Iraq to the Secretary-General and to the Security Council of its acceptance of the provisions above, a formal cease-fire is effective between Iraq and Kuwait and the Member States cooperating with Kuwait in accordance with resolution 687 (1990).” There were certain conditions laid out for Iraq to accept for this resolution to be passed. In the past, conversions to Islam have also occurred, but not so much among tribal members as among the section outside formal Hindu society known as the untouchables (Dalits), who were treated with the worst forms of oppression and stigmatization. They are so low that they are not even inside the formal framework of the oppressive and infamous caste system. Some small sections of these people have turned to other religions—among them Islam—in the search for equality and dignity.

The Hindu bodies allege that illiterates are hoodwinked into changing their religion, but often educated people convert. The most famous incident of untouchables converting to Islam happened in Meenakshipuram, a small town in Southern India. All these people were educated untouchables who held decent jobs and were reasonably well-to-do. But they still faced social ostracization and stigmatization because of their caste status as untouchables. As a result, they turned to Islam, which offered them equal status with each of their fellow humans in the eyes of Allah.

Interestingly, conversions have become a political tool in today’s communally charged atmosphere. A few weeks ago, a colony of untouchables in Northern India threatened to convert en masse to Islam if they were evicted from the government property they had been squatting on for countless years. They were even in touch with a Qadi. The fact is that Hindu society has left the space open for such conversions. Faced with discrimination based on the caste system, finding no dignified space or socio-economic opportunities for themselves within that society, turning to other religions is quite natural. In fact, it is rather surprising that the number of conversions are so low. That this may be the case says something about the attitude of Muslims in India, many of whom have adopted the worst aspects of Hindu society, such as belief in social ‘castes’, demand for dowry from the bride’s family and a tendency to look down on former untouchables in the same manner as the caste-ridden Hindus do.

A Winter’s Song

By: Ruth Anderson

In the heart of a Middle Eastern capital, you can find Ali, a widower, and his four children. On the day of Al Eid, Ali celebrated by visiting the grave of his recently deceased wife. He left his children in his dark, bare house asleep. Anyway, there was really nothing to celebrate. He lost his inadequately paying job as a janitor in a nearby health clinic because he was sick and is now living on a stipend of only $70 per month. This very meager income must feed, clothe and support the needs of four children plus pay rent. To make things more difficult, Ali’s oldest boy, about 15, is deaf.

While some of the more well-to-do were out celebrating the Eid in posh restaurants or at the homes of friends and relatives, the widower was just trying to put some bread and tea on his table and open a window to bring a shaft of light into his dismal one room home. In this home could be found no meat dish like the traditional mensaf for there was no woman to cook it on the one hand, and no money to purchase the ingredients on the other. He could not afford to buy Eid clothes for his children and while other neighborhood kids played with fireworks and toys, his own children spent the day without a meal, without a mother, without hope. Perhaps there is no enemy worse than abject poverty and perhaps no home more dim than one without the soothing hand of a mother and wife.

Ramadan is a month of fasting from dawn to dusk. One of the purposes of a whole month of fasting is so that those who have can empathize with those who do not. In days long gone by, alms given by those who could afford to pay them, sustained those who had no jobs and source of income. “Modern” times see few who give to the less fortunate and no concerted national or international effort to alleviate the financial sufferings of the Muslim poor. If each Muslim nation gave alms in the same manner that the early followers of Prophet Muhammed (PBUH) did, then it would be hard indeed to find people who did not have jobs and food on the table.

Omar Ibn Al-Khattab, who was the second and probably the greatest of the Muslim caliphs made personally certain that no poverty existed under his sovereignty. He would go out at night alone and search for someone, anyone who was hungry and without provisions. If a mule or horse stumbled on a road not properly taken care of, Omar would feel personally responsible. One night he came across a mother who was cooking stones. He approached her as if he were any ordinary person without rank or importance and asked her why she was cooking stones.

She replied, “Because Omar is not taking care of us. I am cooking stones so my children will fall asleep and forget about their hunger.”

Caliph Omar then went out and got her flour and other provisions to feed her children and more than that, he bent down and started her fire again and began to cook the meal for her himself.

Today, there are no caliphs who search for the poor and underprivileged. Most Arab/Muslim leaders these days pocket the money that should go to the destitute and not only that, milk their countries dry of money and resources leaving a stark sharp contrast between the wealthy few and the starving majority.

There is no Caliph Omar to help out poor destitute Ali and his family. And for a man such as Ali, who does not enjoy the best of health and is poorly educated, hope for a decent future for him and his children is more of a pipe dream than a reality. Just another Winter’s song sung to ears that hear but do not really listen.
