Press Cuttings

The Boston Globe (US), 28 February 2000

Beirut- In the decade since Lebanon's civil war ended, this country has tried to rebuild from the ruins by forgetting the brutality of the past.

To many Lebanese, it seemed the only way out of a conflict in which all sides committed unspeakable atrocities.

But Asaad Shaftari, 44, who rose to second in command of the right-wing Lebanese Forces during the 15-year civil war, stunned Lebanon this month with a statement extraordinary in its simplicity and honesty.

He publicly asked for forgiveness.

In a letter addressed to his victims ''both living and dead,'' he became the first high-level militia leader to apologize for the thousands that he and his Christian militia contributed to the 70,000 deaths in Lebanon's agony of war.

The Chicago Tribune, 28 February 2000

Jerusalem- Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak suggested Sunday that he would agree to a full withdrawal from the Golan Heights, making for the clearest indication yet of his willingness to meet a key Syrian demand for peace.

Barak told his Cabinet that four Israeli prime ministers effectively agreed to withdraw from the Golan if certain conditions were fulfilled, his office said.

Israeli officials had in the past denied that such commitments were made, saying that previous offers were hypothetical. Barak's admission appeared designed to boost his credibility by assuring Israelis that other leaders also had agreed to return the Golan.

Former Prime Minister Yitzhak Shamir and an aide to former Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, both from the hawkish Likud Party, denied Sunday having ever agreeing to a full Israeli withdrawal.

The New York Times (US), 28 February 2000

PARIS- Prime Minister Lionel Jospin of France came close to celebrating a highly successful first 1,000 days in office today.

Instead, however, he awoke to an avalanche of criticism, as journalists and political analysts here replayed the weekend's events before examining what most considered to be his biggest political blunder to date: his trip to the Middle East.

Splattered across France's newspapers were less than flattering images: Mr. Jospin running from a mob or huddling in his car as Palestinian demonstrators kicked it.

On Saturday, Mr. Jospin angered the Arab world, departing from France's usual cautious diplomacy to call acts committed by Hezbollah guerrillas in Lebanon "terrorist." But back home the blunder was as much about French politics as about Arab-Israeli peace talks.

The Financial Times (UK), 25 February 2000

When David Levy, Israel's foreign minister, shouted to parliamentary deputies this week that Lebanese children would die if Hizbollah Islamist guerrillas fired rockets into Israel, his outburst shocked many Israelis.

Never before, they said, had Israel threatened under certain conditions to murder Lebanese children. How, they asked, could Ehud Barak, the prime minister, condone such statements.

The pictures of Mr Levy raging from the Knesset podium were quickly broadcast across the Arab world, serving to illustrate the increasingly sharp anti-Zionist articles published against Israel by the Arab media. Israeli officials were not surprised by the reaction, saying they had become accustomed to such cartoons and editorials, especially in the Egyptian press.

Ha'aretz, 25 February 2000

Senior politicians have instructed the Jerusalem Municipality not to demolish Palestinian houses in the east side of the city ahead of Pope John Paul's visit to Israel on March 21.

The freeze was originally timed for February 21 but was pushed back first to February 28, then to March 1, sources close to the ministerial committee on Jerusalem affairs said. The sources said implementing the freeze will depend on the cooperation of the municipality.

A Jerusalem municipal spokesman told Ha'aretz on Sunday that the city does not recognize any agreement to halt demolitions of illegal houses for the papal visit, or for any other occasion.

The Times (UK), 23 February 2000

Jerusalem- Israeli intelligence chiefs believe that violent Palestinian disorder is likely during the Pope's visit next month (Ross Dunn writes). Several arms of the security establishment have concluded that a new intifada could be imminent because of the Government's failure to make progress in the peace process.

Ehud Barak, the Prime Minister, is said to have blundered by concentrating on the Syrian and Lebanese tracks, to the exclusion of the Palestinians.
