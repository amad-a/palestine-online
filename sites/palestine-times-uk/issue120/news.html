Israeli aggression continues despite PR cease-fire

Khalid Amayreh

In stark contradiction to the apparently only verbal cease-fire announced by Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon last week, the Israeli occupation army continued to make almost daily incursions into PA-controlled areas, especially in the Gaza Strip.

According to the Palestinian security chief in Gaza, Abdul Razzak al-Majayada, the Israeli army violated the alleged cease-fire 97 times in the span of four days, or an average of one violation per hour, leaving at least three Palestinians dead and injuring as many as a hundred others.

On 25 May, Israeli tanks and armoured vehicles advanced half a kilometre into PA territory in four areas in Rafah at the southern tip of the Gaza Strip, south of Gaza City, at the Mintar crossing and at Ouja near Jericho in the West Bank.

In each of these incursions, Israeli tanks barraged Palestinian residential areas with artillery and machine-gun fire, while army bulldozers were busy destroying and pulverizing large tracts of Palestinian farmland and orchards, a routine punitive measure aimed at punishing ordinary Palestinians by making the intifada as costly to them as possible.

Earlier, Israeli occupation troops shot dead two boys: Ala Bouji and Shadi Kamal Siyam; the first near the Mintar crossing and the second during a bombardment in Rafah.

Also on 25 May, Israeli tanks bombarded the Palestinian town of Beit Jala, inflicting heavy damage on residential homes, with one home completely burned down.

Artillery bombardment of residential areas was likewise reported on 29 May in and around Nablus, where the Israeli army deployed more tanks onto hilltops overlooking the largest town in the West Bank.

In addition, Jewish terrorists continued to terrorize Palestinian villagers throughout the West Bank.

On 28 May, a Jewish settler opened fire on Palestinian schoolchildren who were returning home near the village of Tku, south of Bethlehem, seriously wounding two boys.

The settler terrorist reportedly fled to a nearby Jewish settlement, which also bears the same name, with the Israeli army making no efforts to arrest or even question him. Moreover, the settler terrorist also set Palestinian grain fields on fire in many localities in the West Bank just as the fields were ripe for harvest, inflicting heavy economic losses on penniless Palestinian peasants.

Normally, Jewish terrorists are not arrested or prosecuted for their crimes, including murder, against Palestinians, a practice that has become especially brazen since the outbreak of the Palestinian intifada more than eight months ago.

Apart from continued attacks and bombardment of Palestinian population centres, the Israeli army continued to lay siege on most Palestinian towns, cutting them off from each other and isolating them from the rest of the world.

The continued encirclement of Palestinian towns and villages means that Palestinian civilians will remain under the mercy of Israeli guns, even under the rubric of a cease-fire.

It means also that Israeli soldiers manning army blocks, deployed ubiquitously throughout the West Bank, continue to humiliate, harass and detain Palestinians suspected of taking part in Palestinian resistance. And when a Palestinian ventures to protest the humiliation and maltreatment, the trigger-happy soldiers are likely to open fire.

Indeed, the Israeli occupation army has effectively come to classify the vast bulk of Palestinians as “terrorists,” meaning they are subject to abrupt detention wherever and whenever encountered. And those who couldn’t be arrested, are simply assassinated by Israeli agents, as happened to Azzam Muzher, a Fatah activist from the Balata refugee camp near Nablus. Muzher was killed on 25 May when a bomb, planted by Israeli agents, exploded in his car, mutilating him beyond recognition and wounding six more people, two seriously.

In reaction to the Israeli terror, Palestinian civilians carried out two car-bomb attacks on 25 May: one against an Israeli army outpost in Gaza and carried out by Hamas; and the other in Khadera, in central Israel, carried out by the Islamic Jihad organization. The human-bombers were killed in the two operations and scores of Israelis were slightly injured.

Two more car-bomb blasts rocked West Jerusalem, the first shortly after midnight on 26 May and the other the following morning. The two booby-trapped cars were parked in the vicinity of a major police station, and one of them reportedly contained several mortar shells. While the two explosions caused a few casualties, they nevertheless generated a tremendous amount of panic and anxiety among Israeli security and political officials, not to mention the public at large, as the explosions demonstrated that the Palestinians could cast terror in the heart of the city Israel unilaterally claims to be its capital.

A further Palestinian retaliation took place on 29 May. Palestinian fighters killed the security officer at the settlement of Itamar near Nablus. Palestinians accused the settler officer of taking part in the murder of a number of Palestinian olive growers five months ago.

Suicidal bomb attacks against Israeli targets acquired additional moral weight this week when the Mufti of Palestine, Ikrema Sabri, said in a Friday sermon that martyrdom operations were a legitimate means for self-defence. He pointed out that fighters killed in these operations are true martyrs, a view that infuriated Israeli officials who demanded that Sabri be deported to southern Lebanon or banished to Gaza for “the incitement.”

The latest religious opinion is added to a plethora of fatwas or religious edicts by prominent Islamic scholars who have given these operations the benefit of the doubt.

Earlier this month, the Rector of al-Azhar, Dr. Muhammed Sayed Tantawi, was quoted as saying that martyrdom operations were valid as long as they are not carried out against non-combatants, especially children.

Earlier, Sheikh Yousef Qaradawi, a widely recognized authority on sunni jurisprudence, ruled that martyrdom operations constituted a high level of Jihad against oppression and that they should not be labelled as “suicidal bomb attacks” but rather “martyrdom operations.”

In the meantime, the Israeli official religious establishment, the High Rabbinate, made a fresh provocation to the Muslim world last week when it disclosed plans to study the building of a Jewish synagogue inside the Haram al-Sharif esplanade in East Jerusalem.

The chief Rabbi of Israel, Yisrael Meir Laou, was quoted by the Israeli State-run radio as saying that he had ordered a thorough study of the subject.

The revelation, apparently aimed at testing Muslim reactions to the increasingly daring Jewish proclamations to that effect, drew angry reactions, not only from Palestinian political and religious leaders, but also from Muslim leaders around the world.

The Mufti of Egypt, Dr. Naser Farid Wasel, described the proposal as amounting to a declaration of war on all Muslims world-wide.

He warned that building a synagogue on the Haram al-Sharif would trigger a Third World War, saying Muslims around the world wouldn’t allow such a provocation to their religion.

He warned the Israeli rabbinate “not to play with fire.”

Sharon steps up killing rampage

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

In a rabid escalation of State-sponsored terror against Palestinians, the Israeli occupation army has significantly stepped up its wanton bombardment of population centres, killing and injuring still more innocent civilians and reducing numerous buildings and homes to rubble.

In nearly 95% of the cases, the indiscriminate artillery bombardment targeted defenceless civilians, who can neither escape to a safer place, lest they be killed by machine-gun strafing, nor stay in fortified shelters, because there are virtually none.

Such was the gruesome case of four-month-old toddler Iman Hijo, in Khan Younis, who was killed on 7 May by Israeli artillery shrapnel while in the lap of her mother.

According to sources at Nasser’s Hospital in Khan Younis, the shrapnel pierced the baby’s abdomen, exiting through her back, and then penetrated her mother’s bosom, injuring her seriously.

In addition to Iman and her 19-year-old mother, Suzan, 20 other civilians, mostly children and women, were also injured, some seriously, by the blind bombardment.

Israeli Defence Minister Benyamin Benalizer, apparently concerned about negative publicity, sought to mitigate the brutality of the latest Israeli crime by saying the army did not intend to kill civilians.

“I assure you that we don’t have a deliberate policy of hitting civilians,” said the gleeful-looking minister, who earlier announced that he had given the army “full freedom to act against terror.”

But just what kind of beings does Benalizer expect to find in those concrete boxes on which the deadly shells are fired? Maybe “two-legged animals,” as former-Israeli Prime Minister Menchem Begin once referred to Palestinians, or maybe “snakes, vermin and dirty animals,” as Rabbi Ovadia Yosef of Shas recently called the Arabs.

While Israeli shells were raining death on Khan Younis in southern Gaza, Israeli tanks were also raining death and havoc on residential neighbourhoods in Tulkarm in the northern West Bank, claiming yet another civilian life.

Tulkarm District governor Izzedin al-Sharif described the bombardment as “indiscriminate, blind and deadly.”

“They (the Israeli army) barrage the town with artillery and tank shells non-stop, and they don’t give the slightest damn where the shells will land and how many innocent people they will kill.”

The victim in Tulkarm is 55-year-old Hussein Abu Tammam, who was moderately injured when his newly built home was hit by an artillery shell. And as the man was being transferred to hospital in a Red Crescent ambulance, the ambulance itself came under heavy machine-gun fire. One bullet hit Abu Tammam in the head, killing him instantly.

Earlier, on 6 May, a large Israeli army force made yet another incursion into the Palestinian town of Beit Jala, killing 45-year-old Muhammed I’bayat.

The incursion, which was preceded by wanton bombardment of residential homes, was half-heartedly criticized by the Bush administration which issued the same platitudinous remarks about the need for “self-restraint on both sides.”

A stronger censure, however, came from the European Union, which reportedly warned Israel that its extensive economic ties with Europe would suffer if the invading force didn’t pull out immediately.

The force eventually withdrew, but not before utterly destroying and ravaging many buildings and residential homes.

Meanwhile, Israeli assassins carried out another assassination in Bethlehem on 5 May, this time targeting a former Islamic Jihad activist whom the occupation authorities accused of “carrying out hostile activities.”

According to an eyewitness, the undercover “soldiers”, disguised as Palestinians, riddled Ahmed Khalil Asad, 38, with more than 20 bullets, while he was standing outside his grocery store.

A few hours earlier, four other Palestinians in the Jenin area narrowly escaped another assassination attempt by the Israeli army. One of the four was seriously wounded, while the other three were lightly wounded.

In addition to the killing rampage, the Israeli army also kept up its characteristic sinister practice of destroying Palestinian homes, a diabolically racist measure aimed at tormenting Palestinians and checking their demographic growth.

On 7 May, the Israeli army demolished another home at the Beit Hanina neighbourhood, just north of Jerusalem, ostensibly to use the site of the demolished home to build a villa for a Jewish immigrant family.

The home is added to more than 400 Arab homes the Israeli army has callously destroyed since the outbreak of the Palestinian intifada more than seven months ago.

More homes are reported slated for destruction, particularly in and around East Jerusalem, where the Sharon government seems intent on creating more irreversible facts on the ground that would make the return of the occupied city to the Palestinians impossible.

Terror creates desperation
Israel’s overwhelming terror against Palestinians, which most Palestinians do not hesitate to compare with the Nazi reign of terror, is devastating to the psychological health of ordinary civilians, especially children and women.

This very real terror, which often transcends reality, recently caused a Palestinian man from the Shujaiyya neighbourhood, east of Gaza, to embark on a “collective martyrdom” to highlight the increasingly unbearable nightmare in the Gaza Strip.

Eyewitnesses said Ziad Hajaj, 36, placed his two children on the hood of his car and then drove with his wife toward the Jewish settlement of Kfar Dorom, with the intention getting killed, in order to “get rid of this life of misery and humiliation.”

Palestinian security men in the area sought unsuccessfully to convince the distraught man to reconsider his decision.

For their part, Israeli soldiers began pointing their guns, ready to pull the trigger.

At the very last moment, however, a representative of the Red Cross arrived at the scene and was able to calm down the man and get him away.

The unprecedented level of terror the Israeli army is using against the Palestinian people is reminiscent of the Palestinian Nakba or catastrophe of 1948.

Today, 53 years later, many Israeli politicians, including some occupying key positions in Ariel Sharon’s government, make no secret of their desire that the present Israeli onslaught against the Palestinians will eventually induce another mass expulsion of the Palestinians from their homeland, a sort of another Nakba.

F-16s against stones and a few old rifles

Occupied Jerusalem – Special to PT

On Friday, 18 May, in a brazen display of arrogance of power, the Israeli government of certified war criminal Ariel Sharon used the second deadliest weapon at its disposal (after its sizeable nuclear arsenal) to rain death on Palestinian towns in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, ostensibly in the hope of coercing the Palestinians to end their intifada and succumb to the Zionist will.

Sharon met with his “inner security cabinet,” which ironically included Nobel Peace Prize winner Shimon Peres and War Minister Benyamin Benalizer, and shortly afterwards, the three concluded by ordering a devastating air-strike at “targets” in several Palestinian towns in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, using F-16 fighter-bombers, considered as “Israel’s long strategic arm.”

And by 5:30 p.m. Palestine time, the lethal combat aircraft dropped munitions as large as 250 kilograms on a prison, a police dormitory, and TV relay stations in Nablus, Tulkarm and Ramallah as well as Gaza.

By midnight, paramedics, rescue workers and ordinary Palestinians recovered 11 bodies, some badly incinerated and mutilated, from under the rubble, often using their bare hands and primitive tools.

More than a hundred people—mostly civilians—were injured, ten critically, and over ten buildings were bombed out.

The Israeli army declared that the devastating strike on the Old Nablus Prison was aimed at “liquidating” a Hamas activist who had killed two Israeli soldiers during an aborted operation to kill him at his village of Asira Shamailya near Nablus.

Mahmoud Abu Hannoud was in the targeted building, but he miraculously escaped death, suffering only minor scratches and cuts.

“What kind of a State sends F-16 fighter jets to chase a single individual,” remarked Abu Hannoud’s father, who criticized the PA for keeping his son in jail.

So far, the PA claimed that Abu Hannoud was being kept in jail for his own protection from a possible Israeli assassination attempt on his life.

However, the bombing of the prison by F-16 bombers proved that the PA was right and justified in releasing the rest of the Hamas fighters and activists from its custody if only to allow Israel to murder them en masse.

Another massacre
If things are to be called by their real name, the bombing was no less than a massacre in terms of its devastating effect, the means used to inflict the resulting death and destruction, and also in the aforethought intent to spill as much Palestinian blood as possible.

Indeed, a few minutes before the strike at the Central Prison building in Nablus, which also houses a police squad in charge of maintaining law and order, Israeli occupation troops stationed on a hilltop overlooking the building barraged the area with machine-gun fire, apparently for the purpose of keeping as many people as possible confined inside the building in order to be killed in the imminent bombing that followed.

In other words, the Israeli rationale was like this: those who won’t be killed in the machine-gun strafing will be killed by the hell about to descend from above.

In Ramallah, other F-16 jets attacked two Force-17 posts, leaving one dead and injuring six others, one seriously who died two days later.

And in Gaza, the same type of aircraft dropped laser-guided bombs on the Ansar Security post, in the vicinity of Yasser Arafat’s headquarters, reducing the premises to rubble.

Initially, Israeli officials bragged about the use of their awesome air power against an essentially unarmed population, saying the Palestinians “should realize how powerful we are and what we are capable of doing to them.”

“Israel will go to any extent to defend Israelis,” a defiant Sharon said in a tone reminiscent of his gleeful mood following the Sabra and Shatilla massacres in 1982.

The Sharon government sought to justify its use of fighter jets, saying it was the “appropriate response” for the martyrdom operation in the coastal town of Netanya shortly before noon on Friday, 18 May.

The attack, carried out by a 20-year-old Palestinian from the town of Tulkarm, a member of the Hamas military wing, was in retaliation for the callous murder on 14 May by Israeli occupation troops of five Palestinian civilian policemen at a guard post south of Ramallah, as well as the murderous killing of four-month-old Iman Hijjo in the Gaza Strip on 7 May as a result of shrapnel by an Israeli artillery shell.

The martyr-bomber, later identified as Mahmoud Marmash, said in a pre-recorded message released after the attack that he did what he did as a reaction to overwhelming Israeli oppression which he could not withstand.

He pointed out, passionately, that he was deeply moved and shocked by the killing of Hijjo and the cold-blooded murder of the five policemen.

Five Jewish settlers were killed in the Netanya bombing, but the psychological effects and fallout on the Israeli Jewish population was much stronger.

Indeed, according to an opinion survey conducted by the mass Hebrew paper Yediot Ahranot, nearly two thirds of the adult Zionist population said they were more worried now about their personal security than they were when Sharon came to power three months ago.

This is roughly the same percentage of Israelis who elected Sharon on 6 February in the hope that he would restore security and “fight terror.”

In light of the bombing in Netanya, and the continued Palestinian resistance to Israel’s brutal, even criminal, onslaught in the West Bank, Sharon has so far failed to fulfil his pre-election promises, which explains his overkill policy toward the Palestinians.

Hence, it is widely expected that Sharon will continue to be active in killing defenceless Palestinian civilians and lightly armed Palestinian policemen in order to cover up his failure to stop the uprising and restore “security”, and also to appease and placate the hard-core, right-wing extremists, particularly the settlers and their supporters, who ostensibly would continue to give him the benefit of the doubt as long as Palestinian blood is being spilled and as long as he continues to torment the Palestinians.

An indication to that effect was the attempted assassination on 20 May of the Preventive Security Chief in the West Bank, Colonel Jebril Rajoub.

Israeli tanks stationed at the settlement of Psigot fired several shells at Rajoub’s villa in al-Bira, injuring five of his bodyguards and badly damaging the luxurious house.

Israeli Defence Minister Benyamin Benalizer claimed the shelling was not intentional, but a high-ranking officer in the Israeli army said the army would bombard the place again “whenever it deemed that [action] necessary.”

Rajoub is the man on whom Israel depends much in reviving security coordination with Israel and, more importantly, in reining in Islamist fighters opposed to the Oslo Accords.

Following the shelling, which nearly cost him his life, Rajoub described Israeli political and military leaders as a “band of street criminals and gangsters.”

Eight million Palestinians reaffirm commitment to Palestine

Occupied Jerusalem – Special to PT

An estimated eight million Palestinians at home and in the Diaspora observed on 15 May the 53rd anniversary of the Palestinian Nakba, when Zionist Jews seized Palestine and expelled the bulk of its people from their ancestral homeland.

Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians, young and old, men and women, marched in the streets of the West Bank and Gaza Strip carrying placards and shouting slogans affirming the right of return for some four million Palestinian refugees eager to return to their native hometowns and villages in what is now Israel.

“No right of return, no peace,” read one large placard in Ramallah. “Denial of right of return means victory for racism and apartheid,” read another.

At noon local time, as sirens blared, Palestinians observed three minutes of silence during which verses from the Holy Qur’an were recited in honour of the more than 200,000 Palestinians who have lost their lives defending their country.

Afterwards, Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat delivered a passionate speech, carried live on radio and television, castigating Israel for trying to “kill justice with tanks, rockets and helicopter gun-ships.” He criticized the international community for its “hypocrisy, double standards and silence in the face of overwhelming oppression of Palestinians.”

“They are trying to falsify history and kill justice with artillery, tanks and helicopter gun-ships, but they shall not succeed, for we have the truth on our side, and justice shall eventually prevail.” The Palestinian leader appealed to the world community to “wake up from its enduring dormancy to see the wanton acts of murder and destruction being perpetrated against our people.”

He asked bitterly: “Don’t our people have the right to be free like the rest of humanity?”

Arafat stressed that a just and durable peace in the region would not be achieved without total Israeli withdrawal from all the territories occupied in 1967, including al-Quds al-Sharif, or East Jerusalem, as well as the implementation of the right of return for Palestinian refugees in accordance with United Nations resolution 194.

Arafat then appealed to Palestinians to withstand Israeli oppression and persecution. “Have you faith and more faith, patience and more patience, an hour’s extra patience could make the difference between victory and defeat,” Arafat said, quoting one of Prophet Mohammed’s hadiths (sayings).

Palestinian poet Mahmoud Darwish, speaking briefly after Arafat, said the “makers of the Nakba have failed to break our will,” arguing that “the Intifada of yesterday, today and tomorrow is a sublime expression of rejection of modern-day slavery.”

Israel responded to the largely peaceful demonstrations and marches with the habitual rubber-coated bullets and live ammunition. Indeed, trigger-happy Israeli soldiers opened fire, often indiscriminately, on demonstrators in Hebron, Ramallah, Tulkarm and Nablus, injuring as many as 100 people, including minors and children.

In the Gaza Strip, Israeli army bullets killed two more Palestinians, one of them, 35-year-old Abdul Hakim al-Mana’amah, an associate of Hamas leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin.

Earlier, the Israeli occupation army carried out some of the most barbaric acts of State-sponsored-terrorism against unprotected Palestinian civilians and the lightly armed Palestinian police force.

At 2 a.m. on 14 May, an Israeli force murdered, in cold blood, five Palestinian policemen at a guard-post near Beitunya, south of Ramallah. Palestinians viewed the killing as an especially treacherous act, as the five victims were assigned to liaise with their Israeli security counterparts in the area.

“This is a vile and unethical crime, and Israel will not get away with it,” said an angry Arafat, pointing out that the victims’ guard post was established in coordination with the Israeli army.

Haj Ismael Jaber, commander of the Palestinian Authority (PA) police force in the West Bank, stressed the perfidious nature of the Israeli attack. “The whole thing was unexpected; it never occurred to us that the Israelis would murder the very people with whom they liaise and coordinate on a daily basis.” He added indignantly, “This is a massacre, a real massacre, except in this case, policemen are killed.”

The Israeli army did not comment on the incident, underscoring the almost total callousness toward the Palestinians. A terse statement carried by Israeli State-run radio only spoke of Israeli occupation soldiers spotting moving objects south of Ramallah, opening fire and killing them.

However, several Israeli officials, including War Minister Benyamin Benalizer, admitted the Palestinian policemen were killed by mistake, a testimony to the trigger-happy approach Israeli army terrorists adopt toward Palestinians.

The Israeli army killed two more Gazans and injured 20 others—among them a child who sustained serious wounds—on 14 May when it barraged Rafah with machine-gun and artillery fire. The murder of the seven Gazans in Beitunya and Gaza came only a few hours after Israeli tanks, helicopter gun-ships and navy gunboats pounded several neighbourhoods throughout the Strip, injuring many civilians and causing widespread destruction.

On 12 May, Israeli helicopters fired several missiles at a car outside PA headquarters in Jenin, killing two Palestinians—a Fatah activist and a policeman.

The two men killed, who were not the intended targets of the assassination operation, join 50 other Palestinians murdered by the Israeli army in a manner reflecting both Israel’s technological edge and its arrogance.

Meanwhile, the Israeli army continues to destroy vast stretches of Palestinian orchards and vineyards on a daily basis.

In an article published on 13 May in London’s Observer newspaper, Lord Ian Gilmour, who toured the Gaza Strip recently, referred to this wanton and widespread vandalism: “Israelis used to boast that they had made the desert bloom; now they can boast they have turned previously blooming Palestinian land into a desert.”

Abu Lughud dies at 72

Occupied Jerusalem - Ibrahim Abu Lughud, the prominent Palestinian author, scholar and Political Scientist, died in Ramallah on 24 May at the age of 72 after a long battle with illness.

Abu Lughud was born and reared at the Ajami neighbourhood in Yaffa into an affluent Palestinian family, and when the Zionist State of Israel was created, his family was expelled at gunpoint, like the bulk of the Palestinian people.

In 1949, he travelled to the United States where he obtained several academic degrees, including a Ph.D. in International Relations in 1957.

Abu Lughud remained devoted to the national Palestinian cause despite the lure of life in the United States.

In his numerous lectures, articles and works, he demonstrated an unflinching faith in the justice of the Palestinian cause and the eventuality of victory over Zionist apartheid.

In 1992, he returned to Palestine where he said he wanted to die and be buried.
