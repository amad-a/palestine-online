Sheikh Yassin receives a hero's welcome in Gaza;

says Palestinians are peace-seekers

Amman- Special to PT

Following a call by King Hussein of Jordan to Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, Sheikh Ahmad Yassin, the 61-year-old Palestinian founder of the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, arrived in Amman at King Hussein Medical Centre on 1 October after having spent eight years in Israeli jails. He received an official welcome by King Hussein and senior Jordanian officials, including Prime Minister Abdul Salam Majali.

The saga of Sheikh Yassin, who was sentenced by an Israeli court to life imprisonment plus 15 years for his role in anti-Israel attacks, followed the assassination attempt by two Israeli Mossad agents of Hamas politburo chief Khaled Misha'al.

Mr. Misha'al escaped the assassination attempt but not before a "chemical substance" was injected into his left ear which caused "dizziness, continuous vomiting and imbalance" immediately after the attack.

Following the attack against Mr. Misha'al, King Hussein phoned the Israeli premier and threatened him that if Mr. Misha'al dies, Jordan would cut diplomatic ties with Israel.

Netanyahu, accompanied by his Defence Minister Yitzhak Mordechai and Infrastructure Minister Ariel Sharon, arrived in Amman on 29 September, but the King refused to meet them and sent his brother Crown Prince Hassan instead.

During the extensive talks between Jordanian and Israeli officials, the two sides agreed to release the two Israeli Mossad agents involved in the attack against Mr. Misha'al in exchange for the release of Sheikh Yassin and tens of Jordanian and Palestinian prisoners in Israeli jails.

"I am very pleased and happy with the swift reaction [and for] the fact that Sheikh Ahmad Yassin arrived safely here. Now he is resting and he receives the best care we can afford him. This is the least we can do and I am happy to meet him for the first time," the King said following the arrival of Sheikh Yassin at the King Hussein Medical Centre in the Jordanian capital.

A statement by the Israeli army said that "the release of Sheikh Yassin was declared at the recommendation of Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu who answered the call of King Hussein."

Addressing his family, supporters and journalists in the Gaza Strip from the Hussein Medical Centre, Sheikh Yassin greeted them and said that he would be returning in the near future and that his stopover in Jordan was merely for treatment.

A statement by Hamas said that the Sheikh's arrival in Amman followed a written pledge, signed by the commander of the Israeli army's southern region, that he would be allowed to return to the Gaza Strip and that his arrival in Jordan is a "release" and not a "deportation".

During his short stay in Amman, Sheikh Yassin received treatment and underwent some operations for his eye and ear.

Sheikh Yassin, who established Hamas and its military wing Izzedin Qassam Brigades in 1987 at the very beginning of the intifadah in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, was visited by the head of the Palestinian Authority Yasser Arafat and other senior Palestinian officials.

The Hamas leader, who is completely paralyzed and blind in his right eye as a result of the torture he received while a prisoner in Israeli jails, was accompanied by Raed Balboul, who was taking care of the old man during his imprisonment.

Sheikh Yassin also suffers from weakness of sight in his left eye, a loss of hearing in his right ear and weak hearing in his left ear.

Prior to his departure from Amman to the Gaza Strip aboard King Hussein's military helicopter, Sheikh Yassin held a press conference and said that Hamas will not abandon armed struggle against Israel until Israeli occupation is over.

"We tell the whole world that we are peace-seekers. We love peace, and we call upon them (the Israelis) to live in peace with us. We call upon (the world) to help us in regaining our rights in a peaceful way. But if that is not possible, we shall not accept the (Israeli) occupation of our land," Sheikh Yassin told reporters.

Speaking from his wheelchair, the Palestinian leader said that "the halt of military attacks (against Israel) is linked with the departure of the occupation."

The Hamas leader said that his movement will not allow any differences in the relationship with the Palestinian Authority to turn into a confrontation between the two sides. "The PA is part of the Palestinian people. We shall remain in one trench," he added.

"We tell the Israeli Prime Minister that power will not last on his side. The weak will not remain weak. He [Netanyahu] has to read history and look into the future. They [Israelis] have to recognize our rights in our homeland. The future is for our and their children," Sheikh Yassin told a crowd of Jordanian and foreign reporters a few minutes before he boarded a military helicopter headed back to the Gaza Strip.

Asked his opinion on the Jordanian government decision to release the two Mossad agents who were involved in the assassination attempt against Mr. Misha'al, Sheikh Yassin declined to comment.

The Director of King Hussein Medical Centre, Maj. Gen. Adel Shraideh said that Sheikh Yassin would be accompanied by two physiotherapists, who will give more treatment and who will remain in the Gaza Strip for a few weeks.

Gen. Shraideh added that Jordanian doctors had performed surgery in the Sheikh's left eye and that his health was good. He added that Sheikh Yassin was provided with a "hearing device" to improve his hearing ability.

Sheikh Yassin received a hero's welcome upon his arrival in Gaza. Several thousand Hamas activists as well as senior PA officials took part in the rally. He was then taken into the Gaza stadium where he addressed the crowd of over 30,000 Palestinians celebrating his return to his homeland.

Once there, Sheikh Yassin called for unity among Palestinians and for all Palestinian factions to work for the release of the 3,500 Palestinian prisoners from Israeli jails.

"I call on our strong people to stand as one body, with no difference between left and right, large and small," the Sheikh said to the crowd, who interrupted him several times with "Allahu Akbar" (God is Great).

"We are all one people and we will remain so despite the attempt of our enemies [to divide us]," he added.

"On this day, Hamas represents a blow against the security of Israel. We will continue our holy struggle until Palestine is liberated," Gazan Hamas leader Abdul Aziz Rantisi told the crowd.

At the stadium entrance, Hamas military wing, the Izzedin Qasam Brigades, stretched a banner which read "We renew our oath and loyalty to the holy warrior Sheikh Ahmad Yassin."

Once at the stadium, Sheikh Yassin had to be lifted up in his wheelchair by bodyguards to wade through the crowds to the stage. After the rally, hundreds of Hamas activists gathered near Sheikh Yassin's one-storey residence in the Sabra Quarter, and many of them lined up to kiss the Sheikh's cheeks and receive his blessing.

Zionist President grants amnesty to Jewish murderers

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem- Israeli president Ezer Weisman has decided to grant amnesty to several Jewish terrorists and convicted murderers, ostensibly to appease right-wing extremists who were dismayed by the freeing on 1 October of Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yasin.

According to Israeli sources, Weisman decided to issue a ìfull pardonî for two Israeli Jews convicted of murdering Palestinians.

A spokesman for the Israeli president said Weisman pardoned murderer Zeiv Wolf and Girshon Irchcovitc who in 1992 tossed a grenade into a crowded East Jerusalem market, killing a 72-year-old Palestinian merchant, Abdul Razzak Idkedek.

Moreover, the two Kahane Hay terrorists had carried out a number of terrorist attacks on defenseless Palestinians in Hebron and Jerusalemís old town.

A third terrorist belonging to the same cell had earlier been released, also by a presidential decree.

In addition to pardoning the two terrorist murderers, Weisman also decided to reduce the sentence terms of three other Jewish murderers convicted of killing Palestinians.

The three murderers include Yoram Skolnick who in 1993 murdered a Palestinian, Musa Abu Sabha, at the village of Yatta near Hebron while the latter was in fetters; and Nir Afroni who murdered a Palestinian gas station worker in Jerusalem; and Nahshon Wolf who killed an Arab woman near the settlement of Kiryat Arbaía near Hebron in 1991.

Observers point out that pardoning Jewish murderers of Palestinians is an ìestablished traditionî in Israel.

Haim Herzog, the former Israeli president who died earlier this year, pardoned more than 10 Israeli terrorists convicted of murdering Palestinians including two students at the University of Hebron in 1983.

Five Israeli soldiers try sadistic urges on a Palestinian worker

Occupied Jerusalem - Five Israeli soldiers on 18 October attacked and nearly murdered a Palestinian labourer near an Israeli army roadblock in Jerusalem.

The worker, Ahmed Shaker Shakarneh, from the village of Nahaline near Bethlehem, said he regained consciousness at the Jerusalem Hadassa hospital after sustaining serious head wounds resulting from what he called ìsadistic tortureî at the hands of five Israeli soldiers.

On Thursday 16 October, a Beitshemesh police officer entrusted the five border policemen to take Shakarneh to the closest Israeli army roadblock along the Green Line.

Instead, the five soldiers took the Palestinian labourer, who had been collecting medicinal herbs from the mountainous terrain nearby, to a remote and uninhabited spot where they ìstarted a party of beating and of torturing.î

ìThey started beating with their fists and kicking me with their army boots until I nearly lost consciousness. Then they started hurling stones at my head from a short distance, causing a big bleeding,î Shakarneh said.

ìThen one of them opened up my mouth and put lots of dirt in it, while the other four were laughing hysterically.î

Shakarneh said he then was able to walk to the main street, 200 metres away, where he found an Arab driver who drove him to hospital.

Palestinian sources stress that acts of savagery by Israeli soldiers against Palestinian labourers are daily occurrences and that most of the time they pass unnoticed by the world because they happen out of sight of the media.

Israeli landmines kill children in Palestine and Lebanon

By Steve Sozby

International charters mean little to a poor shepherd boy in a remote area of the West Bank, unless that boy happens to step on a buried landmine.

Thirteen-year-old Jibreel Adra was tending his sheep two years ago when he stepped on an Israeli landmine and lost parts of both feet. He knows nothing about the promise of Clinton to ban the use of "Anti-personnel Mines" (APMs) or the subsequent refusal to carry through with that promise by the American president in September. However, it will be a lasting memory in the minds of millions of people who feel that Clinton again proved that he is more concerned with his relations with the U.S. military-industrial complex than he is with the welfare of innocent children like Jibreel.

Tens of thousands of civilians suffer injury and death every year from APMs, though they are less and less strategic in their use as effective military devices. According to the United Nations Development Programme's "Human Development Report, 1994", 90% of all war casualties at the beginning of the century were combatants. Now, 90% of war victims are civilians, and landmines play an important role in generating that ugly figure.

In the Middle East, landmines continue to take a deadly toll on innocent civilians. In Lebanon, there are an estimated 200,000 landmines of American, Russian, Israeli and Chinese origin, either left over from the civil war, or as part of the ongoing war in the south. Since 1991, landmines have killed 189 people, and another 212 have been injured. The worst concentration is in the western Bekka region, the scene of many battles between Syrian and Israeli forces.

Ten-year-old Hassan Mardini was out with his father hunting for birds in an area populated by the Druze people in the mountains over Beirut when he stepped on a landmine. He lost his right leg and was eventually treated in the U.S. and fit with an artificial limb all with the help of an American relief group called the Palestine Children's Relief Fund.

In Palestine, every week or so an Arab civilian is injured by a landmine, either an old one remaining from the Jordanian army that had been in the West Bank until June 1967, or an Israeli device used as part of a protection of their bases or settlements.

Jibreel Adra was merely tending sheep in an area that was used by the Israeli military when he stepped on an APM. "Something exploded at my feet and I fell to the ground," recalls the boy from Yatta, a village near Hebron in the West Bank. He is currently in Chicago for surgery courtesy of the Palestine Children's Relief Fund. The APM took parts of both feet and the boy now faces several painful surgeries.

Palestinian Jibreel Adra and Lebanese Hassan Mardini are lucky to be alive. Most children who step on landmines are killed, as they are often too young to lose the amount of blood that landmines take from children. Dozens of other Arab children who have stepped on APMs in Lebanon and Palestine are not as lucky.

In an effort to lessen the burden of their forces occupying south Lebanon, the Israelis have planted thousands of landmines on Lebanese soil. Quite frequently, the victims of these landmines are not Lebanese resistance fighters, but rather civilians who happen to wander in harm's way. The Israelis clearly are not interested in seeing a ban on the use of APMs, as it is an important part of their "defense" strategy.

In May, U.S. President Clinton announced that he was "launching an international effort to ban anti-personnel landmines." A few months later, as that ban was close to becoming a reality, the U.S. vetoed a resolution that would have eliminated these deadly devices from the face of the earth. For the Arab civilian populations who are the victims of Israeli aggression in Lebanon and Palestine, the APMs are just another weapon used to subjugate their efforts to be free from occupation. Eliminating the APMs under the soil may reduce the number of innocent Arab victims, but it will not remove the source of the problem in the Middle East, which is the Israeli refusal to acknowledge the rights of the Palestinian and Lebanese people to live in freedom and with their own legitimate government.

Hamas accuses Rabbis of misleading Ulama

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) has accused Israel's High Rabbinate of misleading Muslim Ulama (religious scholars) by urging them to issue fatwas or religious edicts against Palestinian Islamists' human-bomb attacks on Israeli targets.

"The rabbis are being morally selective; they want our Ulama to condemn violence against Israelis without committing themselves to do likewise vis-¦-vis Israeli-Jewish violence and terrorism against our civilians," said Hamas Gaza leader, Dr. Abdulaziz al Rantisi.

On 14 October, Hamas mentor and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin received Rabbi Menhame Froman of the settlement of Tekoa near Bethlehem who reportedly sought to hand him a letter from Chief Sephardi Rabbi Bakshi-Doron asking Yassin to denounce violence. Yassin refused to accept the letter, saying its proper address is Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat.

The letter read "we have to do whatever we can to stop violence and the only way to do that is through dialogue. I am hopeful that Sheikh Yassin can solve this problem. I call on him as a believer in God." The letter, however, made no mention of the Israeli occupation of Arab-Muslim land or of the daily torment meted out on the Palestinians by the Israeli army and settlers. Yassin told Froman that Hamas would agree to a cease-fire if Israel agreed to pull out of the occupied territories. Rantisi, considered a hard-liner within Hamas, said he was disquieted by "the moral inconsistency of Jewish religious leaders."

"We never hear them condemn the barbaric acts of the Israeli army in the West Bank. Their silence is very frustrating and doesn't augur well for future contacts between them and us," he said. Rantisi added that it would be very unlikely that Rabbi Bakshi-Doron or other rabbis would succeed in obtaining fatwas from the Sheikh of al Azhar repudiating Palestinian resistance against oppression and occupation. "Jihad against oppression and injustice is well-established in the Qur'an, and nobody can undo this," he added.

Bakshi-Doron announced on 14 October that the Sheikh of al Azhar, Sheikh Sayed Tantawi, had sent him a letter denouncing "religiously-motivated terror."

However, a spokesman for Tantawi was reported as saying that "the Sheikh's remarks were distorted and taken out of context." Moreover, the spokesman said the Israeli ambassador to Egypt, Zvi Mazal, omitted "all remarks (made by Tantawi) pertaining to the Israeli occupation and its causative relation to all forms of Palestinian resistance."

Zionists destroy more Palestinian homes

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

In a flagrant measure that flies in the face of the Palestinian Authority and the international community, the Israeli army destroyed two more Palestinian homes at the village of Beit Ummar near Hebron.

The two houses, belonging to Mahmud Abu Dayya and his son Khaled, were destroyed by the Israeli army on 14 October under the pretext that they were built without proper licensing.

Palestinian eyewitnesses said the Israeli army declared the vicinity where the two homes are located a closed military zone before bulldozers began to pulverize them in the full view of crying women and children who have been made homeless thanks to the callous Israeli act.

Mahmud abu Dayya, distraught by the demolition of his home which sheltered his family of 9 children, said the Israelis "were behaving no different than the Nazis...the only difference is the name," he said.

Asked why he had not obtained a building license, abu Dayya said "I tried to obtain a license a hundred times and every time I went to the Building and Zoning office I was told I couldn't get a license."

Palestinian sources at Beit Ummar also revealed that the Israeli army is planning to destroy the homes of as many as 30 Palestinian families for alleged licensing violations.

Palestinians and human rights activists contend that the Israeli policy of home-demolition is considered by far the most callous and evil manifestations of the Israeli occupation of Arab land.

Last month, Hamas mentor and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin cited "home-demolition" as one of the chief factors behind Palestinian Islamists' martyrdom operations against Jewish targets.

Yassin said Hamas would reconsider attacks on civilian Jewish targets if and when Israel desisted from assaulting Palestinian civilian targets, including home demolition.

Interestingly, U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright urged, though half-heartedly, Israel to "stop unilateral acts", a reference to, inter alia, demolishing Arab homes in the West Bank.

Habibullah: Visiting Palestinian prisoners in Israeli jails an agony

Nablus (West Bank)- The head of the Palestinian Prisoner's Friends Society, Ahmad Habibullah, described visiting the Palestinian prisoners and detainees in Israeli jails by their families as a journey of agony highlighted by a painful scene of children and the aged waiting at the jails' gates. Mr. Habibullah said he met with scores of detainees' relatives outside Shatta jail where he listened to and experienced their sufferings during their visit to the jail. He added that he was touched by one young girl, Duíaa, the daughter of Palestinian detainee Mohammed Abed Rabbu of Sur Baher (near Occupied Jerusalem), when she started crying outside the jail and saying "I want to see dadÖ I want to see him and kiss him."

The Palestinian official pointed out that the families of the detainees suffer from difficulties and constraints on getting permits to visit their detainees. They are also forced to wait for long hours outside the jails until they are allowed to go in but not without the humiliating inspection measures.

Habibullah added that when they are allowed in after all their tiring efforts, they are given only a short visit.

110 Israeli soldiers killed in Lebanon

Occupied Jerusalem- Israeli military sources said 110 Israeli army officers and soldiers have been killed in attacks by the Lebanese Islamic militants in South Lebanon since 1985. They pointed out that 37 of them have been killed since the beginning of this year alone. The sources said the toll does not include 73 army soldiers who were killed last February in a crash north of Occupied Palestine between two Israeli helicopters that were in their way to implement a special operation in South Lebanon. The last two months are considered the worst in the history of the Israeli army since its 1985 retreat to the so-called buffer zone in South Lebanon as far as the death toll is concerned, the sources said. They explained that last September was a "black" month, especially for the occupation troops in South Lebanon since as many as 17 soldiers were killed by the Lebanese resistance groups.

Jewish settlement endangers future of Palestinian Cause

Abu Dhabi- A Palestinian official said the unprecedented rates of confiscation of Palestinian lands in the Occupied Territories by Israel seriously threaten the future of the peace in the Middle East. The PA's Minister of Housing Abdel Rahman Hamad told the UAE's Ittihad Arabic daily that the confiscation of lands that has increased since the coming to power of Prime Minister Netanyahu in June 1996 does not only threaten to sabotage the peace process only but also to liquidate the Palestinian Cause which is the core of the Arab-Israeli conflict. He added that the Israeli occupation authorities have confiscated about 192,000 dunams of Palestinian lands in the West Bank during this year alone compared to 30,000 dunams that have been confiscated annually since the Madrid Conference in 1991.

Palestinian organization calls on Israel to release 2 aged prisoners

Occupied Jerusalem- The Palestinian International Solidarity for Human Rights (ISHR) called on the Israeli occupation authorities to release two detainees, a Palestinian and an Egyptian, after spending more than 20 years in Israeli jails. Lawyer Fares Abu Hassan of the ISHR said Mohammed Nu'erat, 60, a Palestinian from Maithalon located north of the West Bank, has been detained for more than 23 years and suffers from back pain and loss of memory. He was sentenced by a military court to life imprisonment on charges of belonging to a PLO military group. Abu Hassan added that Mahmoud Sawarkeh, 67, is an Egyptian soldier who was arrested by the Israeli authorities in 1977 and sentenced to four consecutive life imprisonments. ìSawarkeh suffers from various diseases especially in the stomach,î Abu Hassan said, adding that the release of Sheikh Ahmad Yassin should open the door for the release of the aged and ailing Palestinian and Arab prisoners.
