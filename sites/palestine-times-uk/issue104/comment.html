Will the PA red lines remain red?

The Palestinian Authority of Yasser Arafat never misses an opportunity to emphasize the inviolability of the so-called Palestinian national red lines.

These include full Israeli evacuation from the Palestinian territories captured in the 1967 war (including first and foremost al-Quds al-Sharif [Jerusalem]), the dismantling of Jewish settlements, and guaranteeing the estimated four million Palestinian refugees their inalienable right to return to their ancestral homeland in accordance with United Nations resolution 194.

Indeed, this is the very minimum Palestinian political forces and masses could accept under all circumstances. No Palestinian movement or faction, including the PA, never voiced willingness that it will accept anything less.

On the contrary, there are so many Palestinians, Arabs and Muslims who rightly won’t accept even these red lines on the ground that stopping at the 1967 armistice line would effectively lend legitimacy to the greatest theft in history, that being the usurpation of the bulk of Palestine by Zionist gangs and the establishment of the misbegotten State of Israel.

Nonetheless, many Palestinians have come to harbour certain fears of late that Arafat might be willing eventually to cross the above-mentioned red lines, either under American and Israeli pressure, or because he wants to have a “State” at any expense.

Indeed, some Palestinian intellectuals, such as the signatories to the “Petition of 20” in late November, have openly sounded the bell of alarm, and a far greater number of anxious but suppressed ordinary people seem no longer willing to give Arafat the benefit of the doubt.

In short, Palestinians are extremely apprehensive that the Chairman, whose mental capacity has deteriorated substantially in the last few months, might sign anything the White House would ask him to sign, and then he could justify this on the ground that “something is better than nothing” and “we had no choice”…the same justifications he voiced when he secretly signed the scandalous Oslo Agreement nearly seven years ago.

In fact, there are certain indications suggesting that Arafat cannot be trusted not to betray Palestinian expectations and aspirations.

First, the dangerous statement made by President Clinton during Arafat’s latest visit to the White House on 20 January must be considered.

Clinton said the two sides (Israel and the PA) would have to make difficult decisions and hard choices for peace.

He added that “neither side should expect to attain 100% of their goals.”

Needless to say, a simple reading of this statement suggests that Clinton advocates a “compromise” between Israel and the PA on the West Bank, a sort of partitioning of the territory.

What is especially appalling about this is the fact that Arafat and his sycophantic ministers did not utter a single word in opposition to this dangerous statement, which practically dropped UN resolutions 242 and 338 as the legal reference to the peace process, substituting them with “the process.”

There is another source of anxiety in this regard, and that is the possibility that the scandalous concessions on Arafat’s part would be sold in a single package with another lesser, unattractive commodity, namely an independent Palestinian “State” on a small part of the West Bank—all for the purpose of confusing the people and creating a false sense of victory and euphoria.

This way, those who would stand against the betrayal of Palestinian rights would be labelled “enemies of the Palestinian State” and that “they should look at the half-full, not the half-empty part of the glass.”

In other words, the declaration of Palestinian Statehood would serve as a cover, or the cover, for the great sell-out.

But even if things turned out as such, the deception and false euphoria would not last for a long time, even if the United States—the sponsor of the process—threw its full weight behind Yasser Arafat.

Because then, Israel would have peace with Arafat, not with the Palestinian people. Surely, a peace as such would only last for as long as the Chairman would live.
