(March 2000)

Has The Truth Of Barak’s Peace Been Exposed?

The last five weeks have been nightmarish for the parties involved in the peace process after it suddenly entered a serious deadlock. For various reasons, the “tracks” game Barak played did not accord him the expected pay-off. And as a result, the negotiations on both the Syrian and the Lebanese tracks came to a halt almost simultaneously.

Well, perhaps some would see this halt as a temporary issue owing to the poor manner in which Barak handled the negotiation process. But there are others who would go as far as to say that the present Israeli regime is not very different from its predecessor and that the current peace negotiations may face problems similar to those which were faced by that of the Netanyahu regime as well as some new additions.

However, careful consideration of events connected with the negotiations reveals that this deadlock was not without a preface. Having declared his political manifesto theoretically, it was just a matter of time before Barak’s real agenda surfaced. It is now evident that Barak’s strongly worded election manifesto represented his real agenda and was not just mere electoral promises or sentimental slogans aimed at securing majority votes from the Israelis.

Evaporation of Barak’s Promises

According to official statistics announced by the Israeli Ministry of Housing and published in the Israeli newspapers on 21 February 2000, approximately 7,120 housing units have been constructed in the West Bank occupied territories since the beginning of Barak’s reign in 1999, as compared with 5,400 housing units during Netanyahu’s reign in 1998. Also, the Minister for Housing, Yitzhak Levy, has endorsed a package to construct 3,196 housing units, including 2,500 to be built in Jerusalem, saying that the strategic plan of his ministry stipulates building 50,000 housing units in the Jewish State and the West Bank’s occupied territories. This plan confirms that Barak, under the pretext of peace, is trying to fulfil what Netanyahu’s hard-line government was unable to accomplish.

Other statistics published by the Israeli Peace Now movement indicate an enormous rise in the number of settler Jews in the Palestinian territories. According to those statistics, the number of Jews is approximately 200,000, with the most recent increase of 35% beginning with the advent of the peace process.

Even though Barak claimed to have ordered the suspension of new settlement projects, estimated by some Israeli sources to be around 1,000, these very sources revealed that the Israeli government had ordered the building of 1,100 housing units and that this was approved before the order for the freeze was issued. In fact, the order for the freeze covers only a small portion of the settlement projects. With regard to the Abu Ghneim and Ra’s al-Amoud settlement projects, work has continued unabated with the tacit approval of the Barak government.

In sharp contrast to this fierce settlement activity, the peace process has made very little progress; redeployment of Israeli forces was made on only 12% of the West Bank area, and only 2% of this was handed over to the Palestinian self-rule Authority’s security and political control. As for the third phase of the redeployment, which is the most important phase as it provides for handing over 6.1% of territory (B) to (A) in accordance with the Sharm el-Sheikh interim agreement, this has yet to be implemented. Among other things, Israel released a number of criminals, including those who had completed their terms; opened the so-called “safe passage” of the south; gave the occupation forces the power to intercept or arrest any suspect; and imposed the use of “traffic passes” for using the safe-passage road, virtually making all decisions concerning its use unilaterally, except those issues concerning location and details of future safe passages which have been adjourned (by the Israelis) till negotiations that are yet to be held. For these reasons, Yasser Arafat described the negotiations as a waste of time. Other Palestinian officials are saying that Barak wants to sustain the peace negotiations merely to give the impression that the negotiations on the Palestinian track are progressing whereas in fact they are frozen.

Comments by Israeli press and analysts are confirming our saying that Barak was hesitant and reluctant in actual peace moves. One Uri Avneri said, “To judge Barak’s performance in just eight months would be premature since he still has four years at his disposal and could work wonders. This is possible, but the eight months are enough to judge his efficiency. All the cheques signed by him on the eve of his assuming power have bounced. On the Palestinian track, no single millimetre of progress was made. Barak’s tactics range from ignoring facts completely to desperate attempts to impose things. Thus, all deadlines have come to pass, and the only achievement made was the opening of one of the safe passages after a delay of five years. And this meant nothing at all to the people. The settlement activity and land confiscation are witnessing a growing trend—more than what Netanyahu did (Ma’ariv, 22 February 2000).”

Another individual, Amona Alon, said, “Most of those who hated Netanyahu can not recall today why they should prefer Barak to him. Right from the time he was elected to power, Barak’s desperate moves have exceeded those of his predecessor, just like air bubbles in a day care centre. If those who hated Netanyahu maintained the belief that there was a difference between Netanyahu and Barak, that belief has now eroded. Barak is following the footsteps of his predecessor and is doing an even better job. The striking evidence to this is the news issued yesterday about resumption of the building of roads in the West Bank (Yediot Ahranot, 23 February 2000).”

Barukh Qara confirms the rightist leanings of Barak’s government saying, “Barak’s rise to power has placed the fate of Jabal Abu Ghneim in fine darkness. Bulldozers and caterpillars have replaced the abusive rhetoric of Netanyahu. By now, five construction companies have already obtained permits to begin building. And considering the deteriorated condition of the buildings located on Abu Ghneim, coupled with the political clouds of the present situation, this project has become a success. Building activity is at its peak and sales are flourishing; 1,600 housing units have been marketed, out of a total project of 6,500 houses. The contractors and property dealers who were very scared prior to the elections are now jubilant.

“It is doubtful whether or not after a few years someone would still remember that Abu Ghneim was a symbol of the left-wing party’s struggle against Netanyahu’s government or whether anyone would still remember that it was the two regimes of the Labour Party that actually initiated that project and practically implemented it before and after Netanyahu (Ha’aritz, 6 February 2000).”

Failure of the “Tracks” Strategy

Since his election, Barak has given preference to the Syrian track and set aside July of 2000 as the deadline for pulling out his forces from southern Lebanon owing to the successive losses suffered by the Israelis. For this reason, the Israeli government preferred to reach mutual agreements with Syria and Lebanon respectively that will provide for its withdrawal, although since the beginning it did not rule out a unilateral withdrawal. The optimistic atmosphere that was prevailing in the region about peace following Barak’s election contributed to the resumption of peace talks between Israel, and each of Syria and Lebanon. The process, however, soon entered a deadlock as Israel vowed to change the agenda of the negotiations—beginning with security arrangements and normalization before conceding to a complete pull-out from the Golan Heights—a situation that provoked the Syrian President to order the cessation of the negotiations after his team returned from the U.S. According to Syrian sources, President Assad discovered that the Israelis were using delaying tactics and were reluctant to endorse a pledge to withdraw from the Golan. And it seems Barak is yet to secure from his coalition government the majority needed for a withdrawal from the Golan. This became evident after a draft bill was presented by the opposition Likud Party (backed by Shas, Mifdal and some other members in the coalition government) proposing that there should be more than 50% votes in favour of a referendum for the withdrawal from the Golan before it could be implemented. And that the voters must be registered voters and not only those who participated in the polls. This is an issue that will weaken the position of Palestinian voters residing in the territories occupied since 1948, who would have automatically voted for the withdrawal. Also, the success of this referendum will now depend on its acceptance by a two-thirds majority of the total number of voters given that the highest turn-out ever registered in Israeli elections is 84% of the registered voters.

Even though Barak did not mean to suspend all tracks of negotiations at once, his intransigence caused Syria and Palestine to abandon the talks; it is this same intransigence that caused the failure of his “tracks” game, though temporarily. In this tracks game, Barak concentrated on delaying a settlement on the Palestinian track while accelerating it on the Lebanese and Syrian tracks. Israel has increased its attacks on energy stations in Lebanon following a series of valiant operations launched by Lebanese Islamic Resistance forces against the enemy targets and against that of the Israeli-controlled Southern Lebanese Army. The “Nazist” statement that was made by David Levy, Israeli Foreign Minister, fuelled flames of wrath among the Arabs against the Zionist entity and soured the atmosphere of the peace negotiations to which a great deal of hope was attached when it was launched some few months ago.

Arab resurgence... but..!

Barak’s government and its policies have practically accomplished what those who understood this government better had previously warned the rest of us about drawing inferences as to the structure of the government and the statements Barak made when he first assumed power in the Zionist entity. It became evident that the hopes people pinned on Barak to revamp the peace process and thereafter realize a lasting peace settlement, to secure peace treaties with the concerned Arab States that would provide for the retrieval of the occupied territories of the Arabs and to agree to the sovereign Statehood of Palestine have turned out to be a nightmare—a dream that was never to be a reality, or, at the very least, a premature hope.

Barak, who pretended to be a peacemaker soon after his victory and who made many promises to push the peace process ahead, has differed in approach from his two predecessors: Rabin and Peres. Similarly, he differed from Likud leader Benyamin Netanyahu in that he does not stress too much on integrating the Jewish State with its Arab neighbourhood under peace treaties but rather he strives to please the U.S. and to strengthen his strategic alliance with it. He has succeeded in this as the U.S. administration has promised to back Israel’s political stance at international conferences. But its failure to meet the demands of the peace process, in particular the Syrian track up to now, has dwindled the hope of the Clinton-led U.S. administration to utilize an Israeli-Syrian Peace Treaty to strengthen its position in the upcoming U.S. presidential elections scheduled to be held this year. This pushes the U.S. administration to make additional efforts to bring the Syrian-Israeli negotiations back on track within the few remaining weeks in which the Clinton administration can still work before it becomes defective owing to engagement in preparation for the next election.

As for the Palestinian track, which is considered No. 2 on Barak’s scale of preference, there seems to be no intention whatsoever on the part of Israel to implement the provisions of the Sharm el-Sheikh accord (Wye River-2) as all the deadlines that were set with Israeli approval for its redeployment have expired. Israel continues to maintain that it has the right to decide the area of redeployment without consultation with the Palestinian side and continues to give preference to the Syrian track, signalling to the Palestinians that in order for them to join the peace process they must give more concessions; this is their way of forcing them to accept the settlement Israel envisages for the final status.

Against this background, the recent declaration made by the Central Committee that it would declare Palestinian Statehood by the end of the year 2000 constitutes a fresh move by Arafat to push the Israelis towards implementation of the remaining interim agreements and to reach a framework agreement on the final status that will include Palestinian Statehood.  Moreover, according to Israeli press sources, during his visit to Washington in February 2000, Arafat presented a document to Clinton visualizing Palestinian perceptions of the final status settlement in a bid to pressure the American-Israeli side into pursuing the Palestinian track in favour of the Palestinian perception (or vision).

It is clear from the above analysis that Barak has not been able to make equal progress on all three tracks of negotiation. Similarly, he has not been able to win the support of the Israeli people to enable him to sign peace treaties with Lebanon, Syria and the PA simultaneously—an issue that will, in future, cause numerous and various crises in all three tracks of negotiations.

In essence, the ultimate goals Israel wants to achieve from the negotiations do not differ much from one Israeli regime to another. The main goals are to end the Arab-Israeli conflict with the least price possible in return for political, economic and security hegemony over most of the Palestinian occupied territories; to secure military supremacy over its Arab neighbours; and to enhance its strategic alliance with the United States of America amidst continuous disintegration and disunity within the Arab and Islamic communities at the official level.

In view of this situation and the angry popular reaction by the Arab side to Barak’s threats against Lebanon, would this reality form a basis for a fresh Arab resurgence that will pave the way for a complete overhauling of the Arab stance and breaking the normalization drive with the Zionist enemy? Shall the coming days witness efforts to achieve unity among the Arabs once again and to adopt solid stances before the intransigence of Barak and his government? Or will the reaction of the Arabs at the official level calm down soon for American-Israeli offers (promises), incentives or, perhaps, threats, to give a fresh chance to Israel to take the rules of the negotiation game into its own hands once again and manipulate its results to the disadvantage of the Arab side?
