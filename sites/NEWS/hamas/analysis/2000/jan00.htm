(January 2000)

The resumption of the Syrian-Israeli negotiations—a dangerous
breakthrough regarding the peace process

The U.S. President’s announcement following the meeting between his Secretary of State and President Hafez Assad of Syria regarding the resumption of the Syrian-Israeli talks from the point they stopped at in 1996 was surprising after a media clash between the Syrians and the Israelis over the so-called “Rabin trust”. This “trust” was said to be given by the former Israeli Prime Minister Rabin to the U.S. Administration in which the former pledged to withdraw from the whole of the Syrian Golan Heights which the Israelis have been occupying since 1967.

The interpretation of the term “resuming talks from the point they had stopped at” had also threatened to abort attempts to take advantage of the atmosphere caused by the coming of Barak to power, which was welcomed by the Syrian President who commended Barak’s person.

The U.S. Administration seems to have succeeded in solving this dispute by leaving that phrase vague in order to enable both parties to interpret it in the way it suits them. Although the reason behind the resumption of the talks has not been announced as yet, Israeli Prime Minister Barak was keen to assert that he did not give any relinquishment to resume them. He also avoided any comment on his position towards the retreat of his troops to the borders of 4 June 1967.

Syria, however, kept silent about the pledges it received from the Israelis in order to return to the table of negotiations. But it is likely that Barak gave the U.S. Administration a verbal undertaking to withdraw from the whole of the Golan Heights with slight alterations at the borders.

The resumption of the negotiations reflects an Israeli, Syrian and American desire to make progress towards the achievement of a peace treaty. Barak’s government is keen to implement a promise it introduced to the new Knesset to withdraw from Lebanon by July 2000. This cannot happen without a peace treaty with Lebanon, which rejects this unless a similar peace treaty is reached between Israel and Syria, or unless Israel implements a unilateral withdrawal from Lebanon, an option that is very dangerous for the security of the Jewish State. Moreover, achieving a peace settlement on the Syrian and Lebanese tracks is considered less complicated for Israel than achieving a permanent settlement with the Palestinian Authority, which will have its position in negotiations weakened against the enhancement of Israel’s position because it will appear as a party that is eager for peace. Therefore, Israel’s political position will be strong at the international level. This will also oblige the United States and Europe to exert pressure on the Palestinian party to eventually accept Israel’s vision regarding the issues of the final talks. It will be hard for the PA to accept this vision because it fosters the occupation and the Jewish settlements and ignores the most important issues, especially that of the status of Jerusalem and the refugees.

As for Syria, it insisted on its position, which rejects the resumption of talks unless they start from the point at which they stopped three years ago. Several Israeli press sources pointed out that Barak pledged to withdraw Israeli troops to the borders of 4 June 1967 with some changes on the borders. Therefore, Syria’s acceptance to resume talks with Israel can be seen as an abandonment of its previous demand to get an Israeli pledge to withdraw from the whole of the Golan Heights. This could be considered a Syrian desire to reduce the American pressure on it during this stage and to give the U.S. Administration an important card to achieve a significant political achievement at a time when the Syrian leadership is in bad need of American support to pave the road for Bashar Al-Assad to succeed his father. This will enable the new Syrian leader, Bashar Al-Assad, to run the country smoothly after ending the dispute with the Jewish State.

Regardless of the result of these negotiations in future, their being resumed after a long period of stalemate is considered a breakthrough. The Israeli sources stress that secret talks preceded the announcement of the resumption of the Syrian-Israeli negotiations. This announcement had also come after a long series of European and other mediations, as well as the efforts exerted by the U.S. Administration, which alone bore fruit.

The statements made by Farouk Shara, Syrian Foreign Minister, show that there is a possibility to reach a peace treaty with Israel in a few months because 80% of the issues on the agenda of negotiations have already been agreed upon. He also said there was a real Syrian desire to reach a peace settlement with Israel regardless of the progress attained on the Palestinian track.

The resumption of talks at a high level (Shara-Barak) enhances the belief that the parties are serious about making rapid achievements in the negotiations. This fact encouraged U.S. President Clinton to say that the year 2000 will be the year of peace, keeping in mind a possible breakthrough on the Palestinian-Israeli talks as well.

Although the Syrian Foreign Minister had asserted in the press conference he held in Washington with the Israeli Prime Minister and the U.S. President following the first round of the resumed talks that Syria will have a right to restore its occupied lands by force if the peace talks failed, he stressed on building peace relations and accepted the normalization of ties with the Jewish State. He did not mention Syria’s demand to have a complete Israeli withdrawal from the Golan Heights to the borders of the 4 June 1967. He also did not link signing a Syrian-Israeli peace treaty with an Israeli withdrawal from the Palestinian territories occupied in 1967. All these are indications that Syria does want to achieve a real agreement with the Jewish State without previous conditions that could spoil the negotiations.

Despite the importance of the breakthrough made by the resumption of the Syrian-Israeli negotiations, there are still a host of suspended issues that should be resolved although they will take much time before being solved, especially that the Syrian negotiators are known to be firm in facing the Israeli hard-line negotiators. Syria also owns an important card to restoring its rights, that being the continuation of the resistance in South Lebanon, which annoys the Israelis and forces them to withdraw their troops from the Golan and South Lebanon.

The negotiations between Syria and Israel focus on four issues:

1.            Withdrawal: Syria demands a full Israeli withdrawal from the Golan Heights to the borders of 4 June 1967 and based on United Nations resolution 242. There are no indications that Israel will accept this demand because Barak’s government and the former Israeli governments insist on the withdrawal to the international borders set in 1923 between Palestine and Syria, which made the Syrian “Himmeh” area fall within the borders of Palestine. This area was taken back by Syria after 1949 but was occupied by Israel in 1967. Israel accepts to give the Himmeh area back to the Syrians but insists to retain control over the whole of Tiberias. Israel also demands to retain control over water resources while Syria wants to share them with Israel.

2.             Security: This is the most sensitive issue for the Israelis who put several conditions in this regard, such as:

Building an early warning station on Al-Sheikh Mountain in the Golan Heights. This demand was approved by the Syrians during Rabin’s tenure provided that it is under American supervision. It is not known whether the Syrians agreed on the existence of Israeli security elements under U.S. supervision in that station.

Mutuality on the security measures. Syria insisted on mutuality against the various Israeli demands in this respect. But Syria agreed to disarm an area of 10 kilometres beyond the Golan borders while an area of 6 kilometres depth in Israel from the Golan borders will be disarmed.

There are Israeli demands to reduce the number of Syrian soldiers and to lower the level of arming the Syrian troops. These demands are still disputed.

3.             Peace and normalization: This includes opening embassies, the exchange of diplomatic missions, the normalization of ties, and allowing citizens of each country to enter the other country. Syria had agreed on these measures in 1996, but based on the notion that the normalization of ties must be a decision made by the people themselves and cannot be achieved by an administrative decision by the government. This means that the Syrian-Israeli relations will be normal peace relations, not like what is going between Jordan and Israel. In addition, Syria insists that the normalization should start with the end of the last phase of Israeli withdrawal from the Golan Heights.

4.         Timing: This includes the time required to achieve the peace treaty and the complete withdrawal from the Golan Heights. There are still some disputes between Syria and Israel concerning the time period of the withdrawal. Syria demands that this period should not exceed one year while Israel is talking about a period of 4-6 years.

In addition to these issues, there is the Israeli withdrawal from South Lebanon, which should be negotiated by Israel and Lebanon side by side with the Syrian-Israeli negotiations over the Golan Heights. It seems that Israel will agree to give Syria a free hand in Lebanon after signing the peace agreement with it in order to appease Syria and to make sure of Lebanon’s obligation of preventing Hizbullah from carrying out military attacks against the Jewish State. Syria, which has distinctive relations with Hizbullah, can convince it to stop its military attacks against Israel after the restoration of all occupied territories in South Lebanon.

Israel demands compensation from the United States for the withdrawal from the Golan Heights. This compensation amounts to $18 billion for about 17,000 Jewish settlers living in the Golan who will be forced to leave their settlements after signing an agreement with Syria. This amount also includes the costs of the withdrawal and the operating of the early warning system. Although the U.S. Administration did not reject the principle of giving compensation, it adjourned discussing it later because it knows that it will be involved in a fierce battle with Congress, which is dominated by a majority of Republicans who strongly reject paying compensations for parties involved in the peace settlements. Congress had rejected such compensations for the Palestinian Authority, Jordan and Israel for one year before releasing a sum of $2 billion, which President Clinton promised to pay for these parties after signing the Wye River Memorandum in October 1998. 

Finally, there is Barak’s pledge to make a public referendum on any agreement reached with Syria after being approved by the Knesset. This could hinder the implementation of the expected agreement because the public opinion polls showed that the Israelis tend to reject the withdrawal from the Golan Heights although it is a slight majority, according to a recent poll conducted by Yediot Ahoronot (10 December 1999) in which 54% of the Israelis said they reject a withdrawal against a comprehensive peace that includes withdrawal from Lebanon and security arrangements; 45% of the Israelis supported the withdrawal under these arrangements.

Israeli sources believe that opposing the withdrawal could be weakened if the Israeli government succeeds in reaching peace agreements with Syria, Lebanon and the PA at the same time, which will end the status of war between Israel and the Arab countries. This will increase the chances of convincing the Israeli Street to approve these agreements if Barak succeeds in avoiding opposition of the withdrawal from the religious and right parties inside his coalition government.

Accordingly, the possibility of making progress in the Syrian-Israeli negotiations is now clearer and closer than at any time before. But there are still several obstacles, such as the security arrangements and laying down the borders. These issues include details which are still not agreed upon and will be at the centre of the coming negotiations, especially since all the outlines of the peace settlement between Syria and Israel were agreed upon in 1996.

But the progress on the Syrian track will have a negative impact on the Palestinian track; the Palestinian Authority believes that Israel could suspend the implementation of the rest of the transitional agreements between Israel and the PA and could make the final status talks go slower than before as a result.

Feeling its weak position in negotiations with Israel, the PA may rush to give more relinquishments to the Israelis. Therefore, Israel would achieve important gains in the peace talks with the Arab parties, which will have disastrous consequences, especially at the Palestinian level, regarding the issues of Jerusalem, Jewish settlements and the refugees.
