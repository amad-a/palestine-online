Special political analysis on
The Israeli stance towards the Intifadha

28/10/2000AD

No doubt the outbreak of the Aqsa Intifadha amidst the ongoing final settlement negotiations posed as a big surprise. However, the greater surprise was continuation of the Intifadha for a full month until now. The third and no less great surprise was the labor leftist government of Barak’s decision to escalate the military situation against the Palestinians and to deal a violent blow to the settlement process, which some believed that his government only came for the sake of pushing it forward.

So what is the real stand of the Barak government regarding the settlement and the Intifadha? Where is the internal Israeli reaction heading in the light of the Intifadha? And what is the effect of such development in the Israeli stand on the settlement process and the Intifadha?

Barak’s coup … reasons and motives

When the Camp David summit failed to reach a final peace treaty between Arafat and Barak, the question of Al-Quds (Jerusalem) was atop of the issues that led to failure of that summit. Barak was not ready to accept the Arab-backed Palestinian minimum demand for sovereignty over the old city of Al-Quds. Such a situation was further complicated with Barak government’s loss of its parliamentary majority in the Knesset and the opposition success, after the Shas party (Barak’s former ally) joined it, in passing the first reading of a resolution to dissolve the Israeli Knesset and launch early elections. Ever since that moment Barak gave up responding to American pressures and gave up his attempt to show himself as the Israeli leader that will succeed in achieving peace with the Arab countries. He started to focus instead on surviving atop of the authority for the longest possible period because opting to new elections without an accomplishment on the settlement process would lead to his loss in face of his opponent Netanyahu.

Israeli sources, meanwhile, leaked information that Barak had started coordinating with Sharon (current Likud leader) since the Camp David II negotiations. They said that Barak used to inform Sharon and consult with him over those talks on daily basis. Such information indicated that full coordination was maintained between Barak and Sharon over the latter’s visit to the Haram Al-Sharif on 28/9/2000, which triggered the Intifadha.

For his part, deputy Rubi Revelin, head of the Likud bloc in the Knesset, asserted that Israeli security bodies had encouraged the visit. He told the Hebrew daily ‘Ma’ariv’ in a statement published on the day of the visit, “They have agreed to our position that Jews should not be deprived of visiting the Haram, which is in the heart of the Jewish people and in the heart of Al-Quds, only out of fear of Palestinian violent threats. The security apparatuses have agreed to our opinion that if we surrendered the Haram they will continue to blackmail us.”

Another daily ‘Yediot Ahranot’ affirmed, in an article by its Washington correspondent, that the American administration had expressed in behind-the-scene chatting its anger over Sharon’s visit “behind closed doors they were infuriated by the performance of both Barak and Ben Amy … in one of the meetings, the supposition was tabled that Barak had allowed Sharon to head to the Haram in a premeditated mean manner so that the escalation would occur and as such he would escape from the peace process. Barak, according to such a supposition, was terrified by Camp David and was searching for a way to escape from registering his name in history books as the one who divided Al-Quds …”

Apparently, Barak who reached a conviction that it was difficult to reach a peace treaty that would include Al-Quds with the Palestinians along with the approaching final days of his government with the reopening of the Knesset session this month, he decided to try and form a national unity or emergency government with the Likud in its capacity as his only available rescue boat. Barak’s interests here met with those of Sharon who do not wish for new Knesset elections because that would entail internal elections within Likud to choose a candidate for premiership, which would be Netanyahu and not Sharon.

Within this context, the Israeli army’s excessive use of violence against demonstrators could be explained namely shooting at their heads and chests with the aim of inflicting biggest possible martyrs and wounded in their lines along with boosting Israeli presence at entrance of Palestinian cities and using bombs, tanks and helicopter gunships in shelling Palestinian Authority areas. Furthermore, Barak declared temporary suspension of the settlement process to pave the way for a new national unity government that would group most of the major right and left parties.

Unity in face of the Intifadha

It is an eye-catching fact that the Israeli government’s policy in dealing with the Intifadha was largely supported by other Israeli parties even the leftists. Moshe Raz, secretary of the peace now movement told the Hebrew service of radio Israel on 12/10/00 that the Israeli bombardment of PA areas was “a legitimate act under prevailing circumstances.” He praised the Israeli army steps, adding, “Palestinians should know that by crossing the redlines they would only bring destruction to themselves.” He advised them to stop the violence and return to the negotiating table!

For his part, Yossi Sarid, leader of the Meretz party one of the most prominent leftist parties, said, “The bombing operations were necessary for the peace process, because it is important that the Palestinians must know that any duplicity between negotiations and violence would only lead to more losses in their lines.”

Other right and religious parties such as Mifdal and Shas were fervent supporters of the Barak government, his call for suspending the settlement process and orders to the army to quell the Palestinian demonstrators. Many of them also focused on importance of adopting deterring measures against Palestinians of the 1948 occupied lands who shared in the Intifadha. They doubted those people’s patriotism and considered them a fifth column in the Hebrew state.

The Israeli premier started his intransigent stands by charging the PA with planning the “acts of violence”, charging that Arafat was able to stop them if he wished. Barak then declared that the settlement process had ended as one of his close friends told daily ‘Ma’ariv’ on 13/10/2000 that the “political process had totally collapsed and had terminated and there is no chance to mend it now.”

Barak was keen to point out that Arafat was no longer a partner in the settlement process in an attempt to justify freezing the settlement process.

The un-coronated leader of the Likud opposition party Benjamin Netanyahu, in an article published in ‘Yediot Ahranot’ on 6/10/00, criticized Barak’s weak policy towards the Palestinians and Arabs, which led to the outbreak of the Intifadha, saying, “A divided Israel, riddled by disputes, is a weak Israel that could not achieve peace or crystallize it. Such a situation encourages the Arab concept that the Jewish state was a passing phenomenon and only needs a little push or continuous operations of attrition and corrosion so that the whole building would fall down. This is the main motive behind the events that took place in the past weeks and not the pretext of Ariel Sharon’s visit to the Haram.”

As for the Israeli papers, which usually reflect the opinions of the intellectuals and the general Israeli popular trends, they adopted Barak’s use of force to stop the Palestinian violence, blaming Yasser Arafat for the Aqsa Intifadha. They considered that Arafat could end it, reiterating the Barak government’s declarations and ignoring the true stands of the Palestinian street that was desperate over prospects of the settlement process that did not achieve anything for them on the ground in addition to repeated Israeli provocations to worshippers in the Haram Al-Sharif, which was the spark that kindled the Aqsa Intifadha.

Israeli opinion polls showed that the public opinion was heading towards more intransigence in dealing with the settlement process. In a comprehensive poll on the general Israeli trends launched by the Dahef institution by the end of last September only 26% of those polled said they believed in the settlement process. The percentage three months after Barak’s election in September 1999 over the same issue reached 46%. It is worth mentioning that the same poll pointed out that 83% of the Israelis viewed that “unity of the people” was in a bad condition, a matter that displayed the severity of discrepancy and differences in the Israeli society due to political, social and racial affiliations, which had a clear impact on the Knesset elections of 1999.

Another poll was conducted by the Israeli daily ‘Ma’ariv’ and published on 13/10/00 in response to a single question: Is there a partner for peace in the Palestinian side? It showed that 62% of Israelis responded “no” while only 27% responded “yes”.

When they were asked on Jewish atrocities against Arabs of the 1948 areas, 73% declared their support and sympathy with such acts while only 25% were against them!

Moreover, 70% supported the formation of an Israeli national emergency government under present circumstances.

New war government

Most prominent results of the Aqsa Intifadha was that it displayed how deeply-rooted extremism and fanaticism was in the Israeli street and politics. It also revealed that the Israelis were adamant on subduing the Palestinian people and giving them only the minimum possible limit of lands in addition to depriving them of forming a real sovereign state not even on lands occupied in 1967 only. It further exposed the clear racism in dealing with Palestinian inhabitants of the 1948 areas especially after they shared in the Aqsa Intifadha at its early days in retaliation to Israeli policy of aggression and terrorism against their brothers in the 1967 occupied territories. Israeli premier Ehud Barak had uncovered such a mentality in the press conference held in Tel Aviv on 5/10/00 when he said, “After 52 years of Israel’s establishment, we are still fighting against terrorism, the time had not come yet to put down our swords.”

It was no coincidence that Barak and Sharon had agreed on provoking the Palestinians in a bid to assert Israeli sovereignty over Al-Quds and the blessed Aqsa Mosque and to affirm that they would not allow the Palestinians to impose their political stands through the use of the popular Intifadha or any other form of violence, which should be the sole concern of the Israelis in their capacity as the stronger party.

Within this context, Tanya Rinehart, professor at Tel Aviv University described in an article published in daily ‘Yediot Ahranot’ on 10/10/00 the stands of the Israeli governments, which maintained stringent measures against the Palestinians saying, “On the eve of Oslo the Palestinian people extended their hands for peace filled with hope and enthusiasm and peace was near. But we faced the extended hand with the victorious’ slackening after forgetting the Intifadha. And as long as the Palestinians ceded more, they asked them for more …”

The Israeli government apparently decided its option by heading towards a national unity government that would group it with the Likud and other parties. Such a government would direct a new blow to the settlement process, which many consider as dead or in a state of extended coma. It would also lead to continuation of the Israeli policy of escalation.

Description of the leftist writer Haim Hanegby in the ‘Ma’ariv’ newspaper on 24/10/00 of such a government would be enough when he said, “the government of the two Generals that is threatening an explosion at any time would be a government of blood, fire and gun smoke. Apparently there is no one doubting that fact whether in Israel or outside it.”
