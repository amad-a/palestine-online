(September 2000)

Is Jerusalem the only remaining obstacle in the negotiations?
“We have come to the “Temple Mount” and we will never depart from it” …

This statement, which was voiced by acting Israeli foreign minister Shlomo Ben Amy a few days ago, summarizes to a great extent the Israeli stand vis-à-vis the hectic, marathon negotiations currently being held over Jerusalem after the failure of the Camp David II in solving it. It also explains the big, and sometimes confusing number of proposals tabled to solve the religious sovereignty over the Aqsa Mosque and its precincts, which Egyptian foreign minister Amre Mousa said had reached 21 proposals! The stringent stands of the Israeli “doves” government urged the American administration, which already wishes to accelerate any solution in its short remaining period in office, to crystallize ideas that cope with Israeli demands and do not raise much anger on the Arab countries, which are embarrassed and reject Israeli sovereignty over the Aqsa Mosque.

Proposals for sovereignty and religious administration of Jerusalem

The Camp David II ended without reaching an agreement including Jerusalem despite the fact that other understandings were reached regarding the refugees, settlements, sovereignty and borders. However, the Jerusalem question still remains the most important obstacle before concluding a final peace agreement. The Hebrew state still insists on retaining sovereignty over both eastern and western sectors of Jerusalem within the framework of a “greater Jewish Jerusalem” through annexing nearby settlements without ignoring possibility of concluding solutions that do not bypass this “red line”. It had drawn that line with the support of blind American bias, which was eminent in the American administration’s adoption of proposals closer to the Israeli stands. Such proposals stipulate dividing sovereignty over the Haram Al-Sharif leaving the Aqsa Mosque and Dome of the Rock under Palestinian control along with that of their yards with a symbolic Israeli presence while giving the Israeli control over the Buraq Wall (Wailing Wall) and the area underneath the mosques along with a Palestinian symbolic presence. The Palestinians rejected the proposal backed by an Egyptian-Saudi position calling for complete sovereignty on those areas and rejecting any division of it.

Egypt, following a series of American pressures, tabled a non-written proposal suggesting Palestinian sovereignty on all areas of the Haram Al-Sharif and old Jerusalem while allowing Israeli control over the Buraq Wall and the Jewish suburb. However, the proposal was not accepted by the Barak government, which insisted on retaining control over all areas of old Jerusalem including the Haram area while allowing the Palestinians administrative control on mosques and yards on the ground leaving what is beneath to the Israelis who claim presence of the Solomon’s temple there.

The Palestinian Authority showed flexibility towards the American proposal, which Barak enthusiastically accepted as basis of negotiations in the event the Palestinians accepted it. Sources said that the Authority offered sharing control over the Buraq Wall with the Israelis in return for accepting joint sovereignty over the mosques.

Yet the Barak government was still insisting on rejecting Palestinian control   on the Haram, old Jerusalem and the east sector of the city. It expressed readiness to grant the PA religious jurisdictions in the Haram area in addition to municipal authority in old Jerusalem and the eastern part of it and allowing PA control on outskirts of the city. This continued to be the main Israeli position that hurdled any progress in the negotiations despite the PA and other Arab countries’ concentration on religious sovereignty over the Haram and the old quarters, which indicate a retreat in demands of controlling eastern Jerusalem.

Authorizing sovereignty … between the Palestinian and the Israeli stands

Such a stringent stance led the PA to declare, as voiced by head of the Palestinian legislative council Ahmed Qrei, its readiness to accept internationalizing Jerusalem in a bid to pressure Barak to be more flexible so as not to discuss a proposal always rejected by the Hebrew state because it included western Jerusalem that was not tabled for negotiations in the first place. However, few days after that suggestion Arafat announced a new proposal, which is different from the first one since it was tabled for real negotiations and not merely propaganda tactics. He declared his preparedness to accept converting sovereignty over the Haram to the Jerusalem committee, an affiliate of the Islamic Conference Organization. He specifically mentioned granting Egypt, Saudi Arabia and Morocco such dominance that would entrust the Palestinian side with sovereignty over the areas, that is granting the PA “religious administration” of those areas. Despite the PA’s denial of what was published in the Hebrew daily ‘Ha’aretz’ that Arafat had proposed to Clinton a joint Islamic-international control of the Haram, yet the offer was being developed. The Americans accepted the proposal while the Israelis viewed it with concern since it did not contain Palestinian sovereignty over the Haram and could be developed into a mere Islamic administration of Islamic holy shrines and an international control of Christian sacred shrines while retaining Israeli sovereignty on the area beneath the Haram, the Buraq Wall and the whole of the old city and eastern Jerusalem.

Shlomo Ben Amy repeatedly affirmed that his government was ready to allow “independent Palestinian administration” of the Islamic holy shrines as long as that meant ignoring Palestinian sovereignty over eastern Jerusalem.

The Israeli government blackmails Palestinians and Arabs by focusing on its weak internal situation

Apparently the proposal that was seriously discussed in the ongoing negotiations, which shifted from the US to the region, was shifting sovereignty on the Aqsa Mosque area to a certain party (international and Islamic) then authorizing the PA to run those areas. Despite the absence of clear indications on success of that suggestion, yet the few coming weeks would provide clear positions particularly when Barak was interested in reaching an agreement with the Palestinians before end of the Knesset’s summer vacation to avoid no confidence motions or dissolving the Knesset by rightist opposition parties. Meanwhile, the American administration wished that the concerned parties would sign a final agreement for the Palestine cause before end of the current session of the Congress and the general elections in November.

Barak is trying to use the card of his continuation in office to pressure the Palestinian party through saying that time was running out for concluding an agreement and spreading fears that the Israeli right parties would take over power returning the situation to that of Netanyahu’s era in the period 1996 to 1999.

Hence the repeated Israeli suggestions of reaching an agreement that leaves the question of Jerusalem to later negotiations hover. Arafat did not turn down the offer on condition that the agreement would not include an item declaring an end to the conflict as demanded by the Israeli government. Egypt, for its part, proposed reaching a final framework agreement in view of the complicating issue of Jerusalem that needed longer periods to solve but that proposal need a general settlement to the Jerusalem issue, which is what the American administration is currently seeking to hammer out.

Jerusalem ... a red line … will it be bypassed by the Palestinians and Arabs?

Continuation of negotiations and their concentration on sovereignty over old Jerusalem point to a retreat in the Palestinian and Arab positions at a time when no satisfactory solutions were reached on the overall issue of Jerusalem and other basic questions, in which big concessions were offered in Camp David II.

While the Arab stand apparently insists on sovereignty over Jerusalem yet it focuses on old Jerusalem, which raises fears that the issue of sovereignty over eastern Jerusalem was bypassed after the issue of tabling both sectors of Jerusalem at the negotiating table and other sensitive questions were also avoided in a way coping with the Israeli viewpoint.

However, the Jerusalem question remains the most sensitive and complicated issue and the one that activates the Palestinian and Arab masses as evident in what we witnessed of intensified popular moves in the past period.

Will the PA and the major Arab countries sidestep such a fact through accepting formal solutions to Jerusalem or will their realization of its seriousness prevent them from involving in such a step especially when faced with continued Israeli adamancy? This will be clarified within the few coming months.
