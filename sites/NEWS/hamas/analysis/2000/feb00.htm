(February 2000)

Peace talks swinging between
Syrian and Palestinian tracks

When the Syrian-Israeli negotiations were re-launched last December and when the negotiators began discussing the details of the understandings they reached during the tenures of Rabin’s government and the present government of Barak, optimists who believed in reaching a quick peace treaty between Syria and Israel began withdrawing their predictions as a result of the Israeli attempts to change the agenda of the negotiations and to adjourn the withdrawal of the Zionist troops from the Syrian Golan Heights. Instead, the Israelis wanted to achieve progress on the issues of security and normalization of ties with Syria. They even called upon Syria to introduce “goodwill” initiatives in order to convince the Israeli Street to vote for a peace treaty with Syria when it referred to an Israeli public referendum. The Israelis used to claim that it would be difficult for Barak’s government to introduce relinquishments and hence they need positive steps from Syria to help Barak to convince the Israelis that what the Jewish State will take from a peace treaty with Syria deserves the cost they are going to pay, i.e. the withdrawal from the Golan.

The third round of talks was adjourned when Syria rejected the Israeli agenda calling for giving priority to discussing the Israeli withdrawal. The talks were adjourned despite the significant progress achieved when the U.S. Administration introduced the so-called blueprint of a Syrian-Israeli peace treaty. But there remained some issues that were not agreed upon, especially setting the borders of the Israeli withdrawal, the security arrangements between Syria and Israel, the timetable of the withdrawal, the normalization of ties and the opening of embassies.

Pending the resumption of the third round of talks in which the U.S. Administration will play an important role and which is expected soon, what has been achieved until now indicates a possibility to reach a peace agreement in the next few months. However, this may not be inevitable because the talks agenda contains many disputatious issues, which could extend the period of the negotiations and cause many crises similar to the present one. Barak will do his best to convince the Israelis to support a peace agreement with Syria, which will take a long time since any agreement should include a complete or semi-complete withdrawal from the Golan Heights. The Israelis are still divided over the acceptance of the withdrawal. According to the latest opinion poll conducted by the Israeli Gallop independent institute, 50% of Israelis supported the withdrawal from the Golan, 37% opposed and 13% were undecided. Also, the huge demonstration staged in Tel Aviv by 150,000 Israelis protesting against a potential withdrawal indicates that such an opposition is strong, which in turn forces Barak to give it serious consideration, especially since some parties in his coalition government oppose the withdrawal as well.

It is clear that Barak still prefers a peace agreement with both Syria and Lebanon as one package in order to obtain votes from the Israelis who strongly call to sign a peace treaty with Lebanon. Such a treaty is necessary, according to these Israelis, to guarantee a withdrawal of Israeli troops from South Lebanon because of their continuous defeats and relapses caused by Hizbullah fighters, who also threaten the Jewish settlements in the north of Occupied Palestine. Barak, through revealing that former Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu had accepted in 1996 a complete withdrawal from the Golan, tried to weaken the Israeli Right, which now declares its rejection of a complete withdrawal from the Golan. Barak asserted that the deeds of the Israeli Right do not conform to its rejection and that in fact it does not differ from the Labour Party or the Israeli Left in general regarding the position over the withdrawal from the Golan.

As it was expected, Barak succeeded in playing on the peace tracks by pressuring the Palestinian Authority to accept his proposals regarding the implementation of the redeployment as agreed upon in the recent Sharm el-Sheikh agreement. In particular, Barak wanted to make the PA accept his proposal on the second phase of redeployment even though it rejected it a few days after the resumption of the second round of Israeli-Syrian talks. On 6 January 2000, the Occupation troops redeployed from 5% of the West Bank after a three-month delay. However, the Israelis suspended the redeployment from 6.1% of the West Bank in order to pressure the PA again into accepting the new maps of Israeli redeployment which aim at unifying the Israeli settlements in the West Bank while creating cantons of the Palestinian restored territories without a geographic connection.

While the final status talks are still going in two separate lines—public and secret—the Israeli position regarding the issues of the refugees, Jerusalem and Jewish settlements has not been changed. It was also learned that the PA clearly exhibited flexibility in an attempt to achieve the agenda of the final status talks by its scheduled timetable—7 February 2000. It seems that the PA is convinced that it is necessary to rush to reach any agreement with the Israelis even if the PA is forced to introduce more relinquishments. The PA wants to prevent Barak from marginalizing the negotiations with the Palestinians in favour of the Syrian track. It also wants to make use of the U.S. Administration’s desire to reach a Palestinian-Israeli agreement on the final status before the end of Clinton’s term in the White House, which in turn could support the position of the Democratic candidate for the upcoming presidential elections. Contrary to what the PA opines, Barak might be concerned in reaching a quick agreement with the Palestinians to benefit from the fragile position of the PA and to achieve the American Administration’s desire to sign this agreement very soon.

The Israeli fears of forging such an agreement focus on security, on which Barak’s government is keen to concentrate with its talks with Syria, Lebanon and the PA. Therefore, the Israeli government wants to reach certain arrangements, such as creating buffer zones with its Arab neighbours, establishing early warning stations, reducing the number of Syrian army personnel in the Golan Heights, and preventing the Palestinians from establishing an independent State and from taking control of the borders with the neighbouring Arab countries. The crossing points and the bridges will continue to remind the Palestinians that their dream to expand their State and to restore more of their occupied land would be disappointed and frustrated.

The coming phase will be decisive and critical regarding the peace process. It will draw the characteristics of the Syrian-Israeli peace settlement and the final solution on the Palestinian track. It is likely that Barak will seek to achieve parallel agreements with Syrians, Lebanese and the Palestinians and will introduce them as one package to be voted on by the Israelis. However, it is more likely that Barak will concentrate on reaching agreements with the Syrians and Lebanese, without the Palestinians.

The Palestinian Opposition, which will face difficult circumstances especially when Syria signs a peace settlement with the Zionist Regime and because the Arab governments will rush to normalize relations with the Jewish State, is preparing to set a new Palestinian national project that is able to treat the imbalance and the relinquishments introduced by the PA and which seeks to protect the rights of the Palestinian People.

As the Palestinian negotiations failed to restore the basic Palestinian rights which are being discussed in the final status negotiations, the project of the Palestinian Opposition could be a suitable option for the Palestinian People in the coming period. But it will require a strong will to be implemented in this difficult period. It is also a real examination of the Palestinian Opposition in the coming phase.
