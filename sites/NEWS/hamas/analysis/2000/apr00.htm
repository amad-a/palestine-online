(April 2000)

Crisis of the settlement process…
Will it result in positive developments for the Arab stand?

When Bir Zeit University students stoned French premier Lionel Gospen in response to his description of Lebanese resistance as terrorism, they were thus reflecting the greater Arab stance as far as the geographic framework is concerned. They were also reflecting a more comprehensive political stand. For after freezing the settlement process on the Syrian and Palestinian tracks, the Israeli government spread rumours that an atmosphere of escalation and war was prevailing in the region. That was evident in Israeli foreign minister David Levy’s calls for shedding the blood of the Lebanese people and children in retaliation to attacks by the heroic Lebanese resistance against occupation soldiers and their proxy militias in South Lebanon.

The atmosphere led to a negative Arab reaction vis-à-vis the settlement process. It started within a popular framework and ended with an official one represented in the meeting of the Arab foreign ministers in Beirut, which passed resolutions expressing disappointment at the progress of that process. The resolutions also displayed a certain extent of differences among Arab countries and their reaction to American pressures that tried to prevent any decision affecting the normalization policy with the Hebrew State or changing the overall Arab position regarding the settlement process.

 

The Beirut meeting in the face of Israeli threats

A few months after the election of Ehud Barak as Prime Minister of the Zionist entity, the settlement process witnessed progress in the form of the resumption of negotiations on the Syrian and Palestinian levels. However, the progress was quickly confronted by obstacles owing to hesitance by the Barak government in offering the necessary requirements for that process. These are topped by the withdrawal to the 4 June 1967 borders and the implementation of the third redeployment stage stipulated in the Wye River and Sharm el-Sheikh agreements, in addition to concluding a framework agreement for the final status settlement and reaching a comprehensive and final agreement by 5 September 2000. The speedy collapse of the negotiations on the Syrian and Palestinian tracks at almost the same time, coupled with the Zionist entity’s escalation against Lebanon by striking its civilian installations in response to resistance attacks, had a direct negative impact on the Arab region. That impact led the Arab foreign ministers to hold a meeting in Beirut earlier last month, most resolutions of which included: asking the Arab countries to reconsider normalization of relations with the Zionist entity and participation in the multilateral negotiations. They also affirmed the Arab countries’ absolute backing of Lebanon in resisting Israeli occupation in its capacity as a legitimate self-defence. They also affirmed rejection of re-settling the refugees outside Palestine and called for their return to their homeland while focusing on the importance of solving the issue of Palestinian refugees residing in Lebanon.

The Beirut meeting was faced by a relapse even before convening. Egyptian President Hosni Mubarak succeeded in holding a summit meeting grouping him with Barak and Arafat in Sharm el-Sheikh that resulted in the resumption of Palestinian-Israeli negotiations and paved the way for the Palestinian Authority’s approval of the redeployment maps as presented by the Israeli government. The redeployment was slightly changed by eliminating the village of Abu Dees, near Al-Quds (Jerusalem), from the withdrawal plan and replacing it with two other villages: Obeidiya, near Abu Dees, and Beytonia, located to the west of Ramallah. New dates were agreed upon during that summit namely fixing May as the final date for reaching a framework agreement for a final settlement, June or July for a third withdrawal from the West Bank while retaining the former date of September.

The negative effects of the Sharm el-Sheikh summit extended to the Beirut meeting when differences erupted between Egypt and Syria over the topics of normalization and multilateral negotiations. Egypt refused Syria’s demand for halting normalization and non-participation in the multilateral negotiations, which led to a compromise resolution that called for commitment to the settlement process while the most important and prominent decision was supporting the Lebanese resistance, which raised Washington’s and Tel Aviv’s worries.

Regardless of the weak Arab League’s resolutions and the fact that they did not meet the ambitions and demands of the Arab peoples, yet the mere fact that it met in Lebanon and passed decisions in support of its resistance is in itself a relatively positive spot in the light of what has been witnessed of retreat in Arab stands following the election of Barak.

Nonetheless, the real test for those resolutions is their actual implementation in the light of the continued Israeli intransigence regarding the settlement process.

 

Syrian-Israeli negotiations bogged down

Within the same framework, the Syrian-Israeli negotiations were bogged down following lengthy reports of secret talks held through American mediation and the emergence of pointers indicating Barak’s readiness to recognize Rabin’s deposited pledge with the American administration. For the summit meeting held between the Syrian and American presidents in Geneva last 26 March had failed to reach a suitable formula for resumption of the Syrian-Israeli negotiations. The failure was attributed to the Israeli party’s refusal to offer a clear-cut pledge to withdraw to the 4 June borders and their insistence on retaining the northeastern beach of Lake Tiberias, which falls within the Syrian side according to those borders. That stance was adopted despite Syria’s flexibility in accepting the Zionist entity’s retention of the sources of river waters that fall in that Lake, in addition to indications that it was ready to accept changes on the border line with the Hebrew State so that it would pass between the international borders delineated by the 7 March 1923 agreement between France and Britain and the 4 June 1967 border line, which demarcates Syria’s borders before the 5 June 1967 war.

Israeli press reports affirmed that Barak had asked Syria to make amendments on the 4 June border line that does not include Al-Himma area but also drawing back the 4 June line at Tiberias towards the east by 100–200 metres, a matter that would guarantee Israel’s control over Lake Tiberias from all sides. The Israelis accepted, meanwhile, to allow the Syrians to reach the northeastern beach without having to obtain a prior Israeli entry visa in return for reciprocal arrangements allowing the Israelis to enter Al-Himma that would be returned to Syria in the expected peace treaty.

The Israeli press sources described that Israeli proposal as “an exchange of lands” between Syria and the Zionist entity.

Perhaps it is too early to pass a failure verdict on the Geneva summit since differences between the two sides are no longer that big. Yet they are linked to details that would make the negotiations arduous and tough but the differences’ gap might be bridged within the few coming weeks, though the matter is still uncertain.

As for the Israeli party, it will remain pending hopes on signing a peace treaty with Syria that would allow it to arrange for a withdrawal from South Lebanon within an agreement with Lebanon before the date of 1 July fixed by the Israeli government to pull out its troops from there. This is confirmed by the fact that Barak postponed approval of the unilateral withdrawal plan presented to him by the Israeli army in a bid to allow more room for success of negotiation with Syria, taking into consideration the fact that the unilateral withdrawal plan will be different from a pullout following a peace agreement with Lebanon. The latter, which was code-named “New Horizon”, stipulates the evacuation of the Israeli occupation army to the international borders while the other one code-named “Morning Dusk” stipulates retaining a number of areas within a narrow security border strip along the borders with Lebanon to protect Israeli settlements in north of occupied Palestine.

 

Israel gives priority to negotiations with Syria…but?

The image of the settlement process in the light of latest developments on the Palestinian and Syrian levels can be summarized as follows:

On the Palestinian level: The third section of the second stage in the redeployment plan was carried out, in particular turning over 1.6% of the West Bank’s area to the full (security and political) control of the Palestinian Authority, so that now it controls 40% of the total area of the West Bank whether partially (18%) or wholly (22%).

While a round of talks was held in Washington to reach a framework for a final settlement, there was increasing talk of secret negotiations with the help of a third party under the patronage of Barak and Arafat to reach a solution to the thorny issues of the settlements, Al-Quds and the refugees. At the same time that the Barak government was hesitant on implementing the remaining items, declaring anew that there were no sacred dates, it was facing domestic problems and threats by the Shas and Mifdal parties to withdraw from the government in the event any new redeployment covered areas near Al-Quds.

On the Syrian level: The negotiations bogged down in the presence of American bias in favour of Israel through holding Syria responsible for the failure of the negotiations as evident in Clinton’s statement after the summit that the ball now was in the court of Syria. Furthermore, there is the Israeli threat of giving priority in the coming stage to the negotiations with the Palestinian Authority.

On the Lebanese level: There is concern and cautious waiting for an imminent military escalation following the failure of the Geneva summit. The possibility that Hizbullah might launch new resistance operations could raise the fever in South Lebanon. There is also the Israeli preparations for a withdrawal from South Lebanon with or without an agreement while both parties, the Syrian and Lebanese on the one hand and the Israelis on the other, prefer a pullout with an agreement that would prevent future military escalation in the light of the Israeli threat of a violent response to any military operation by the resistance after the withdrawal.

Such a mosaic picture full of complications on all tracks will be cleared within the few coming weeks; determining either a breakthrough or further complications will ensue. The Israeli government’s priority these days, however, is forging a peace treaty with Syria, but in the event it failed, it would activate negotiations with the Palestinians at the lowest possible political price out of its conviction that the Palestinian Authority is prepared to offer new concessions for the sake of gaining what it calls “An independent State”. In the event negotiations on the Syrian track were obstructed, the region will witness a military escalation in South Lebanon even before the Israeli evacuation.

 

Importance of developing the Arab stand

The Arab stand, which witnessed a relative positive development as far as slowing down normalization of relations with the Hebrew State is concerned, will develop either negatively or positively in accordance with developments in the Syrian-Israeli negotiations. Most of the Arab countries linked their normalization of relations with the Zionist entity to reaching a Syrian-Israeli peace treaty and not to a final settlement with the Palestinian Authority.

Anyway, more crises are expected in the upcoming stage of the settlement process owing to the Israeli intransigence vis-à-vis the negotiation topics, which we mentioned at the start of this analysis. Furthermore, the Barak government will be free from American pressure owing to the Clinton administration’s preoccupation with the forthcoming presidential elections and its keenness on wooing the Israeli government to ensure the success of the Democratic candidate and current vice president Al Gore.

Thus the Arab countries are called upon to deal with the expected stalemate and the Israelis’ attempts to downplay the Palestine cause, which is the core of the Arab-Israeli conflict.

There is no other alternative to a strong and united Arab stand in face of the Israeli arrogance even at the minimum level by halting normalization of relations and consolidating the Palestinian position through boosting the Palestinian resistance and checking the Palestinian Authority’s attempts to give more concessions that will carry negative effects not only on the Palestine cause but the whole Arab-Israeli conflict.
