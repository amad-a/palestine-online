(August 2000)

Camp David II: Between Failure and 
Continuation of Negotiations

Results of the Camp David summit, which concluded late July, posed as a main axis in the settlement process that started in Oslo almost seven years ago. The summit was no different from the overall ambiguity that shaped that process as evident from the little information leaking on its results so far.

The summit, which America hoped would end up in a “semi-final” agreement or what could be described as a “final-transitional” agreement, did not approve any solution to the sovereignty over Jerusalem based on the principle of “intentional ambiguity” that governed the basic agreement in Oslo–a matter which made the American administration declare the negotiations a failure after trying to postpone the issue for a few days.

The Camp’s prologues

The Camp David negotiations started on 11 July 2000 after the secret channel talks, which opened in Tel Aviv then moved to Stockholm before returning to Tel Aviv, reached specific understandings between Arafat’s and Barak’s negotiators on final status issues. Those included Jerusalem, refugees, settlements, sovereignty and borders with the exception of sovereignty over Jerusalem’s old quarters, which is part and parcel of East Jerusalem. This question was left to direct contact between Arafat and Barak. Despite realizing the sensitivity of that question, Clinton finally decided to host a summit for Barak and Arafat with the aim of reaching a final settlement even if it did not include a solution on Jerusalem. Apparently he believed that it could be postponed to later negotiations and that he could guarantee Arab pressure on Arafat to accept such a solution. However, this did not happen. He, along with his foreign secretary, contacted leaders of Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Jordan and Morocco to perform that role. It was eye-catching to note that Egypt refused to perform such a role but rather tried to further solidify Arafat’s stand and pressure him not to offer any concessions regarding sovereignty over Jerusalem. The same stance was adopted by Saudi Arabia, which considers itself responsible for Islamic shrines in the Arab world.

The Egyptian-Saudi position vis-à-vis the sovereignty over East Jerusalem had a direct impact on Arafat’s stand which had previously included many concessions even during the Camp David negotiations regarding the issues of the refugees, settlements, sovereignty and borders. However, he could not go further because of the united Arab stand and the internal Palestinian situation, which was not in favour of negotiations and capitulation but rather backed resistance and force against occupation following success of Hizbullah in defeating the Israeli occupation and forcing the Barak government to withdraw its forces from South Lebanon.

As for the Israelis, they were not prepared to yield anything on the issue of sovereignty over Jerusalem especially when Barak looked weak before his coalition government that started planning his downfall as a punishment for showing “flexibility” regarding the final status issues. This was topped by his preparedness to give up Palestinian areas to the north and south of Jerusalem in return for annexing the main settlement blocs near the holy city. Furthermore, Barak’s five “NOs” with which he went to Camp David II remained unchanged during the talks with only formal solutions proposed for them. Those are: A religious and municipal solution to Jerusalem (reparations and re-settlement of refugees while allowing 100,000 of them to return within family reunion programmes after considering such a step as an Israeli recognition of resolution 194 but without shouldering any civilian or legal responsibility regarding their forced emigration); retaining 80% of settlements and annexing them to the Zionist entity in return for eliminating other minor pockets (maintaining a security presence whenever necessary at the Jordan Valley and preventing the Authority from having military presence in that area or forging alliances with Arab countries as well as banning the entry of any foreign army through that area); annexing settlements to the east of the Green Line; expanding Jerusalem’s borders giving the Palestinians around 94% of the West Bank areas (excluding Jerusalem); and retaining part of that 94% in the hands of the Israelis for a ten-year period and renting others for a period that could extend to 25 years as leaked from the secret Stockholm negotiations.

Despite Clinton and his administration’s realization before the Camp David II summit, he was keen on its convening equipped with his ability to pressure Arafat and the Arab countries to secure a political notch on his belt which in turn would reflect well on his deputy Al Gore in his bid to secure the presidency in the upcoming elections. The summit, however, proved that the Jerusalem question could serve as an explosive factor to the negotiations and that it would remain, for a certain time at least, a major obstacle to reaching a final settlement in view of its religious and historic importance, surpassing all other complicated issues such the refugees and settlements.

Understandings except sovereignty over eastern Jerusalem

No accurate information is yet available on the results of Camp David II owing to the imposed secrecy on the part of the American administration over progress of the negotiations. It is not clearly known whether understandings were reached on the issues of refugees, settlements and sovereignty along with the extent of those understandings and their details. However, the Israeli press was full of reports on results of those talks and proposals tabled therein. The reports pointed out that the Israeli party was the one that tabled speculations on a final solution and that the Palestinian side was content with responding to such speculations and tabling amendments. For its part, the Clinton administration was trying to bridge those two stands by presenting a speculation that was nearer to the Israeli side owing to well-known strategic considerations and to that administration’s need for the Jewish vote in the American presidential elections.

Most leaked reports, which were not denied by either party, pointed to features of the final settlement as follows:

1. Israeli approval on establishment of the Palestinian State on around 94% of the West Bank areas (excluding Jerusalem) in addition to the whole of the Gaza Strip on the following conditions:

(a) That State should not have an army with heavy weapons, should not be free to forge alliances with other countries and should not introduce forces west of the River Jordan.

(b) Allowing the presence of Israeli occupation forces in lands and air of that State through:

-Granting Israel the right to deploy troops in the Jordan Valley in the event the Zionist entity was endangered from the East.

-Allowing Israeli flights in Palestinian airspace.

-Installing Israeli early warning stations in certain areas in the West Bank, especially in the Jordan Valley.

(c) Keeping that State divided (separating the West Bank from the Gaza Strip–splitting the West Bank into two big cantons at least) and linking those areas via safe passages under Palestinian administration but at the mercy of occupation, which would impose conditions of passing through them.

(d) Granting the Palestinians control on border crossings with Jordan and Egypt along with Israeli security observation.

(e) The Israelis would retain management over water sources in the West Bank while approving a limited quota to the Palestinians.

(f) Allowing the Zionist entity either to rent certain areas in the Jordan Valley or maintain temporary sovereignty on them for years to com

2. Solving the refugees’ issue through reparations and re-settlement after returning small numbers of them in accordance with the following:

(a) The Hebrew State will not accept any legal or civilian responsibility over their displacement.

(b) The Hebrew State agrees to the return of around 100,000 of them under “humanitarian” grounds in the form of family reunions and considers such a step as an approval of resolution 194.

(c) Permitting the Palestinian State to absorb half a million refugees according to a fixed timetable.

(d) Establishing an international fund to compensate refugees in which the Zionist entity, America and Europe are to contribute, on condition that it will compensate Jews who left their residence areas in the Arab countries after the creation of the Hebrew State in 1948!

3. Resolving the Jerusalem issue according to three framework agreements:

(a) The geo-political framework stipulating that the Hebrew State would give up suburbs in the north and the south of Jerusalem that would be annexed to the West Bank, including Abu Dees, Alezariye and eastern Sawahre where the Palestinians can call those areas Jerusalem and their capital.

(b) The civilian municipal framework, which comprises East Jerusalem, where the Palestinians would be granted in that part (Beit Hanina-Shaafat) a civilian administration affiliated with the Palestinian Authority with the possibility of linking it to West Jerusalem through a municipality covering both sectors. The Palestinians would run a branch municipality within the framework of the Israeli higher municipal council while depriving them from planning and construction jurisdictions.

(c) The religious framework allowing a Palestinian, Arab, Islamic and Christian administration of holy shrines in the old city of Jerusalem in accordance with one of the tabled proposals stipulating the division of the Christian management so that part of it would be under Islamic administration and the other under Jewish administration. The Palestinians, in accordance with that proposal, would be allowed to hoist the Palestinian flag over the Islamic and Christian shrines along with a safe passage linking northern Jerusalem, which would be annexed to the West Bank, to those areas so that Palestinians and Muslims would not pass through lands under Israeli sovereignty.

It is noteworthy that the proposal on Jerusalem can be summarized in allowing the Israeli side to have the upper hand in sovereignty issues over Jerusalem after expanding its borders to reach around 550 square kilometres which would double its current area five times after annexing the Israeli settlements. The Palestinians would be granted civilian, municipal and religious jurisdictions over inhabitants and not the lands in East Jerusalem. The dual sovereignty proposed by the American administration did not get far from the previous proposals with the exception that it adds the term “sovereignty” over Palestinian civilian and administrative jurisdictions suggested by the Israelis within East Jerusalem while postponing a solution over old Jerusalem to later negotiations, that being it endorses Israeli sovereignty over Jerusalem, which made Israeli premier Ehud Barak accept it while the Palestinians rejected it because it did not differ in essence from the Israeli proposal.

What would follow the failure of the Camp David negotiations?

Hence, Jerusalem was the obstacle at which the negotiations crashed, especially the issue of sovereignty over the eastern sector, including the holy shrines. The Israelis stuck to their position and won a clear American backing at the summit while Arafat could not extend his concessions to include Jerusalem during that summit.

The summit failed to reach a “semi-final” agreement owing to the Israeli party’s insistence on securing a Palestinian-Arab statement ending the conflict. Barak wished to sign an agreement comprising all issues (Jerusalem-refugees-sovereignty-borders) and refused to sign any partial agreement without including Jerusalem and ending the conflict. The agreement was supposed to contain articles stipulating the continuation of talks over Jerusalem. Apparently, even Arafat was not prepared to accept such an agreement at the present time.

Camp David II, though, did not reach a written agreement on a final settlement, yet it could serve as a step towards that target and it might have displayed the real obstacles that prevented conclusion of such an agreement.

The coming weeks will be decisive in determining the place and time of signing the final settlement, especially when the summit did not end in complete success nor complete failure.

Furthermore, the limited remaining period for the present American administration (till November) coupled with the Arab solid stance in insisting on sovereignty over East Jerusalem, which prevented the collapse of the Palestinian Authority, indicate that probability of reaching a final settlement within that period was not realistic.

Reports are currently circulating that a summit will be soon held between Arafat and Barak, yet its convening was not sure before the uninterrupted (Palestinian-Israeli) negotiations result in “creative” solutions to the question of Jerusalem since “creativity” of former solutions did not lead to solving that complicated block since the Abu Mazen-Pelen document in 1995.

The Jerusalem problem will remain a major hurdle before the settlement process but it is not the only one. There is the refugees’ issue, which will continue to urge millions of Palestinians to reject any solutions that do not include their right of return to their country and lands. Furthermore, the limited space of the Palestinian State and partitioning its areas will not serve as a suitable atmosphere for economic and political stability in the Palestinian lands. Such a situation would boost importance of federal relations with Jordan and chances of continuing the Palestinian problem owing to the absence of settlements meeting Palestinian aspirations for national identity and restoring Palestinian national rights.

The proposed final settlement would not serve as a final solution in the true sense of the word nor through the practical formulas it would accomplish. The negotiations on pending issues (refugees-Jerusalem-settlements-borders) between the Hebrew State and the meager Palestinian State, which would be found in accordance with the final-transitional settlement, will continue. Meanwhile, the problems, which would not be solved immediately, would carry negative repercussions on later agreements and negotiations.

However, in the event no final settlement is reached, the results would carry even greater negative effects and might lead to Palestinian confrontations and uprisings against the Zionist entity and even against the Palestinian Authority, whose repression of the Palestinian people would greatly increase. This would lead concerned parties in the next stage–in the event no final agreement was reached–to search for alternative formulas, the priorities of which would be allowing establishment of a Palestinian State that should accord pledges to the Israelis cancelling any future demands for the withdrawal of the Hebrew State to the pre-1967 borders.
