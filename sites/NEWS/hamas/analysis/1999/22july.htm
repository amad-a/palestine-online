22 July 1999

The New Israeli Cabinet and the Future of a Peace Compromise

The newly elected Israeli Premier, Ehud Barak, nominated his new ministers recently within the 40-day break given to him. He was able to present the outline of his new joint cabinet before the Israeli Parliament after he had signed several agreements with his allied parties. This cabinet, which consists of both the middle parties and the religious ones, won the confidence of Parliament by a majority of 75 out of 120 votes on 6 July 1999. The first step carried out by Barak was his visit to Egypt to meet President Husni Mubarak in order to resume the peace process. He then met the PA chairman, Yasser Arafat, in Gaza and King Abdallah II of Jordan. Finally, he departed to the U.S. to meet President Bill Clinton.

The New Constituency of the Israeli Cabinet

After several attempts by Barak to negotiate with the Israeli parties, the new Israeli Cabinet was formed consisting of the following Israeli parties: Israel is One (coalition of the Labor, Ghescher and Memad parties); Shas (the Eastern Religious); Israel Be’alia (the Russian Jews); the Middle and Mefdal (the Western Religious); and the Leftist Murits Party. The most significant remarks about this Cabinet can be summarized as follows:

1.  This Cabinet is supported by 75 Parliament members because these parties have MPs. It is also supported by the extremist United Torah Judaism Party from the coalition which has 5 MPs. The Arabs, who have 10 MPs, also support this Cabinet with a moderate attitude concerning the peace process, an attitude that is not found throughout this coalition.

2.  This Cabinet is based on the coalition between the One Israel coalition and the religious party Shas, which is second in terms of the number of MPs in the new Cabinet and is not an extremist one but it does focus on realizing the religious appeals of its followers. Therefore,  Barak was able to negotiate and win over this Party (although he deprived it from the Ministry of Interior post, which has been long sought after by this Party) through negotiations with the Likud in order to make Shas give up this Ministry through which it can be decided who is the true Jew who can return to the Jewish State and receive nationality. Consequently, he was able to deprive the Israel B'aliya Party from this Ministry lest thousands of Russian immigrant Jews return to Israel who, according to Shas, do not have the proper Jewish requirements. Barak not only made Shas disregard its chief, Ara’eih Der’e, but he also assigned the Ministry of Interior to Shas’ real opponent, Israel B'aliya. The Shas Party accepted to take part in the Cabinet by obtaining four minor ministries. However, Shas will keep on asking for the Ministry of Interior, especially after two MPs of the Israel B'aliya had abandoned their party owing to ideological and financial matters. Consequently, the MPs of this party decreased from six to four.

3.  The Likud Party did not take part in this Cabinet because Barak refused to equate this party with the Labor Party in sharing the ministries and formulating the guidelines of the Cabinet. However, this does not mean that the Likud will not take part in any potential Israeli cabinet in the future, especially after the final negotiations with the Palestinians had already started.

4.  It is expected that Barak will increase the ministries of his party to 24 instead of 18 in order to reconcile some indignant MPs of his party as well as his allies in the coalition.

5.  The distribution of these ministries among the members of the Labor Party and the appointment of the advisors caused indignant and furious reactions in the party because Barak excluded the top five members in the initial election such as Yousuf Burgh and Uzy Ber’am while General Mattan Felna’e has been waiting for any extension in the Cabinet for the future. Moreover, Barak appointed Yussie Belien as a Minister of Justice (although he is not a law man) and Abraham Shouhat—one of his closest friends—as a Minister of Finance (considered to be one of the major ministries), which created indignant reactions among the Labor Party MPs because Belien played an important role in Barak’s electoral campaign. The matter is the same for Shlomo Ben Amy, who was top in the initial elections of the Labor Party and was appointed as Minister of Internal Security instead of the Ministry of Finance or the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, which was assigned to David Levy. Barak appointed three advisors to his office: Danny Yatoum (the ex-Chief of the Israeli Intelligence) as Chief of the political board in order to coordinate the information coming from the security systems such as the intelligence unit known as Shabak and the Military Intelligence (even though the chiefs of these systems prefer to deal with Barak directly and they all have a negative attitude toward Yatoum, especially as a result of the failed attempt to assassinate Khaled Misha’al, the chief of Hamas political bureau, and because Yatoum had not informed them of the detailed plan before it was executed); Haim Shkeid (an ex-army officer) as Chief of the Premier’s office; and Tsefy Stouber (the advisor who led the electoral campaign of Barak). These appointments have been severely criticized by the magnets of the Labor Party because they include Barak’s intimate friends and they are not competent to hold these positions. These magnets also accused Barak of gathering his closest people around him so he would not be alone in making any decisions, especially after he deprived all of the influential Labor Party MPs from the major ministries such as Defense, Finance and Foreign Affairs.

6.  Barak did not appoint any Arab MPs to any ministries or even consult them despite the absolute support they gave him during the elections. However, he did inform them of the results of his final negotiations after they had been completed, which infuriated the Arab MPs who supported Barak with 90% of the Arab votes in the Israeli Parliament. This will stop any support by these parties for Barak or his future proposals except those which will be in favour of the Arab attitude in the negotiations against the Israeli Rightist parties in the compromise process.

The only thing that Barak has done for the Arab parties was to accept the Arab MP, Hashem Mahameed, as a member of the Committee of Foreign Affairs and Security in the Israeli Parliament for the first time in the history of Israel. This Committee supervises the Israeli security systems. However, this is not considered a great achievement for the Arabs because two other Arab MPs were candidates for this post and their role and influence will be limited as the majority of the members are Jews. This indirect support by the Arabs of the Jewish State only serves to realize a false democracy with its obvious racism even in the territories occupied in 1948.  

The Labor Party Program: Is There Anything New?

Before Barak formed his new Cabinet, he emphasized his well-known “No’s”: No return for the refugees, no removal of the colonies, no abandonment of Jerusalem, no army west of the Jordan Valley, and no return to the borders of 4 June 1967. The Israeli Foreign Affairs Minister declared that Barak had informed Clinton of these headlines, which he presented before the Parliament and upon them won their vote of confidence, in the first visit the former paid to the U.S.  Any final peace agreement with the PA or Syria should be voted on and with a full commitment to withdraw the Israeli forces from Lebanon. Moreover, Barak has been keen on showing his full desire to complete the peace process in order to have real achievements just to be different from his predecessor Netanyahu. He then carried out several negotiations with Egyptian President Husni Mubarak, PA Chairperson Yasser Arafat, King Abdalla II of Jordan, and U.S. President Bill Clinton.

Although Barak has shown his commitment to apply the Wye River agreement, he emphasized what had been reported by the Israeli mass media that he had been trying to convince Arafat to change his attitudes and to apply this agreement gradually and as soon as possible because he believed that the full application of this agreement would create a lot of trouble which would minimize the chances to realize a lasting peace.

This opaque justification of Barak’s attitude toward a partial application of the Wye River agreement had been declared by Barak’s close advisors who said that Barak wanted to move to the final negotiations after applying one stage of the Israeli withdrawal as stated in the said agreement under the pretext that the full application of this agreement would create more trouble for Barak, and a lot of protests by his Rightist allies and the settlers, which was not desirable by him at that stage. These sources indicated that during the second round of negotiations Barak intended to tempt the Palestinians into postponing the application of this agreement in exchange for declaring the Palestinian State, according to the Israeli conditions, of course. If we disregard the Israeli overt justifications, we realize that the actual reason behind postponing this application is to prevent the PA from capturing more land in the West Bank, and so the negotiations concerning this stage will be carried out during the final negotiations in order to bargain with keeping the major colonies and settlements as well. To make this attitude different from Netanyahu, Barak showed his desire to finalize all of the tracks of the peace process by November of 2000 in addition to the declaration of the independent Palestinian State because this date coincides with the season of the American elections, which will help the Democratic party support their candidate, Al Gore, to win the American presidency. Meanwhile, Barak has been benefiting from the American current attitude which does not exert any pressures on Israel at this stage in order to achieve more progress and make the Arab partners responsible for any delay in this respect. In this context, Barak has tried to minimize the American role in the peace process on the Palestinian track through minimizing the role of the CIA in supervising the execution of the agreements to prove that both Palestinians and Israelis are capable of performing this role without the interference of the Americans.

It seems that Barak has been trying to minimize any American role that would exert any pressure on his Cabinet in the future and to outline the peace process with the Palestinians for the American partnership but not with their close supervision of the details of this process as was the case during Netanyahu’s reign. In addition, he considers himself able to convince the Americans of what he presented to the Palestinians and to get the necessary American support for his security considerations and demands which are quite essential for the continuity of the peace process. Barak has finally obtained American aid for his security demands by signing another security agreement with the them in order to form a joint committee concerned with the security issues of the two countries as well as promoting cooperation between them in the fields of defense, rockets, missiles, weapons of mass destruction and purchasing 50 F-16 advance American fighters. In addition to this absolute American support for his security demands, Barak insists that the PA has to be committed to the security demands as shown in the agreement; this is exactly what Barak emphasized to Arafat when they met at Airies Barrier in Gaza, and he will be more determined than the previous rulers in this respect. Also, unless the PA helps resist the so-called terrorism, he will cancel the peace process altogether. Moreover, Barak suggested that a 47-kilometre overpass, including a motorway and a railway, should be built to connect Gaza and the West Bank so the Palestinians do not pass through the territories occupied in 1948.  

Inappropriate Arab Optimism

The advent of Barak has created a wave of optimism among Arab countries and the PA, but the Palestinians have been more reserved this time because Barak has emphasized the Syrian track in the peace process which has made the Syrians remarkably positive in their reactions. The PA has been doubtful of Barak’s using the game of tracks in order to delay the final compromise on the Palestinian track and weaken the attitude of the PA in the negotiations. This game was played by the Israeli ex-Premier, Yitzhaq Rabin, who succeeded in blackmailing a lot of deference and yielding from the PA. Consequently, the statements made by the Palestinian officials lately have emphasized the fact that the real yardstick for Barak’s intentions is the practical application. Meanwhile, the PA has rejected any combination between the Wye River agreement and the final agreement, which could be parallel to each other, and the third stage of the Israeli withdrawal whose percentage should be decided later on. Undoubtedly, the beginning of the negotiations of the final solution before fulfilling the demands of the previous stage will weaken the Palestinian attitude even if the PA insists on rejecting to combine the Wye River agreement with the final negotiations. This will definitely lead to greater Israeli superiority.  The Israelis have been procrastinating in applying the agreements. Consequently, the PA will have only one option: combining the delayed matters with the final negotiations. The PA’s attitude will be weaker in the light of the new merge between the Syrian and the Israeli attitude, especially the strong desire among the Syrians to make Barak fulfill the Syrian-Israeli peace treaty, withdraw his forces from Lebanon and settle the Arab-Israeli conflict by withdrawing from most of the occupied Golan Heights.

The variety of Arab welcomings for Barak, the show of support expressed by Egyptian President Husni Mubark when he asked everyone to give Barak a two-month break in order to be able to fulfill his promises, and the unusual American arrangements for Barak’s late visit to Washington make us believe that Barak will have more manoeuvrability in keeping his acceptable and good image among the Arabs even though he did not fulfill his commitments in the peace process.

On the other hand, Syria has been the most hopeful Arab country in terms of making peace with the advent of Barak. This was made clear in a statement made by Syrian President Hafiz Al-Asad to the English writer, Patrick Seal, who recently visited both Damascus and Tel Aviv where he met both Al-Asad and Barak before the latter formed his Cabinet. It was for the first time in the history of Syrian diplomacy that the Syrian President volunteered to talk directly about Israel and the Israeli Premier, Barak, describing him as truthful and powerful and who is keen on making peace with Syria but in a well-planned and well-studied approach. Thereafter, the Syrian Ministry of Foreign Affairs responded positively to Barak’s speech of confidence before the Israeli Parliament, showing the readiness of Syrians to fulfill the peace agreement as soon as possible, describing it “within their reach.” Although Barak did not declare his plans to apply the Wye River agreement, one of his advisors announced in Washington that he intended to present a limited initiative within a few weeks, which shows that Barak is quite concerned with fulfilling the peace agreement on the Syrian and Lebanese tracks, although he did not express any commitment to withdraw from the Golan Heights, an issue Syria has been appealing for since 1967. On the contrary, he emphasized that Israel would never withdraw to the borders of 4 June 1967, which is against the Syrian demands; in addition, Barak still rejects any Syrian forces on the banks of Lake Tiberias for security considerations.  

Conclusion

There are no indications in Barak’s policy concerning any radical change in the Israeli policies toward the Palestinian Cause because the red lines still exist on the Palestinian level. The new Israeli Cabinet will be different from the previous one only in its tactics and its detailed attitude.

Although Barak has emphasized that he would not build any new colonies or remove the existing ones, he gave an oral assurance to his allies—Mefdal (the party of the settlers)—to support the colonial projects in Abu Ghneim and Ma’aleih Adumim. In addition, a report by the Israeli Peace Now Movement showed that the process of construction is being continued in the settlements, which were built during Netanyahu’s reign, and it accused the new Cabinet of following its predecessor’s steps.

Barak’s agreement to the independent State is just in words as long as he has put certain conditions which make this State unreal, such as the issue of Jerusalem and the refugees. As a result, his attitudes are identical to his predecessor, Netanyahu. Under such conditions, this optimism concerning Barak and his policies is not based on objectivity except the justification of following his footsteps which leads to immense and free deference and a yielding attitude on the part of the Arabs and Palestinians. Moreover, the separation of the negotiation tracks as approved at the Madrid Conference will lead to more deterioration in the Arab attitudes as long as each Arab State deals with the peace process separately according to its own interests in order to sign a peace treaty with Israel, regardless of the so-called just peace on the other tracks. Waiting for the results of the negotiations on the Lebanese and Syrian tracks, the Arab States will remain optimistic about Barak just to give him the chance to fulfill his promises. However, the reality is that these promise are no different from the promises given by his predecessor, Netanyahu, especially concerning the Palestinian Cause—the core of the Arab-Israeli conflict.
