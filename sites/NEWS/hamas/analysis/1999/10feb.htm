10 February 1999

 

Between freezing the Wye River agreement
and the Israeli elections

The peace process in the region is experiencing a new stage of freeze after the declaration of early Israeli elections to be held on 17 May 1999¾ 18 months prior to their normal date¾ and after the Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu announced the freezing of the implementation of the Wye River agreement claiming that the Palestinian Authority did not abide by the provisions of the agreement, especially those related to the security issues.

It seems that the Palestinian-Israeli negotiations will become again subject to the wavering political life of the Jewish State. The Israeli elections will witness attempts to polarize public opinion and gain votes by giving promises.

When we review the Israeli elections map, we find that it includes various groups, many of which are known rivals, representing all political colours of contradicting tendencies. These include:

Left          : Labour, Meretz, Gesher and the Communist Party
Right  : Likud, Tsomet and Moledet
Religious  : Shas, Agudat Israel and the National Religious Party (Mifdal)
Centrist     : Centre Party
Racial  : Israel B’aliya (immigrants of Russian origin)
Arab lists  : United Arab List, Hadash (Jewish-Arab communist), the National Democratic                       Bloc and the New Arab Party

The Israelis may run two electoral rounds: the first to elect members of the 120-seat Knesset, while the second will be specialized to elect the Prime Minister among the candidates who occupied the first and second position in the first round if none of them gains more than 50% of the votes in the first round.

The Israeli elections coming up this year has witnessed an apparent phenomena, that being the split. At the Right, the Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu succeeded in becoming the Likud’s candidate for the prime ministry elections after the Likud lost a number of its prominent leaders such as Dan Mirodor, the former finance minister; Ze’ev (Beni) Begin, the Right extremist; and Yitzhaq Mordechai, the former defence minister.

Regarding the Labour opposition party, it seems that Ehud Barak, chief of the party, is the only candidate for the prime ministry elections at the present moment.

The creation of the Centre Party by prominent figures such as the former Chief of Staff Amnon Shahak, and Mordechai, who left the Likud and who was nominated as the new Party’s candidate for the prime ministry elections, as well as Roni Melo, the former mayor of Tel Aviv, reflected the deep split inside the Israeli society on political and personal grounds.

Despite the fact that the Palestinian Arabs of the 1948-occupied Palestine are divided into four lists and despite the fact that a large portion of them refrain from supporting any of the lists or even from showing any interest in the elections, the members of the lists agree, to a considerable extent, on one objective that goes in harmony with the Israeli Left, that is to topple Netanyahu and to take part in any Labour government in future. It is expected that Chairman Yasser Arafat will play an important role in directing these lists to support the Israeli Left, a thing that seems to be the best and only option for the Palestinian Authority.

As for the Islamic Movement within the 1948-occupied Palestine sector, it has declared that it would not run in the Israeli elections, nor would it support any candidate. This decision came after the reunion of the Movement following the decision made by Abdullah Darwish and his group to return to the Movement under Sheikh Ra’ed Salah.

The date of the Israeli elections was advanced after the Right parties had abandoned its full support of Netanyahu’s government in the Knesset because it signed the Wye River agreement. This situation was seized by the Labour party to press the Likud to accept the advancing of the Israeli elections. This indicates the increase of the political influence of the extremist Israeli Right. It also shows that Netanyahu has lost his known ability to maintain a balance between the continuation of the peace process at its lowest level and the appeasing of the extremist Right by suspending the implementation of the agreements with the Palestinian Authority.

The Peace Process exposed to storms
Under the present situation, the region seems to be witnessing a period that its basic features are difficult to predict given the absence of U.S. pressure on the Israeli government to abide by its agreements with the PA. The U.S. Administration cannot exert any pressure on the Israeli government because the U.S. President is busy with his impeachment trial and because his administration is busy dealing with crises in Iraq and the Albanian Kosovo. Moreover, the American role retreated to only the coordination with Europe to reach a deal with the Palestinian Authority in order to convince it to postpone the declaration of the Palestinian State.

Also, the options before Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu in order to win the new elections support possibilities of seeking to enhance his electoral position through selective bombings against Lebanon as his predecessor Shimon Peres did before the previous elections. On the other hand, Netanyahu will follow a hard-line policy with the Palestinian Authority in order to remain in power over the next four years. This means he will continue in his position to freeze the Wye River agreement.

Many political analysts opine that the peace process has actually become exposed to storms and that Netanyahu’s government may resort to a military operation, especially after the appointment of Moshe Arens as Defence Minister. Arens is known for opposing all agreements with the Palestinians, which increases the possibilities of an Israeli military adventure in South Lebanon. The agreements between the Israeli government and the PA will continue to be frozen or subject to amendment or rejection in future as long as each new Israeli government announces that it is not committed to agreements reached by the former government. This shows how fragile the future of the peace process really is.

PA… declaration of the State… and the game of alliance with the Israeli Left
“The Palestinian leadership will abide by the fourth of May as the date to declare the Independent State, and it will not abandon this date,” said Dr. Asaad Abdel Rahman, member of the PLO Executive Committee and the one who is in charge of the Refugees portfolio, on 23 January. But this issue has become one of the cards being used by the Palestinian Authority to influence the Israeli elections. The Labour and the Centre parties are seeking to convince the PA to postpone the declaration of the Palestinian State until the end of the Israeli elections. Barak and Mordechai fear that the declaration of the Palestinian State before the elections will give the Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu a chance to take extremist and hard-line positions, which will make him gain more Israeli votes.

It seems that the Palestinian Authority, under U.S. and regional pressure, will postpone the declaration of the State claiming that it will gain an international decisive promise to stop Jewish settlement and to support the Palestinians in building an independent state before the end of this year. In addition, the PA is hoping that the U.S. Administration and the European Union will guarantee the implementation of the Wye River agreement, including the third phase of redeployment of the Occupation troops in the West Bank, of which the percentage will be determined during negotiations and not by the Israeli government alone.

However, it seems that the Palestinian Authority will not succeed in stopping the Jewish settlements nor will it be able to force the Israeli government to implement the Wye River agreement. For the settlement issue cannot be stopped by any Israeli government. On the contrary, the survival of any government depends on its support of the settlements. As for the implementation of the Wye River agreement, it will depend to a large extent on the party that will assume power in the Jewish State. The signed agreements will have no real weight but their implementation will depend on the previous positions of the Labour and Likud towards these agreements, which focus on the necessity to amend their provisions in ways that serve their interests and positions.

The Labour’s experience with the implementation of the agreements when the Party was in power proved that the Labour role in suspending the implementation of the Oslo accords was similar to the Likud’s role in freezing the Wye River agreement. The Palestinian Authority has an overall control of only 3% of the West Bank and joint control with the Occupation troops of 24%. The settlement activities did not stop. On the contrary, they markedly increased without a public fuss such as what happened with the Abu Ghneim settlement and the way in which the Likud government dealt with it. Labour, under the cover of the economic summits and the normalization of relations with the Arabs, was pretending that it abided by the peace process and wanted to give the Palestinians their rights. The ‘Grapes of Wrath’ massacre against the Lebanese village of Qana in April 1996 was committed by the Labour Party, not the Likud. One of the journalists was right when he said the only difference between the Labour and the Likud is that the first builds ten settlements but announces only two, while the latter builds ten settlements and announces all of them. Moreover, the present leaders of the Labour and the Centre parties are hard-liners. Both were chiefs of the Israeli army, which makes the political difference between them very small.

Therefore, the future does not carry good omens for the Palestinian Authority. Also, the game being played by the PA in the Israeli arena is a natural result of the state of weakness in which the PA has been suffering because of isolating itself from the Arab and Islamic world. It is also a result of its policy of fighting the resistance option that is being adopted by the Islamic movement in Palestine instead of attempting to invest it on the negotiations table.

The Palestinian Opposition and its role
If the Palestinian Opposition forces had responded to the nullification of the Palestinian National Charter by holding a series of national conferences in the Occupied Territories and in Damascus to reach a Palestinian position rejecting the relinquishment of the rights of the Palestinian People, would they do anything that might affect the status quo in the Palestinian and the Israeli arenas?

It seems that the Palestinian Opposition does not prefer one political party to another in the Jewish State whether it is from the Right or the Left. This is because the differences between the two are very small and formative. However, their main programmes regarding the issues of settlement, Occupied Jerusalem, Palestinian refugees and the Palestinian issue are very close in general.

The unification of the Palestinian factions and independent figures against the nullification of the National Charter, although on a very low scale, opens the door for finding a formula of cooperation among the different forces of the Palestinian People to revive the spirit of resistance among them away from the calculations of the Israeli elections.
