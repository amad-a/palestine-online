18 May 1999

4 May—Oslo’s Epitome of Failure

The fourth of May passed by without heralding any real achievements for the Palestinians. Neither the Palestinian State was declared by the Palestinian Authority nor the Palestinians’ hope to see the removal of occupation realized. In fact, the Zionist Occupation troops only withdrew from a tiny area of the Palestinian territories.

Five years after the signing of the Oslo accords, the outcome appears to be an incomplete self-rule. The final status talks have not started as yet because the PA could not oblige the Israeli side to implement the provisions of the Oslo accords according to their scheduled dates. The timetable of redeployment of the occupation troops was subject to change and deletion according to the Israeli minds.

The Oslo agreement was an attempt to settle the Palestinian Cause through the dismantling of the struggle elements and through separating them on the basis of theme and time. The Oslo process also postponed the strategic and sensitive issues to the final status negotiations. It gives the Palestinians self-rule on a limited percentage of the Palestinian territories occupied in 1967 against a security role by the Palestinian Authority, which serves the interests of the Occupation, and against the relinquishment of what remained from the Palestinian land as well as the establishment of peace and mutual recognition between the Palestinians and the Israelis.

But the ambiguity of the provisions of the Oslo accords and the detailed agreements that followed made the Israeli side impose its own perspectives regarding the implementation of these provisions. Even the clear provisions were changed by the Israelis to serve their interests such as what happened when the redeployment of the Israeli occupation troops in the West Bank was not implemented in Hebron, which was divided into two parts: (H1) and (H2).

Although all the Israeli troops were completely redeployed from all the Palestinian towns in the West Bank, 20% of Hebron remained under the direct Occupation security control.

On several occasions, the timetables of redeployment were altered in the towns and in the villages and other rural areas. In the end, Netanyahu invented a new zone different than the A, B and C areas, which are stated in the Oslo accords, calling it natural protectorate in order not to be given back to the PA.

In addition, the safeguarded passages between Gaza and the West Bank have not been opened as yet. The Israeli authorities released only a few of the numerous Palestinian prisoners and detainees under different security pretexts. And the Palestinian territories remain subject to closure whenever the Israelis feel like it, giving little regard to the Palestinians protests.

The most significant aspect of the Oslo accords was the so-called security coordination between the occupation authorities and the Palestinian Authority, which was detailed in the 1994 Cairo agreement. It was also one of the main provisions in all of the detailed agreements between the two sides. The Wye River agreement added a new dimension to the security cooperation by involving the CIA in monitoring the PA’s implementation of the security provisions in the agreement. This created new burdens and pressures on the Palestinian Authority along with the pressures already exerted by the Israeli government to oblige the PA to abide by Israel’s security instructions to run after, arrest and try members and supporters of the Palestinian Resistance.

In terms of figures, the Oslo accords failed to established the PA’s superiority over the self-rule areas which, until now, cover only 30% of the total area of the West Bank (5,800 square kilometres) and 60% of the Gaza Strip area (360 square kilometres). The Israelis kept sovereignty over the border exits, the main roads among the West Bank towns and between the West Bank and Gaza, and the natural resources, especially the water.

Moreover, the Occupation authorities kept the upper hand over the security issues in all areas of the West Bank and Gaza. The agreements failed to check the ongoing activities of Jewish settlement, which increased significantly during the Rabin-Peres and the Netanyahu governments, albeit in varied means.

The consecutive waves of settlement resulted in the separation of the northern part of the West Bank from its western part and enhanced the separation between the West Bank towns. The Israelis are completing their plans to Judaize Jerusalem at all levels, making any talks regarding the status of Jerusalem useless. As for the return of the refugees and displaced persons, this issue remains within a theoretical framework of quadrilateral and multilateral working groups. Israel rejects the return of any Palestinian refugee to his home. There was an offer by Israel to allow the return of 200,000 of the 1967-displaced Palestinians but over a period of 50 years!

In addition to the failure of the Oslo accords in stopping the settlement expansions, repatriating the refugees and establishing Palestinian superiority over Palestinian land, the PA failed to run the self-rule areas to any degree of satisfaction, given that corruption, favouritism, bureaucracy, suppression of the Palestinian citizens, and control over the public and press freedoms runs rampant. These failures were felt by the Palestinians in and out of the occupied territories.

During the course of the above circumstances came the “4 May” issue. The PA tried to cover up its failures by attempting to declare a Palestinian State without actually owning any of the real basics of this State. The PA’s objective in declaring Statehood was to exert pressure on the Israeli side to implement what remained of the Wye River agreement. But the advancing of the Israeli elections stripped the PA’s attempt of its essence and power and pushed the self-rule authority to seek and obtain American-European promises to support the implementation of the agreements with the Israelis and to reach a final resolution with the coming Israeli governments.

Within this framework, a meeting was held at the end of April with the PLO Central Council. The PA wanted to obtain a cover to postpone the Statehood declaration in response to American, European and Arab requests, which the PA succeeded in achieving. The Central Council did not declare the State claiming that it was already established in 1988 by a decision made by the Palestinian National Council at its 19th session. The Central Council, however, called for the completion of the elements of this State and its institutions even though the PA does not own the mechanism to complete these elements. Nor can they be made available under the current agreements because of the continued Israeli military, political and economic domination over the self-rule areas. The different parties in Israel: reject the establishment of a real Palestinian State; insist on keeping Jerusalem the “eternal and unified” capital of the Jewish State; fight to keep the Jewish settlements, refusing to dismantle them (except for some small and scattered settlements which the Labor Party has no objection to the dismantling as long as it is within the framework of the final status settlement); and reject any Palestinian control on the border exits and any Palestinian military presence west of the Jordan River.

Although the declaration of a Palestinian State under the current status quo would not form a real achievement, the PA preferred to adjourn it against promises to implement the remaining parts of the Wye River agreement, believing that the Oslo accords—not the Palestinian People—have become its only choice.

Hence, 4 May 1999 has passed without the realization of the PA’s expectations of the return of the Palestinian territories and its independence. It is likely that many similar dates will pass without having these expectations realized because the Oslo accords are not the proper mechanism to achieve the goal of liberation and independence.

The passing of five years since the Oslo accords without achieving anything of essence concerning the Palestinian People and their land is a strong indication of the failure of these accords. It again confirms the position and viewpoint of the Palestinian forces that oppose the Oslo process. It proves that the resistance programme that is based on the right of the Palestinian People to resist the occupation is still the only realistic means to restore the legitimate rights of the Palestinian People.
