8 June 1999

Israeli elections: Results and implications

A careful reading into the outcome of the elections of the 15th session of the Israeli Knesset indicates important changes. It also shows that the reasons for these changes focus on ethnic, economic and personal factors and that the political factor did not play a big role this time because of the similarity of the platforms of the main Zionist parties, especially their respective position regarding the current peace process.

Contrary to the wishful thinking of the supporters of the peace process, the new map as drawn by the Knesset and the coming Israeli government will not have a strong positive effect on the process.

Review of the results
Following are the details comparing the trends and results of the Israeli parties, which succeeded in occupying seats in the Knesset, to the elections of the 1996 14th session of the Knesset.

1. The Left parties
A. The “One Israel” list, led by Ehud Barak (a coalition of parties of Labour; Gesher, led by David Levy who split from the Likud; and Meimad, a small religious movement that supports a peace settlement with the Palestinians and calls for an understanding between the non-secularists and the secularists), gained 34 seats in the 1996 elections. It gained 26 seats in the recent elections, a loss of 8 seats.

B. Meretz Party, led by Yossi Sarid, occupied 10 seats in the 1999 elections compared to 9 seats gained in 1996, a gain of 1 seat.

C. One People Party, led by Amir Peretz, gained 2 seats in the 1999 elections. Peretz is a former leader of the Labour Party and head of the Histadrut. The Party was established recently to defend the workers and the retirees.

Accordingly, the leftist parties have gained 38 seats in the 1999 elections of the Knesset, losing 5 seats from the 1996 Knesset.

2. The Centrist parties
A. The Centre Party, led by Yitzhak Mordechai, did not participate in the 1996 elections and occupied 6 seats in the recent elections. The Party was established by the former leaders of the Likud and the Labour.

B. Yisrael B'aliya (new immigrant), led by Natan Sharansky, gained 7 seats in the 1996 elections and 6 in the recent May elections. It is the party of the Russian Jews and voted this time for Barak while they voted for Netanyahu in 1996.

C. Shinui (change) Party is a newly formed party led by Tomi Lapid. The party participated in the 1999 elections after splitting from the Meretz Party and gained 6 seats. Shinui is a secular party and politically extreme.

D. The Third Way Party, led by Avigdor Kahalani, occupied four seats in the 1996 elections, yet failed to gain any seats in the recent May elections.

Therefore, the Centrist parties occupied a total of 18 seats in the 1999 Knesset, marking an increase of 7 seats from the 1996 elections.

3. The Rightist parties
A. The Likud, led by Benyamin Netanyahu (resigned), gained only 19 seats in the last elections although it occupied 32 seats in the 1996 elections.

B. Yisrael Beiteinu Party, led by Avigodor Liberman, participated in the 1999 elections for the first time and gained four seats. Established by Liberman, a former advisor of Netanyahu, the party is another one for the Russian Jews. Its supporters voted for Netanyahu in the Prime Minister elections.

4. The Extreme rightist parties
A. The National Union Alliance, formerly led by Benny Begin, gained three seats in the last elections compared to two seats in 1996. It is a coalition of three parties: the new Herot, led by Benny Begin who split from the Likud; Tokoma, a new settlement party that supports the idea of Greater Israel and established by former figures of the religious Mifdal party; and the Moledet, led by Rahba’am Zevy who supports the “optional” expelling of the Palestinians.

B. The National Religious Party (Mifdal), led by Yitzhak Levy, gained five seats in the last elections compared to nine seats in 1996. It is classified as a religious party for the Jews of Western origin.

The total number of seats of the rightist and extreme rightist parties in the new Knesset is 32 seats, a loss of 8 seats from the previous Knesset.

5. The Religious parties
A. Shas, led by Arie Deire, gained 17 seats in the last elections compared to only 10 seats in 1996. It is the party of the oriental Jews (Sephardim).

B. The United Torah Judaism is an ultra-Orthodox party for the Western Jews led by Meir Bouruch. It gained five seats in the last elections compared to four seats in 1996.

Accordingly, the total number of seats gained by the religious parties in the 1999 Knesset is 22 seats compared to 14 seats in the 1996 Knesset.

6. The Arab parties
A. The Arab United List (Ra’am), led by Abdel Malek Dahamsheh, gained five seats in the May elections compared to four seats in 1996. It is a coalition of a wing of the Islamic Movement, the Arab Democratic Party and the Communist Party.

B. The Democratic Front for Equality (Hadash), led by Mohammad Baraka, gained three seats in the new elections compared to five seats in 1996.

As for the election of the Prime Minister, Ehud Barak gained 56.1% of the votes against 43.9% gained by Netanyahu.

We can conclude the following:

1. Netanyahu was heavily defeated and the Likud was the biggest loser for it lost 13 seats it was occupying previously in the 1996 Knesset.

2. The Labour Party was the No. 2 loser since it lost eight seats. Taking into consideration that the “One Israel” list included Gesher and Meimad, which won a total of three seats, Labour actually lost 11 seats.

3. The No.3 loser was the National Religious Party (Mifdal), which lost four seats because some prominent figures in the Party had split to form the Tokoma party, which joined the National Union Alliance under Benny Begin.

4. The biggest winner was Shas of the Sephardim Jews, which gained an additional seven seats, making it the third biggest party after the Labour and the Likud.

5. The failure of a number of racist extremist leaders such as Rafael Eitan (Tsomet party) and of political extremism such as Avigdor Kahalani (Third Way Party) because of their inability to gain the minimum 1.5% of votes.

6. A number of newly established parties recorded a relatively reasonable success in the May elections such as Shinui and the Centre.

7. The racial and sectarian factors and their interests played a significant role in the formation of the electoral coalitions and lists such as the Russian Jews, the Sephardim Jews and the Arabs. These factors attracted the attention of the Israeli electors.

8. In continuing the old phenomena, the military figures enjoyed more popularity than the political leaders.

9. The Arab lists maintained their number of seats in the Knesset which were gained in the 1996 elections. They also succeeded in gaining one more seat. Now there are 13 Arab members occupying seats in the Knesset (9 from the three Arab lists, two from the Labour, and one each from the Likud and Meretz).

Why Did Netanyahu Lose?

It was clear that there was an overwhelming desire among the Israeli parties and the Zionist public alike to get rid of Netanyahu. This is attributed to some personal characteristics of Netanyahu such as his selfishness, his arrogance, his tyrannical behaviour, his lying, his deceiving, his swindling, and the marginalization of the people. These characteristics were a direct reason why his partners in the ruling party left him such as Mordechai, Meridor, Milu and Begin. This is also what made a man such as Shamir, the former Likud leader and Prime Minister, not vote for him and not even for the Likud. Shamir accused Netanyahu of destroying the Likud.

Netanyahu’s term recorded a marked reverse in the economic growth and an increase in the rate of unemployment, which in turn developed racial and opportunistic links and lists. Barak and the Labour Party were clever when they succeeded to neutralize the security element (which Netanyahu had claimed for himself) by stressing on Barak’s military record. As Barak was on equal footing with Netanyahu regarding the security issue, the Israeli voters preferred Barak for his attitudes regarding the social and economic issues.

Although the U.S. Administration declared it would not interfere in the Israeli elections, it also had the desire to remove Netanyahu. Therefore, it supported Mordechai and encouraged him to form the Centre Party. Later, it encouraged him to tactically withdraw from the elections to leave Netanyahu and Barak alone in the race. Barak had also brought three American experts to help him in his election campaign.

On the other hand, the frustration with Netanyahu’s policies and the encouragement by the Palestinian Authority, convinced 94% of the Arabs of 1948-Occupied Palestine to vote for Barak.

The exact description of Netanyahu’s defeat is that it aimed at stripping Netanyahu of power more than being a support for Barak. What happened was a personal defeat for Netanyahu.

Indications of the elections

  1. A semi-consensus existed among the top three Israeli parties (Labour, Likud and Shas) regarding the rights of the Palestinian People, especially regarding the issues of Jerusalem, Jewish settlements, Palestinian refugees and the importance of security and peace as a base for the peace process, and not the principle of “Land for Peace”. They also have similar attitudes concerning the rejection of the Palestinian independent State, and the return to the borders of 4 June 1967.
  2. The peace settlement and negotiations with the Palestinian Authority were not basic items on the platforms of the major Israeli parties, especially Barak’s party, which focused on the economic issues and the split and fragmentation within the Zionist Society. Therefore, Barak’s victory should not be seen as a change in the Zionist trends towards giving the Palestinian People their legitimate rights.
  3. The change in the number of seats held by the Zionist parties in the Knesset does not reflect a social, ideological or political change in the Zionist Society. But rather it refers to a change of positions among these parties for local reasons concerning the interests of these parties or their leaders.
  4. The economic regression was a direct reason for forming the electoral lists and coalitions on racial or opportunistic grounds.
  5. The unification of the Zionist stand and the prevention of more splits and fragmentation have become well-known Zionist demands. This was reflected in Barak’s slogans and his initial plans to broaden the government coalition.
  6. The tension and coolness that affected Israel’s relations with the United States and Europe during Netanyahu’s term pushed the Zionist Street to vote for Barak and against Netanyahu in order to improve Israel’s image in the world, especially in the United States.

The future of the Peace Process in light of the new change

The combination of members of the new Knesset gives Barak various options to form his government. But it does not seem that Barak prefers a narrow leftist coalition. The current negotiations to form a new Israeli government indicate that the likely formation is a national unity government or a centrist leftist government allying with some religious parties.

Anyhow, and regardless of the formation of the new Israeli government, the positions of this government towards the peace process will be governed by the Labour’s vision and by the balance between the religious and rightist parties. This will make these positions similar, in general, to those of Netanyahu’s government, especially regarding the final status issues, differing only in the tactics used.

It is obvious that Barak will restore the negotiations to their track, but he will not implement the agreements with the Palestinians easily. For he stated, after winning, that he would study these agreements before giving his opinion. It is likely that Barak will give priority to negotiations on the withdrawal from South Lebanon and to reach an agreement with Syria concerning the Golan Heights.

Barak’s military history and his positions are more hard-line than those of his predecessors Rabin and Perez. This was evidenced in his famous “four Nos”: No to negotiating the future of Jerusalem as a unified and eternal capital of Israel; no to the return to the 1967 borders; no to dismantling the Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza; and no to the existence of a “foreign” army West of the Jordan River. This will make the positions of the new government in the final status talks very similar to those of Netanyahu’s positions with the exception of some tactical differences.

The new government may show a relative response in the peace process but this may happen after some time. It is also expected to last for a short period because of disputes that will more than likely arise over the implementation of the agreements.

One of the negative outcomes of this change may be the return of the Arab parties to rely on the Israeli party to achieve a reasonable political settlement. This could also be followed by the resumption of normalization of ties with the Jewish State under the pretext of encouraging its government to go ahead in the implementation of the agreements.

However, the Arab reaction toward the new change in the Jewish State was not at the same level of the positive reactions toward the outcome of the Israeli elections in 1992. This is attributed to the fact that the Rabin-Perez government was not encouraging concerning the progress of the peace process. But time will prove the tendencies of the new Israeli government cannot be relied upon by the Arab parties.
