Deir Yassin remembered

Occupied Jerusalem (Palestine Times) - In the context of commemorating the Nakba (Arabic for Catastrophe), which takes place parallel with Israel's ostentatious and high-profile celebrations of its 50th anniversary, Palestinians have been marking the 50th anniversary of the Deir Yassin massacre. On 14 April, a memorial meeting organized by the "Deir Yassin Memorial Society" was held in East Jerusalem. Participants included Arab and Jewish political activists, historians, and representatives of the diplomatic corps, as well as a few survivors who painstakingly recounted their nightmarish experience 50 years ago on 9 April, when members of two Jewish paramilitary groups, the Irgun and Stern murdered, en mass, around 250 men, women and children in the peaceable Arab village.

Speakers stressed the landmark status of the massacre in contemporary Palestinian history, as it represented the general pattern employed toward the Palestinians--first by the Jewish terrorist groups and later by the Israeli State.

"The massacre was a stark but representative example of the Israeli policy toward our people," said Yousef al Qazaz, a noted broadcaster and writer.

He added that the Deir Yassin genocide was followed by similar grisly massacres at Kufr Kassem, Dawaymeh, Kibya, Eyun Kara, Sabra and Shatilla, Hebron and Kana, which showed the Zionists were emulating their former oppressors.

Lea Tsemel, an outspoken Jewish critic of Israeli racism towards the Palestinians, pointed out that the massacre of Deir Yassin was a deliberate evil act on the part of the Zionist leadership aimed at "casting terror into the hearts of the Arabs for the purpose of driving them away in order to accommodate Jews."

"Deir Yassin was not the only village decimated and obliterated...As many as 400 Arab villages were systematically destroyed," she said.

Dr. Hasan Silwadi, Dean of the Jerusalem Arab College, refuted the oft-repeated Israeli claims that the massacre was an isolated incident in the "war of liberation." He quoted several Israeli historians, like Aryeh Yitshaki and Bney Morris, who admitted (belatedly) that the massacre was part of a premeditated plan aimed at liquidating the Arab majority around Jerusalem as well as in the Palestinian midland.

Silwadi pointed out that the massacre was a key factor in the escape of hundreds of thousands of Palestinians from their towns and villages who thought that what happened at Deir Yasin would happen to them if they stayed.

The participants were brought to tears when a survivor of the massacre, Haja Zaynab Aqel (um Salah), recounted how her father and three children were gunned down and how she herself was going to face the same fate had she not pretended to be dead.

Latter on, Um Salah, along with some participants, commuted to the site of the massacre at Deir Yassin, now renamed Giva'at Shaul.

There the Israeli authorities have obliterated all but a few vestiges of what was once a prosperous Arab village. However, Um Salah's home, where her father and three children were butchered before her eyes by the Irgun terrorists, is still intact. The occupant of the premise, a pharmacist, emerged shouting at Um Salah, who was at that time deeply in tears, "Who are you and what do you want?" "Nothing," she said, "nothing."

According to Larry Collins and Dominique Lapierre, the Deir Yassin genocide was "a deliberate act aimed at frightening the Arabs into fleeing the area." Even Menachem Begin, one of Israel's arch terrorists, describes the massacre in his book, The Revolt, on pages 164-65, as "the biggest miracle to take place in the war of liberation because it made the Palestinians flee..."

Interestingly, Deir Yassin was only one massacre out of many, as the list of Jewish atrocities of the helpless Palestinian people is too long to be described in one article or even in one book.

Over 500,000 Palestinians arrested by Israelis since 1967

Nablus- The International Solidarity for Human Rights issued a report that the Israeli occupation authorities have arrested about 535,000 Palestinians between 1967 and 1987. It added that more than 3,000 Palestinians are presently being held in Israeli jails. The Palestinian human rights group released the following details and figures regarding Palestinian detainees:

* Israel arrested about 175,000 Palestinians during the years of the intifadah (1987-1994)

* 12,500 Palestinians have been arrested since the signing of the Oslo Accords on 13 September 1993

* 10,500 Palestinians have been arrested since the signing in Cairo of the Oslo 2 agreement on 4 May 1994

* 6,000 Palestinians were held in custody at the time of the signing of the Taba agreement on 28 September 1995

* 114 Palestinians have died in Israeli jails since the occupation in June of 1967 of the West Bank and Gaza Strip

* Yousef Jabali was the first Palestinian to die under interrogation in the Central jail of Nablus in January 1968

* Abdel Qader Abul Fahem was the first Palestinian detainee to die because of a hunger strike on 11 July 1970

* The first hunger strike staged by Palestinian detainees in Israeli jails was reported in Kfar Yona and Ramleh jails in 1967

* The longest hunger strike staged by Palestinian detainees lasted for 45 days and started at Ashkelon jail on 11 December 1976

* Ali Awadh Jamal of Jenin, north of the West Bank, spent the longest period of administrative detention (without trial), which lasted for 6 years and 9 months. He was released in March of 1982, but remained under house arrest until February of 1984.

* Khalil Al Ra'e of Gaza is the senior Palestinian prisoner in Israeli jails. He has been serving a life imprisonment sentence since March of 1974

* The eldest Palestinian detainee is Mohammed Nu'erat of Maithalon near Jenin. He was born in 1929. He has been serving a life imprisonment sentence since March of 1967

* Hassan Salameh of Khan Younes (Gaza), 27, is serving the longest sentence ever issued by an Israeli court. Salameh (Hamas military leader) was sentenced to life imprisonment for 46 times plus 20 years, a total of 1,170 years!

* About 200 Palestinians are presently held in what is called administrative detention (without trial) in three jails

* 350 detainees suffer from various diseases, including 70 cases that need urgent treatment

* 100 detainees being held in administrative detention are under 18 years of age

* About 800 detainees are serving long imprisonment sentences, including 450 sentenced to life imprisonment

* 176 Arabs from Lebanon, Syria, the Golan Heights, Jordan, Egypt, Algeria, Libya, Sudan and Iraq are held in Israeli jails, including 18 Lebanese who served their full sentences but are still being held in administrative detention

* 40 Palestinian detainees are from the 1948-occupied Palestine. They were charged with affiliation with the Palestinian Resistance

Liberty Calls for Protection of Palestinians from Israel and PA

London-PT

Liberty for the Muslim World expressed its concern over the fate of hundreds of detainees in Palestinian Authority (PA) jails, saying that past experience has shown that PA interrogators do not hesitate to kill suspects who fail to come up with the required confessions.

Liberty denounced the Israeli government for applauding the PA, its 'peace' partner, for detaining and torturing Palestinians on the mere suspicion of association with the military wing of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas).

"It is regrettable that the United States government has played a major role in creating a serious rift within the Palestinian community. This rift is likely to lead to the most dangerous inter-Palestinian feud since the PA was created in Oslo in 1993", a statement by Liberty said.

"Both Israel and the United States are held responsible for having pressured the PA to act in order to prevent Hamas from responding to what it insists has been an Israeli assassination of one of its leaders, Mohyiddin Ash-Sharif. The PA has acquiesced by renouncing its initial position of accusing Israel of being responsible for the murder. Instead, the PA has devised an incredible scenario accusing Hamas itself of liquidating one of its acclaimed heroes."

Liberty condemned the Palestinian Authority for adopting as an official policy the use of torture during interrogation. "PA security agents have repeatedly applied extremely painful physical and psychological pressure on a number of Palestinian detainees in order to extract confessions from them that they took part in the killing of the Hamas' military leader," the human rights organization said. "

Liberty pointed out that several members of the Addassi family, including women, have been severely persecuted in order to confess to a crime they have consistently maintained their innocence from. The only confession PA interrogators have been able to obtain is that of young Ghassan Addassi, who now insists his confession was extracted from him under duress. In a letter smuggled out of prison, Ghassan said:

"I gave that testimony after having been subjected to torture and threats I had never before seen in my life. They threatened me with the charge of collaborating with the Israelis and that I took part in the assassination of Mohyiddin on orders from the Israelis. I was beaten considerably, I was denied sleep for three successive nights and I was hung from my hands and suspended from the ceiling of the prison cell."

Liberty exposed the role of PA officials in faking accusations against the Addassi family. "Senior PA officials such as Jebril Ar-Rajoub, head of security, and Tayyeb Abderrahim, spokesman for Yasser Arafat, took part, personally, in the interrogation and punishment of detainees. According to Ghassan Addassi, who now fears for his life, it was one of these two officials­Ar-Rajoub­who threatened him that if he did not reiterate his confession before some other PA officials who intended to visit him, his sister­whom they claimed was detained in a nearby cell­would be 'abused'. Fearing for the life and honour of his sister, Addassi repeated his confession before Hatem Abdulqadir, member of the Palestinian Legislative Council. When he told another visitor, Sheikh Mahmud Muslih, that his confessions were false and that they were made under duress, the visitor himself was arrested and imprisoned in order not to speak to the media or the public," Liberty said.

Liberty considered that the measures adopted so far by the PA point in the direction of a conspiracy to conceal information and most probably to vindicate Israel from the assassination of what it has itself always described as its most-wanted enemy. "Several key witnesses have been detained, and a number of Hamas officials who questioned the credibility of the alleged PA investigation have been arrested and jailed. The media, which sought to cover up the case, has not been spared either. The Reuters' office in Gaza was shut down, and a number of journalists working for the agency were arrested and forced to sign a declaration that they would refrain from pursuing the matter further."

Liberty for the Muslim World called for immediate measures in order to protect the Palestinians under occupation from "a new plot hatched against them by Israel and its allies in the Palestinian Authority, which has­very regrettably­proven to be no more than another arm of Israeli occupation," adding that it would not help the cause of peace and stability in the region for the Palestinians to fight each other. "What is really needed is for Palestinian rights to be recognized and for the rule of law to prevail instead of the rule of the growing PA Mafia," Liberty concluded.

Arafat's police round up hundreds of Palestinians

Occupied Jerusalem- Following the murder of Mohyiddin al Sharif on 29 March, Palestinian Authority police detained as many as 200 Palestinian Islamist activists. The detainees included prominent Islamist figures such as Dr. Abdul Aziz al Rantisi, Dr. Ibrahim Makadmeh, as well as student leaders at the Islamic University in Gaza and the Najah National University in Nablus.

Moreover, PA police arrested a number of the Khalas (Islamic Salvation party) members for "expressing non-conformist views incompatible with the official line." The party's official weekly newspaper, al Resala, said PA interrogators abused detained members in a variety of ways, including "beating, cursing, insulting and making threats."

The PA said the arrest of several Khalas members took place after Khalas criticized the spate of arrests and administrative detentions of Islamist elements by the PA police in the aftermath of Sharif's murder.

An Islamist spokesman in Gaza said PA police raided homes in Gaza, Khan Yunis and Rafah, usually after midnight, "in ways very similar to past Israeli practices."

Amnesty International, the London-based human rights organization, expressed anxiety over the illegal detention of Islamist activists at the hands of PA police in possible coordination with Israel.

Amnesty International issued a statement in mid-April saying several detainees were being tortured in PA interrogation and detention centres. The international human rights organization cited Abdul Aziz al Rantisi as "a prisoner of conscience." It pointed out that he was being held incommunicado as he was prevented from coming in contact with anybody.

The ostensible justification for placing Rantisi behind bars lies in several statements he had made to the international media in which he described as "nonsense and silly" the PA account of Mohyiddin al Sharif's death.

Free At Last

The Israeli occupation authorities finally released from "administrative detention" Ahmed Katamesh, the longest-serving Palestinian administrative detainee. Katamesh, a political activist associated with the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP), spent 68 months in Israeli jails for reasons he still does not understand. The Israeli occupation authorities refused repeatedly to free him or formally charge him of any wrongdoing, arguing that he posed a danger to public safety and to the security of the State and to the IDF.

"They told me you will die a slow death here; they wanted to break my will," said Katamesh on arrival home in Ramallah, adding that "my ordeal was in the normal order of the Zionist occupation because justice and occupation are inherently incompatible."

Katamesh said in an interview with the Arabic daily al Quds on 22 April that "administrative detention was meant to inflict emotional and psychological pain on Palestinians critical of the Israeli occupation."

"They (the Zionists) act as criminal sadists who enjoy seeing us suffer...this being the whole point behind incarcerating us for so many months without charge or trial."

Katamesh castigated the Oslo accords, saying the accords were "an American-Israeli dictate on a weak Palestinian leadership."

Katamesh, 48, was released after Israeli Arab and Jewish activists opposed to administrative detention launched a sustained campaign to win his release.

Another long-serving PFLP activist, Hasan Fatafta, was also released with Katamesh. Fatafta spent 48 months in administrative detention. Palestinian and human rights sources say there are as many as 300 Palestinian administrative detainees languishing in Israeli prisons, not knowing why they are being imprisoned or when they will be released.

Moreover, the Zionist authorities still detain as many as 3,500 Palestinian prisoners as "bargaining chips." The Oslo accords and the subsequent Hebron Protocol stipulated their release, but the Zionist authorities have so far refused to honour either agreement.

Netanyahu to include Fascist Party in his government

Occupied Jerusalem (25 April) Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu announced on 23 April that negotiations were under way with Rahba'am Zeivi, head of the ultra-racist Moledet party, for the purpose of including the party (2 members in the Knesset) in the government coalition.

An Israeli government spokesman said the talks with Moledet reached a decisive juncture, adding that "the details remained to be ironed out."

Moledet, a Jewish Nazi-like party, calls for the expulsion of all non-Jews from the West Bank, the Gaza Strip and Israel proper, the Palestinian territories occupied by Jewish terrorist gangs in 1948.

A number of Likud and Labor party officials have criticized the intention to include Moledet in the government.

Yossi Belen of the Labor Party described the move as a "disgrace."

Even some of Netanyahu's allies objected to the imminent inclusion of Zeivi in the government on the grounds that this would be "a liability rather than an asset."

Palestinians Mark Prisoners Day

By Steve Sozby

The Annual Palestinian Prisoners Day in Palestine was on 18 April. To mark the occasion, thousands of Palestinians in and out of Israeli prisons marched, demonstrated and clashed with Israeli soldiers throughout the West Bank and Gaza Strip. Over 3,000 Palestinian political prisoners went on a one-day hunger strike to protest their continued detention in Israeli jails. "Today is Palestinian Prisoner's Day, and the detainees in Israeli jails are marking it by going on a one-day hunger strike to protest their continued arrest," said Hisham Abdel-Razek, the Palestinian Authority (PA) official in charge of prisoners' affairs.

Meanwhile, on the streets of Palestine, thousands of people carrying placards that read: "There can be no peace until Prisoners are released", and "Prisoners are the heart of the Palestinian Issue Now" demonstrated in solidarity with the striking prisoners. In East Jerusalem, Israeli troops broke up a march with tear gas and clubs, injuring several civilians and arresting a half dozen more.

A campaign is being mounted by various forces within the territories to confront the continued detention of over 3,500 Palestinian political prisoners in violation of the Oslo Accords. According to Israeli government figures, Israel continues to hold 3,648 Palestinian prisoners. Since the signing of the 1993 PLO-Israel peace deal, the Jewish State has freed more than 7,544 Palestinians in partial fulfilment of signed accords. For years Palestinians have increased their demand that Israel fulfill its signed obligations and release the rest of the prisoners.

Under interim peace deals signed between Israel and the Palestinian Authority, Israel was supposed to free Palestinian prisoners in three stages: after the 1995 West Bank deal was signed, before Palestinian elections in early 1996, and during final status talks. The final status talks are as far off today as they were when the accord was first signed.

"There can be no peace without the release of thousands of Palestinian in Israeli jails," said Um Jihad, the wife of the slain Palestinian leader and the PA Minister of Social Affairs. She led a demonstration outside of the International Red Cross Committee on the eve of the visit to Gaza by British Prime Minister Tony Blair, who was asked to intervene with Israel to further the cause of Palestinian political prisoners by PA leaders.

Palestinians are not the only people suffering inside Israeli prisons. On 20 April, an Israeli court extended for another six-month period detention orders against 21 Lebanese prisoners held as "bargaining cards" in negotiations over missing servicemen in Lebanon. These hostages include Sheikh Abdel Karim Obeid, the Hizbollah cleric who was abducted by IDF commandos in 1989, and Sheikh Mustafa Dirani, another Islamic leader who was kidnapped from his home in Lebanon in 1994. Under Israeli law, so-called administration detention orders are subject to judicial review every six months. "Iran and other Middle Eastern countries are called 'terrorist States' and suffer international sanctions for suspicion of supporting the taking of hostages," says Lebanese journalist George Karim in Beirut. "Israel blatantly holds Lebanese nationals, who have not committed any crime, as hostages and no one says anything. It is this type of hypocrisy that breeds so much anger in the Arab world."

On 15 April, the longest held administrative detainee, Ahmed Katamesh, was released after spending more than five years in an Israeli prison without charge. Katamesh, a journalist suspected of being a member of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP), expressed mixed feelings at his home in the West Bank town of el-Bireh following his release. "I am torn between happiness at being home with my family... and the painful memory of the death of my mother, father and brother--I have just come from the cemetery--and my anger at the policy of administrative detention.''

Israel is not the only country holding people without charge or trial in prison. The PA continues to hold 31 suspected Hamas activists without charge following the alleged inter-Hamas killing of Mohyiddin Sharif in Ramallah earlier in April. Dozens of other Palestinians remain in PA prisons without trial or charge for opposition to the Oslo Accords. In a move that reflects Israeli measures in the West Bank during the intifadah, the PA closed the Reuters Gaza office on 9 April for "distributing false news" related to the assassination of Sharif.

While both Israeli and PA officials employ arbitrary measures to deal with opposition, a growing movement on the streets of Palestine is confronting the issue of political prisoners through an international campaign. On 15 April, a Middle East prisoners aid group began collecting signatures of 1 million people in and outside of Palestine, urging Israel to release the remaining 3,500 political prisoners. Dr. Haider Abdel Shafi, a former member of the Palestinian Legislative Council, was the first to sign the petition, which was organized by the Palestinian Society to Defend Prisoners in Israel. "This campaign aims to approve the legitimacy of our cause, and to tell Israel that in the era of peace, there must not be one single Palestinian prisoner in its jails," said Hisham Abdel Razek, a PA official and the head of the Society, who spent 19 years in Israeli prisons.

Political prisoners in Israeli and PA jails continue to be a rallying cause for a grass-roots movement on the streets of Palestinian towns, camps and villages. The longer the Netanyahu government continues to hold thousands of prisoners in violation of the Oslo Accords, the greater the movement will grow to demonstrate and confront Israeli forces throughout the region. This will certainly lead to further bloodshed and violence in the immediate future.

Israelis destroy 170 Palestinian homes in 1997

Occupied Jerusalem (25 April) The Israeli occupation authorities destroyed in 1997 as many as 170 Palestinian homes throughout the West Bank as part of punitive measures against the Palestinian population.

A report published on 24 April by the Jerusalem-based Legal Centre for the Protection of Land said most of the demolished homes were located in area "C", where the Israeli army maintains complete control.

Some of the destroyed homes, however, were in area "B", where Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority is supposed to exercise "civilian control".

The demolition of three homes late last year (in full view of Palestinian policemen) at Asira al Shamaliya, which is under PA civilian control, is a pertinent example.

The report also pointed out that the Israeli government had transferred large sums of money to the settlements for expansion.

Moreover, the report put the number of Jewish settlers throughout the West Bank and the Gaza Strip at 161,000, of which 155,000 are in the West Bank and the rest in the Gaza Strip.

Finally, the report pointed out that the Israeli government was about to transfer 3,500 Ethiopian immigrants to West Bank settlements.

Palestinian, Jewish settler killed in shoot-out

Occupied Jerusalem- A Palestinian villager was seriously injured and a Jewish settler was killed on 23 April in a shoot-out that took place outside a Jewish settlement southwest of Hebron.

According to Palestinian sources, the incident occurred when three Jewish settlers opened fire at a group of Palestinian peasants, apparently in an attempt to drive them away and seize their land.

The peasants reportedly resisted the three settlers, described as "trigger-happy," and seized their weapons.

In the ensuing quarrel, one settler was killed and two others sustained gun wounds.

Palestinian sources said it was highly likely that the settler was killed by bullets mistakenly fired by fellow settlers.

One Palestinian was critically injured and died later in hospital.

An Israeli government spokesman claimed the incident was "a terrorist attack."

However, eyewitnesses denied this, contending that the settlers started the shooting.

"They have been harassing us for many years, and their aim is to intimidate us into leaving our land so that they can build more Jewish settlements thereon," said Musa Makhamreh of the village of Yatta, whose cousin Muhammed was badly injured in the shoot-out.

Another Palestinian, Mahmud Dababseh, also of Yatta, argued that the settler killed in the incident had previously provoked Palestinian peasants with sinister acts, including shooting at their livestock, burning their fields and spreading poisonous substances on the grass on which their herds graze.

On 23 April, the PA allowed Israeli Shabak interrogators to question the Palestinian shepherd at his hospital bed in Hebron.

Meanwhile, the Israeli army carried out a spate of arrests in Yatta, which is supposed to be "a liberated Palestinian area."

Local sources said as many as 30 villagers were arrested during nightly raids on villagers' homes in Yatta between 20 and 23 April.

Hamas wins big in student elections at major Palestinian universities

Occupied Jerusalem (25 April) The Islamic Bloc (al Kutla al Islamiy-ya) has accomplished a series of successes recently by winning the "lion's share" of students' votes at major Palestinian universities in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. The Bloc, which is loosely affiliated with Hamas, won the biggest share of votes at al Najah National University in Nablus, the Hebron University, the Beir Zeit University, the Islamic University in Gaza, the Al Quds (Jerusalem) and the Polytechnic University in Hebron, as well as in other student elections at several other smaller colleges in the West Bank.

The Islamic Bloc actually was able to dislodge PLO supporters from some of their strongholds such as that at the Najah University, where the pro-Fatah Shabiba dominated the student council for eight consecutive years prior to the Oslo Accords and the establishment of the Palestinian Authority.

The successes of the Islamist camp in student elections at Palestinian institutions of higher education has been studied and analyzed.

According to Professor Omar Abdul Razzak, professor of Economics at the Najah National University, the Islamist successes were made possible thanks to the free college environment whereby Islamist activists could speak out freely and convincingly, challenging and refuting some of the long-established views on a variety of subjects.

Abdul Razzak said the repeated encroachment on academic freedoms by PA security agents (such as raiding college campuses and student dorms) shifted sympathies in favour of the Islamists, as many students felt indignant at the uncivilized behaviour by an authority that claims to be a "liberator of the people" but one which is acting as "tormentor of the people."

Abdul Razzak added that Islamist successes in Palestine should be viewed in the context of the overall Islamic revival the world over. This assessment is shared by Munzer Mashaki, a noted leader of the Islamic bloc at the Najah University. He says the popularity of the Islamic bloc was only a reflection of the overall Islamic revival, adding, however, that the "Islamic Choice" has always been popular among the more educated segments of society.

Garaudy's life in danger

Doha- The French Muslim thinker Rajaa Garaudy said he received threats on his life claiming he would not complete this spring alive. The Qatari Al Raya Arabic daily quoted Mr. Garaudy as saying that because of those threats he obtained a license to carry a weapon. French police believe that Jewish extremists and Zionist activists in France may be standing behind these threats against Garaudy, known for his hostility towards Zionism. The Zionist organizations had launched a campaign against him and proceeded against him before a criminal court because of his book, "The Founding Myths of Israeli Politics".

Palestinian universities call to protect their students' right of education

Nablus- Al Najah National University in Nablus, north of the West Bank, last month organized a rally to express solidarity with students who cannot reach their universities because of obstacles imposed by the occupation authorities. The rally mainly meant to support about 1,200 Gazan students who study in the West Bank universities because of being prevented by the Israeli soldiers to cross Israeli checkpoints on their way to their universities in the West Bank. Most of these students take a longer time to graduate than the students from the West Bank because of the Israeli arbitrary measures. Speakers at the rally urged the Palestinian Authority and the international human rights organization to exert efforts in order to facilitate crossing Israeli checkpoints by the Gazan students. One of the Gazan students said more than 20 Gazans studying in the West Bank could not go back to Gaza to bury their dead parents or relatives because they were afraid of not being allowed to return to their universities in the West Bank.
