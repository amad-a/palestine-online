Press Cuttings

 

Ha'aretz, December 24, 1998
Hamas founder Sheikh Ahmed Yassin was released yesterday from two months of house arrest in his Gaza home, and immediately went to pray in a nearby mosque.
Tomorrow is the final day of the ultimatum presented by Iz a Din al Kassam, the military wing of the Hamas, for Yassin's release.
Speaking to reporters in his house yesterday, Yassin declared the Wye accord a failure, and said his release was a sign of that.
"At the time that Wye was signed, the intent was to prove that no voice was higher that that of Wye," he said. "Today, after the sound of Wye has become inaudible, it is inevitable that other voices will become audible."
Yassin added that Hamas' fundamental beliefs had not changed. Our pact with God is more important ... We will hold on to our strategy," he said.

The New York Times, December 24, 1998
Muslim guerrillas in Lebanon fired Katyusha rockets into Israel Wednesday, wounding 13 people. The attack was retaliation for the killing of a Lebanese woman and six of her children in an Israeli bombing raid on suspected guerrilla targets on Tuesday..
Israeli Defense Minister Yitzhak Mordechai expressed regret, saying the deaths were accidental. An army spokesman said the family's house had been hit by mistake in an air strike aimed at a Hezbollah training base and communications station near Janta in the Bekaa.
The Israeli Army chief of staff, Lt. Gen. Shaul Mofaz, said in a television interview that a pilot's "incorrect target identification" had caused the bombing error.

The Washington Post, December 24, 1998
With Israel now on its way to early elections, the most interesting wild card in the contest may turn out to be the Palestinians.In the campaign for prime minister in 1996, Palestinian militants played a major role in Binyamin Netanyahu's come-from-behind, upset victory. A series of Palestinian suicide bombs three months before the election provided a critical boost for Netanyahu and his view that Israel had to get tough and slow down the peace process. So it was not by chance that on Monday, when Netanyahu rose in the Israeli parliament to deliver what amounted to his first campaign speech, he spent most of his time attacking the Palestinians, not his domestic adversaries on the left and right.

The Washington Post, December 23, 1998
A day after Israel's parliament gave initial approval to a measure that would dissolve the government and trigger general elections in the first half of next year, the campaign season opened abruptly with a new challenge to Prime Minister Binyamin Netanyahu. A pillar of Netanyahu's ruling Likud party, Dan Meridor, announced that he would form a centrist party and oppose Netanyahu in the race for prime minister. And the latest of several opinion polls showed Netanyahu starting the race behind his presumed main rivals -- including a former army chief of staff who has yet to announce any political plans.

The Financial Times, December 22 1998
The Middle East peace process faces months of delay after Israel's parliament last night voted overwhelmingly to dissolve itself and call new elections, putting an end to the divided government of Benjamin Netanyahu. After intense debates and last-minute efforts to form a government of national unity, 81 of the 120 MPs in the Knesset voted for early elections. Thirty were opposed, four abstained and five were absent. The decision means further implementation of the Wye peace accord with the Palestinians will be on hold until at least the spring. A committee will meet next week to decide the date of the poll, which could be any time within the next several months.
