Press Cuttings

Ha'aretz, 26 April 1999

The Likud's official party platform for the upcoming May 17 election does not rule out territorial compromise on the Golan Heights. While the platform calls for strengthening Jewish settlement on the heights captured from Syria in 1967, it also calls for negotiations with Damascus over the Golan, without pre-conditions, and does not specifically rule out an Israeli withdrawal.

The Chicago Tribune, 24 April 1999

Netanyahu's government ordered the closure of the Palestinians' main offices at Orient House this week, propelling the issue into the Israeli election campaign and raising concerns about public disturbances over a building that the Palestinians consider a symbol of their refusal to give up Jerusalem.

Netanyahu says his government is closing the Orient House offices because the diplomatic visits violate the 1993 Oslo peace accords. Until a final peace deal, the accords forbid Palestinians from having official representation in the city that Israel controls but that both Israelis and Palestinians claim as their eternal capital.

The Independent, 23 April 1999

AN official report on the 1992 crash of an El Al jet into a block of flats in Amsterdam has found a "direct link" between the crash and the medical problems of residents of the suburb where the cargo plane came down.

The Israeli government admitted last year that the cargo included chemicals used in the production of the deadly nerve gas sarin.

Severe criticism was levelled in the report at senior ministers in Prime Minister Wim Kok's government for their handling of the affair. A number of ministers have already said they will not stand down but MPs may order a vote of confidence to force them out when they debate the findings in a few weeks.

The Guardian, 22 April 1999

Egypt's prosecutor general has ordered the trial of four journalists from a pro-Islamist opposition newspaper for allegedly libeling the deputy prime minister, who is also agriculture minister, in articles criticising his ministry's co-operation with Israel.

The decision is part of a crackdown against the opposition press in Egypt, which the government claims is needed to stop sensationalist reporting but which is denounced by journalists and human rights groups as an attempt to muzzle freedom of expression.

Libel cases are tried in criminal courts in Egypt. If found guilty, the journalists could face a minimum of two years in prison.

The New York Times, 20 April 1999

JERUSALEM -- Israel has adopted a compromise plan to resolve a violent dispute in Nazareth between Muslims and Christians. The plan would allow a small mosque to be built next to a planned plaza for millennium pilgrims.

Muslims set up a protest tent and held prayers at the site, next to a structure revered as the tomb of Shihab a-Din, a 12th-century Muslim leader who died in a Crusade battle. Asserting that the land is the property of an Islamic trust, Muslim leaders announced plans to build a sprawling mosque there.

Salman Abu Ahmad, a leader of the Islamic Movement, which has the majority in the council, denounced the government plan.

"We cannot give up sacred land that is an Islamic trust," Abu Ahmad said. "We will not leave this land. We will stay there and continue our prayers."

The Los Angeles Times, 17 April 1999

Alarmed by the rapid pace of the (Jewish) settlement-building, the Clinton administration is turning up the heat on the Israeli government to stop the construction. In a series of unusually pointed rebukes, U.S. officials recently called the expansion "very destructive" to the peace negotiations and accused Israel of reneging on a promise to contain it.

..The aim: to keep the land from being turned over to the Palestinians in any permanent peace agreement.

"We're pushing in order to establish a reality," said Rabbi Natan Greenberg, the director of a small Jewish seminary at Bat Ayin and a former member of its town council. "We want to make sure we keep as much land as we can under Israeli control."
