Sovereignty over Jerusalem: Issues of concern

Muqtedar Khan*

The only reason why Israel is willing to even consider making any concessions for peace at this moment is because it expects to get the best possible outcome now. The balance of power between Israel and the Arabs is decisively in Israel’s favour. The honest broker–Uncle Sam–is firmly in Israel’s pocket. With the disappearance of the Soviet Union, the Arabs have no one to turn to. The Arabs in general and the Palestinians in particular can neither threaten Israel with “dire consequences” nor promise any major benefits in the post peace scenario. They can only remain a nuisance value to Israel and constantly spoil its victory parties. Israelis have realized that while they may make peace with governments, peace within the peoples is too far away to even feature as a factor during the negotiations.

Israel has achieved nearly all its goals! A Jewish State has been firmly planted in the heart of the Arab world. All members of the Arab world have explicitly or tacitly acknowledged and accepted the Jewish State. Except for a few organizations such as the Muslim Brotherhood and militias such as Hamas that Arab governments are determined to marginalize, no one anymore challenges the existence of Israel or rejects its colonial origins. Israel now has more land than it had wanted and also has significant assets, such as the Golan Heights and the West Bank and Gaza, that it can bargain away for peace. Israel, with its state-of-the-art technology and having the richest economy in the region as well as a monopoly over nuclear weapons, can now think of standing on its own even in its tough neighbourhood. And it has complete control over Jerusalem–its so-called eternal and undivided capital–in its entirety!

So why does it want to talk peace and why even suggest its willingness to share Jerusalem? There are several reasons. Any change in the region with regard to distribution of power or economic wealth will probably be at Israel’s expense. Israel is at its zenith and the Arabs are at their nadir. Things can only get better for the Arabs and worse for Israel. So negotiated peace can result in very favourable circumstances for Israel. Israel also realizes that with the eternal control over Jerusalem comes the eternal enmity of Arabs/Muslims. In the entire history of Islam, enemies of Islam have never succeeded in taking and keeping Jerusalem from Muslims for more than 70 years. History clearly suggests that the odds are against Israel’s keeping Jerusalem through force.

The growth of Islam in the U.S. and the West is another consideration. Slowly the balance of power in the sense of leverage over Western foreign policies is moving in the direction of Muslims and will certainly reduce Jewish control over Western policies in the Middle East. So it is best that Israel makes peace now and forever eliminate the so-called Islamic threat and keep the gains it has made in the last 50 years.

Now that we understand why Israel is willing to make peace, let us consider issues with regards to sharing sovereignty over Jerusalem. There are two issues that Arafat and his aides must focus on. They are the extent and content of sovereignty. What does Israel mean by “sharing” and what does Israel mean by “Jerusalem”?

Sharing control can imply spatial/geographical division of East Jerusalem with each party enjoying complete and exclusive control over the divided regions. But then this would mean “dividing” Jerusalem. Is this what Israel is willing to concede? I don’t think so. They are suggesting sharing governance over some designated areas. While Israel will have complete and exclusive control over most of Jerusalem, some areas will be designated as Arab Jerusalem and will become the capital of Palestine. Arafat and his cronies will have “control over some aspects”, such as municipal issues and law and order, and they will be allowed to have their offices there.

Will foreign embassies to Palestine be allowed to have their offices in Jerusalem? This is not clear. Will the Israeli army have the right to enter the area to hunt Hamas activists? Not clear.

I believe that Israel will not concede “exclusive control” over any space or issue of Jerusalem to Palestine. They are merely looking for some “vague concept” that will enable the Arab leaders to build upon using the rich language of Arabic to save their defeated and pathetic faces.

As a Muslim, who is neither an Arab nor a Palestinian, I am neither interested in Palestinian “nationalism” or “Arab pride”. I personally believe that once free of Israel and into the “clutches of Arafat”, the people of Palestine will have less freedom to practice Islam and more pressure to worship him and his ruthless State. There is no reason why one should expect a free Palestine to be more Islamic or more democratic than any of the other present-day Arab regimes. I am also convinced that “Arabism” and “nationalism”–two ideas nurtured by colonial interests and Arab Christians and Arab socialists–are the biggest enemies of Islam in the region and responsible for its current pathetic state, so beautifully captured by the cameras when Barak was seen physically herding and pushing Arafat into the slaughterhouse as Clinton watched and waited.

I am interested in Baitul Maqdas: the Islamic holy site, not the city or the State. Who gets to keep it and then what happens? Who do I have to pay hundreds of dollars to in order to go to the place I revere out of love for my Prophet and my submission to my God? And, of course, be treated like an animal while on pilgrimage. But that is another story. Meanwhile, we wish that Arafat emerges with some real control over Baitul Maqdas and all the important mosques, not just some suburbs of East Jerusalem. The optimists among us should feel free to line up outside wherever Palestine will have its embassy for a “visa” to get Arafat’s permission to go and pray in Masjid Al-Aqsa and to celebrate its return to true Muslim control while the cynics ponder over how Muslim will Arafat’s control over these places be.

*Dr. Muqtedar Khan is Assistant Professor of Political Science at a liberal arts college in Michigan. He writes on International Relations, Globalization, Foreign Policy and Islam.

It’s not a question of legality!

Dr. Musil Shihada

There are no legal bases that can support the existence of Israel: no Biblical myths, no Balfour Declarations, no dispositions by the League of Nations or even United Nations resolutions 181 and 194. The Biblical argument–if taken seriously–would grant the seed of Abraham all the areas between the two great rivers: the Nile and the Euphrates (Genesis 15:18). This claim is so preposterous given that it would necessitate the relinquishing of all the countries between the two great rivers, consequently causing an additional 100 million Arab refugees having to join the Palestinian refugees in places only God knows where. The Balfour Declaration and subsequently the League of Nations (1922) exemplifies the apex of travesty when the British, who were assigned as a trustee to Palestine, gave away land that did not belong to the donor in the first place. The UN contravenes its own charter when it involves itself in partitioning a country between two disputants when such an act is beyond the jurisdiction of the international organization. Even then its resolution could not have been binding since it was approved under Western pressure in the General Assembly and not the Security Council. If one seeks other international conferences such as Geneva and The Hague, one would find that it would be a crime to dispossess or evict anybody from his home or property, which Israel has flagrantly violated. Therefore, Israel has no legal status at all if all international laws, conventions and charters were to be researched. To avoid being called an outlaw State and to keep its image unblemished in the eyes of the world, Israel still clings to an artificial thread of legality by insisting that the UN, in accordance with resolution 181, created it. If we were to entertain such a position, Israel would still have problems because it has flagrantly violated the very resolution that created it. The resolution prohibited the expulsion of anyone from either State to the other, and it certainly made it illegal to seize somebody else’s land by the use of force. Had Israel abided by the exact implementation of the UN resolution, we would have, at least, avoided the emergence of the tragic refugee problem. The semi-legality of the existence of Israel is therefore directly related to the emergence of the State of Palestine. What else would the Partition mean? To implement half of UN resolution 181 and violate the other half negates the legality of the whole resolution.

Therefore, we should realize that Israel’s existence on our land is not a question of legality as much as an illegal fait accompli realized through the use of force while supported by the Western powers. All subsequent UN missions directed at Israel in trying to rectify its violations of all such international legality have been practices in futility. In May 1949 at Lusanne, Israel accepted the principle of peace with the Arab delegations by promising to implement the UN Partition in its entirety while allowing the Palestinian refugees to return to their homes and properties in accordance with resolution 194. Such conditions were proposed by the French delegate to the Lusanne conference and were supported by the United States as pre-requisites to its admission to UN membership. Once Israel was finally admitted to the UN, it reneged on its commitments, and the following 50 years witnessed all kinds of turmoil, wars and violence to make Israel honour its commitments under the UN regulations. It’s no secret that Israel has declared more than once that it cannot withdraw from occupied Arab territory nor will it honour UN resolutions supporting the return of the refugees.

After the Intifadah and the subsequent Oslo accords, Israel did not change its policy one iota vis-à-vis its withdrawal from the occupied lands or the right of return of the Palestinian refugees. Therefore, no one in his right mind should expect Israel to negotiate peace with the Palestinians based on international legality as legality would downsize Israel to unacceptable land allotments while diluting its Jewish-ness with the undesired return of non-Jewish Palestinian refugees. By accepting to implement UN resolutions 181 and 194, Israel would contravene its Jewish exclusivity over Palestine and render its “security” needs vulnerable and at the mercy of the surrounding Arab regimes. One then cannot escape from the irritable conclusion that all peace negotiations–whether in Oslo, Palestine, Sharm El-Sheikh or Washington–have one thing in common: Israel (the mighty party) can only dictate its terms of total surrender to the weak and vanquished Palestinian party. No negotiations will ever succeed between two parties if one is strong and greedy while the other is weak and desperate.

If a framework of legality is the basis of any negotiations, then we should realize that the return of the homeland and the right of return of the Palestinian refugees are NOT negotiable. These issues are to be resolved within the demands of international law and not by the moody dispositions of the parties to the dispute. Maybe a method of implementation should be organized with Israel but not whether the issues at hand should be placed on the agenda of negotiations. The right of return is an inherent right and a basic human right that should not be subject to discussion or any further deliberations. Such important issues should not be left to public consensus or any party’s opinion.

Any approach that does not place these issues in their proper framework will render any negotiations a practice in theatrics. No Palestinian leadership has the right to deliberate on these issues just as no one has the right to dispose of the land that belonged to our ancestors and should automatically belong to our next generation. I think it should behoove our leaders in Washington to simply pack up and leave and start organizing from square one. We should not underestimate the strength of the Palestinian card. We still hold the future of peace and war in our hands; we are still the bridge without which Israel could never cross to enter the entire Arab and Muslim world. We hold the card that can still decide the destiny of Israel and the whole Middle East. We should never succumb to the might of the oppressor as our determination can in the long run give us our rights to the land and the repatriation of our people.

Camp David II: Will Israel try
to fool history, again?

Ramzy Baroud*

Israel’s overwhelming dominance over the Palestinian leadership throughout their relatively long negotiation experience is little contested. The acknowledgment of such a realization, however, can by no means negate the fact that the Palestinian people, with humble means and scarce meaningful support, have undoubtedly maintained their ability to survive as a nation with a just cause.

The Palestinian struggle goes back many years before Camp David I and II, as well as Oslo. Yet as conquerors came and departed, leaving behind devastated land and people, Palestinians stood solidly and patiently repaired the damage inflicted by their enemies, and with durability tried to heal their wounds and emotional scars. Israel, another conqueror, which was established where historical Palestine once stood, foolishly thought that it could trick history into changing its course.

Not as if much had changed since former Israeli Prime Minister Golda Meir uttered her famous statement, “There is no such thing as a Palestinian people...It is not as if we came and threw them out and took their country. They didn’t exist.” Sure, Israel’s politicians nowadays acknowledge the existence of such people, as if they were suddenly found, but the racist policy, which Israel was assembled upon, is still in place, highlighted by Barak’s “red lines”. According to Barak, Palestinians’ religious rights, territorial sovereignty and right of return are largely denied and non-negotiable. If Meir’s denial of the Palestinians’ existence is viewed abstractly, her statement is almost an exact match to Barak’s red lines (considering that the denial of the most basic human rights is a denial of existence and vice versa.)

Based on that shared Israeli belief, the Palestinian people ought to be removed from the scene at any cost so the Israeli myths could finally be claimed. What else could explain the expulsion of nearly a million Palestinians in 1948 by Jewish gangs and the killing of numerous numbers of innocent people and courageous fighters in the process? What could possibly explain the forcing of Palestinians into scattered and extremely impoverished refugee camps, denied of all rights yet forced to pay heavy taxes from which the Israeli “defense ministry” purchases weapons used to suppress the already overpowered refugees? Those Palestinians living outside the Israeli scope were neither safe from Israel’s reign of terror or extermination philosophy, as they too received their share of death and devastation. The massacred martyrs of Sabra, Shatila and Bourj el-Barajneh in Lebanon, and others hunted and killed in Cyprus, Tunisia and the rest of the world is a clear indication that Israel’s fight was not only aimed at the land of the Palestinians but at the Palestinian people themselves, no matter how far from Palestine they may have been.

But as Israel’s goal was to lay upon the Palestinian people a destiny like that of the Aborigine of Australia and the native “Indians” of the Americas, Palestinians appear to be nowhere near cultural extinction. And as the Palestinian problem ages, as more Palestinian land is confiscated, and as the number of refugees grows, the battle for justice keeps on raging.

For Palestinians, resistance has never ceased to be an option that picks up where “peace” talks fall short. The Palestinians’ fear of the Camp David II outcome is neither a sign of desperation nor does it mark the end of their journey. Palestinians have always loathed unworthy compromises that fail to recognize their full rights and that fail to acknowledge their decades of suffering. The same anxiety experienced now by many Palestinians was once felt as Egypt and Israel reached a Camp David agreement years ago. Rejection of the agreement turned into a fuel that prolonged the resistance for years, and Camp David II will not be the exception.

Palestinians, too, have options and strengths, although they may not be articulated as arrogantly and as viciously as Israel’s. Unlike what Israel had for so long misunderstood, strength is not only a piling up of weapons and artillery. Real strength stems from the spirit of justice and one’s unbendable belief in freedom, a trait that Palestinians will always possess.

* Ramzy Baroud is a Seattle-based Palestinian. For more about Ramzy’s writings, visit The Palestine* Chronicle at www.palestinechronicle.com

American policy toward the
Palestinian refugees since 1948

Dr. Daud Abdulla*

The world’s oldest and most intractable refugee problem is that of the exiled Palestinians. Since 1948 more than half of the entire Palestinian population have lived outside of their ancestral land. The immediate cause of their dispersal was the 1947 United Nations decision to partition their land and grant 56% of it to Jewish immigrants mainly from Europe. Driven by an apparent sense of guilt for their own persecution of European Jews, the victorious allies led by the United States sought atonement by supporting large-scale Jewish emigration to Palestine. When confronted with resistance from the indigenous people, the Zionist settlers in turn unleashed an unprecedented campaign of terror which triggered an exodus of Palestinians that has continued until this day.

Though essentially a political problem, the Palestinian refugee crisis soon developed into a major humanitarian disaster as an estimated 805,067 inhabitants streamed into camps in the Gaza Strip, Jordan, Lebanon and Syria. During the six months leading up to Ben Gurion’s declaration of Statehood on 14 May 1948, United States policy makers began to toy with the idea of replacing the Partition Plan with a UN trusteeship. Until 1947 the State Department had traditionally acknowledged Palestinian rights including that of self-determination. After the failure of the Partition Plan, however, the Americans were forced to devise a new strategy toward Palestine. To begin with, they encouraged the Jordanian take-over of all areas not occupied by the Jewish State and its absorption of the refugees. From the summer of 1948 all references to Palestinian rights, especially that of Statehood, disappeared from both internal and external records of the State Department. Throughout the subsequent decades the only Palestinian claims recognized by the U.S. were those of the refugees.

The first initiatives
Having recognized the Jewish State in Occupied Palestine, the United Nations itself adopted resolution 194 on 11 December 1948. It specifically stated that those “refugees wishing to return to their home and live in peace with their neighbours should be permitted to do so at the earliest practicable date.…” The resolution also called for the creation of a UN council to prepare the way for the return of the refugees. The council began its work in March 1949 with visits first to Beirut and then Tel Aviv. While the Arab States insisted on immediate repatriation before any negotiations could begin, the Israelis demanded a comprehensive settlement before considering the return of the refugees.

The Truman administration was particularly perturbed by the Israeli intransigence. The Americans had themselves played a key role in drafting the final version of resolution 194 which they believed should form the legal basis of all policies toward the refugee problem. It was in this context, therefore, that President Truman on 6 September 1948 gave his unconditional support to the proposals of the UN mediator, Count Folke Bernadotte, “that the local government in Israel should take measures to relieve the suffering of the refugees” by expediting their return at the fastest possible opportunity.

Bernadotte was, observably, no stranger to humanitarian crises. He had gained international acclaim in 1945 for his work on behalf of the International Red Cross to save thousands of Jews from Nazi concentration camps. One day before he was assassinated (16 September 1948) by a Zionist terrorist in Jerusalem, Bernadotte wrote that there was absolutely no chance of reaching a just and comprehensive settlement to the Palestinian Question unless their right to return was recognized. The story of his killing has since remained a standing testimony of the betrayal and injustice that has always been a hallmark of Zionism. For although the head of the Stern Gang, Nathan Friedman-Yellin, was sentenced to five years’ imprisonment for the murder, he was quickly pardoned and elected to the Knesset in 1950.

Count Bernadotte’s plea for the restoration of Palestinian rights did not go unheeded. While addressing the UN General Assembly a few days after the assassination, U.S. Secretary of State, George Marshall, urged the world body to adopt the mediator’s proposal, which he believed constituted the “best possible basis for realizing peace.” Several months later President Truman went further and issued an ultimatum to the Israelis. In a letter dated 29 May 1949, he told Ben Gurion that the U.S. was utterly dismayed with the Israeli violation of international law and warned that his administration may be forced to review its relationship with the Jewish State. When the Tel Aviv administration stood its ground, it was the Americans who had to humbly back down.

The Eisenhower years
With the arrival of Dwight D. Eisenhower to the White House in January 1953, American interest in the refugee problem entered a new phase. A dual approach was adopted and remained in force until 1967. It entailed, in the first instance, the provision of relief supplies and assistance to the refugees and secondly, a search for a practical political solution.

Shortly after taking office, Secretary of State John Foster Dulles visited the region and recommended that some of the refugees should be allowed to return. At the time it appeared that Congress was inclined to an economic rather than a political solution. It involved the exploitation of the waters of the Jordan River. Eisenhower was himself convinced that “the acceptance of a comprehensive plan to develop the Jordan Valley would help immensely in ensuring stability in the Near East.” It was envisioned that an estimated 300,000 refugees stood to benefit from the Jordan Valley Development Programme. Arab rejection of the plan and Israel’s insistence on continuing with its own water-carrier project aborted the American plan. In September 1953 Eisenhower ordered the cancellation of all aid to Israel pending its cessation of attempts to divert the Jordan River. The American message was clear as well as effective and it took the Israelis a mere two months to climb down from their pedestal of belligerence.

While the 1953 suspension of aid was enough to force an Israeli retreat from the waters of the Jordan River, it was, ostensibly, not enough to force the repatriation of the refugees. The Eisenhower administration therefore drifted toward the provision of material assistance while the search for a political solution continued. While unveiling the joint U.S.-British “Operation Alpha” on 26 August 1955, Secretary of State Dulles called for Israeli compensation to the tune of $280 million to be paid to the refugees. Both the U.S. and Britain had agreed that Israel should raise 30% of the funds while the former two would provide the rest through long-term, low-interest loans. The onset of the Suez crisis the following year aborted the plan and effectively brought to an end all efforts by President Eisenhower to solve the refugee crisis.

Enter AIPAC
Whereas the appearance of the American Israeli Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) on Capitol Hill in 1959 signaled the emergence of a new era of U.S. policy toward the region, it did not spell anything good for the refugees. Being the most powerful special interest group in the U.S., AIPAC succeeded in those early days to convince Washington that Israel was worthy of being a strategic ally in the Middle East. In the aftermath of the Suez crisis, the U.S. welcomed the notion of a regional ally to counter the growth of the Arab nationalist movement and the spread of Soviet influence in an area perceived as the soft under-belly of NATO.

Following the visit of Senator Hubert Humphrey to the camps in 1956, the question of the refugees continued to haunt policy makers. Humphrey warned that further failures to resolve the crisis would lead to a spread of communist ideas among the refugees. Accordingly, the Kennedy administration adopted a plan developed by Joseph E. Johnson, a former president of the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. Based essentially on UN resolution 194, the Johnson plan sought to apply the formula of repatriation or resettlement and compensation. The mere thought of considering repatriation was anathema to Prime Minister Ben Gurion. Thus in order to induce them the Kennedy administration offered the sale of major weapons to the Israelis with the hope that they would accept the Johnson plan. Although the Tel Aviv authorities accepted the American weapons, they categorically refused to reciprocate on the question of the refugees. Thus ended the last major U.S. initiative toward the refugees.

The fact that 82% of the Jewish electorate had voted for Kennedy in 1960 was no doubt a telling factor that prompted him to lift the 1947 U.S. embargo on the sale of weapons to the Zionists. Both his immediate successors, Lyndon Johnson and Richard Nixon, were considered even more supportive of Israel on major issues such as arms, territory and the refugees. The former’s belief that Israel was the “underdog” made it possible not only for the capture of East Jerusalem in 1967, but also the expulsion of another 500,000 Palestinians from their homes.

Meanwhile, the gains and position of the Israeli lobby machine–AIPAC–was considerably strengthened after the arrival of Henry Kissinger at the helm of the State Department during the Nixon and Ford presidencies respectively. Himself a Jewish immigrant from Austria, Kissinger played a major role in convincing the U.S. political establishment that the Arab-Israeli conflict was a by-product of the Cold War. Yitzhak Rabin paid tribute in his memoirs to Kissinger recalling, “The story of Kissinger’s contribution to Israel’s security has yet to be told, and for the present suffice it to say that it was of prime importance.”

Abandonment under Clinton
The acceleration of Zionist influence in the State Department after Kissinger coincided with a corresponding rapid decline in the fortunes of the refugees. Under the Clinton administration their plight degenerated significantly from bad to worse. Since his first inauguration in January 1993, President Clinton’s refugee policy had shifted from one of compromise to total abandonment. The current strategy pursued by Washington has been to dissolve the international nature of the refugee problem and confine it to bilateral negotiations between Israel and the Palestinian Authority. Toward this end, the U.S. has made several attempts to dismantle the United Nations Relief and Welfare Agency (UNRWA), the organization that has, since 1949, looked after the affairs of the refugees.

Similarly on 8 December 1993 the Clinton administration took the unprecedented step of refusing to support the reaffirmation of UN resolution 194 in the General Assembly. This, quite astoundingly, was the first time in almost 50 years that the U.S. had failed to support the very resolution that it had drafted. According to White House officials, the September 1993 Israeli-PLO accords have made all previous resolutions “obsolete and anachronistic.” Secretary of State Madeleine Albright summed up her government’s position on the refugees in a letter to members of the General Assembly dated 8 August 1994, “We believe that resolution language referring to the ‘final status’ issues should be dropped...These include refugees.” Thus in total violation of international law, the U.S. House of Representatives in June 1997 took the unprecedented step of recognizing Jerusalem as the “undivided and eternal capital” of Israel.

Having supported the Partition Plan, the U.S. had, in the immediate years thereafter, recognized its shared responsibility for the Palestinian refugee problem. After making several tentative political efforts to find a solution within the framework of UN resolution 194, the Americans gradually succumbed to Zionist pressure and abandoned the refugees. Aided by a favourable uni-polar international system, they have further seized the opportunity to impose an Israeli status quo over Jerusalem contravening the Fourth Hague Convention (1907), the Fourth Geneva Convention (1949) and UN Security Council resolutions 242 (1967) and 478 ( 1980).

For the handful of politicians and technocrats who wheel and deal in secret, international resolutions concerning the refugees and other final status issues may seem “obsolete”. Yet to the millions of Palestinians who continue to bear the burdens and share the memories of exile, their right to repatriation remains as “inalienable” as the right of every American to life, liberty and justice. Being signatories to all the international conventions on humanitarian law, the democratic nations of Europe must accordingly take immediate steps, with or without American support, to honour their treaty obligations and duties toward the Palestinian refugees.

Interview with Sheikh Ahmed Yassin

Yassin: Camp David summit liquidates
The Palestinian Question

Occupied Jerusalem- Interview by Khalid Amayreh

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) castigated the Israeli-PA summit talks at Camp David, arguing the talks were aimed at liquidating the Palestinian cause. The Movement’s founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin called the summit “an exposed attempt to liquidate the Palestinian Question.” He called on Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat to leave Camp David and return to Palestine. On 18 July, the Palestine Times interviewed Sheikh Yassin. The following are excerpts of what transpired.

Question: What are your expectations of the summit at Camp David?

Answer: I believe it is likely to end in failure because the Israelis and the Americans are seeking to impose surrender on the Palestinian leadership. Moreover, the Palestinian cause has deep historical and religious roots, and a few days of superficial discussions will not lead to a historical solution to the question of Palestine.

Q: Why do oppose the summit?

A: The summit is aimed first and foremost at pressuring and bullying the Palestinian leadership into surrendering to the Zionists and accepting a deformed settlement whereby Israel would retain its occupation of the bulk of Palestine, including Jerusalem, and would deny the Palestinian refugees the right of return. I hope the Palestinian leadership will be strong enough and brave enough and patriotic enough to withstand American pressure.

Q: What if it doesn’t?

A: Any agreement signed at Camp David that doesn’t meet the legitimate rights of the Palestinian people will be null and void, and the masses and the present and future generations will not be bound by it, neither in the present nor in the future. Even if a small group of Palestinians caved in, the vast majority of the people and the future generations will not accept it.

Q: So you don’t think that an agreement would end the Palestinian problem?

A: Absolutely. The Palestinian Question is a question of a lost homeland that needs to be liberated and usurped rights that need to be redeemed. A concocted deal here and there will prove futile, sooner or later.

Q: Do you feel that your opposition to the Oslo Accords has been vindicated in light of the Israeli rejection to give up Jerusalem and come to terms with the Palestinian right of return?

A: Well, our opposition to Oslo was vindicated from its very inception. Now, every ordinary Palestinian citizen realizes that Oslo was a gigantic disaster. The Oslo agreement is what we are witnessing today.

Q: Don’t you think Arafat will not buckle under American pressure?

A: We hope he will say no. However, it is clear that the Americans want to isolate him there and impose on him a capitulatory settlement whereby the Palestinians would give up the vast bulk of Palestinian and Muslim rights in Palestine, including Jerusalem and the right of return.

Q: Some say that Arafat has no choice but to take all that he can take in light of the balance of power?

A: No, this is wrong. If the current Palestinian leadership is powerless to liberate the land and restore the usurped rights, at least it shouldn’t concede. In any case, the task of liberating the land can be left for future generations if the present generations are powerless. Moreover, nobody is authorized to concede on the rights of the people. Nobody has the right to give up even an atom of Palestine. Palestine doesn’t belong to the Palestinians alone; it belongs to all Arabs and Muslims from Indonesia to Morocco.

Q: You called on Arafat to return to Palestine immediately?

A: Yes, because I am convinced, just as most Palestinians are convinced, that the Palestinians will lose from this summit however its outcome may be. Moreover, it is scandalous that we trust the United States as an honest broker. We all know the role the U.S. has been playing in tormenting out people and enabling the Zionists to persecute us and usurp our rights.

Q: What if Arafat returned to Gaza with an agreement that gave Jerusalem to Israel and scrapped the refugees’ right of return?

A: This would amount to a political suicide on his part. Who can concede on Jerusalem and concede on the right of refugees?

Q: This week, Hamas supporters joined Fatah supporters in urging Arafat to cling to the national constants. Does Hamas support these constants?

A: We, too, urge the Palestinian leadership to stick to the “red lines” it said it won’t cross. However, we in Hamas consider Palestine from the river to the sea as an Islamic land, and we can never lend recognition to the Zionist regime because it was built on oppression and usurpation of our homeland.

Q: It seems that virtually all the Palestinians will not accept any settlement that does not include the restoration of East Jerusalem?

A: Al-Quds al-Sharif (Holy Jerusalem) is the first Qibla (the direction to which Muslims turn their faces during prayer) of Muslims and the third holiest Islamic shrine in the world. Giving up Jerusalem would be an act of grand perfidy and unforgivable treason. Jerusalem belongs to the Muslim Umma, even to the yet-to-be-born Muslims. Nobody has any right to tamper with Jerusalem, and any agreement or pact or deal that does not include the full liberation of Al-Quds will be null and void.

Q: And the right of refugees? Do you think it can be implemented?

A: All heavenly and man-made laws and norms grant refugees the right to return to their homes. Nobody can deny me the right to return to my town and village from which I was wrongly expelled. The right of return is a sacred right and nobody has the right to give it away.

Q: Do you feel that Israel’s refusal to carry out UN resolutions pertaining to the Palestinian cause justify Hamas’ uncompromising stance?

A: This question should be addressed to the people of Oslo (the PLO) who made many Palestinians believe that the Oslo process would culminate in the implementation of UN resolutions. In fact, what you said shows that Israel understands only the language of force.

Q: So what is the alternative?

A: The alternative is to revive Jihad and resistance against the Zionist occupation. Only through Jihad and the mobilization of the Muslim Umma can we recover and extract our rights and lands from Israel’s hands.

Camp David revisited

Francis A. Boyle*

When the Israeli government originally presented the Oslo document to the Palestinian delegation to the Middle East Peace Negotiations in the fall of 1992, it was rejected because it obviously constituted a bantustan. This document carried out Menachem Begin’s disingenuous interpretation of the Camp David Accords–expressly rejected by U.S. President Jimmy Carter–calling for autonomy for the people but not for the land as well.

Soon thereafter, unbeknownst to the delegation and to almost everyone else, the Israeli government opened up a secret channel of negotiations in Norway. Magic thought and wishful thinking

Edward Said

Shortly after South Lebanon was liberated and Israeli troops evacuated the area they had occupied for 22 years, I had a discussion of the event with a good friend who works for the Palestinian Authority. When I said that as Palestinians we had a lot to learn from the Lebanese resistance, I was met with a flood of disclaimers and denials. Palestine and Lebanon are totally different, I was told, and to make comparisons is a serious error. I said that I certainly agreed that the two situations were different and that ours was indeed a more difficult one. However, I nevertheless continued by arguing that Hizbullah’s discipline, its willingness to make sacrifices and its extraordinary, relentless dedication to its objectives were things that applied to all situations, not just to South Lebanon. The response was even more adamant. I was told that we had no alternative but to do what we did at Oslo, and when I said that I could partially understand that but that I didn’t feel that despotism and corruption–the Authority’s unfortunate hallmarks–were the only alternatives after signing so disadvantageous an agreement with Israel, I was instructed definitively that I was taking a narrow view and that we are passing through a transitional period and these setbacks are part of that. When I remained unconvinced by all of this, I was reminded by my friend that I had no experience in real politics, which were not the province of academics and intellectuals.

Precisely this argument was echoed on 18 June in a column by Jim Hoagland of the Washington Post who when asking Abu Alaa about my criticism of the Authority’s behaviour could only get the following reply (rather a weak one, I must say): “It is not intellectuals who are going to make the deals needed for peace.” This is like saying that only a chicken can tell a good egg from a rotten one. Besides, Abu Alaa’s expertise in peace-making derives neither from education nor prior work (he used to direct the PLO’s factories in Beirut, all of which failed or went bankrupt). One supposes therefore that he was born to make peace deals, just as Louis XIV was born to rule France.

I would call this the first kind of magic thinking, a style of reasoning that blurs the distinction between truth and fiction so as to make a man-made, deliberately constructed disaster seem like a necessary or at least an acceptable thing. Members of the Authority follow their leader in assigning credit for Israel’s shabby withdrawal from South Lebanon to Mr. Barak’s desire for peace, not to military defeat. By the same token, they conclude that corruption and anti-democratic rule are necessary stages of history rather than actual choices made by them to be anti-democratic and corrupt. I suppose the main question is: Who do they think they are fooling by this logic? No sane person would fail to mark its dreadful lapses or its patent weaknesses. So then we must conclude that they are its victims, very few others.

A second type of magical thought is common to those whose position of great power allows them to be insulated from the facts, to impose on those facts a reading that is at total odds with what anyone else using common sense would see there. For the past seven years I have heard every American policy maker from President Bill Clinton on down sing the praises of the peace process, extol the new world that was coming into being, waxing ecstatic about the promise of peace and the age of prosperity that was dawning. Palestinians made brave noises about being like Singapore, an island of prosperity in a sea of poverty. Israelis built their rhetoric on equally sandy foundations. They looked forward to being welcomed all over the Arab world, marketing their goods from the Gulf to Morocco, doing business with everyone, and so on. Perhaps I should also add that all these effusions came from rulers, presidents, government functionaries and some journalists, in other words, all those people whose position of power allowed them VIP status, men and women who didn’t have to queue up at four in the morning at Gaza’s Eretz border, or those whose passports and identity cards in Lebanon, for example, designated them as aliens without rights, or those whose houses had just been demolished. Free of that kind of annoyance, then, these people of privilege could indulge in magical thought and wishful thinking to their heart’s content.

This isn’t just a matter of saying things that have no real connection to ordinary reality, but also imposing a logic on the past that simply spirits it away altogether. I first heard this kind of magical thought when young King Abdullah of Jordan made his first visit to the United States last year. While acknowledging all of Jordan’s political and economic difficulties, the king then shifted gears immediately after saluting Ehud Barak’s recent election victory in Israel. Once the peace process is back on track, he said, we can gain the kind of stability that will bring us prosperity and make Jordan a very attractive place for major foreign investment. This is an argument that American policy makers like to use: once we have “peace”, everyone will be happy and we go on to prosper, invest freely and make money, live happily ever after. I call this magic because it denies the weight of the past any role at all in the future, as if all the years of dislocation, suffering, dispossession and distortion imposed on those millions of Arab citizens who lost their families, homes, means of livelihood, who lived under military occupation, who were forced to endure states of emergency in Arab countries with scarcely any democracy or social and economic equality–as if all this with its burden of anger, sorrow, frustration, humiliation and sheer human fatigue–would suddenly disappear the moment a peace agreement is signed on Mr. Clinton’s lawn.

It is the essence of magical thinking, therefore, to make light of what is in fact heavy, that keeps a formidable pressure on everyone in the Middle East. This is not a matter of vindictive memory, but of living actuality. Israeli and American Middle East experts repeat like a mantra that young people have forgotten 1948 and are more interested in the local Internet café than they are in recovering or returning to their villages. How can that be so? Palestinian refugees in Lebanon and Syria and elsewhere remain Stateless aliens and, whether or not they cherish visits to the Internet café, they are compelled by their intolerably precarious status to recall 1948 and their inalienable right of return.

As for Palestinians who live in Palestine itself, of course they want to lead normal lives, send their children to school, get good medical treatment, travel and enjoy all the benefits of security. The fact that none of these things is really possible therefore compels them to ask why their situation is unlike that of Israelis, whom they see daily enjoying their much greater freedom and prosperity. Palestinians would have to be stones not to feel resentment and anger at why they must give up ancestral land to Russian Jews like Anatol Scharansky, who not only was born and brought up in Russia but is now challenging Barak not to give up Abu Dis, an Arab town which as a Russian Jew he feels he can dispose of at will.

These grotesque, not to say bizarre inequities and distortions suggest something far graver, more mutilating and wounding to the spirit than can be rectified by an imperfect peace treaty between a nuclear power like Israel and a poorly led, destitute people like the Palestinians. Only a miracle of thought–a sort of magic trick–can quickly set things straight, bring back tranquility and peace of mind and restore Arabs to a state of redemptive hope.

Unfortunately, the real world affords no such magic and only an occasional miracle. In the meantime, those who suffer must continue to do so–the mothers whose sons and daughters are in prison, the fathers who cannot cross into Israel for work, the teachers who remain on strike and thousands more like them–while those who fantasize about the quick benefits of peace plan more seminars, give more speeches and embark on new projects. But is there any hope at all that magic and reality can ever be reconciled? Alas, no.

(2-2)

We need not engage in a debate on history or a round of objections and counter objections. But some quick notes are in order on what the “Arab embellishment mentality” presented as givens. Despite the relative victories achieved by the Syrian and Egyptian armies in 1973, the strategic goal of the war for those two countries was not the liberation of Palestine. In the end, both armies were almost defeated. One entire Egyptian battalion was surrounded and would have been wiped out if it were not for international intervention to end the war.

The crisis of Arab governance after Iraq’s occupation of Kuwait and the ensuing intra-Arab disputes and military triumph over Iraq- which changed the strategic balance- cannot be separated from the governance of the Arab regimes. And who is responsible for the intra-Arab conflicts? Is it not the Arab regimes themselves? How was Iraq destroyed? Was it not due to the intensity of intra-Arab disputes and the use of Arab air space to reach Iraq?

The same thing goes for the Soviet collapse. Arab rhetoric, ever searching for excuses1 presented the Soviet Union as the loyal supporter of legitimate Arab rights. The Soviet collapse created a dangerous strategic imbalance in the world by taking us from a bipolar to a uni-polar world. The Soviet presence had created a type of international balance and gave Third World countries more options in decision making, because two powers with radically different ideologies and policies but military equality were competing in their courtship of the Arab countries. Occupied Palestine or Independent Israel?

A deep look into Arab and Palestinian political behavior indicates that the Arab objective has slowly moved from “occupied Palestine” to “independent Israel.” Since the Madrid conference of October 30, 1991, when Israel entered into negotiations with the Arab countries, it had achieved official Arab and Palestinian recognition not only of its existence but of its right to exist.

This analysis is confirmed by the Gaza-Jericho First agreement. In the letters of mutual recognition between the PLO and Israel signed on September 9, 1999, PLO Chairman Yasser Arafat, in his letter to Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin, wrote:

The [PLO] recognizes Israel’s right to exist and renounces all forms of violence and terrorism.

In response Rabin sent a letter to Arafat reading:

Based on your prior commitment, Israel recognizes the Palestine Liberation Organization as the sole, legitimate representative of the Palestinian people.

Here are clear proofs indicating that Arab objectives have changed and that the goal is no longer an independent and sovereign Palestine, but an independent Israel.

The first letter deals with the nature of the mutual recognition. The text refers to “Israel’s right to exist,” and there is a difference between recognizing existence and recognizing the right to existence, especially in the Palestinian context. Recognition of the right to exist carries religious and historic connotations. In Zionist thought, the foundation and justification for the state of Israel is religious- the Promised Land- and historically- Land of the Forefathers. Also it is the land where the Temple was built (religious and historic).

There are four levels of recognition in international law:

1) Recognition of a country’s right to exist is the strongest. This level of recognition differs from the others because it cannot be withdrawn, canceled, or changed. Such recognition is rarely achieved because it is dangerous and sensitive and carries with it many obligations from the state that grants it.

2) Recognition of The status quo is the type of recognition that is most popular among independent and sovereign states, and is the one most exchanged. It can be withdrawn, changed, and canceled. There can be war between two countries while this type of recognition still applies.

3) Tacit recognition does not allow for an exchange of embassies, diplomatic missions, ambassadors, or charges d’affairs. Two countries deal with each other at the consulate or representative office level, or less. Commercial trade is allowed and visas are granted for entry.

4) Recognition of a revolution or armed coup is the weakest type of recognition in law and diplomacy. It can be changed, canceled, and forgotten. It carries no weight in international bodies.

The PLO’s recognition of Israel was at the highest level. It dealt with the recognition of Israel as a natural and legal right1 saying in essence that Israel’s existence is the way things should be. This recognition brought about a rise in religious Jewish rhetoric, because Israel was vindicated.

There were other conditions for Zionist recognition of the PLO. This is the incentive for the Palestinian Authority’s relentless pursuit, oppression, and vilification of the Palestinian resistance.

Partition Plan of 1947 and armed struggle as the strategic path of liberating Palestine. Article 19 was also cut out. It rejected the Balfour Declaration of 1917. Judaism is a heavenly religion and not an independent nationality. The Jews are not one nation with an independent identity; but citizens of the countries they belong to.”

The historic Palestinian right to Palestine was denied in favor of the historic Jewish right to Palestine. This is clear from the above two articles that were deleted from the Palestinian National Charter.

When the managers of the Arab State system, namely Syria, Egypt and Saudi Arabia, oppose a fourth “Middle Eastern” Economic Summit in Doha, Qatar, and bring it to utter failure, when they put the brakes on smaller Arab States rushing to normalize with the invader, when they oppose another military strike on Iraq, and when they oppose the settlement of Palestinian refugees in Arab States, we should push them further along that road by seizing the moment to escalate the conflict with the invader as Hizbullah has done.

However, let us not wallow in illusions about where these States come from and about the limits of their opposition to post-colonial plans. For they agree with peace with “Israel” in principle, but they only want better terms for the deal, and their opposition measures all purport to serve as tactical cards to improve their hand on the negotiating table and to preserve their ruler-ships. In short, they want to ameliorate and find a formula to coexist with the Zionist project, not to overthrow it. But the Zionists would not let them have that, because the Arab State system itself helped the balance of power reach the unsavory point it is at now. In a way, that’s very good.

Yes, it was commendable that the Arab State system should condemn the recent sprint by the Algerian State to normalize relations with the Zionists! That, however, is but a tactical move. A true strategic move to tilt the balance of power against “Israel” would be to lift the siege on Iraq now. This would be a wise move even if one’s intention is to negotiate with “Israel” on better terms. Unfortunately, the Arab State system does not always act wisely. Thus the Qatari initiative to lift the siege on Iraq was preempted by the official Arab system, for the very reason that initiative itself was launched by Qatar in the first place: regional rivalries, specifically, to loosen the Saudi grip on the States of the Arab Gulf.

Hence, there is no room for keeping faith in Arab regimes of any kind. Surely, protecting their regimes requires them to do good sometimes, as in supporting the liberation effort in South Lebanon, but that is not good enough. Genuine social and political change in the Arab world entails the establishment of fighting popular grass-roots movements with an independent and unwavering leadership that is committed to a strategic vision of liberation, Arab unity and social and economic justice and progress. And that is the primary lesson of the victory in South Lebanon.

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 11

The Two Main Haredi Groups

By the beginning of the 1990s, the confrontation between the unbending Haredi version of Ashkenazi exclusiveness and Oriental traditionalism, which previously was potentially explosive, erupted. The Ashkenazi Haredi movement insisted upon completely freezing the situation that existed in central and eastern Europe around 1860. The Oriental Jews, trained by Ashkenazi Haredi Jews, were forced to discard their traditional garb, wear the black Ashkenazi clothing and learn and speak Yiddish. Yiddish was the language of oral instruction in the Haredi yeshivot; Hebrew was reserved for writing. The Oriental traditionalists were also forced to adopt the Ashkenazi manner of praying, which differed in numerous ways from their former method. Revered rabbis, who commanded authority and encountered almost no opposition, imposed those radical changes. By contrast, the various attempts by the Labor movement to impose modernizing constraints upon the Orientals in the 1 950s sparked furious opposition among the Oriental masses, who would often criticize politicians but hardly ever criticize rabbis.

The Oriental students in Ashkenazi Haredi yeshivot, after years of docile submission to demands and after being ordained as rabbis, were not granted status equal to that of their fellow students and rabbis. They have continued to accept and even today seem to be content with their inferior treatment. An excellent illustration of this is the inequality in intermarriage with their Ashkenazi peers. All Jewish communities share the time-honored custom that the head of the yeshiva arranges all marriages of yeshiva students. He carefully picks the daughters of rich and pious Jews as wives for students. The better students are matched with the daughters of the wealthiest parents. (The head of the yeshiva also matches daughters of rabbis with sons of the wealthiest parents.) Yeshiva students have selflessly complied with this matchmaking; resisting has been -and still is- considered to be a grave sin.

Ashkenazi Haredi Jews have never formally prohibited marriages with pious Jews from other communities. Such marriages, nevertheless, often have been -and still are- considered disgraces. Because of this, the heads of Ashkenazi Haredi yeshivot adopted the custom, still followed, of matching Oriental students, however distinguished in their studies, with either physically handicapped Ashkenazi brides or ones from poor families.

Not surprisingly, an unwritten rule developed whereby Oriental students, however distinguished, would not be appointed to any responsible teaching positions even in lower-rank yeshivot, attended solely by Oriental students. These teaching jobs were reserved for Ashkenazi rabbis, the underlying assumption being that Oriental Jews were not yet sufficiently mature to hold responsible religious positions. When Rabbi Shach, one of the foremost Haredi leaders, explicitly reiterated this assumption shortly before the 1992 elections, he was denounced as being racist by many Ashkenazi secular Jews; neither Oriental rabbis nor Oriental political activists uttered one word of public criticism.

No Oriental initiative was responsible for the creation of the Haredi political party, Shas. Rabbi Shach formed Shas before the 1988 elections, because he, in his rivalry with other prominent Ashkenazi Haredi rabbis, needed to have Knesset members that would be subservient only to him. He, therefore, ordered those rabbis that were his students and retained personal allegiance to him to form two new, separate, Haredi political parties: Degel Ha’Tora (Banner of the Law) would be purely Ashkenazi; Shas (an acronym for Sephardi List for Tradition) would be purely Oriental. After the formation of both parties, the party leaders publicly regarded Rabbi Shach as their highest spiritual authority and vowed to obey him unconditionally. In order to make Shas also attractive to non-Haredi Orientals, Shach handpicked a non-Haredi Oriental rabbi upon whom he could rely -Rabbi Ovadia Yoseph, the former chief rabbi of Israel- to act as the nominal party head. Shach, of course, retained authority. For Shach, Yoseph’s greatest virtue was that, after failing to win re-election as chief rabbi due to the NRP’s refusal to exert influence on his behalf, Yoseph hated the NRP as fiercely as did Shach himself. As is well known in Israel, hatred between secular Jews cannot match in intensity the mutual hatred between diverse groups of religious Jews, especially in the quarrels between rabbis representing those diverse groups. Shach had good reason to expect that, because of his wish to retaliate against NRP rabbis, Yoseph would remain loyal to him and be content with his subordinate role.

For a while everything worked as Shach had planned. The two parties, controlled by Shach, obtained eight Knesset seats altogether in the 1988 elections; Degal Ha’Tora had two seats; Shas, six seats. The Haredi party, Agudat Israel, against which Shach formed his parties, obtained only five seats. Degel Ha’Tora and Shas preferred a Likud government and after the 1988 elections supported Yitzhak Shamir as the prime minister. Their support may have been decisive. After 1990 Shamir would not have had a Knesset majority without their support. The self-demeaning attempts by the Labor Party leader, Shimon Peres, to reverse this situation failed. Peres spent months attending lessons of Talmud, given in his home by Rabbi Yoseph. Peres attempted unsuccessfully to be received by Rabbi Shach; Shach received many petty

secular politicians but not Peres. Peres made repeated, public pronouncements about how deeply he respected Judaism in general and the Haredi rabbis in particular. Everything Peres attempted was in vain. Shach and his rival Haredi rabbis did not bend in their support for Shamir. Yitzhak Rabin’s victory over Peres for the leadership position in the Labor Party primaries preceding the 1992 elections was largely due to Labor’s rank-and-file disillusionment with Peres’ attempts to ingratiate himself with Haredi Jews and to win their support. In spite of this experience, Peres repeated the same attempts that resulted in the same results in the 1996 elections.

The Haredi parties wielded political Dower after 1988, most especially in the 1988-90 period. Peres, still in the government after 1988, supported their demands; Shamir, while Prime Minister, was even more resolute with support. Haredi political success can best be measured by the amounts of money the two Haredi parties were able to obtain from the state through so-called “special money” grants, not subject to fiscal controls of the state. These special money grants were made through a voluntary association, formed to remain under the real control of a Haredi Knesset member or his friends. The ministry of finance made grants from the state budget to such associations, most often on the basis of flimsy purpose statements and with no control exerted over expenditures. The resultant corruption was enormous, reaching a scale unprecedented in the entire history of the State of Israel and finally causing the withdrawal of such special money grants.

The extensive corruption involved in the obtaining of this special money did not necessarily mean that the money itself was used illicitly. Shas spent most of this money to establish a network of institutions designed to exert a lasting influence and to train cohorts of militants that in the future could enable the party to maximize its control over its public. This network consisted of a chain of educational institutions designed to revive traditional Jewish education for boys with only sacred and not secular subjects taught. (Shas largely ignored the education of girls.) Adult males between the ages of 40 and 50 were encouraged to leave their professions or give up their businesses in order to enroll in institutions and study sacred subjects with guaranteed remuneration. The remuneration, that is, salaries for studying, were admittedly low, but numerous individuals considered the life of study preferable to their persisting to do menial work or to maintain decaying businesses. The recruits did more than study Talmud. They were required to do political work for Shas. These recruits soon constituted Shas’ political cadre, which has been and remains instrumental in turning Haredi neighborhoods into electoral constituencies under almost any conceivable circumstances.

The split between Shas and Rabbi Shach came after the 1992 elections and was sparked by a triviality. The split in reality was over the rival claims by Shach and Yoseph to be regarded as the spiritual head of Shas. Rabin, when forming his coalition, approached and accepted the demands of Shas. Before signing an agreement, Shas asked Rabbi Shach for approval. Shach refused, because, as discussed in another chapter, Shulamit Aloni was to be named Minister of Education. Shach’s newspaper, Yated Ne’eman, editorialized that this appointment was worse than the killing of one million children during the Holocaust. The reasoning employed here was that the Nazis killed the children but did not prevent their son’s from going to paradise, whereas the appointment of Aloni could corrupt Jewish souls and deprive them of paradise. Rabbi Yoseph and the Shas Party, nevertheless, decided to risk the souls of Jewish children and joined Rabin’s government. Rabbi Shach and his followers reacted negatively in a furious manner that persisted thereafter.

The confrontation between the two Haredi movements has been waged in the magical area over the contest of spiritual authority. In keeping with commonly held and magical Haredi beliefs, the Shas leaders’ sin of resisting Rabbi Shach’s will could be punished by a few curses resulting in either the deaths or sicknesses of those leaders and/or their family members. The result would allegedly restore heavenly equilibrium. In order to further this magical result, Rabbi Shach’s supporters resorted to conduct previously employed in similar situations. They published fake announcements of deaths, hospitalizations and/or traffic accidents of Shas leaders and then either notified the families accordingly by telephone or sent ambulances to their homes. As noted above, internecine hatred between religious Jews, and especially between Haredi rabbis, is often virulent. The existence of such hatred has continually resulted in disunity within ranks that limits Haredi political power. The methods of internecine infighting have been so customarily employed within Haredi culture that, unfortunately for Rabbi Shach’s followers, the impact is severely limited. In the domain of magic, moreover, Shas has on its side the great authority and renowned miracle worker, Rabbi Kaduri, who announced that he would shield all Shas leaders by casting cabbalistic spells. Rabbi Kaduri also claimed that God revealed to him that harassment by other Haredi Jews would qualify Shas leaders for the greatest Jewish virtue, sanctification of the Lord’s name through martyrdom.

In the contest of spiritual authorities, debate ensued over whether Rabbi Yoseph’s spirituality was sufficiently great to validate his challenge to Shach’s rabbinical authority, especially in light of Yoseph’s former allegiance to Shach. Following the debate all the Shas rabbis decided to obey Rabbi Yoseph. Shas rabbis and followers then began to extol Rabbi Yoseph as “the greatest rabbi of his generation,” greater even than any Ashkenazi rabbi. This honor had previously been awarded to Rabbi Shach. Shas had won its independence. The Ashkenazi Haredi Jews thus could not defeat but did sever all connections with Shas. No Ashkenazi rabbi distanced himself from Shach’s pronouncements; some added even more venom. The leader of the largest Hassidic sect, the Gur Hassids, reiterated his previously expressed view that Israel lost the Yom Kippur War (of October 1973) because a woman, Golda Meir, was prime minister. He implied that Israel would lose its next war because of Shulamit Aloni. Ashkenazi rabbis and their followers used weapons more hurtful than their curses and pronouncements.They desecrated Shas synagogues, usually just before the beginning of the Sabbath, thus making it difficult to clean in time without desecrating the Sabbath. Many Shas leaders, who had been educated in Ashkenazi institutions and who continued to pray in Ashkenazi synagogues, were harassed or beaten during the reciting of prayers. One Shas leader, Rabbi Pinhassi, was spat upon and beaten in an Ashkenazi synagogue in the Haredi town of Bnei Brak during a Sabbath prayer session. Some children of Shas leaders were terribly abused. The then Minister of the Interior, Yitzhak Der’i, had to remove his sons from an Ashkenazi yeshiva after they were publicly humiliated. Der’i was repeatedly harassed, often when attempting to pray in synagogues, by Shach’s followers and by religious settlers. Shas followers fought back. On several occasions they beat up those who had harassed Der’i; they also desecrated Ashkenazi synagogues in retaliation. Shas retaliations ultimately served their opponent’s cause by escalating the conflict.

The split and conflict within Haredi ranks illustrate the religious transformation of Oriental Jews. For over two decades many secular Oriental groups were founded; they all failed to obtain the support of the populations they claimed to represent and, as a result, collapsed ignominiously. Their failure can be attributed to their obstinate refusal to recognize that the Oriental Jewish communities define themselves primarily in religious terms. The Haredi Shas Party will in the foreseeable future likely remain the sole Oriental political party in Israel. This particular case study may help illustrate the nature of religious transformation of a not fully modernized population.

An Episode in the Park

E. Yaghi

To the tune of a thin man dancing around a monument, the son and his parents sat in the park.  The time was summer. Some blackbirds hopped on the grass in search of worms surfacing to the top after days of rain. Several people with dogs larger than themselves followed the leashes that were mastered by their canine friends.   It wasn’t really that the people were out to walk their dogs, but rather that the dogs were out to walk their people.

Parks are normally supposed to be a place where children play and adults stroll, taking time away from their work and busy lives to enjoy themselves.  But in many countries, parks are dangerous to be in at night and sometimes uncomfortable to be in during the day.   And so it was in this New York park.

However, the son had whisked his parents off to the park in order to make peace with them. As if peace were so easy.  His father sat on a bench next to the son while the mother sat in her wheelchair. As the two men talked, father and son, she gazed at the thin man dancing around the monument.  He looked like he was in a trance, maybe on drugs, the mother thought.  It seemed as though the man was totally oblivious to everything going on around him yet somehow demanding the attention of his audience. Had he not appeared that he came from the slums and that he was not only
on drugs, but a dealer, his movements might even have seemed poetic, instead of tragic.   By his actions he gave the impression that he thought he was on top of the world, but his actions were deceptive and he looked like he came from the bottom of the pit.

It was not just the man dancing around the monument that was strange the mother noted. There was this lady looking even more degenerate than the dancing man.  She pushed a cart before her. At the top of the cart perched a small American flag.  Next to the flag was a black fur coat. The scraggly looking lady parked her cart next to the bench across from the son and his parents and laid herself down and within moments, began snoring.  Then, along the parkway path, an older man shuffled over to a
neighboring bench with a brown paper bag wrapped around a whisky bottle. He sat there, also oblivious to whatever his eyes looked at, staring into nothingness, guzzling his firewater at intervals.   Surely he had not bathed in the longest of time.

Next to the alcoholic who clutched his bagged treasure sat a trio of hip older men.   Men who were out of their generation. Gray-haired men who had never made that great leap beyond their teenage years.  They looked as alcoholic as the man with the brown paper bag and as degenerate as the dancing man who still danced and as low as the lady sleeping on the bench.  It was on this beautiful summer day to the tune of this parade of people that the son sat on the bench next to his father, close to his mother in the wheelchair.  Out from nowhere suddenly appeared another lady who was followed by a mongrel on a leash.  In her anger, she had forgotten her mongrel and stomped her way across the park like a bulldozer, cussing as she walked.  All this profanity poured out of her mouth, all this ugliness poured out of her soul, all these words burst from her, words that any decent person would never utter.

Yes, there were flowers of every description growing in ordered rows throughout the park, and everything emitted freshness and promise.  But all the freshness was not man-made but God-given.  Given to spectators unaware of the immensity of such gifts and uncaring.

The son opened his mouth to speak while the dancing man continued on whirling around the monument. “Let what has happened be in the past,” he said looking at his father then his mother.

The father’s face reddened. The mother’s face whitened.  Then his father with all the reserve he seemed to be able to muster answered, “What happened is not so easy to forget, maybe impossible to forgive. I do not want to see your wife as long as I live. You married her against my will even though I warned you what might happen. You disobeyed me, you did not listen to me, and everything that happened is a result of your poor choice of a wife to share your life with.”

And as he spoke, the last scenarios and last encounters with the son’s wife flashed through the mother’s mind.  She remembered how she had painfully climbed up the stairs to her son’s apartment with her broken leg to pack her and her husband’s things out of fear that her daughter-in-law would destroy some of her husband’s valuable papers.  The only proof that he possessed of his age was in the brown briefcase that
sat in the bedroom the parents had been given by their son during their visit to him.

The first encounters with the daughter-in-law almost dispelled former conceptions.   Perhaps the niece, now turned daughter-in-law, was kind and sweet after all.   Perhaps she would indeed make their son a good wife, one that would protect his belongings and help make him a better man, one that would encourage him to retain his faith and even enhance it. Perhaps they had been too quick to judge her, too harsh in thinking that she would not be a good wife.  During the first week that the parents were there, she whisked about the house dusting and mopping. She brought them food, not good food, but food nevertheless. The parents did not complain for as the mother had told her husband, “She’s young and with time might become an excellent cook.

It wasn’t really important what they ate after all, but how they were treated that mattered.  And all this changed the day the father tried to bring his two warring sons together. Son’s that had always gotten along fabulously well until the new daughter-in-law had separated them and planted hate in her husband’s heart against his brother.  It was for this reason that the visiting father wanted to reconcile his sons and it was
for this reason that he had gathered them together in his married daughter’s house.   From the onset things went poorly.  The new daughter-in-law entered her husband’s sister’s house with an attitude. Her haughty eyes looked cold and distant. She stood angrily crossing her arms over her abdomen while her nose sneered down at the family that she was related to by blood and now by marriage.  Though she was the niece, she still felt herself better than all other members of the family and that day it showed all too clearly her true feelings.  As soon as the two warring sons went to the dining room to make peace with their father as mediator, the niece rushed in to pull her husband away, cussing her uncle with, “Damn you and your father,” as she spat not only the words out but her wrath and hate.

She pulled her husband, that same husband who now sat in the park with his parents in an attempt to make peace, to the car parked in front of his sister’s houses and at the car shouted out further insults, cussing and spitting. The son made no effort to shut his wife up and they both disappeared into the car swallowed up by a black thunderous cloud of haughtiness.

Later, when the mother with the broken leg went to get the belongings of both herself and her husband, she said to her son, “I am really very concerned about you son. To have such a wife is very dangerous. Remember, if she does not respect your father, then she does not respect you either.”

The son lamely tried to justify his wife’s actions with, “She only did that because she wanted to protect me.”

Further warnings were spoken and then, the mother knowing that it was all useless, took her belongings and began to go down the stairs when she suddenly saw her daughter-in-law whip by with her nose even higher in the air.  Seeing this the mother said, “Well thank you and good-bye.”

But as she painfully climbed back down the stairs she heard her daughter-in-law rushing towards her and then got hit by the clothes that her daughter-in-law threw at her. As if this was not enough, the daughter-in-law screamed at her, “Get out, get out of here.”

The sweet thing had turned into a wild insane animal with no control. Something clicked in the mother’s mind and she went back upstairs saying to the crazed animal before her, “No, this is my son’s house and I will not get out.”

But the daughter-in-law tried to throw herself on the mother in a frenzied attack, spitting out more vulgar words and profanity. It was all too wild and shocking. It was all the son could do to keep his wife from hitting his mother.  The mother finally decided that to leave was better than stay around for further abuse.

That day in the park all she could do was tell her son about the story of the prophet Abraham and how when he went to visit his son’s house, his first daughter-in-law opened the door. She did not invite her father-in-law in and all she could do was complain about her husband. The prophet was offered no food, no comfort. He told his daughter-in-law, “When my son comes home, tell him that his father sends him his greetings and to change the entrance of his house.”

The next time Prophet Abraham visited his son, he was met by a new daughter-in-law. She was very polite to him and fixed the best meal she could and offered it to him.  She was humble, respectful and a blessing to any who knew her.  Prophet Abraham told her, “When my son comes home, please give him my greetings and tell him that I tell him to take care of the entrance of his home.”

What the prophet meant the first visit to his son’s house was that the first wife was no good and that his son should get rid of her, but the next time he visited his son’s house, he told his son’s second wife to tell her husband to keep his wife.

The talk in the park went just about nowhere.  The son could not see how wrong his wife was, how disrespectful she had been to both his parents and what a curse she would be to him if she remained this way.  Yet he spoke of peace and asked his parents to forget all that had happened.

As the trio left the scenario in the park, the dancing man, the guy with the whisky wrapped in a brown paper bag, the lady sleeping on the bench, the dogs walking their owners, the mother did not think it would be easy to forget or forgive what had happened. She now knew for certain that her daughter-in-law was evil, not just bad, but evil and that she had tried her best and succeeded in not only making the two brothers hate each other because of the lies that she told, but hated her husband’s whole family as well and wanted to keep her husband as far away from his family
as possible. She had been quite victorious. His parents were out of their son’s house and he was more distant from his brothers and sisters than he had previously been. But when the daughter-in-law’s parents had been visiting, things had been totally different. They had been treated like royalty.

The scariest thing was that though the parents were now no longer staying with their son, their son had chosen such a woman to be his wife. Surely not a religious woman who respected her husband’s family, but a shrew who had so much hate for all her husband’s family and thought herself so much better than everyone. The scary thing was that no matter how poorly the daughter-in-law had treated her husband’s parents and how she had even hit and scratched her own husband at different intervals, he could not clearly see at all what a mess he had gotten himself into.  He was as
oblivious to his own predicament as the dancing guy on drugs and the alcoholic who guzzled his addiction from the brown paper bag and the snoring lady sleeping on the bench in broadlight.
