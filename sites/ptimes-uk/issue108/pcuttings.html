Press Cuttings

The Boston Globe, 27 May 2000

BEIRUT- Proudly greeting fans in liberated south Lebanon and foreign envoys coming to congratulate him, Sayed Hassan Nasrallah is the Arab world's newest hero, the man behind the guerrilla ''victory'' over the mighty Israeli Army.

When he visited the liberation zone for the first time yesterday, tens of thousands of people came to hear him speak.

In a black turban signifying descendancy from the Prophet Muhammad, the stocky secretary general of Hezbollah takes credit for his ''resistance'' movement's role in driving the Israelis out of Lebanon. Typically, he leaves the world guessing about the guerrillas' plans for the last active war in the Mideast.

''We do not have any intention of attacking civilians, but not all Israelis are civilians,'' Nasrallah told foreign journalists shortly after the occupation ended.

Ha'aretz, 28 May 2000

A strike planned by Palestinian teachers was canceled after the events of Nakba Day, but the struggle is far from over. They want higher wages and a freely elected union, but the Palestinian Authority wants them to forget all that in the name of 'national unity'

Where the persuasive efforts of the Palestinian political organizations, the pleas of the heads of the Palestinian education system, the (unaccepted) resignation of the director-general of the Education Ministry, the threats, arrests and venomous rumors failed, Nakba Day succeeded. On the day that marks "the catastrophe," [the Palestinians' term for their experiences following the War of Independence], bloody confrontations between Palestinian demonstrators and the Israel Defense Forces left three killed by IDF gunfire and hundreds of young people injured. After the carnage, the Supreme Coordinating Committee of Palestinian teachers at state schools decided, in an urgent telephone conference, to stop the battle it has been waging since February. The general strike that the 17,935 teachers at all 1,069 government schools in the West Bank were to have held on May 16 was canceled, and all 412,190 students returned to the regular school schedule.

The Chicago Tribune, 26 May 2000

JERUSALEM- Transport Minister Yitzhak Mordechai, one Israel's most prominent politicians, will be charged with sexual assault and harassment following complaints from three women, Israel's attorney general said Thursday.

His adviser, Leor Horev, said Mordechai plans to resign on Sunday. He will remain in the parliament, but will waive his immunity and stand trial.

Mordechai, a former defense minister and highly decorated army officer, has maintained that he is innocent.

The initial charge came from a 23-year-old office worker who complained to police that on Feb. 25 Mordechai forced her onto a sofa and put his hand under her blouse. After the complaint was made public, two other women came forward.

The Los Angeles Times, 26 May 2000

JERUSALEM--When then-Prime Minister Menachem Begin of Israel first sent troops to war in Lebanon, he assured his people that the mission would last 48 hours and would venture no deeper than "40 kilometers" into the neighboring country.

That was a generation ago, and Israel has occupied parts of Lebanon ever since. Until this week.

On Thursday, Prime Minister Ehud Barak quoted the Bible as he contemplated how his nation became mired in the mud of Lebanon for more than two decades, and whether it was worth it.

"When we went in," he said, "there were those who said, 'And peace will reign in the land for 40 years.' But the fact is that our presence did not change the reality in south Lebanon and did not prevent Katyusha rockets from being fired" at northern Israel.

The New York Times, 20 May 2000

JERUSALEM- A week that began in violence ended violently here, with bloody clashes in the West Bank and Gaza and intensified fighting in southern Lebanon.

On the one-year anniversary of his election by a sweeping majority, Prime Minister Ehud Barak was trying to put out fires on many fronts at once.

Because of trouble in the north, the south and within his political coalition, he was weighing a cancellation of his scheduled departure this weekend to the United States to confer with President Clinton and meet with Jewish American leaders.

Despite the violence, back-channel peace talks continued in Sweden.

Israeli, Palestinian and American officials have characterized them as a serious and constructive dialogue on the process itself and on the final status issues.
