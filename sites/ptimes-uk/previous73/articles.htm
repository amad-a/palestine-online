Corruption Prevails in PA Institutions

By Mahmoud al Khatib

A few weeks after the Israeli "Haartz" newspaper revealed the fact that Chairman Yasser Arafat had secret accounts in some Israeli banks in Tel Aviv, reports on corruption in Palestinian Authority ministries and institutions began to be publicly announced. The Israeli newspaper mentioned an amount of $150 million in these accounts, while other sources emphasized that the figures were larger.

These funds are not under the control of the PA's Ministry of Finance and many PA officials were kept in the dark about the secret accounts. More corruption was revealed in the annual report of the PA's Control Board. The report spoke about serious financial and administrative violations by most of the PA's ministries and establishments. The report estimated losses of up to $326 million as a result of those violations. The report accused a number of Palestinian ministers, under-secretaries and director-generals of misappropriation of funds and rejecting reports by the Control Board which frequently requested them to stop wasting public money and to be more cautious in spending.

But the Palestinian officials went on opening private accounts in banks without notifying the finance ministry. They were also charged with receiving foreign contributions and not including them in their ministries' budgets.

During the past year, the Control Board prepared 634 reports, and 50 studies. It received 205 complaints against the PA's ministries and officials concerning financial and administration violations.. The final report which was submitted to the Legislative Council in June consisted of 600 pages covering 22 ministries, 295 establishments and 435 municipalities. But surprisingly, the report did not cover the financial and administrative violations committed by the Legislative Council and the security and military services which the Board claimed were out of its control.

One of the most important violations revealed in the report was the large amount of money the PA's ministries and establishments spent on telephone calls. A telephone bill submitted by one of the Palestinian senior officials amounted to $6,500 in one month. Most of the calls made by that official had nothing to do with his official work. They were purely personal calls. The report said more than 5,000 mobile telephones were distributed to PA officials noting that the costs of calls made through these phones are very expensive compared to ordinary phones.

Public money was also wasted on cars and fuel used by PA officials. The cars are usually given to ministers and under-secretaries but in many cases they were given to junior officials as well. Some ministers were given more than one car each. Dr. Nabil Shaath, Minister of Planning and International Cooperation, and Jamil Tarifi, Minister of Civil Affairs, for example, were given four cars each.

The report pointed out that the rentals of some ministry buildings are more expensive than the already bought buildings. Dr. Shaath's ministry, for example, pays $440,000 a year in rent for its different buildings although only 300 employees are working in that ministry. Dr. Sa'adi Kuronz, head of the financial committee at the Legislative Council said in a press conference that the amount of money spent on rentals for buildings used by ministries forms 36.5% of the total operational costs. He added that four years' worth of rents for these buildings would be enough to build permanent complexes for the PA's ministries and establishments.

Dr. Kuronz pointed out that no restrictions are imposed by the PA on the usage of mobile telephones by Palestinian officials. But he said his committee recommended every ministry to use 3 telephones only one each for the minister, under-secretary, and the director-general. It also recommended that each telephone bill should not exceed $150 per month. In certain cases, the PA paid the bills of the private telephones of the ministers and senior officials, Dr. Kuronz said.

Despite the decision made by the Legislative Council which prohibited the ministries from publishing advertisements of a social nature, such as congratulations or condolences, the ministries paid no heed to this decision and continued to waste money on such advertisements, the Palestinian official said. One ministry, for instance, published 30 of these advertisements in one month which cost about $4,500, Dr. Kuronz said.

No Internal Structure in Ministries

There is no internal organizational structure in all the PA's ministries. Therefore most of the new recruitments are not made in accordance with the actual needs of every ministry or establishment.

Figures in the report show that the number of director-generals employed in the PA's civil service bodies (1,200 director-generals in 25 ministries!) are more than the number in the United States. These officials are appointed regardless of their poor qualifications or of the actual needs of the ministry.

In one ministry, for example, there were between 50 and 60 director-generals. Most of them were appointed because of favouritism and/or their blood kinship with ministers and senior officials.

Monopolies

The famous article by David Hirst published in the "Guardian" on 21 April entitled "Shameless in Gaza" and the extensive report published in the Israeli "Haartz" newspaper on 4 April entitled "The Man Who Swallowed Gaza" shed light on the spread of corruption throughout the various bodies of the self-rule authority. David Hirst talked of the misappropriation of funds by a number of PA officials such as Mahmoud Abbas and Abu Mazen, who signed the Oslo Accords for the PLO. According to Hirst, Abu Mazen built a villa in Gaza which cost $2 million! One wonders where did this man, who spent the last 32 years of his life as a PLO freedom fighter, get the money from! The answer, written plainly on the villa's wall by a Palestinian boy, reads "This is your reward for selling Palestine."

Dr. Nabil Shaath has a number of monopolies and enjoys good relations with the Israelis. When Dr. Shaath got married last year, he held four reception parties: one in Cairo, another in Gaza and two in Jerusalem- one of them specifically for his Israeli friends.

The Israeli newspaper revealed that the secret accounts in the Israeli banks are run by Arafat's economic adviser, Khalid Salam, who is considered one of the PA's most prominent men. He also controls several monopolies, namely the Bahar (Sea), which monopolizes the PA's imports and exports. The Gazans, as David Hirst says, make fun of the name describing it as "ocean".

Budgets of Security Bodies and Arafat's Office

There was no reason for the Control Board's report not mentioning the public funds spent by Chairman Arafat's office and the various security and military bodies. Reliable Palestinian sources estimated the annual expenditure of Arafat's office at more than $80 million and that of the security bodies at $248 million.

To avoid angry reactions from the Palestinian street, Chairman Arafat set up a committee to investigate the violations listed in the Control Board's report. Besides that, the only other action taken by Arafat was his decision to appoint a new director-general for the Income Tax Authority. He also transferred two directors from the same Authority to other establishments. The PA Chairman also signed a decision changing the Bahar company into a government one! But these actions did not, by any means, affect the Palestinian senior officials who are directly involved in the financial misappropriation.

Hebron May Set Pace for a New Intifada

By Khalid Amayreh

Al Khalil (Hebron)- Intermittent clashes between Palestinian demonstrators and Israeli occupation soldiers in down-town Hebron continued unabatedly last month. The clashes, which started on 13 June, generally assumed a monotonous pattern whereby Palestinian youngsters would hurl stones toward rather than at or on Israeli soldiers taking positions along the "partitioning line," with the latter responding rather harshly with "rubber bullets" containing a lethal iron nucleus, tear gas, stun grenades, and eventually live ammunition. According to hospital sources in Hebron, around 150 mostly teenaged protesters sustained gun-shot wounds. At least 10 people were reportedly in critical condition, including a 12-year-old, Murad Jamjum, whose head was hit by an Israeli sniper bullet causing his brain to hemorrhage, and a 13-year-old, Lina Rashid Misk, who sustained a serious wound in the belly, forcing doctors to sever 40 centimetres of her damaged intestines.

Palestinian medical sources said many of the inflicted injuries were meant to kill or cause irreversible damage, e.g. paralysis or permanent disability. "We have seen many of the injured were hit by bullets in the upper part of their bodies, particularly the head, the chest, and the spinal cord," said Yousef al Sharawi, Chief Physician at the Amira Alya hospital in Hebron. Sharawi dismissed Israeli allegations that rubber bullets don't kill as "rubbish talk."

"These bullets are quite lethal as they can pierce into the body several inches often causing irreparable damage." Al Sharawi cited a case of a 75-year-old man from Halhul who was seriously wounded in the latest clashes and remained in critical condition at al Ahli hospital. He said a rubber bullet penetrated the elderly man's head, destroying some of the vital blood vessels.

Deep Frustration

Palestinian protests in Hebron were ostensibly fuelled by U.S. House of Representatives' decision to recognize Jerusalem as "Israel's united and undivided capital." Indeed, the American decision drew indignant reactions from most Palestinians and was viewed as another proof, if proof was needed, of flagrant American hostility to Arab and Muslim causes.

Nevertheless, the underlying reason behind the current wave of unrest seems to have more to do with the dying or already-dead peace process than with the American decision. Observers note that most Palestinians have come to feel cheated and betrayed by a peace process that further narrowed their horizons and made them ever more vulnerable to Israeli insolence and hegemony.

Manifestations of the Palestinians' collective ordeal are everywhere. Settlements are spreading out in all directions, closing in on Palestinian towns, villages and hamlets, turning them into "shut-in ghettos" deprived of any prospects of future growth or demographic expansion. The Judaization of Jerusalem, meanwhile, is continuing on a frenzied pace, and concerted efforts are being made to achieve demographic "Jewish" continuity between the Holy City and Jericho, thus cutting the West Bank into two halves.

For its part, Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority seems to be completely naked, powerless, and demoralized. To illustrate its dismal state of affairs, some PA officials are beginning to speak of reverting to the previous status quo, a return to the direct Israeli occupation of Palestinian towns now under exclusive PA control.

One week before this report, a leading Fateh leader in the West Bank, Abbas Zaki, recognized that "the boat could be rocked altogether in the occupied territories." Speaking at the PA Ministry of Information office in Hebron on 19 June, Zaki pointed out that the Palestinian people are increasingly fed up with the peace process and with what he called the PA's "futile treatment of the current crisis."

"This situation can't be sustained for much longer. Either we advance forward or return to confrontation, and should we be forced to revert to violence, it wouldn't be confined to us; there would be a regional conflagration with unpredictable ramifications."

Anger in Nablus

Zaki's predictions do not seem to be far-fetched. On 21 June, as many as 10,000 townspeople took to the streets to protest the House of Representatives decision on Jerusalem and America's sinister alliance with the Zionist entity. The huge mass of humanity shouted in unison "with soul and blood, we shall protect Jerusalem" and "we are willing to die so that Jerusalem may live." The rally, the first joint Islamist-Nationalist endeavour of its kind, was addressed by several speakers representing Hamas, the PA, Fateh and leftist groups.

PA Corruption: The Other Side of Patriotism

By M. Sadiq

The city of Al-Khalil (Hebron) is once again raging. Its stone-armed youths take to the streets every day from dawn to dusk. Their daily mission is to engage the Israeli troops that have been, by virtue of the Hebron agreement between the Palestinian Authority (PA) and Israel, deployed in the heart of the city to protect several hundred Zionist Jews whose illegal occupation of the old quarters was legitimized by the said agreement.

Khalilis (citizens of Al-Khalil) feel more than anybody else in the 1967 occupied Palestinian territory the betrayal of their National Authority. Elsewhere in the West Bank, and especially in East Jerusalem which is shrinking day after day, the situation resembles a boiling volcano that is likely to erupt at any time. Few people remain who have not yet been disillusioned with the so-called peace process. Very few of them still imagine that one day Palestinians and Israelis will live side by side in harmony and peace.

At the same time, the Palestinian community in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank is much more acquainted with the nature of the PA and its officials -- a Mafia that neither belongs to the people nor cares for them. All its members are interested in is to seize this golden opportunity, that is of having been contracted by the Israelis to shield them from martyrdom-loving fanatics, so as to increase their wealth and fulfil their lustful desires.

So much has been said and written about the corruption of the PA and about its human rights violations that PA leader Yasser Arafat has been sending emissaries to explain that Israelis fabricate these stories, or at least exaggerate them, in order to pressure him to concede more to them.

Such an argument, recently defended through Saudi-owned media in London (Al-Hayat newspaper and MBC television), an Arafat envoy, is not convincing for those who know well that not much is left to concede. Umm Jihad (Intisar Al-Wazir), PA minister of social affairs -- who visited London between 16 and 21 June, defended her administration by claiming that hostile circles and foreign media have been deliberately exaggerating isolated cases of corruption and violations of human rights, which in her opinion exist normally in any administration. A recent live debate on the Qatari Al-Jazeera satellite TV between Arafat's spokesperson Nabil Amr and Hamas Political Bureau member Muhammad Nazzal in the beginning of June has uncovered a deep crisis within the ranks of the PA over its image and the failure of its PR operation. It is rumoured that Amr was chastised by Arafat and condemned by other PA officials for having lost the debate.

However, the real dilemma for the PA is what to do about its image, not as a corrupt or oppressive administration -- for in this it is not unique in the Arab region -- but about collapse, seemingly beyond redemption, of its peace treaty with Israel. PA official may get away with corruption, at least for a while, and may be able to defend their appalling record of human rights, but they have no excuse, or let us say explanation, for the continuing expropriation of land and expansion of Jewish settlements by Israel.

As a face-saving ploy, the PA reportedly has been embroiled in the liquidation of alleged Palestinian land brokers suspected of selling Arab land to Israelis. The ex-judicial killings have been condemned by Israel and the United States of America, clearly not out of sympathy with the victims or in support of the rule of law, but as unequivocally stated by officials from both sides because the PA has no right to forbid the selling of Arab land to Jews. The U.S. Congress has gone as far as considering the ban anti-Semitic. Among the Palestinians who under normal circumstances would welcome the punishment of traitors of all sorts -- informers, land sellers and brokers -- the response to the ex-judicial killings has been one of skepticism. People ask: "If a person deserves to be executed for selling a piece or more of land, what about those who have sold the entire country?" This is in reference to the peace deal which recognizes Israel as a legitimate state, and thus concedes more than 90% of the Palestinian territory. Some joke: "The executed brokers incurred the punishment not because selling the land is a crime, but because they undertook to do what happens to be the sole prerogative of Yasser Arafat and his men. No one has the right to sell land or concede Palestinian rights except the 'sole legitimate representative of the Palestinian people'." But more serious critics explain that the PA had to do something to show to the people that it is opposed to the Israeli expansion of settlements. Naturally it could not stop the Israelis; it neither had the jurisdiction nor the power. According to the Oslo agreement, the PA's sole jurisdiction is over the Palestinian population. It has no jurisdiction over the land, not even over what grows on it or what exists within it. So, from the point of view of the Israelis, what they do is perfectly legal and legitimate.

By executing a number of alleged land brokers, the PA has hoped to restore its patriotic image at a time when the public has had nothing to talk about except the scandals of PA senior officials, some of whom have been associated with rape, drugs, lavish lifestyles, and the spread of pubs and nightclubs along Gaza beach. This is, of course, not to mention the disclosure in Israel, as a result of a parliamentary investigation, the hundreds of millions of dollars deposited in a Tel Aviv bank account belonging to Yasser Arafat, and the numerous stories told in the Palestinian street about the extravagance of his wife, who allegedly prefers to do her shopping in Paris. Interestingly, the chief editor of the London-based Al-Hayat in his daily column rallied to the help of the Arafat "dynasty" by stressing that Suha Arafat is the poorest wife of an Arab president. She might well be the poorest among them, but that does not in any way mean she does not already sit on a huge fortune, much of which it is alleged came from the 5% deducted from the salaries of Palestinians working in the Gulf to support the struggle for the liberation of their country as well as from the millions of dollars donated by Gulf governments to the PLO.

Last Call For Jerusalem

By Lena Winfrey Sayyed

Lately, we have seen the reality and the truth of America's stance on Israel as it has unfolded its rosy petals to show us the ugly, dark truth. We see that the American government does not care to come into the light and let the world see its "blood" relation to Israel--- their parent-child relationship. They are so confident in their power that they believe no one can stop what has been put into play. One day, their arrogance may be their downfall.

America has boldly announced its movement of the American embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem. This shows us that America no longer sees Jerusalem as an international city for Muslims, Christians and Jews, but now it recognizes it as the sole capital of Israel. What will happen to the Arabs living there? People who were born in Jerusalem are already being kicked out. Imagine how freely the Israelis will behave with Americans nearby to protect them.

The American government stated that it would spend 100 million dollars to build their new embassy. Why is so much American tax dollars going to be wasted? Are they planning to build in half of the city, or do they think they will need to pay for large numbers of troops and weapons to protect themselves and the Israelis?

The American government has been an indirect enemy to the Palestinian people for years as they have sent 10 billion dollars a year in foreign aid and weapons to Israel as well as their undying support. Now their presence in a sacred city--- sacred to all Muslims and Christians--- will show an open change in their policy. They will become the direct enemy to the Palestinians. The American government never asked its people if they wanted to get involved in a war or to waste their tax money in this region. If the American embassy moves to Jerusalem, it cannot bring peace; all I see is a future of war.

How can this be reversed? How can we try to halt, or at least delay, their construction? The only solution I see is to "fight fire with fire" by fighting them in the territory that they know. We must meet them on the Congressional ground. The Israelis found this out long ago.

The Israeli lobby is the largest and most powerful lobby in the US, and it has influenced many governmental decisions. There are enough Muslims (between 6 to 10 million), Arab-Americans and other interested parties who could make such a stance if they would unite and mobilize as the Israeli lobby does. All that the Congressmen understand is power, money and position. I recommend that anyone who wants to see lobbying methods, to take some examples, should read the ex-Congressman Paul Findley's book They Dare to Speak Out. However, some of the methods used by the Israeli lobby, we would not want to stoop so low to use.

We should make sure the supporters of Jerusalem and the Palestinians are registered to vote. Then, petitions and letters could be mailed and phone calls could be made to US Congressmen. Also, rallies and demonstrations could be held. We must try this method. If it fails, then we must remember Jamal Abdul-Nasser's words "What is taken by force, must be returned by force."

Palestinians Castigate Congress Decision

By: Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem- The recent decision by the U.S. Congress to recognize Jerusalem as "Israel's united and undivided capital" has drawn indignant reactions from Palestinians at all levels in the occupied territories and in pre-1967 Palestine. On Friday, 13 June, the Juma'a speaker at al Masjidul Aqsa (Aqsa Mosque) launched a vitriolic attack at the decision by Congress describing it as "provocative, obscene and diabolical."

"This sinister decision is new proof, if proof is needed, of the contempt with which the United States treats the Arab-Muslim world, masses and regimes," said the speaker. However, he blamed Arab and Muslim leaders and peoples for giving "this Satanic force" sufficient reason "to treat us lightly and not to take us seriously."

"We have only ourselves to blame because we willingly accepted to be subservient to the Americans in ways resembling the subservience of the tail to the body," said the speaker, alluding to the close ties between the United States and many Arab regimes.

In Hebron, Sheikh Nayef Rajoub argued that al Quds (Jerusalem) represents the heart of the Muslim Umma (nation) "and its conscience, its dignity, present and future", adding that "should the Umma, God forbid, continue to allow Israel to retain Jerusalem, then death would be better than life for Muslims."

Rajoub, an eloquent and quotable preacher in the Hebron area, pointed out, however, that the United States wouldn't have dared embarked on such "insult to the Muslim world," had it not been for Arab subservience to the White House. Rajoub turned on the Palestinian Authority, saying that it is time this "absurd show of the peace process is terminated."

"Haven't you realized by now that the peace process is a sheer illusion and the so-called sole sponsor of the process is a viscious enemy of Muslims," Rajoub said before several thousand worshippers. For its part, the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, strongly condemned the decision by Congress saying it represented a new provocation against Muslims the world over. Moreover, the Islamist Movement warned American decision makers that Muslims would not keep silent indefinitely in the face of American insolence and hegemony. "Jerusalem, East and West, and the whole of Palestine is Muslim. The American decision is therefore null and void as far as Muslims are concerned."

Condemnation of the Congress decision also came from Muslim leaders in Israel. Sheikh Raed Salah, Mayor of Um Al Fahem, sent a telegram to the U.S. Ambassador to Israel, Martin Endyk, expressing total rejection of the decision. Sheikh Salah described the decision as one amounting to "a declaration of war against the Muslim Umma."

The Congress decision on Jerusalem coincided with the near collapse of the peace process and with determined Israeli efforts to impose the fait accompli on the Palestinian Authority. Hence, it would be reasonably safe to expect tense and potentially violent days and weeks ahead.

PT interviews the leader of the Islamic Action Front in Jordan

Al-Farhan: Public freedoms in Jordan in decline

Amman - By Mahmoud al Khatib

The leader of the Islamic Action Front (IAF) in Jordan Dr. Isaaq Al-Farhan expressed his party's views on how democracy can be improved and why political parties in general should play a more vital role in the formation of the ideal democratic society. The interview with the Islamic leader went as follows:

 

Q. How do you assess the position of your party on the Jordanian political map?

A. The Islamic Action Front (IAF) is a party that was established in 1992 following the resumption of democratic life in Jordan in 1989. The IAF is different than other political parties in Jordan because it's creation depended upon the achievements of the Islamic Movement over the last five decades. Therefore, it is considered an extension of the Islamic Movement and of the Muslim Brotherhood (MB) in particular.

The majority of the IAF members are from the Muslim Brotherhood. IAF is the only political Islamic party working in Jordan and it is open to all Muslims who are free to join without restrictions. This is different than the leftist and the national political parties which are numerous in Jordan. In addition, IAF is the largest party in Jordan and has the largest number of representatives in the Lower House of Parliament. There are 15 deputies in Parliament. Our deputies also made up about one quarter of the Parliament in the 1989 elections. The party leads the 11 opposition parties in Jordan and its secretary-general is the secretary-general of the Popular Conference for the Protection of Homeland and Anti-Normalization (the normalization of ties with Israel), which includes more than 1,000 Jordanian political and public figures.

Q. How do you assess the situation of democracy, the public and individual freedoms in Jordan?

A. In order not to be misunderstood, we want democracy in its technical term which means the people's participation in vital issues that affect their lives. However, the Western democratic system, as understood by us, contains many gaps and negative aspects. We take from democracy what goes in line with the Islamic Shura (consultative) values. Democracy in Jordan is not in an ideal condition but there are some forms of democracy. Therefore, we were keen on enhancing the democratic conduct in the country.

The democratic aspects that exist in Jordan are far from the ideal democratic system. The conduct of the consequent governments and their different institutions are still martial. The Interior Ministry, for example, examines the lists of people who pay contributions to the political parties which puts the public in a state of fear and makes them refrain from assisting the parties. Many members of these parties have been denied access to public jobs, scholarships and facilities that the government usually provides for ordinary people. The government's suspicious and negative view of the parties keeps the general public away from these parties.

In addition, the prime ministers have not put the leaders of the Jordanian parties in the political picture. In ordinary democracies, if an important or serious event occurred, the prime minister would call the leaders of the pro-government or the opposition parties for a meeting to inform them of the developments in order that the parties' policies and attitudes towards these developments would be based on facts and not affected by rumours. If government wants to enhance democracy, it should consider the parties as national institutions and should meet with the parties' leaders regularly.

We also felt that the government has deformed the picture of democracy. Democracy is based on different institutions such as the parliament, political parties, media and the professional syndicates. The government began marginalizing the role of the political parties and the parliament by issuing temporary laws without being passed by the parliament. For instance, it issued the new elections law, "One man, one vote", which has no equal to it anywhere in the world. In other countries that adopt similar laws, each constituency is represented in the parliament by one member. However, in Jordan, it is represented by two, three or more members and the elector is allowed to elect only one candidate. This means that each elector is given a one-half vote, one-third or one-quarter and so on. This system violates the Constitution and we insist that the "One man, one vote" law is unconstitutional.

Finally, the government surprised us with issuing the recent temporary law of press and censorship which abuses the freedom of press and expression. This law imposes unacceptable fines on any journalist convicted by the court. The fine reaches 15,000 Jordanian dinars (JD) (about $20,000), which translates into approximately the total amount of his earnings during his career life! The government can wait until the parliamentary session is convened to study and vote on these laws. The issuing of such temporary laws by the government is an abuse of democracy.

In short, I have concerns over the democratic situation in Jordan. It is unsatisfactory. Yes, there is a margin of democracy, but it is narrowing. The level of public and individual freedoms is declining. Unless the government reconsiders its attitude towards this issue, I am afraid the course of democracy will continue to collapse.

Q. You personally participated in previous governments in the 1970s, and a number of Muslim Brothers participated in a former Jordanian government. What did you achieve from such participation?

A. I do not understand the concept of a political party not participating in the executive or legislative authorities. From my personal experience in the government, except for the big decisions concerning the future of the country, any minister can undertake big authorities inside his ministry. My participation in three or four previous cabinets as minister of Awqaf and later as minister of education was positive and constructive regarding the development of the cadres and programmes of the two ministries.

When I was minister of Awqaf and Islamic Affairs, we took the initiative to organize "preaching camps" in different areas of Jordan. The preachers were asked to tour and visit people in their residences and farms. The governor of al Karak, south of Jordan, told me that the rates of crime in that area were reduced after the regular visits of the preachers. The minister can play an effective role in developing his ministry and serving his people, especially if he has a programme to carry out.

As for the experience of the five Islamist ministers who joined Mr. Mudar Badran's cabinet in 1991, it is difficult to evaluate their participation because they were in office for only five months including three months during the Gulf War. But our participation in both the government and the parliament led, in general, to many significant achievements at the time. We succeeded, for instance, to cancel Martial Law and to re-appoint all public servants who were dismissed from their jobs for political reasons during the Martial period.

I remember that Dr. Abdulla Akaila (deputy for IAF), who was the education minister in 1991, issued a decision on his first day in the ministry returning all teachers and ministry's employees who were dismissed for their political affiliation in the 1970s and the 1980s. There were thousands and some of them were communists or belonged to the Baath Party. Our minister of Social Development, Mr. Yousef Al Azem, had also approved a decision to establish 20 charitable organizations which were forbidden, for security reasons, from being developed. We also persisted and succeeded to return more than 20,000 passports to their owners after being confiscated by the government during the Martial phase. Therefore, all political parties should take part in the government.

Q. Do you have any restrictions on participation in the government?

A. There are certain restrictions regarding our participation. We did not participate in the governments that were involved in peace talks with Israel or those which signed the peace agreements with Israel. We shall continue our opposition to the peace treaty. Even if we participate in any future government, we shall maintain this opposing attitude. It is wise for the regime to call for the Islamists to form a government that does not abandon its principles and that can cease the normalization of ties with Israel in the light of the stalemate of the peace process. (Israeli Prime Minister) Netanyahu has adopted stiff positions pertaining to the implementation of the peace agreements, Jewish settlements, Jerusalem and the establishment of the Palestinian State.

Q. In a previous interview with the Islamist thinker, Dr. Abdulla Nafisi, he said in his comment to Mr. Erbakan's participation in the Turkish government that the Islamic movements and parties are not prepared to form a government but only to be in the opposition. Do you agree?

A. I somehow disagree with him. But because the nature of the opposition only requires raising a voice against the unacceptable policies adopted by our governments, the opposition does raise its voice against such policies. The role of the Islamists is one of strength because they stick to their principles. But strategically, the Islamic movement should establish a stand and confront the realities and practice the "art of possibilities" -the politics. They should ascend the stairs step by step beginning with reality and ending with the last step which is the principles and the strategic aims.

It is early to evaluate Mr. Erbakan's participation in government. But in general, I think the positive aspects outweigh the negatives. He took the ministries of services which made his party very close to the people and gained more popularity. I even think that his decision to increase the salaries of the army soldiers had positive reactions among the various ranks of the army.

Q. What is the relation between the IAF and the Muslim Brotherhood?

A. It is a strong relation. The MB refused to register their organization as a political party because their arena of activity covers more than just politics. They have their own religious, educational, cultural, charitable and political programmes. But when the Law of Parties was issued, the MB found that they should be involved in the political activity with a new mechanism and means. Therefore, a decision was made to establish a political party.

Anyone from the MB is free and not forced to join this party which is the IAF. Also the Islamists and the common people can join this party. Even the Christians can join our party provided they believe in the Islamic Shariah (Law) as the authority and the law that should be implemented. Therefore, the IAF is open for the Muslim Brothers and for the ordinary people although the majority of members of the party are from the MB. According to the Law, any political party should be administratively, politically, and financially independent from any other organization. Therefore, our party is independent from the MB. But this does not prevent the two from coordinating with each other. They also have policies and programmes that conform with each other.

RELIGIOUS ZIONISM IN FOCUS

A Jew who killed a non-Jew was exempt from human judgment

By: Israel Shahak

Colonial Bulldozers Uproot Palestinian Existence

By: Ruba Farkh

It does not take a genius to realize what the current Israeli government is planning to do regarding the final status negotiations. It has been forming a new 'de facto' situation to be the basis for the final talks in which a final agreement is to be reached regarding the refugees, occupied Jerusalem, Jewish settlements and the borders.

Concerning the new map being drawn by the Israeli procedures and practices, signs show beyond any doubt that 60% of the Palestinian land in the West Bank will stay under Israeli occupation. Benjamin Netanyahu, the Israeli premier, has allocated about $350 million for constructing new Jewish settlements and fattening the already existing ones, despite the fact that over 25% of the houses built in the settlements are vacant, according to a recent U.S. government report.

During the period from June 1967 to September 1996, the Israeli occupation authorities confiscated over 73% of the land in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. While making it easier for the settlers to build houses, the Palestinians - the original owners of the land- had to face a wide range of Israeli procedures and policies which in many cases obstructed their housing rights and the right to build and spread out. They had to face house demolitions and confiscation of land and real estate, under the allegation of the absence of their owners from the occupied territories, as well as the construction of the so-called bypass roads, fattening the Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, and the levying of high taxes, fees and fines.

Since the current government came into office, colonial policies flourished in fulfilment of the Prime Ministerís promises to his electors. Jerusalem was the main core in implementing such policies. The recent construction on Jabal Abu Ghneim was just one example of the Israeli governmentís insistence on "changing the ethnic and demographic distribution in the city" according to Shlomo Gazeen.

In the belief that Jerusalem is the eternal united capital of Israel, consecutive Israeli governments have worked hard at evacuating Palestinian residents from the Holy City, or at least reducing their numbers to the minimum level possible.

As a result of these unjust policies, the Arabs who were a majority in the city in 1967 are now a minority forming only 30% of the total population of Jerusalem, although population growth among Arabs is 4.5% compared with only 1.3% among Jews. The Israeli authorities were not satisfied with the 10 settlements in Jerusalem, but went on building new settlements, in addition to fattening those already existing by adding 20,000 new housing units. As if that were not enough to minimize Arab existence in the Holy City, the Israeli authorities began dealing with the Palestinian citizens of Jerusalem with a set of procedures which included: confiscation of their identity cards, cancellation of residential permits of any of those living abroad or even those living outside the district of the Jerusalem municipality, and denying the registration of newly born babies on any of the parents' identity cards if born outside the occupied territories or beyond the municipal borders of Jerusalem. The Israeli authorities also refuse to renew travel permits for those who work outside the city or are studying in foreign countries.

In addition, Jerusalem municipality is demolishing Arab houses built without licences without prior warning. In the last six months, more than 1,000 houses were built without licences by Palestinians in East Jerusalem after failing to obtain official licences from the Israeli municipal authorities. Israel also imposes very high licence fees which Palestinians cannot afford. The Israeli Mayor of Jerusalem, Ehude Olmert, has promised to demolish these houses. He considers them a "cancer threatening Israeli sovereignty in Jerusalem, and damaging the infrastructure needed for living in a united city."

Pouring oil on troubled waters or adding salt to the wound

Probably one of the ironies of the colonial process now taking place in Palestine is the role played by the land brokers. Some Palestinian land brokers facilitate selling land to Israelis. They use fraud and forgery or even violence to force Palestinians into selling their land. Because of the dangerous role these brokers are playing, the Palestinian Authority set a law prohibiting the selling of land to Israelis, ignoring the big fuss the Israeli government is making over the law. Needless to say that such a law could also lead to disastrous consequences upon the Palestinian society if recklessly used.

Al-Qastal Before 1948The village stood on top of a high, rounded hill, overlooking wide expanses in all four directions. It overlooked the Jerusalem-Jaffa highway to the north and northeast, to which it was linked by a secondary road. Its position over the highway accorded al-Qastal a strategic significance. The village name was derived from the Latin castellum, the term for the Roman castle that was built on the site. The old Roman castle was renovated or rebuilt and named Belveer des Croises during the Crusader period. [Thompson, Goncalves, and van Cangh 1988:78] In the late nineteenth century, al-Qastal was located on a rocky hilltop. There were several springs outside the village to the east. [SWP (1881) III:18] Classified as a hamlet by the British Mandate government's Palestine Index Gazetteer, the village had a semicircular plan, and its houses were built of stone. More recent construction extended along the eastern slopes of the region around the al-Qastal hill. The village residents, who were predominantly Muslim, maintained a shrine for a local sage, one Shaykh Karaki, on the western edge of the village. The villagers relied on the neighboring city of Jerusalem for many of their needs. They cultivated rainfed grain, vegetables, and fruit and olive trees. Their agricultural lands were concentrated in a rectangular strip southwest of the village. In 1944/45 a total of 42 dunums was planted in cereals; 169 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards, of which 50 were planted with olive trees.

Occupation and Depopulation

The occupation of al-Qastal took place before the official start of Operation Nachshon (see Bayt Naqquba, Jerusalem District). Israeli historian Benny Morris claims, improbably, that it was "the first Arab village during the 1948 war to be taken by the Haganah with the aim of permanent conquest and occupation."

The Palmach's Fourth Battalion attacked and occupied the village on 3 April. A spearhead unit of commandos stormed the village before dawn; it was soon followed by the main force, which fought off counterattacks into the night. The New York Times correspondent reported that there were battles around the village over the following few days. According to Palmach documents, the initial attack was unopposed and the village's defenders and the inhabitants had left beforehand. But Palestinian historian 'Arif al-'Arif states that it was defended by fifty of the village militiamen, who only withdrew when their ammunition ran out. A report in the Palestinian newspaper Filastin confirms that the villagers had evacuated after an attack in mid-March but says that a number of the village's men remained to defend it. The paper said that the defenders broke through the siege laid by the Haganah forces and retreated to a nearby village. On 5 April, the Haganah occupiers built a barbed-wire fence around their positions and two days later used training planes to drop bombs on Palestinian forces surrounding al-Qastal, according to the New York Times. The village was retaken by Palestinian guerrillas in a battle on 8 April during which their leader, Jerusalem commander 'Abd al-Qadir al-Husayni, was killed. The Haganah account states that a number of Jewish commanders also were killed while retreating. Several days of heavy fighting had preceded the final Palestinian counterattack, which was launched at 1:30 P.M. under cover of mortar and machinegun fire. The New York Times correspondent wrote, "They swarmed up the steep boulder-covered slopes shouting their battle cries." By 3:00 P.M., a force drawn from a number of Palestinian guerrilla groups had broken into the village.

However, Palmach forces reoccupied it in an assault which began on the night of 8-9 April. When they approached the village this time, the brigade was surprised to find it deserted; on 9 April, most of the men who had recaptured it were participating in the funeral of their slain commander, which was being held at the mosque of the Dome of the Rock in Jerusalem. The Palmach forces surrounded the village with a number of armored vehicles to prevent reinforcements from arriving and proceeded to overrun it. The attack occurred at the same time as the massacre at Dayr Yasin, less than 5 km away. The History of the Haganah states that "el Qastal returned once again and forever into Jewish hands."

As soon as they had reoccupied it, the Palmach units started to destroy its houses, thus setting a precedent for all the villages taken during Operation Nachshon. The reason given for the levelling of the village was that it would make the site easier to defend and prevent it from falling back into Arab hands. Palestinian historian al-'Arif states that all buildings in the village, including the shrine/mosque, were destroyed by Zionist forces. A New York Times story confirms that the village mosque was destroyed, but states that this had occurred in the previous month, during the night of 16 March. The newspaper article quoted 'Abd al-Qadir al-Husayni, who was later to lose his life fighting for the village, as saying that the demolition was a "shameful act" and that the mosque "has never been used for anything but worship." The Village Today

The southern, northern, and eastern slopes of the site are covered with stone rubble and the ruins of stone terraces, half-concealed by wild grass. The ruins of the old castle lie on the mountain top. An underground shelter has been built on the site, southwest of the fortress. Military trenches can be seen north and east of the fortress. Olive, carob, and fig trees grow on the north and west sides of the site, and cactuses grow on the southern side The entire site, including parts of the fortress, serves as an Israeli tourist attraction.
