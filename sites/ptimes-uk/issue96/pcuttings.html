Press Cuttings

The Guardian, 25 May 1999

In Dir Kadis, just across the Green Line which demarcates the pre-1967 border many Palestinians fondly imagine will become their future border with Israel, Sa'ida Hadad came home from hospital after losing the five-month baby she was carrying when Israeli soldiers invaded her home.

The miscarriage was only one of the family's losses. Their olive trees were bulldozed by settlers from Kiryat Sefer, a settler town a few kilometres to the south.

This is what is called "creating facts on the ground" and the settler movement, in a last frantic spasm of activity, is seizing whatever gains it can before Mr Barak lives up to his election promises and ends such "unilateral actions" by implementing the Wye agreement.

The mayor of Dir Kadis, Abu Mohammed, fears that his village will be crushed between the four settlements surrounding the 1,600 Palestinian residents. It is unusual for even the most extreme settlers to grab land so close to an established community.

Ha'aretz, 25 May 1999

The (Israeli) Civil Administration has begun marking new municipal boundaries for Kiryat Arba (a settlement near Hebron), including lands claimed by Palestinian owners that were previously declared to be beyond the settlement's municipal limits.

Two weeks ago, the administration evicted settlers attempting to grab lands for settlement, unilaterally altering the status quo on the ground on the same lands now included within the town's limits.

The Palestinians who claim ownership of the land and their Israeli lawyer have denounced the administration's steps as "a surrender to settlers' pressure to railroad the measure, in order to increase Kiryat Arba's land reserves prior to [Prime Minister-elect Ehud] Barak's government taking office."

The Palestinian owners, including the Sultan family and the Hebron Teachers Association, purchased the land in 1976, planning to build a new neighborhood on the property.

The Boston Globe, 23 May 1999

Shas, a ''haredi'' movement that represents mostly first- and second-generation immigrants from North Africa and the greater Middle East, captured 17 seats in Israel's 120-member parliament. By picking up seven seats, Shas, a Hebrew acronym for Sephardic Guardians of the Torah, consolidated its place as the third-largest party in Israel and reached a political parity with the conservative Likud Party, which plunged to only 19 seats from the 32 it had before the elections.

The secular left parties also scored big victories and many secular voters, even those from the political right, said they voted for Barak's left-of-center One Israel list because they thought it would contain ultra-Orthodox influence in politics. So the results of the election revealed a deepening of the religious-secular divide.

The Chicago Tribune, 23 May 1999

Ahmed Abu Galwis isn't celebrating the emergence of another pro-peace Israeli prime minister. He lost money the last time he did that.. Galwis bitterly recalls how he invested in land after the 1993 Oslo peace accords. So hopeful were Palestinians then that he was selling as soon as he bought, for cash and nice profits.

But few Palestinians have been in a land-buying mood since the peace process bogged down, And to Galwis, who just sold his last parcel of land at a big loss, Prime Minister-elect Ehud Barak started off sounding like the man he is to replace, Benjamin Netanyahu.

"As we say in Arabic, `Hassan is the brother of Hussein.' They're all alike," Galwis said. Those sentiments are echoed again and again across the West Bank in the wake of Barak's landslide victory last week. Palestinians are hopeful, but Barak's army record, his provocative statements and disappointments under his Labor predecessors make people wary.

The Financial Times, 22 May 1999

In the army, Mr Barak was dubbed the "little Napoleon" and was more used to leading commando units than delegating authority. The criticism stuck even when he became chief of staff.

For the moment Israelis are giving him the benefit of the doubt. They voted for Mr Barak because they think he is honest and capable, in contrast to Benjamin Netanyahu, the defeated incumbent, who had a reputation for deceit and manipulation.

Saeb Erekat, Mr Arafat's peace negotiator, says the Palestinians have no illusions about Israel's new prime minister. "Barak is no dove. He will be a tough negotiator. I am not so sure he is prepared to make the necessary compromises to make our state viable or secure a lasting peace."
