We have been fooled!

Dr. Musil Shihadeh*

I have to admit that, at the beginning, I was not vehemently opposed to Oslo, since out of frustration and desperation for an honorable peace, I have convinced myself that Oslo could be the proper vehicle to achieve our national objectives. Oslo was based on UN Resolution 242, which embodied the principle of the inadmissibility of acquiring land by the use of force, and even stipulated a just solution to the Palestinian refugee problem. With a proper emotional anxiety, one can easily find some strings from these principles to create false hopes that Oslo embodies in it the final just solution to the entire Palestinian Israeli problem. Such beautiful dreams were dashed to small fragments against the onslaught of Israeli expansionism on the West Bank, while the violation of our civil and human rights have become the daily motto of Zionism cruel practices against our people, as the ugly de facto took over the hopes of Oslo’s de jure.

Let us face it, the status quo cannot contribute in any manner toward an optimistic vision for the future of peace in Palestine. The Israeli daily creation of new facts on the grounds, by expropriation of more lands and the demolitions of more homes cannot be construed as a step toward a final solution. The late Sharon-Natanyahu plan for the future of the West Bank unveils a grotesque picture of the future, as “buffer” zones built on Arab lands, are to be sliced off from all direction surrounding the West Bank with super-highways connecting the 165 Jewish settlements into Israel proper. What would be left cannot under any optimistic terminology be called a state, or cantons for that matter, but a collection of Palestinian ghettoes dissected and discontinued from each other by Israeli checkpoints. The final debacle would end up with the Palestinian authority controlling less than 10% of Palestine while the Israelis would have 90% of the lands under their cruel control. One cannot escape the irony that if retrospectively compared to their land ownership prior to the 1948, the Jews total ownership of the lands was less than 10%! So they have switched positions through the biggest land grab of the 20th Century.

To pacify the disgruntled Palestinian masses an interim agreement has been signed to persuade us that all Israeli violations on the ground are only transient and could be rectified by the final status negotiations. This should legally mean that, at the end of the final negotiations, all homes demolished would be rebuilt by the Israelis to allow the original owners to return peacefully to their original habitat, all land expropriated would be restored to their original owners, all evicted Palestinians would be allowed repatriation, all super-highways would be removed to allow the continuity of the Palestinian state and all the settlements would be dismantled, and its Jewish inhabitants would be returned to Israel proper! That is what our naïve leadership is trying to sell to the Palestinian masses, and here where we should draw the line that can separate fact from fiction. To believe such parody one has to either become an ignoramus or have “faith” in miracles. Israel is not going to return the expropriated lands, nor would it, in our wildest dreams, ever entertain the thought of dismantling the newly built settlements and highways, nor would it ever return our refugees. With the present trend, created by our disillusionment in Oslo, we could never dream of achieving our national objective of acquiring a viable and independent Palestinian State.

We do have other options and we should explore them and pursue them immediately before we reach the end of our rope.

What has not been adequately exposed publicly, is the established fact that the Palestinians hold the most powerful card for the future of the entire region, as they alone hold the vital decision on the future of war and peace in the entire area. The PA ostensible weak position cannot obviate the fact that without the Palestinian cooperation, peace in the whole area could not be finalized. Without the Palestinians the Israelis could never reach a stage of normal relations with the Arab and Moslem world, to include political and economic cooperation. The Israelis realize that solving the Palestinian problem could be the only bridge that can carry them across to its normal existence as an acceptable member of the Middle East community. The Arab masses from the Arab gulf to the Atlantic Ocean have more than a strong affinity with the Palestinian cause and no leader would risk his own position to discourage the Palestinian from fighting for their national rights. Jerusalem stand as a sacred city in the hearts of all Moslems and no one would acquiesce to have it permanently subjugated under Zionist hegemony while oppressing the indigenous Palestinians into leaving the Holy City. With this in mind armed struggle should not discounted as a viable option for the liberation of Palestine, as we have the resources, the determination and the enormous support of the masses in both the Arab and Moslem world. We should never succumb to all outside pressure to surrender our rights under the dictates of a strong oppressor.

Yes we do have other options, but such options could not be actuated by simply “ wait and see attitude” as time may not be necessarily on our side especially if our leadership is actively participating fully with the Zionist dreams. We need to organize in a unified front and work very hard toward such objectives. No one ever said that liberation of our patrimony is a road full of roses, as the road is long and more sacrifices are expected. We should learn from history that when the oppressed keep their torch of freedom lit they would reach the end of the tunnel. We have to understand that and make sure that Israel does too.

* American writer of Palestinian origin

Israel, U.S. press Arafat for further concessions

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khalid Amayreh

The Zionist regime and the United States are expected to exert tremendous pressure on Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat for further concessions, particularly on such fundamental final status issues as Jerusalem and the refugees, as well as the borders of the prospective Palestinian State on parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

Palestinian sources have intimated that in return for recognizing a Palestinian State in principle, the U.S. and Israel will demand that Arafat accede to the Israeli “red lines” as repeatedly spelled out by Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak.

These red lines include Israel’s refusal to return to the pre-1967 borders, Jerusalem remaining under Israeli control, the bulk of Jewish settlements to be annexed and incorporated into Israel, and no repatriation or restitution for the estimated 4 million Palestinian refugees who were expelled from their homeland at gunpoint when Israel was created over 50 years ago.

The above-mentioned sources said the United States and Israel would make it clear to Arafat that unless he agreed to come to terms with Israeli demands to that effect, he would not be allowed to embody Palestinian Statehood on the ground, a step which would further discredit Arafat in the eyes of the Palestinian people and could ultimately bring about his downfall.

Furthermore, according to the sources, the U.S. and Israel will also warn Arafat that unless he gives in, they would seek to sever all financial and economic aide to the Palestinian Authority as well as incite instability within the autonomous enclaves.

Officially, the PA has maintained its position that the Palestinians would not accept anything less than full Israeli withdrawal from the West Bank and Gaza Strip in conformity with United Nations Security Council resolutions 242 and 338 as well as finding a just solution to the refugees’ cause in accordance with UN resolution 149, which grants the refugees both repatriation and restitution.

Indeed, PA officials have of late made a series of uncharacteristically tough statements, insisting on the full implementation of pertinent UN resolutions.

However, observers in Palestine contend the tough talk is only tactical in nature and designed to extract the best possible deal from Israel, given the extremely weak negotiating position of Arafat’s regime.

Moreover, the uncompromising statements are unlikely to reflect the thinking of Yasser Arafat, the sole decision maker in the PA.

And in light of Arafat’s long history of giving concessions to the Zionists in the name of “safeguarding the peace process” and in return for some “symbolic achievements”, the Palestinian leader does not seem to warrant the benefit of the doubt, as many Palestinian intellectuals now openly admit.

Indeed, Arafat himself has only rarely spoken of the need to guarantee the right of return for the refugees.

He instead spoke in general terms about a “just and equitable settlement of the refugee problem” and whenever he mentioned resolution 194, he did not use emphatic or strong language to ascertain that inalienable right.

In addition, there have been some extremely worrying signs leading observers to believe that Arafat may eventually cave in to the Zionists and their American puppets.

For example, the Israeli Hebrew daily, Ma’ariv, reported on 28 April that Arafat deposited a position paper with U.S. President Clinton recently whereby Arafat demonstrated a willingness to compromise on the issue of refugees and borders in return for receiving a Palestinian State with East Jerusalem as its capital.

Ma’ariv reported that the position paper, which contained 13 clauses, was a detailed draft proposal for the framework agreement with Israel.

The main gist of the proposal speaks of the PA’s willingness to meet Israel half way in everything relating to security arrangements.

The paper also notes that the Palestinian leadership was referring here to Israeli early warning stations on hilltops and other forms of military presence in the Jordan Valley.

The PA is also prepared to be more flexible regarding border corrections, which will enable Israel to annex the large settlement blocs near its borders.

As regards the refugee issue, the paper reportedly suggests that the PA will agree to a solution which will lead to the mass return of refugees to pre-1967 Palestine.

Palestinian official Sa’eb Ureikat denied the existence of such a paper.

He said during an interview with the PA-run radio station “Voice of Palestine” on 29 April that the “alleged document has no truth to it” and that “it is likely to be another balloon test.”

Ureikat’s thinking, however, may not and often does not reflect that of Yasser Arafat’s, who has neither confirmed nor denied the report.

Khalid Misha’al: No stability in the region
without restoring the Palestinian rights

Interview in Doha by Mahmoud al Khatib

The head of the political bureau of the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, Khalid Misha’al has said that Hamas is still calling for resistance as the only option to restoring the legitimate rights of the Palestinian People.

Speaking in an interview with the Palestine Times in Doha last month, Mr. Misha’al strongly denied that his movement’s political status was weakened. He also criticized the optimism showed by some Arab regimes when Barak was elected as Prime Minister in Israel. He also described his deportation from Jordan with three other members of the Hamas political bureau as a “loss” but said Hamas was not absorbed by that issue and did not consider it as its battle.

Following is the full text of the interview:

Question: We notice that Hamas’ military and political actions have been going backward in the last few months. Do you agree with this remark? What are the reasons?

Answer: There are certain circumstances that affect the political and military work. There is no doubt that the military resistance of the occupation had been targeted by the Zionist and the PA’s security services alike during the last period. There are also the hard efforts exerted by the CIA to help the Zionist and the PA security agencies under the pretext of combating terrorism. However, there were various resistance operations carried out by the Hamas military wing, the Ezzul-Deen Al-Qassam Brigades. Some of these operations have been successful while some others were aborted, such as the recent one in Al-Taibeh. All these successes support the idea that resistance is the only option for the Palestinian People and that we are not going to give up because of these security obstacles. As for political action, I emphasize that we are retrograding. However, everyone can see for themselves that the Arab official arena was busy with the prospects of the peace process during the last few months. Since Barak came to power in the Zionist regime, some Arab officials pinned their hopes on his coming–a thing that was not based on firm ground–but they were eager to get rid of the nightmares caused by Netanyahu. Hamas has also experienced similar circumstances before. But the political events and the nature of the Zionist conduct during this stage all enhance our conviction that the Arab official regimes will find themselves facing hard options and that their relying on the peace process is baseless. They will eventually reach a conclusion that the Arab regimes and people cannot do without the resistance forces, at the top of which is Hamas.

Q: It seems that the peace process is now experiencing a decisive stage. How do you assess the future of the current negotiations between the Zionists and the PA?

A: The negotiations on the Palestinian track has entered the final stage although their substance has interfered with the remains of the transitional stage. The Zionists have been exerting two kinds of pressure on the Palestinian Authority: the first is by ignoring the PA under the pretext of concentrating on their talks with the Syrians and the Lebanese in order to blackmail the PA and get more relinquishments from it. The other is to pressure the PA in order for them to sign the “frame” agreement quickly with the delay of the basic issues, especially the issues of the refugees and Jerusalem. In order to achieve this goal, the Israelis pretend to be flexible regarding the declaration of the Palestinian State in order to substitute for the liberation of the occupied territories. The Zionists are now seeking to conclude a frame agreement with the PA similar to the 1993 Oslo accords, postponing the negotiations of the details and of the main issues to the future in order to impose their own agenda on the PA. We are sure that the basic rights of the Palestinian People will again be the victim of these negotiations, especially the issues of the refugees and Jerusalem.

Q: The PA claims that it has made achievements on the ground through the negotiations, in particular the Israeli withdrawals from the West Bank and the Gaza Strip as well as the indications introduced by Barak for the first time regarding his agreement on the declaration of the Palestinian State. How do you see these achievements?

A: These indications from Barak were made in order to extract more relinquishments from the PA and to accept the Israeli demands regarding the issues of the refugees and Jerusalem. Also, the change in the Zionist position regarding the Palestinian State is just in appearance, but the content of this position remains the same. There is no change in the Zionist position regarding the sovereignty on the land and on the borders. The four “NOs” declared by Barak after he was elected last year have been increased and became five during his recent visit to Washington. The newest Israeli “NO” was with regard to the Palestinian refugees. He said the Zionist regime cannot bear any moral or legal responsibility towards the Palestinian refugees and that their problem should be solved by resettling them where they are living now and by giving them compensation although Israel would not be responsible for paying the compensation. In general, there are two facts regarding the current negotiations: the first is the change in the priorities. Instead of liberating and extracting the Palestinian occupied land and then the establishment of the Palestinian State, the PA has become busy with the establishment of the State without having the land liberated. What is the value of this State without land and without sovereignty? The other fact is the stereotyping of some limited achievements in order to cover up the relinquishments given by the PA regarding the issues of the refugees and Jerusalem. I would like to ask the Arab regimes how they can accept such consequences that eventually lead to holding them responsible for settling about 4.5 million refugees on their soil instead of keeping it a Zionist responsibility. We warn of the consequences of the final status talks. The Arab and Muslim nations should bear responsibilities towards this central issue.

Q: But what is Hamas’ programme to face these consequences?

A: Hamas is concerned with enlightening its people, in the first instance, and the Arab and Muslim people about what is going on. It is also concerned with defending the rights of the Palestinian People represented in the issues of the refugees, Jerusalem, the prisoners and the defending of the Palestinian land that has been confiscated daily. We are concerned with alarming the Arab and Muslim nations in order to bear their responsibility towards the Palestinian issue. During the last few months, Hamas focused on the Jerusalem issue and called for the holding of an Arab and Islamic conference to defend Jerusalem. During our meetings with the Arab officials and popular forces, Hamas sought to mobilize an Arab and Islamic position to support the Palestinian Cause. In addition, Hamas is still abiding by resisting the occupation despite the difficulties it has been facing.

Q: You are an active organ in the Palestinian Opposition Front. Do you believe that this Front has no role to play nowadays?

A: There is no doubt that the situation of the Palestinian Opposition is not satisfactory. But one of the merits of the Palestinian situation is that it is moving. This leads to the conception that the Opposition factions are not the only players in the Palestinian arena, but there are popular forces which are formed continuously and there are also the independent figures. The Opposition factions, popular forces and the Palestinian notables and figures all are reacting with the Palestinian different concerns and issues such as the return of the Palestinian refugees to their homeland. This environment is that which will produce a Palestinian situation that supports the resistance.

Q: How do you see the future of the Palestinian Opposition factions based in Damascus in case the Syrians sign a peace agreement with the Jewish State? Will this affect Hamas when it loses important allies such as these factions?

A: I don’t think that any development in the talks between the Syrians and the Israelis could lead to closure of offices of the Palestinian Opposition factions in Damascus because any achievement on the Syrian track does not mean the stopping of justifications of the work for the Palestinian Cause. The Palestinian People have the right to continue their struggle regardless of any agreement signed between the Zionist regime and the Arab parties. No one can confiscate the right of the Palestinian People to work for their cause under all circumstances until they restore their full rights. I also do not believe that the Syrian official position will prevent the Palestinians from working for their cause in case a peace agreement is signed between Damascus and Tel Aviv.

Q: The issue of the Palestinian prisoners in the Israeli jails is an important human one. What is your role in solving this issue?

A: There are more than 2,000 Palestinian prisoners and detainees in the Zionist jails. Most of them belong to Hamas and are ignored by the agreements signed between the PA and the Zionist entity. Most of those who were released from the Israeli jails were members of the Fateh Movement–the PLO mainstream faction–and the pro-Oslo organizations. I think that the Palestinian prisoners have the right to be supported and have their cause defended by their people and the freedom-lovers because by their sacrifices they achieved many things for their people. But the PA had ignored their sacrifices and did not exert serious efforts to release them. Hamas has been adopting a programme to support the Palestinian prisoners. The programme began with rallies in Hebron last month. There will be other activities to support this cause in order to pressure the Zionists to release them. When we talk about prisoners in the Israeli jails, we feel shame to say that there are hundreds of Palestinians in the PA jails, especially when they have been detained only for the interests of the Zionist Enemy. This shows that the only achievement made by the PA is its security cooperation with the Enemy and the arrest of its own people.

Q: What are the latest developments regarding your return to Jordan?

A: Unfortunately, all mediations and political efforts regarding this issue have failed. There is no explanation to this Jordanian official position except the response to external pressures. If the Jordanian position was based on pure Jordanian national interest, it would not take this unjustified position which rejects our return to Amman. This position contradicts the Jordanian Constitution since we hold the Jordanian nationality. But our case has been continued by the Jordanian Supreme Court of Justice. During the last session of the Court, it appeared that the legal position of the Jordanian government was weak. They [the defense lawyers] also exposed the fact that the Jordanian government wanted to keep this case going for a long time and so they [the defense lawyers] tried to kill it by all means.

Q: You know the Jordanian government’s position rejects the presence of Hamas leaders in Amman owing to external pressure. What is the result you are looking for by insisting on your return to Amman?

A: Whatever the Jordanian position is and whatever the pressure exerted on it, we are talking about our rights as Jordanian citizens which should not be confiscated because of this pressure. As Jordanian citizens, we have the right to live in our homeland and should not be deported. Meanwhile, I want to emphasize that Hamas is not totally absorbed by this issue. We also do not consider it as a battle to be fought. Hamas has its own concerns and programme. It is absorbed by the concerns of the Palestinian Cause, the Palestinian man and the developments on the Palestinian soil.

Just Another Arab

Ruth Anderson

On April 14, 1948, the Irgun and Stern Gang units performed a heinous operation in the quiet peaceful village of Deir Yassin. It was on this day that 254 men, women and children were brutally massacred as part of Plan Dalet which went into effect the first week of April 1948. The Dalet plan was made up of two operations, Nachson and Havel, which were designed to occupy and destroy the Palestinian villages along the whole length of the Jaffa-Jerusalem road. The report of the Israel Defense Forces Intelligence Branch admitted that the massacre of these unarmed and helpless civilians was a “decisive accelerating factor” of the Palestinian exodus. Israeli army witnesses said that the Irgun and Stern Gang “shot everyone they saw in the houses, including women and children... and that “the commanders made no attempt to check the disgraceful acts of slaughter.

Forty-eight years later, Israeli Prime Minister Peres, who was paradoxically granted the Nobel Prize for Peace, ordered operation “Grapes of Wrath,” a bombing blitz in Southern Lebanon, which demolished 17 villages, left more than half a million people homeless, killed 200 and wounded several hundreds more. On April 18, 1996, in a ruthless Israeli strike against a UN peace-keeping compound in Southern Lebanon, the civilians who sought refuge at the UN compound in Qana, from operation Grapes of Wrath, were shelled, resulting in the deaths of 102 people.

The first Prime Minister of the illegal state of Israel, Ben Gurion, had the attitude that all Palestinians should be gotten rid of as if they were not even human. How they were done away with was of no importance, what mattered was that they were disposed of either by atrocious massacres or by being driven out of Palestine in mass exoduses. The Holocaust exchanged hands. The so-called victims of Hitler’s Nazi regime became the perpetrators of even more vicious and violent crimes against humanity. The difference was that the media, now controlled mostly by Zionists or Zionist sympathizers became either totally oblivious to the genocide of the Palestinians or completely circumvented accurate coverage of what was going on. They were aided by American dollars, American lack of interest and the greed/ignorance of Arab leaders who basically were out to make certain their little kingdoms or dictatorships survived even at the cost of losing the Holy Land and the lives or well being of the indigenous inhabitants of this Land of Honey.

The Israeli military commander responsible for the Qana attack said,
"We did our duty. They deserve to die because they are only Arabs."

An Israeli soldier told "Kol Ha'Irr", an Israeli magazine, "I obeyed the orders. My conscience does not bother me at all. We should have fired more shells to kill more Arabs . . . One Arab more, one Arab less, you know.

Even the battery commander said, "Anyway, there are millions of Arabs."

It is difficult for the rational mind to justify the fact that the oppressed have become the oppressors. What is more amazing is the oppressed do such a splendid job of oppressing. What is shocking is that they perform their atrocities by classifying them as campaigns to wipe out terrorism, or to surgically remove threats to the security of Israel when in reality, it is merely a campaign to gain more Arab land at the expense of Arab civilian lives.

Women and children are not the enemies of a heavily armed military machine. Ambulances carrying the dead and wounded are not guided missiles but vehicles of mercy. Yet, such an ambulance in Qana was shelled, killing all those inside who were being rushed to the nearest hospital for emergency treatment. The same thing applies to those innocent civilians of the peaceful village of Deir Yassin. They were the enemies of no one. They had committed no crime. They were not even aware that on one Spring day when life held so much promise, their lives would be so brutally taken away from them. And no one was spared, not a woman, nor a child and especially not any adult male.

What the Qana of April 1996 in Lebanon and the Deir Yassin of April 1948 in Palestine, have in common is the ruthlessness of an Israeli conscience that does not even exist. And after all, what is one more Arab killed? There are millions of them!

Americans experience the pain of remembering

Ray Hanania*

There is a tradition in the Arab world of “remembering”. We often commemorate events of tragedy that have occurred in our national lives.

The list of tragedies is long. Deir Yassin in Palestine in 1947 witnessed more than 100 civilians butchered by Zionist terrorists who later became Prime Ministers of Israel.

There were many subsequent massacres of Palestinians orchestrated by Israelis as recent as a few years ago.

Many Americans don’t relate to that experience. They tend to want to commemorate “positive” events, such as holidays and national achievements.

But, in recent years, they have been forced to reconcile pain and suffering. And they are coming closer to understanding why the Arab world commemorates tragedy.

Just this month (April), for example, there were two big commemorations. One concerned the year-old anniversary of the massacre at Columbine High School in Littleton, Colorado, carried out by two fellow students. The killers–kids who were in their early teens–worshipped Adolph Hitler and the Nazis and planned their murderous rampage to take place on Hitler’s birthday, 20 April. In a rampage that lasted about an hour, the two “kids” murdered 12 fellow students and a popular teacher. They even recorded a videotape before the attack to explain their disdain for their fellow students who, they complained, bullied, badgered and mocked them in school. (Bullying others is an American tradition, although it is no justification to murder innocent people.)

And then there was Oklahoma. It’s been five years since Americans went berserk blaming “Arabs” for the worst terrorist attack since George Washington levelled a fort manned by British soldiers in the eighteenth century. It was exactly five years ago the same week that two Americans plotted and then blew up, in a massive explosion that rocked this “heartland” city, the Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building. The explosion ripped apart the multi-storied building, tearing away nearly half of it and exposing its inner-office rooms and dwellings. The image of the bombed-out Murrah Federal Building so closely resembled building’s destroyed in numerous American bombing raids n Beirut, Lebanon, that at first, when I saw it, I thought we were witnessing another American-led attack against an Arab target.

But, alas, despite the anti-Arab hysteria that reached a frenzied crescendo, it turned out that the vicious, blood-thirsty, murdering baby-killers turned out to be Americans after all. Yes. When the dust settled in Oklahoma and media commentators from around the country had called for the arrest and murder of Arabs and the bombing of Arab countries and cities finally stopped screaming for blood, the truth came out.

Arabs didn’t do it, like everyone wanted to believe and thought. Americans did it. Just like they have done it in hundreds of other countries that Americans don’t feel are worth their time remembering at all.

Middle Americans with names like Timothy McVeigh and his accomplice Terry Nichols were arrested by accident. A police officer noticed McVeigh’s car had a faulty turn signal and he pulled him over. Smelling the strong odour of the chemicals used in making explosives, the officer searched the vehicle. And in a singular moment of enlightenment for an American, this Oklahoma officer realized that the driver, a non-Arab, was in fact involved in the explosion. The blast killed 149 adults and 19 children–crushed the same way Iraqi babies must have died during the American-led attack on Baghdad many years earlier.

While the Columbine students were commemorating Hitler’s birthday, McVeigh and Nichols were commemorating the 19 April 1993 attack against the weird inhabitants at Waco, Texas. There, federal authorities attacked a commune of religious fanatics who did not want to surrender their weapons. David Koresh and more than 60 people died in the gun battle the federal authorities initiated, burning the building with all its inhabitants, including the children.

The entire American nation took pause for the innocent American lives that vanished during the two tragedies.

The name Qana, Lebanon, has meaning to the Arab people.

On 18 April 1996, Israel attacked Lebanon, again. Using American-made weapons, Israeli soldiers, with fresh paychecks funded by American taxpayers, blasted their way into the civilian village of Qana.

More than 17 high-impact shells, made by the United States, landed on a United Nations base that was protecting Arab civilians that had fled the brutal Israeli assault. When the smoke cleared, as it eventually did at the Murrah Federal Building, more than 106 men, women and children were dead. Men, women and children. Mothers, fathers, sisters, brothers. Children. Infants. Babies. All dead. And not one American thought it worthy to stand up and say they were sorry.

I understand the pain of Columbine and Oklahoma City, more than the American people could ever understand. I know it better than them.

Thanks in large part to Americans and their allies, the Arab world has experienced some of the most grisly acts of carnage and killings that have been committed on this planet. We know what it’s like to remember tragedy. We’re sorry you finally know what it’s like, too. But you don’t make it easy to feel sorry for you.

You make movies and documentaries, and the American media gives it such detailed coverage. But when it comes to an Arab death, little is said. Little is reported. Little is documented.

There’s little more I can say.

*Ray Hanania is an award-winning Palestinian Arab author and writer based in Chicago. His columns are archived on the web at www.hanania.com

JUSTICE FOR ALL?

Fatima Hamilton

In today’s world, we hear many people expounding upon human rights and desiring to punish war criminals. We find that people want justice. At least they say they want justice. Yet, justice has a double-standard, depending on what part of the world one lives in and in whose “camp” he resides in.

We always hear in the news of Jews being compensated by Germany–and now Switzerland and France–for the atrocities of the Second World War. France and Switzerland aim to compensate the victims or their families for the assets that their banks and corporations took from them during the war. We find there are many films and books concerning of the Holocaust. Always the Jew draws pity. Well, the Holocaust was indeed pitiful and it is a crime for any human to suffer such atrocities. Yet, in the midst of these reparations to apologize and to compensate the Jews, the world has forgotten that others also suffered at the hands of the Nazis, such as Gypsies and Poles, among others. Where’s their compensation?

If we really want justice for all and for atrocities committed decades ago, then we must be fair to all victims. Justice is supposed to be for all people. The United Nations created the “Declaration of Human Rights”, which many countries must not know about. So, if the West, and others, claim to desire justice, then all victims of unfairness must be compensated.

The Native American Indian should receive reparations from the United States Government because they stole their land and tortured their people; even today, they are not totally well-treated, and those still living on reservations live in bad conditions. Black Americans should receive compensation because they were enslaved for centuries by America, as well as by other countries. Africans, Arabs and Indians should receive compensation as a result of the atrocities they’ve suffered at the hands of the British, the French and the Italian occupations of their lands during the times of colonialism. Japan should receive reparations from the United States and Europe for the atomic bombs that were dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki which killed innocent women, children, elderly and handicapped. Where was America’s mercy? If they were following the Jewish tradition from the Torah of “an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth,” then they went too far; they took a whole limb for a tooth.

What about Serbia’s genocide and ethnic-cleansing that was carried out in Bosnia and Kosovo? Where’s the reparations for these poor, defenseless people who suffered the loss of lives and peace of mind? Their lives have been torn apart. Their women were raped and damaged psychologically. Children witnessed horrors that may have damaged them forever. Can these people ever learn to live a normal life?

What about Chechenya now? Who will defend them? After the war is finished, who will compensate them? Besides, where’s the World now? They went to Kosovo but forgot about Chechenya. Maybe they are afraid of losing their Russian business market and their political ally. Maybe they are too afraid of Russian nuclear weapons. The Chechen rebels are braver than the rest of the world. The West must enjoy dealing with Russian criminals.

Then, we look at Palestine and South Lebanon. Who will help them overcome Israeli atrocities? Will anyone compensate the Palestinian refugees who are scattered throughout the world? Will they ever have a home? Will they ever have an official identity? Will the world ever stop their suffering and torture that Israelis daily inflict on them? Will those who have been damaged psychologically and physically ever get their rights, and who will compensate their losses? Who??

Isn’t it funny how the Jews once suffered and now that they have created Israel, out of someone else’s land, they inflict the same wounds and beyond that upon another people? Is this right? Is this justice?? If they wanted peace with the Palestinians, and Arabs in general, they could have achieved it a long time ago. They are just playing games and buying time in the hope of the world eventually forgetting the truth and forgetting that there are a Palestinian people who need justice. The so-called peace they have signed agreements for only exists on paper. These Israelis carry in their hearts historical baggage of revenge and hate, and they’re willing to avenge it on others. American Foreign Policy and the Art of Demonization

Ramzy Baroud

One of the United States’ primary assets in influencing and shaping world politics is its mastery of the art of propaganda. The use of propaganda resulted in great success during and after the Second World War. The United States, which left the war with few scars and a greater say over global politics, developed its own propaganda and turned this practice into a leading variable in its foreign policy outlook.

While there appears to be little disagreement regarding the legitimacy of U.S. democracy, one cannot deny that in many ways mainstream U.S. media unquestioningly agrees with and supports the government’s stand on many world issues. Such an attitude is often applicable in war times, when it becomes a national duty to eliminate disagreements and side with the government, regardless.

Many years later, the U.S. enhanced the borrowed art, added and deducted accordingly to make it fit with the changing political environment. By far, the art of demonization is the United States’ most unique and most effective technique of them all.

Starting in the early 1950s and escalating to the present and presumably for many years to come, the U.S. tends to view its worldwide conflicts in terms of individual fanaticism rather than nations’ choices. When Jean Bertrand Aristide, for example, was elected president of Haiti in December of 1990 by a 67% landslide victory, the U.S. viscously rejected the Haitian majority choice. The U.S., which supported Aristide’s opponent Marc Bazin, was upset to see that all the money spent to bring its man–former World Bank official–to power went to waste. The demonization process thereafter kicked in. Immediately, Aristide was perceived as a dictator by the American mass media, although his war on corruption and enhancement of social programmes to benefit the poor would have, in a fairer world, drawn much more positive images of him. Eleven months later, Aristide was removed from power through a military coup.

The same political experiment conducted on the Haitians was later used on others, although more visibly in other places. This, of course, should not imply that some of those demonized by the American media and government were flawless and were necessarily good. However, the American scheme attempted to isolate the fact that many of these individuals were a mere reflection of a much larger phenomena.

Why does the United States persist in using the same strategy time and again? The benefits, from the viewpoint of the U.S., are abundant. By demonizing a person rather than confronting the circumstances of which that person was a product, the U.S. is able to largely downplay the question, “Why are these nations opposed to the U.S.?” They can simply justify their actions by stating, “As long as a leader is evil, wicked, undemocratic, and insane, the U.S. is doing the world a favour by fighting him.” Thus, the revolution in Cuba had little to do with the U.S. abuses of the Caribbean nation. It was Castro who was the real hurdle, it is Saddam Hussein who is standing in the way of peace progress in the Middle East, and it is Osama bin Laden who is stirring up unfounded and baseless hatred toward the U.S. and its favoured allies.

The U.S. mastery of the art of demonization is now more successful than ever. Many Americans, whether politically involved or not, are more often heard saying, “Why can’t the government just get rid of X or Y?” During “Desert Fox”, an American man was stopped in the street and asked by a local TV reporter what the government should do to combat “Iraq’s defiance of UN resolutions.” His reply went something like this, “I think that we should bring Saddam Hussein to the U.S., give him a fair trial, and then execute him.” What is even more bizarre than the answer itself is the fact that in many ways, that approach is nothing new in terms of American Foreign Policy.

Regardless of how we, as individuals, choose to perceive certain leaders or key figures in world politics, we should differentiate between those who were a product of events that effected their nations and those who are not. In the case of Osama bin Laden, for instance, the man was certainly an expression of a phenomena that expanded as the Untied States brought increasing harm to Arab and Muslim nations. While many choose to fight the destructive American policy through speeches or literature, others have sought alternative means, which some consider ethically permissible, while others find it unjustifiable.

When Fidel Castro believed that Socialism was the path to follow as the Cuban revolution aimed at reconstructing their shattered society, bin Laden found in Islam the variables to enlighten, inspire and motivate the nations with whom he identified. The issue therefore has little to do with “radical Islam”, as many claim. Islam, however, was an available option that was left unexplored throughout the twentieth century, while borrowed ideologies were the hot commodity in the Muslim and Arab world’s revolutionary market. The U.S., however, tends to single out individuals and beliefs as the sole responsible component to peoples’ behaviour. Many are to blame but hardly the United States’ blind and greedy policies around the world, which lend a considerable helping hand to dictators, stirring up coups and devastating economies.

Individuals don’t last forever, but that hardly changes a thing. If the evil of the world was bound to Hitler, Stalin, Nasir of Salvador Allende, the world would now be a better place. Such truth is never accepted by the U.S. administration, who has been leading an endless wild hunt for individuals and leaders throughout the years and throughout the world. It is time for U.S. policy makers to take a realistic view of the world and respect peoples’ aspirations and nations’ choices. If the U.S. refuses to do so because of the “goodness of its heart”, it should do so if it really values its economic interests and world ties.

Oslo And The Demise Of The Two State Solution

Dr. Naseer Aruri*

(2-2)

Palestinian Strategies

For the Palestinians, the path of the single state is not a new form of acceptable political development. It surfaced as their first program of liberation after the 1967 occupation, namely, the democratic secular state. This program, however, which was linked to armed struggle, was summarily dismissed before it had even been debated in order to accommodate the Arab states' agenda of a diplomatic struggle. In fact, neither the PLO, nor its program, has ever taken roots among the people. There were institutions and there was rhetoric, but the money came from the Arab states. Accordingly, the renunciation of the unitary state idea came as a quid pro quo: the PLO would scale down its ambitions, while the Arab states would provide diplomatic and material help for a mini, independent, Palestinian state alongside Israel. On the surface, diplomacy was declared a great success, particularly as Israel was isolated in a world that came to endorse Palestinian self-determination. In reality; however, it was a phyrric victory as the endorsed Palestinian state never was actually established.

By contrast, the South Africans, who had also declared armed struggle, continued to cling to a unitary state and to reject pressure to renounce armed struggle as a form of terrorism. A separate independent existence was not high on their diplomatic agenda. In the case of Palestine, the United Nations focused on the human rights of the people under military occupation and on their right to a separate existence. But in spite of international recognition of their right to independence, they were never able to experience true emancipation. They continued to endure a status of refugees, of occupied people, and of an ethnic minority in a country in which during the life time of many, they constituted a majority of the population.

When the Palestinian struggle finally shifted toward the political dimension during the Intifada, the goal of a separate independent existence remained intact. It was, however, a struggle suited more toward empowerment and social and economic progress than toward co-existence in a single state. Its principal goal was to make the end of occupation not only desirable, but also manageable.

Alternative Strategies

Now that the pursuit of independence has been impeded by the structural arrangements inherent in Oslo, the Palestinians are challenged more than ever to resurrect the political struggle of the Intifada: building mass organizations and alternative institutions that would enable them to cater to the social, economic, and political needs of the populace. The on-going and ever-increasing land grab will not be halted by diplomatic action, but will be slowed down only by mobilization and mass action. Undoubtedly, the task is daunting, for it challenges not only Israel's occupation regime, but the PA's authority as well, since any form of a struggle, even that designed to effect international law principles, would be considered seditious and hence subject to repression under the guise of fighting terrorism, guarding Israel's security, and meeting the requirements of what Israel calls "reciprocity." And yet there are no short cuts to genuine emancipation. The privations and hardships associated with the status of refugees, of an occupied population, and of second class citizens, will continue to retard Palestinian political development despite all the trappings of statehood, e.g., a President without executive power, a council without legislative powers, courts with insignificant jurisdiction, an over-blown civilian bureaucracy prone to corruption, and a pervasive military apparatus focused on suppressing dissent.

The alternative to Oslo must guarantee the removal of disabilities inflicted on the Palestinians in the three spheres. No degree of independence or liberation is meaningful without removing the legal, social, and economic disabilities that set the Palestinians apart and classify them under the three categories. This requires a determined, systematic, and protracted struggle, which combines the three segments of the Palestinian people, jointly with Israeli Jews who wish to be neither master of another people, nor privileged in an apartheid system, nor colonial settlers denying the existence of the indigenous natives or wishing their disappearance.

The goal of the struggle would have to be equal protection of the law, as in the 14th amendment to the U.S. Constitution, the illegality of any unreasonable classification in the law, and the end of group segregation and its removal from the social, economic, and legal fabric of society. Equality for every single human being in Palestine/Israel would be the motto of the new struggle.

This, of course, is bound to collide with the interests of the major players in Washington, Tel-Aviv, and Ramallah. For it would signal that U.S. domination of Middle East diplomacy has failed. It would serve as an indictment of Zionism, the classical version of Rabin, Peres, and Barak, as well as the revisionist brand of Jabotinsky, Begin, and Netanyahu. It would serve as an indictment of the narrow brand of Palestinian nationalism which seems either unwilling or incapable of re-examining the past with all its errors, pitfalls, and misconceptions. Call this kind of struggle unrealistic and the goal idealistic or utopian, but it certainly has more prospects for success than the present open-ended formula, whose explanation continues to exact higher energies than its application, and whose future is doomed by a grotesque disparity in power, divisions within the respective camps, and a declared reluctance by the self-styled peace-maker to devote any more energies to an issue that is no longer strategic or vital for the U.S. national interest.

Arafat's Dilemna

Negotiations between Israel and the PA are like encounters between the elephant and the fly. And now after the Clinton Administration has been put on notice by Ehud Barak and Israel's congressional lobby to reduce the U.S. role in the negotiations (as if Washington had been the PA advocate!), the weaker party has no place to go except to its own wailing wall in Cairo. Having traded its people's legitimacy for U.S. and Israeli legitimacy; the PLO has placed itself in a no-win situation. The ongoing stalemate will also continue to be fueled by diviajons inside Israel, which now center not on whether Oslo Would end the occupation and restore a measure of normalcy to Israelis and Palestinians, hut on the most efficient and least disruptive approach to preserving the status-quo under a more benign label. The method of repackaging the occupation is what really divided Rabin and later Barak from Netanyahu. This fact has not been lost on a sizable sector of the Palestinian community; inside and outside Palestine. While some comprehend it welt others feel it instinctively, irrespective of Arafat's constant expressions of nostalgia for and repeated devotion to Rabin's legacy.

Arafat has placed himself in the untenable position of being unable to deliver to either Israel and its U.S. patron, on the one hand, or to his own constituents, who were ready to scale down their aspirations but not to surrender their rights, not to legitimize what the South Africans and the whole world have renounced -the bantustans and apartheid. His denunciations of terror and vows to eradicate violence, under repeated urgings by the United States and Israel, are seen in the Palestinian street as an ominous attack on civil liberties and the right of dissent. Moreover; his assumption of responsibility for Israel's security is becoming increasingly incontrovertible when that security keeps on assuming an accelerated dimension that negate Palestinian rights -water security; settlements security, and demographic security (which negates the rights of refugees).

All of these factors confirm, prolong, and expand the stalemate. Oslo was not designed as a normal traditional agreement. It has now become a guarantor of disagreement and the intended legitimizer of the status quo. The Palestinians have no other choice but to struggle for equal rights and equal dignity. Their society has been deformed by Oslo, and their future development has been blemished by it. Not only had Oslo foreclosed their option of a separate and sovereign existence; it has also denied them the right to struggle for that existence, inasmuch as most variants of the struggle are bound to be classified as either terrorism, lack of reciprocity; failure to abide by commitments, or acting against peace.

Oppositional politics in the West Bank and Gaza will be considered as an issue of security and law and order, despite the privileged position it occupies in Israeli and American political life. Thus Oslo, whether managed by Labor or Likud, will remain part of Israel's negotiating strategy calculated to put the onus on Arafat to prove his ability as an effective gendarme for Israel, while the latter is released from the pressure of finding a solution to its continuing occupation. Should the wave of suicide bombings resurface, Arafat will discover that his obligation to Israeli security will continue to dominate the diplomatic agenda and to overshadow the national rights of the Palestinian people.

It is for this reason that the Israeli-supplied framework of Oslo and its various corollaries, which irt themselves are part of Israel's negotiating strategy; have placed Palestinian leaders in a “no win” situation. Arafat's deal with Israelis predicated on an impossible equation. What Israel wants, Arafat cannot deliver without becoming Israel's puppet and quisling.

The process that began in Oslo will reach no where because its path relates to the nature of the Israeli state, which precludes genuine co-existence with the Palestinian people on an equal basis. As long as the Zionist ideology of acquiring the land without the people prevails, a negotiated settlement based on the right of the two people to dignity and self-determination will continue to be elusive. Benjamin Netanyahu did not repudiate Rabin's strategy; he only rejected his tactics. It should be recalled that when Rabin diverted the negotiating venue from Washington to Oslo in 1993, he was making an important shift away from the hardly concealed stalling tactics of his Likud predecessor, Shamir, while creating his own gridlock that had the appearance of diplomatic progress. In a subtle contrast to Shamir, Rabin opted for an agreement with a built-in conflict over meaning and objectives. It is because of that gridlock and the built-in conflict, that the Oslo process was born in a stalemate and continues to be stalled.

Segmenting the Palestine question by issues, population categories, regions, towns and villages, and stages of negotiations has thus far constituted one of the biggest obstacles to peace. Had the issue of land and settlement not been deferred, the question of settlement security would not have become a barrier for redeployment. Had the issue of Hebron not been singled out and also deferred, the question of "further" redeployment would not have arisen. Had the issues of Jerusalem and sovereignty not been put off, the violence associated with the closure, the tunnel, Ras al Amoud, Abu Ghneim (Har Homa) would not have replaced them as root causes on the agenda of the "honest broker." Had the deferral pattern not been set, matters relating to self-governance, further redeployment, easing the closure, releasing tax funds, and even resuming negotiations would not have been treated as probationary. Could such a self-defeating process be meant for real implementation?

A Reconsideration of Zionist Ideology?

Any forward movement beyond the present no peace - no war situation would require a debate on Zionist ideology and history, in which the difficult questions, suppressed since the establishment of Israel, would surface. At the heart of the debate would be the main Zionist narrative, its negative portrayal of Arabs and its distortion of history and the requirements of peace. Already, we are told, a post-Zionist debate is taking place inside Israel. The question is how extensively has it been followed by the general public. Political scientist Ilan Pappe' has written a series of studies on this post-Zionist critique and its manifestations in the various Israeli cultural products, including films, plays, music, novels; and short stories, as well as in the scholarly discourse. Pappe's writings reveal how intertwined the lives of Israelis and Palestinians have become. There is an implication in his work that Israel cannot prosper as an isolated Western outpost in the region. A democratic pluralist Israel as a part of the Mediterranean is also an Israel with many historical narratives. Such an Israel has a chance at a common future.

The question of whether Zionism is a movement of national plundering or a movement of a persecuted people acting according to a human ethic, seeking compromise and peace, is being increasingly raised by Israeli intellectuals. The historian Benny Morris framed the question in terms of the accuracy of the "Zionist ethos claims that we came to this land not to exploit the natives and expel them, and not to occupy them by force."

Only when this kind of critique is broadened to include the mainstream and penetrate the consciousness of the average Israeli, will the so-called peace process begin to assume real, peaceful dimensions. Only when the Palestinians decide to rediscover their democratic secular state framework and begin to adapt it to the present realities, will hopes for real peace be rekindled. Call it a binational solution, a federal system, a cantonal system on the Swiss model, the common denominators are still equal rights, equal citizenship, plurality, co-existence, and common humanity.

Israel’s media and the right of return

Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Last month’s visit to Moscow by Eytan Bentsur, Director-General of the Israeli Foreign Ministry, was described as a rare diplomatic undertaking. Its purpose was to hand over two volumes, namely “Documents on Israeli–Soviet relations from 1941-1953”, to the Russian Foreign Minister. Whilst acknowledging the critical support given to the Zionist project by the Soviets, the 498 documents, soon to be published, pours extra fuel on the burning issues of Palestinian expulsion and return. The following is an assessment of the issue as it is currently played out in the Israeli media.

To the same degree that its politicians have been obsessed with questions of military security throughout the past 50 years, so too Israel’s intellectuals were always troubled by the existence of a Palestinian diaspora. The presence of millions of refugees on its borders awaiting repatriation has, for all intents and purposes, been a source of permanent discomfort and shame for the Jewish State. After the declassification of government documents concerning the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, a number of Israeli historians and sociologists began to publish in the late 1980s a series of works aimed at putting across a “New Historiography” of the Arab-Israeli conflict and expulsion of the Palestinians. But, like a festering sore that grows ever larger in the absence of effective treatment, the twin issues of expulsion and repatriation have now spilled over into Israel’s mainstream media. Gideon Levy broached the subject in a feature article published by Ha’aretz on 26 March 2000.

Under the caption, “Ask forgiveness? Who, us?”, Levy wrote; “We may have been the righteous victims in 1948, but on the road to realizing our claim to justice, we perpetrated a terrible wrong on another people who had absolutely no connection with our calamity. That wrong continues to bleed in the refugee camps, in the occupied territories and in the Palestinian diaspora, and it will continue to haunt us and prevent the achievement of a genuine settlement.”

Notwithstanding his purported horror, Levy failed to develop his argument to its logical conclusion. Quite like the new historians whose spirit he appeared to invoke, the author seemed prepared only to weep for the people who were expelled or forced to flee from their villages. He rightly admits they deserve recognition of the wrong done to them and requests forgiveness. Yet, as is always the case, there was no explicit demand for the Palestinian right to return. This, we may conclude, is still an area of taboo, which even ‘reformist and progressive’ Israelis are yet to transcend.

Before the echoes of Levy’s call were silenced, another writer, A.B. Yehoshua, offered a strikingly candid and unapologetic rendition of the official line (Ha’aretz 2 April 2000). In his piece entitled, “Ending the Palestinians’ circle of misery”, he argues that “there is no returning home for the displaced persons and refugees of 1948, and there cannot be such a return. There can only be a return to the homeland…” The beginning of their tragedy, Yehoshua suggests, must be traced to the refugees who fled to Egypt, Syria, Lebanon and Jordan. They could have returned and built new homes on their homeland. But instead of returning, they chose to lead a life of humiliation and poverty; “a welfare existence without rights.”

In the end, the Israeli novelist concluded the problem with the Palestinians is that they confuse the concept of home and homeland. Their misunderstanding must, therefore, be rectified in order to solve the refugee problem. They would be allowed to return only to the Palestinian homeland whose borders must soon be defined and recognized. As for the return to 1948 Palestine, his message is clear-cut. They can only be brought back to their homes if they brought back to life the 6,000 Israeli dead who were killed in 1948 when Israel was “seeking peaceful coexistence.”

From London to Oslo

A critical reading of Yehoshua’s narrative leaves a lingering concern. Where does the history end, and where does the fiction begin? The newly declassified documents on Israeli-Soviet relations offer the answer. Though by no means a novelty, these documents belie every claim that the Zionists ever wanted “peaceful coexistence”. They reveal that when Haim Weizmann met with Ivan Mikhailovich, the Russian Ambassador to London in early 1941, his request was clear, “Give me the land occupied by a million Arabs and I will easily settle five times that number of Jews on it.”

Having said that, one may naturally ask, if the Zionists really wanted ‘coexistence’ that badly, why did they reject it in 1939? Or, more specifically, why did they refuse the proposals of the London Conference later embodied in the MacDonald White Paper of May 1939? After all, His Majesty’s Government had then declared its intention to establish an independent Palestinian State where Arabs and Jews would share government in such a way to secure their essential interests. As it appears, the Zionists rejected the Policy Statement because it stated “unequivocally” that it was not part of British policy that Palestine should become a Jewish State. Furthermore, this was not attainable democratically because in 1939 the Jews constituted only 30% of Palestine’s population.

Thus, the claim of “seeking peaceful coexistence” in 1947 had only come about after the Zionists had obtained guarantees from Moscow and, more importantly, Washington. Under the circumstances, it was the Americans who pressured the British government to shelve the White Paper and declare Palestine a Jewish State–after Whitehall had previously announced that it would implement its plans, regardless of the opposition.

This was neither the first nor the last time that Palestinian/Arab national aspirations were sacrificed in order to safeguard Zionists interests. Their proclamation of independence from Ottoman rule in 1916 failed to secure British recognition as promised. Quite unexpectedly, it was met with the Balfour Declaration that altered the course of Palestinian history and changed the political and demographic landscape of the entire region.

If the Zionist leadership had gained notoriety for its misrepresentation of history, similarly the Arab political elite has become renowned for its unwillingness to learn from the past. Acting on a promise of independence for Palestine in 1939, they called off the revolt that was started three years earlier and had imposed de facto control over most of Palestine. It was this rising power of Palestinian nationalism that prompted the Zionists to turn to Moscow and Washington for help in order to speed up the process. When Britain became fully on board in the mid-1940s, the stage was finally set for the United Nations to rubber stamp the birth of the Jewish State.

As the abortion of the 1936-1939 Intifadah led to the loss of 80% of Palestine in 1947-1948, likewise the suspension of the 1987-1993 Intifadah has left Israel with more than 60% of the occupied West Bank and Gaza Strip. There are, of course, two significant differences. The United Nations “authorized” the establishment of the Jewish State in 1948 (as Mr. Yehoshua carefully points out in his article), yet no such endorsement was obtained for the accords that were concluded since 1993. And, if the issues of Statehood and occupation were the predominant issues of 1947-1948, the question of repatriation has today emerged to impose itself on the agenda of politicians and media personnel alike.

Writings on the wall

As a rule, diplomats often begin their negotiations with maximal demands, however outrageous and callous they may seem. When the horse-trading is over, they invariably settle for lesser gains. Israeli opposition to Palestinian return is a good example. Behind the façade of hard bargaining, they know perfectly well that in the end they would have to recognize the Palestinian right of return. The truth about the refugees is no longer an issue that can be misrepresented or hidden from public scrutiny and accountability. Hence the conscience of humanity refuses to accept that victims should oppress others to get their rights, especially when the latter had nothing to do with their suffering.

No other person was better placed to remind Zionist Israel of the impending deluge than Nelson Mandela. Speaking to the Jewish Board of Deputies in London in early April, Robin Island’s most famous prisoner and South Africa’s former president told his audience that power and force will not be enough to secure peace in the Middle East. Where is Caesar, Alexander and Hitler?, he asked his hosts. Any schoolboy could have decoded this message.

Though suppressed by guns and jackboots for the last century, Palestinian stamina to regain their rights has never waned. Nowhere is this more pronounced today than in the refugee camps. The evidence was brilliantly illustrated in the graffiti written on the walls of Dheisheh camp during the Pope’s visit. “Sorry Pope!!!” “We will not call any of the alleys of our camp after your name. These camps can’t last forever!!” Those who argue that the West Bank “homeland” is a substitute for stolen homes in historic Palestine must visit the camps and see for themselves the writings on the wall.

Our reading of the Israeli media notably afforded an insight into the attitudes of the Israeli public on the question of Palestinian return. The results of a survey conducted by Prof. Ephraim Yaar of Tel Aviv University, and published by the Jerusalem Post (4 April 2000) underlined this. While 64% of Israeli Jews support the Law of Return which enables Jews from any part of the world to immigrate to Israel and become a citizen, 87% oppose the Palestinian right of return. This high number (87%), the professor explained, “shows that even among the secular and left-wing, there is a desire to preserve the Jewish character of the State.”

Such are the values that determine Israeli policies and shape its social structures. They are contrary to the spirit of our age and should be dismantled. This was the theme of a speech recently given by Britain’s Prime Minister, Tony Blair, to newspaper editors in London. “The breakup of the post-war international order and globalization are calling into question systems constructed around the nation State.” Since it has “become fashionable to predict the death of the nation State”, as Blair observed, we would surely be justified to expect the death of the ethnic State also. To delay this inevitability, Israel continues to deny the Palestinian right of return.

MIAMI (Florida) - Eleven-year-old Yara Al-Najjar has not hugged her father for more than a year. She has seen him only through Plexiglas at a jail. So when he entered the federal courtroom before a hearing last week, Yara went to see him.

She cannot understand why they were treating her so cruelly. “Why do they care if I cry?”

Her father, Mazen Al-Najjar, has been jailed for almost three years without charges on secret government evidence that he is a dangerous associate of terrorists. He made his first appearance in federal court last week to ask a judge to free him.

Yara, her two sisters, her mother and about 30 members of their Tampa mosque and the citizens’ group Hillsborough Organization for Progress and Equality, or HOPE, travelled by bus from Tampa to show their support.

They were treated, they said, like suspects.

When the group of men, women and children walked onto the downtown courthouse plaza with signs reading “Free Mazen Now”, a half-dozen U.S. Customs officers ran from the building to confront them.

When they complied with directions to move their protest across the street, a man they believed to be an FBI agent stood six feet away and snapped photographs, they said.

When they passed through the metal detector at the entrance to the courtroom, U.S. Marshals required photo IDs of all but the children and wrote down names and birth dates. One woman said they noted her height.

“They are trying to intimidate us,” said Sami Al-Arian, a University of South Florida professor and Al-Najjar’s brother-in-law.

Al-Najjar, a 42-year-old Palestinian refugee, has been ordered to be deported for overstaying a student visa. His case is on appeal. He was denied bail by an immigration judge who heard government testimony in secret and found Al-Najjar to be a threat to national security.

Last week’s hearing before U.S. District Judge Joan A. Lenard requested his immediate release on constitutional grounds: His right to due process was violated, his attorneys said. Lenard will issue her written decision after further review.

Al-Najjar says he is not a terrorist. He was targeted by the U.S. government, he says, because he worked at a USF-affiliated think-tank with Ramadan Abdullah Shallah, who left Tampa in 1995 and a few months later became head of the Palestinian Islamic Jihad.

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 8

The Rise of the Haredim in Israel

In order to proselytize and to spread their superstitions, Haredim often exploit the distress of people. Relatives of terminally ill hospital patients, especially if they are traditional, are often approached by messengers of a charismatic rabbi, who first reiterate that the doctors cannot help and then suggest that the relatives buy some sacred water, consecrated by a certain rabbi, and smear the patient with it. The messengers relate stories about miracles that occur after the use of this sacred water, which is never distributed without a non-returnable payment. The messengers, of course, never mention the failure of sacred water miracles. The secular Hebrew press at times will report on the failure of these miracles, especially when a large amount of money is known to have been spent for the sacred water. Such reporting, however, most often only deepens the chasm between those who read and those who do not read but loathe the secular Hebrew press. In their own press the Haredim not only attack the secular press but also display their general hostility towards secular Israeli Jews. Until the later part of the 1980s, most of the Israeli Jewish public paid little attention to the Haredi press Since then, general public attention has increased considerably. Dov Albaum, one of Israel's foremost experts on Haredi affairs, focused upon this point in two Hebrew-language articles, one published in the August 30, 1996 issue of the newspaper, Yediot Ahronot, the other published in the July-August issue of the bi-monthly periodical, Ha 'ain Hashvi'it (The Seventh Eye), which is published by the Israeli Democracy Institute and is devoted to analyzing the Israeli press. Albaum discussed the structure of the Haredi press in Yediot Ahronot and then proceeded to a discussion in Ha'ain Hashvi'it of the Haredi attitude as a whole towards secular Israeli Jews. According to Albaum, the violent attacks in the Haredi press upon Aharon Barak, the president of the Israeli Supreme Court, attracted increased public attention. The Haredi press called Barak "the most dangerous enemy ever to face the Haredi public." Albaum pointed out that the earlier Haredi press attacks upon the left wing kibbutzim, the Israeli army, the secular media and many other secular institutions and figures aroused little general interest. The attack upon the Supreme Court, long regarded as the holiest symbol of Israeli secular democracy, piqued the interest of many secular Jews. The violent Haredi press attacks upon Yitzhak Rabin, while he was prime minister, did not have the same effect. Shortly before Rabin's assassination an article in one of the most popular Haredi weekly publications, Ha 'Shavua (The Week) predicted:

The day will come when the Jews will bring Rabin and Peres to the defendant's bench in court with the only two alternatives being the noose or the insane asylum. This insane and evil pair have either gone mad or are obvious traitors Rabin and Peres have guaranteed their place in the Jewish memory as evil Jews of the worst kind. They resemble the apostates or the Jews who served the Nazis.

Reiterating that secular Jewish interest in Israel heightened after the attack upon Barak and the Supreme Court, Albaum observed that increasing numbers of secular Israelis are insulted when they read in the Haredi press that their lives are garbage and their children are hallucinating, lifeless drug addicts. Albaum explained:

Haredi journalists deliberately exaggerate all marginal phenomena in secular society. They describe all murders, cases of alcoholism and hard drug situations as characteristics of secular Jewish society. In addition, they allege as facts incorrect statements, engage in the wildest forms of slander and often use the most derogatory terminology. Their aim is to condemn absolutely the secular, Jewish lifestyle.

It is difficult to avoid considering such depiction as analogous to the Nazi methodology.

The structure of the Haredi press is significant. Albaum pinpointed as the main Haredi ideological trendsetter Yated Ne'eman (Faithful Tent-Peg), the official newspaper of the Degel Ha’Torah faction, headed and controlled by Rabbi Shach. Albaum explained that Yated Ne'eman is strictly monitored by a committee of five rabbis, all appointed by Rabbi Shach and headed by Rabbi Natan Zohavsky. At least one of the committee's rabbis is in the newspaper's office each evening except the Shabbat. Every word of every article, advertisement and announcement must be approved for publication by the rabbi(s) on duty. Certain words and expressions, such as aids or television, are not allowed to be printed. The term "Red Cross," supposedly associated with Christianity, is especially prohibited from usage.

Yated Ne'eman articles often ferociously attack rival Haredi factions. One example is that all advertisements about social events of the Shas Party, which is despised by Rabbi Shach, are not allowed to be printed. The importance of this prohibition was highlighted when, after an apparent lull in the spiritual war between Rabbi Shach and Shas, one of the newspaper's editors dared to publish an advertisement announcing the bar-mitzvah of Aryeh Der'i's son. (Aryeh Der'i is a Member of the Knesset and an important Shas leader.) Upon learning of this, Rabbi Shach strongly reprimanded Rabbi Zochovsky, the head of the overseeing committee of rabbis.

Spiritual censorship committees exist and monitor everything printed in other Haredi newspapers. Albaum asserted: "Freedom of the press is an unknown concept in the Haredi press." Haredi editors, according to Albaum, proclaim a different kind of freedom: "the right of our public not to know certain things.” The censoring rabbis decide what the public should not know.

In reflecting the general Haredi attitude towards secular Jews, Haredi press articles often present arguments reminiscent of anti-Semitic statements about all Jews. Albaum pointed to a February 1996 article, for example, in which Israel Friedman reiterated the position that the land of Israel belongs only to the Haredim and that secular Jews and Palestinians should leave it. In addressing secular Jews, Friedman in his article stated: "Go away from here…We tell you this in a friendly manner. Go away. American crime will easily absorb the criminal secular youth who are all enchanted by alcohol, drugs and earrings. They are bloodsuckers who drink our blood. They dare to live on land that belongs to us." In another article Albaum quoted Nathan Ze'ev Grossman, the editor of Yated Ne'eman, as attributing the rise of neo-Nazism in European countries "to the influence of the Rabin government." Grossman described all kibbutzim as Nazi institutions and proposed "to put them on trial according to the precedent of the Nuremberg trials."

The Haredim demand that other Jews should, at least in public and especially in regard to matters of symbolism, behave according to their dictates. Haredi demands, often supported by traditionalist Jews, so frequently cause political scandals that they can be described as a staple of Israeli politics. More Israeli government crises have occurred because of religious scandals than for any other reasons. To further their political interests, the Haredim insist upon employing certain symbols. This insistence has played an important role in Israeli politics. Many Israeli Jews, together with a much greater number of diaspora Jews, in deference to what they believe is Jewish tradition and the commandments of Judaism, support Haredi demands to keep and display symbols of religious observance. Such support has produced scandal. One particularly illustrative scandal occurred in Autumn 1992 and occupied Israeli politics for many months. During the time of this scandal, the Haredi Shas Party threatened to leave the Rabin government, not because of Rabin's plans to deal with the Palestinians nor because of possible concessions to the Syrians but rather because the then Minister of Education Shulamit Aloni, on a visit to Nazareth was photographed eating in a non-kosher, Arab restaurant and thus violating the religious symbol of the ritual purity of food. Only six months prior to the Aloni affair another scandal involving a Member of the Knesset had occurred; MK Yael Rayan was photographed on a Tel Aviv beach, dressed in a swimsuit and reading a book on Yom Kippur. All the religious political panics then protested furiously against what they termed this "profanation of Judaism." After hearing traditionally religious Labor Party Knesset members echo the same sentiments, Prime Minister Rabin, who was not traditionally religious, reinforced the accusation.

During her tenure as minister of education, Shulamit Aloni made numerous statements that were viewed as being in opposition to symbols in Judaism and thus blasphemous; these statements resulted in scandals. One month before arousing scandal by eating in an Arab restaurant, for example, Aloni publicly acknowledged that the denial of the world's being created in six days was a tenable hypothesis. She also publicly struck the controversial, although hardly earth-shattering, position that the teaching of Judaism in the state's secular schools should be slightly changed. (She was content to leave as it is the teaching of Judaism in the state's religious schools.) Aloni caused even more furore when she publicly slighted some biblical figures. Ranny Talmor, a respected Israeli journalist, rightly observed in her October 14, 1992 article in the newspaper, Hadashot;

[Aloni] scarcely escaped Galileo's fate after he persisted in maintaining that the earth moved around the sun. Some supposedly enlightened, secular Jews whispered to one another:

"Of course she is right, but why does she need to say this in public?" The Jewish Grand Inquisitors were delighted in their realization tat they had scored another victory against the weak-minded infidels.

The Jewish Inquisitors harassed Aloni even more after Rabin forced her to apologize publicly in an open letter to Rabbi Ovadia Yoseph, the spiritual head of the Shas Party. Yoel Markus, a well-known Israeli journalist, reflected widely held opinion when he observed in his October 13, 1992 Haaretz article:

As is well known, each concession in such matters only encourages the demand for more. This is why the abject surrender to Jewish religious demands by members of the Labor and Meretz Parties makes us wonder. Rabin has solemnly undertaken to check closely an intelligence report, submitted to him by the National Religious Party [NRP], describing how Aloni violated the Sabbath and ate non-kosher food in Israel and abroad. The Chairman of the Labor Party faction in the Knesset [Elie Dayan] publicly rebuked Aloni and Member of the Knesset Yael Dayan.

The NRP hired detectives to spy on ministers in order to discover what transgressions of Jewish religious commandments they committed. Such spying continued while the Rabin and Peres governments were in power. Rabin and Peres, while prime ministers, obtained all the findings of the detectives and continually attempted to keep their ministers from transgressing any religious laws in public.

In his Haaretz article, Yoel Markus articulated many fears, shared by a sizeable segment of the Israeli Jewish public:

We can also expect demands that each minister and member of the Knesset be accompanied by a kashrut inspector, who holds a full-time job for this purpose and that similar inspectors be appointed to insure that kashrut is observed in every neighborhood and on every street in Israel. A demand may also be made to establish vice squads, authorized to raid private homes in order to ascertain whether kashrut is being observed and whether, God forbid, a wife does not by chance have sex with her husband in the period of impurity during and after the time of menstruation [lasting eight to fourteen days.]

Other Israeli journalists expressed similar fears and went further than did Markus in their published articles. Some attacked not only the religious but also the secular Jews who remained silent about the attacks upon them and their behavior and who would allow continual efforts by religious surveyors to brainwash systematically. Many Israeli Jews, whose opinions were represented by certain journalists, saw the activities and actual victories by religious factions as advancements towards a full-scale Jewish "Khomeinism" in Israel.
