Hamas: “We won’t attack Israeli civilians
if Israel stops attacking Palestinians”

Occupied Jerusalem - The main Palestinian opposition movement, Hamas, has reaffirmed its principled opposition to the peace process, which won’t enable the Palestinian people to recover even the very minimum of their legitimate rights or realize their national aspirations.

In a series of interviews with Israeli and Arab media last month, Hamas’ founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin reiterated his Movement’s conviction that any arrangement or settlement that might eventually result from the Oslo process would be inherently unfair and therefore unacceptable to the vast majority of Palestinians.

“Israel and the United States might succeed in imposing on the weak Palestinian Authority a deformed arrangement which they could call peace, but soon enough everyone would realize that this sham peace wouldn’t last for a long time since it didn’t meet the basic and most elementary requisites of the Palestinian people,” Yassin told the Qatari television channel, al-Jazira.

Asked why Hamas has refused to join the PA in the paramount final status talks, as the Popular and Democratic Fronts for the Liberation of Palestine ostensibly were about to do, Yassin complained that there was no guarantee that the Palestinian leadership would be bound by any collective Palestinian consensus on the final status issues.

He said Hamas would like to see all Palestinians form a united front in the face of Israeli insolence , adding, however, that this hoped-for front ought to be based on solid ground, apart from the restrictions of the “Oslo process”, which, he said, “bequeathed disunity and disintegration and turned Palestinians one against the other.”

Nevertheless, Yassin strongly lauded the so-called “Palestinian red lines,” repeated several times of late by Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat and other PA officials.

In a recent interview with this correspondent, Yassin said, “I urge our brothers in the Palestinian Authority to stick to this stance.”

“This is a great thing,” he said, with a broad smile, adding, however, that “our experience with Palestinian negotiators is not encouraging…we witnessed them adopt a given stance and soon afterwards back down from it under American and Israeli pressure.”

In another interview with the Israeli State-run television on 15 October, Yassin sought to reassert Palestinians’ right to “resist the Israeli occupation.”

“We can’t give up the principle of resistance; this a legitimate right guaranteed by man and God…as long as there is occupation, there is resistance; the occupation is the cause and resistance is the effect”, said Yassin in his usual extemporaneous style.

He refuted Israeli insinuations that Hamas was anti-Semitic and that the Movement’s campaign against Israel was part of its “anti-Jewish ideology.”

“This is simply untrue…even if my own brother attacked me and usurped my home and land, I’ll fight him; our struggle against Jews is not because they are Jews but because they happen to be our oppressors and tormentors.”

Yassin went so far as to propose an open-ended moratorium on attacking Israeli and Palestinian civilians by both sides.

“The Ezzul Deen al-Qassam fighters are aware that Islam is against targeting civilians who are non-combatants.”

He suggested that attacks by Hamas’ military wing on Israeli civilians were mostly a reaction to bloody Israeli attacks on Palestinian civilians, including the murder of 29 worshipers by an Israeli settler in February of 1994.

Yassin even reiterated an earlier proposal by the Movement’s military wing, proposing a freeze of armed attacks against Israelis, including settlers, in return for a freeze on all settlement activity.

Yassin also denied rumours suggesting that Hamas was seeking to recruit Israeli Arabs, saying the Movement understood the “special situation of our brothers across the Green Line.”

Yassin’s statements came after the right-wing Israeli daily, Ma’ariv, quoted Yassin as saying that “an Israeli bus will be blown up tomorrow morning.”

“This is an outright distortion and falsification,” he said, adding, rather tersely, “I didn’t say what the paper said I said.”

Yassin’s statements that Hamas won’t target Israeli civilians are by no means new.

Even before his release from an Israeli jail in September of 1997, as part of a deal between Jordan and Israel following the Mossad’s botched assassination attempt on Hamas politburo chief Khalid Misha’al, Yassin proposed several times a protracted truce between Israel and Palestinians in return for full Israeli withdrawal from the West Bank and Gaza Strip, including East Jerusalem.

Predictably, all successive Israeli governments rejected the proposal outright, since Israel could make a much better bargain, as evident from the Oslo process.

Likewise, Yassin’s proposal that both sides refrain from attacking civilians is certain to be utterly ignored by the Barak government, which is interested in weakening Hamas and corroding its standing among Palestinians.

Indeed, Israel, and presumably the PA as well, is in no mood to initiate serious contacts with Hamas on the latter’s terms. Hamas, after all, is being hounded, even de-legitimized, not only in Israel and the Palestinian self-rule areas, but in a country like Jordan, where the Movement had enjoyed a certain sojourn for several years. And talking to Hamas now could lead to weakening the PA and its leader Yasser Arafat and result in redefining the peace process in ways unaccepted by Israel.

Indeed, Israel’s sole approach toward Hamas is to continue to narrow its horizons, in the hope that the Movement will be pushed toward some kind of demise. A weak Hamas, after all, is an important pre-requisite for a successful peace process, and vice versa.

However, it doesn’t seem likely that narrowing Hamas’ horizons will succeed in forcing the Movement into political demise in the foreseeable future. There are two reason for that. First, the Israeli occupation of the Palestinian homeland is Hamas’ ultimate raison d’être, and this variable is not about to radically change, let alone disappear. Second, the peace process, as it looks today, is not likely to drastically reduce, even mitigate, Palestinian grievances and bitterness, upon which Hamas thrives. Indeed, the opposite might be proven true.

Jordanian Monarch says there is formula
to end impasse with Hamas

Amman- King Abdullah II of Jordan said on 22 October he was studying the most appropriate means to resolve the standoff between his government and the Amman-based leaders of the Palestinian Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas..

The official Jordanian news agency, Petra, quoted the King as saying that he was confident Jordan and Hamas would be able to resolve outstanding differences.

The King, who was speaking on returning from an extended European tour, that “with a disciplined and objective dialogue, we will be able to find the means to end this problem.”

He went on saying that the Jordanian government was studying the best possible formula to settle the outstanding problems with Hamas.

He added that any settlement would have to be in the context of rule of law, and maintaining Jordan’s sovereignty and security.”

Hamas leaders have welcomed King Abdullah’s statements.

Jamil Abu Baker, the spokesperson of the Muslim Brothers in Jordan, quoted Hamas leaders based in Damascus as saying that “Hamas doesn’t mind exploring a new formula governing their presence on Jordanian soil.”

King Abdullah II had repeated similar statements to the Jordanian Television during his visit to Washington last month. He was quoted as saying that there was “ a certain formula” to end the current impasse between his government and the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas.

He added that “Jordan was a country of one family; and we shall resolve this matter, and I believe there is a certain formula in this regard and when I return home I shall receive a report from the government on what is going on and what should be done.”

Following these statements by the Jordanian Monarch, the chief of Hamas politburo, Khalid Masha’al, and the movement’s official spokesman Ibrahim Ghoshe, reportedly suspended the hunger strike they had started for five days to protest their continued detention at a Jordanian lockup near Amman.

Masha’al reportedly said the hunger strike was “a political expression” direct at the government, not the prison authorities.

Musa Abu Marzuk, the former chief of Hamas politburo, insisted that Hamas would not accept any solution of the current impasse with Jordan that didn’t include the release from jail of Masha’al and Ghoshe.

He described rumors that Hamas was seeking to find a replacement for Khalid Masha’al as politburo chief of Hamas as “nonsense.”

Meanwhile, thousands of Jordanians and Palestinians marched through the streets of Wehdat refugee camp in Amman on 15 October, to protest recent Jordanian measures against the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas).

Taking part in the demonstration, which took place immediately after the congregational Friday prayers, were Jordanian citizens and Palestinian refugees, signaling that support for Hamas in Jordan is not confined to Jordanians of Palestinian origin.

At the forefront of the demonstration were the leaders of the Islamic Action Front and the Muslim Brothers, the largest and most popular Islamist political organization in the Arab world.

Hamza Mansour, of the Islamic Action Front, who participated in the demonstration, said Islamists in Jordan were increasingly impatient at the Jordanian government’s failure to free detained Hamas leaders.

He accused the Jordanian authorities of carrying out a “politically-motivated crack-down” on Hamas in order to meet Israeli and American pressure.

Hamas, he said, had every right to resist the Zionist occupation of Muslim land.

This right, he said, was guaranteed by God and man alike, and no power or government can confiscate that most sacred right.

Abdul Majid Dneibat, Head of the Muslim Brothers in Jordan, also lashed out at the (Abdul Ra’auf ) Rawabdeh’s government for “adopting a vindictive approach toward Hamas in the service of Israel.”

The demonstrators shouted “God is Great”, “God is greater than America” and “with soul and blood, we shall defend Hamas.”

Hammam Said, a former MP, lashed out at government’s hostility to Hamas.

“You are incarcerating our brothers at a time when (Israel’s Mossad chief Dany) Yatom freely and securely tours Amman and spies on us.” He said.

Redeployment on paper, annexation on the ground

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khalid Amayreh

In what many Palestinians describe as a move to predetermine the shape of the eventual final status settlement between Israel and the Palestinian Authority, the Israeli government of Ehud Barak has been accelerating settlement expansion and land confiscation throughout the West Bank.

According to PA officials, the stepped-up pace of “settlement activities” is now even faster than under the government of Benyamin Netanyahu.

“Barak is carrying out Netanyahu’s policies as far as settlements are concerned, “ complained PA information minister, Yasser Abed Rubbo, whom Yasser Arafat appointed to head the final status talks with Israel.

He added that “there is no real difference between Netanyahu and Barak” and that “we apparently were wrong in thinking that Barak was the dove of peace we had all expected him to be.”

Sa’eb Ureikat, the erstwhile Palestinian negotiator, even voiced greater despair at the ultimate fate of the peace process.

“This unprecedented onslaught of settlement expansion and land confiscation is eroding the peace process and killing off hope for a meaningful and just settlement of the Palestinian-Israeli conflict,” said Ureikat.

He added, rather angrily, that “Barak must choose between settlements and peace, he can’t have them both, since one is inconsistent with the other.”

Palestinians powerless in face of settlement expansion

Palestinian indignation, fraught with virtual powerlessness to stop the settlement onslaught, was triggered by a recent and ongoing large-scale, some say phenomenal, land seizure by the Israeli army of as many as 10,000 hectares of arable and pasture land in the central and southern parts of the West Bank.

In the Hebron area alone, more than 3,000 hectares of pasture land were “closed off” as a “training field” for the Israeli Defense Forces. The Israeli army commander of the West Bank, General Illan Biran, argued that the seizure was part of the revised Wye River agreement of Sharm el-Sheikh, adding with a whiff of snide cynicism that the “Palestinian Authority is aware of this.”

Moreover, hundreds of hectares were also confiscated in recent weeks in the Ramallah area, particularly along the former armistice line between the West Bank and Israel proper. This suggests that Israel is planing to annex a 3-4 kilometres-wide “security zone” along the former border in the contest of any final arrangement in the West Bank.

The obvious purpose of this spate of confiscation is to enlarge nearby Jewish settlements in the area, and presumably confront the Palestinians and the rest of the world with an irreversible fait accompli.

This imposed fait accompli is probably nowhere more blatant than at the Ma’ali Adumim settlement, 8 miles east of Arab-East Jerusalem, which itself has been undergoing an unabatedly aggressive campaign of Judaization.

In the first week of October, the Israeli government awarded tenders for as many as 2,600 settlement units to be constructed at Ma’ali Adumim and other neighbouring settlements, which are described by Israeli officials as “final-status red lines.”

According to Khalil Tufakji, who heads the Cartography department at the Arab Studies Society in East Jerusalem, the ultimate Israeli goal at Ma’ali Adumim is to achieve a “Jewish demographic continuity” by expanding the settlement westward up to Jerusalem proper.

In other words, the goal is to “consolidate Jerusalem as Israel’s capital” and to make any “possible re-division of the town impossible if thinkable.”

Israeli Housing minister, Yitzhak Levy, of the pro-settler National Religious Party (Mifdal), defended the intended expansion at Ma’ali Adumim, saying most of the planned units will be located in settlements around Jerusalem which he said fell under “national consensus,” meaning they would remain under Israeli control in a final status settlement.

“We joined this government on the basis of its basic guidelines, and the basic guidelines don’t mention any freezing of construction.”

H added, rather rightly, that “neither the Sharm el-Sheikh nor Wye agreements stipulated a freeze of settlement activities.”

Barak himself seems to support this view, though quietly. His oft-repeated remarks that the peace process should progress “despite contentious issues with the Palestinians” can be construed, as many Palestinian officials do, as an allusion to settlements.

The two agreements (at Sharm el-Sheikh and Wye Plantation) call on the two sides to refrain from “unilateral acts” that undermine the peace process, a deliberately vague clause which the Barak government says (as said Netanyahu’s) doesn’t apply to settlements.

Indeed, the very decision by Barak to appoint Levy, an unhesitant advocate of Eretz Yisrael (the Greater Biblical Land of Israel) and a fierce opponent of the Oslo process, to the helm of the Ministry of Housing (which oversees settlement buildings in the West Bank) suggests that Barak has no intention of reversing the settlement policies of his predecessor.

In a characteristic reaction to this, the Palestinian leadership continued to issue the usual platitudinous remarks about the “illegality of the settlements” and the fact that their continued expansion “endangered the peace process” and “corroded confidence and trust between the two sides.”

In a bit more, Palestinian Leader Yasser Arafat sent letters to the American administration, the King of Jordan, and Egyptian President Husni Mubarak, urging them to restrain the Israeli government.

However, as in the past, his protest letters had virtually no effect on Israeli bulldozers in the West Bank.

On 11 October, the Israeli ministerial committee on settlements authorized Barak to deal with scores of new settlement sites established after the signing of the Wye agreement a year ago, when the then-foreign minister, Ariel Sharon, flagrantly urged the settlers to hasten to take control of hilltops in the West Bank “because whoever gets there first will get them.”

On 13 October, Barak met with the leaders of the settlers and reached a “gentleman’s agreement” with them whereby 27 of the 42 settlement sites created after Wye would be considered “legal”.

However, 24 hours later, Barak backed down from his earlier decision and decided that only 12 settlements would be “voluntarily” removed.

Settler leaders hailed the decision as a great victory since it left the vast bulk of the settlements intact and gave them “added legitimacy.”

Israeli Housing Minister, who is also head of the pro-settlers Mifdal party or (NRP), said the settlers succeeded in extracting from Barak recognition of 34 settlement sites.

“That is quite a victory,” he said.

Levy pointed out that the settlement sites slated to be dismantled, which are not inhabited by settlers, would actually be transferred to other sites, presumably new hilltops slated to be grabbed from Palestinians.

Predictably, this classification of settlements into “legal or illegal” is considered inherently unacceptable, even offensive, by the Palestinians.

“All settlements are illegal; this is an occupied territory, and Barak must decide if he wants to make peace with the Palestinians or with the settlers; he can’t have the loaf and eat at the same time,” said Sa’eb Ureikat in an outburst of anger on 11 October.

Ureikat’s comments, however, shouldn’t be taken seriously, since it is rumoured that Arafat, the sole decision maker of the PA, has quietly consented to Barak’s settlement policy.

When asked in Tokyo, where he was attending the Donor Countries’ annual conference, if he felt there was a crisis with Israel over the settlements, Arafat said “there is no crisis; every thing is going well.”

The Hebron fire disaster: the bigger picture

Hebron - Hebron, a city accustomed to Israeli-inflicted misfortunes for many years, was hit recently by a self-inflicted tragedy when an unchecked gas leak at a small cigarette lighter factory on the town’s northern outskirts suddenly turned into an inferno, incinerating 15 young female workers beyond recognition.

The fatal incident, which took place on the ground floor of a residential multi-storey building, apparently started when one of the lighters being transferred to the packaging section inadvertently fell down, sparking the huge blaze.

The fire raged for nearly two hours before the poorly equipped Hebron fire fighters, joined by their counterparts from nearby Kiryat Arba’a, managed to get it under control.

Inside were the charred bodies of the 15 young women huddled together in one corner, having been overwhelmed by the huge fire and unable to escape.

According to survivors, the 15 women couldn’t escape because the stall, where the factory operated clandestinely, had no emergency exits. Still more, the outer gate of the premises was padlocked, which also prevented help from outside to get through quickly.

Added to that was the fact that local fire fighters arrived at the scene belatedly, more than 30 minutes after the fire broke out, and even then, they couldn’t put out the fire before it was too late. Indeed, Palestinian sources revealed that one of the fire-fighting vehicles arrived at the scene with no water and that the hoses of the other vehicle had holes in them.

Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat, who was lambasted for not placing “the appropriate person in the appropriate job,” visited Hebron on 23 October to offer condolences to the victims’ families.

Arafat, who earlier hastily ordered the formation of an inquiry committee to look into the tragedy, granted each victim’s family the sum of US$ 3,000 and promised to punish those responsible for “this crime.”

However, very few Palestinians, indeed very few people in Hebron, even within Arafat’s Fatah movement, gave much credence to his words.

This was manifested on 23 October, when hundreds of angry youths from Hebron and the surrounding villages took to the streets shouting, “Our people’s lives are not disposable”, and demanding that “Corrupt officials be sacked immediately.”

The demonstrators, who included supporters of Fatah as well as ordinary citizens, hurled stones at the Municipal Council building and smashed the windscreen of the mayor’s car, triggering clashes with PA police and Mayor Mustafa Natshe’s bodyguards during which six people were injured, including a journalist.

The protests were resumed the following day, with high school and college students from Hebron’s two main universities (the Hebron University and the Engineering College) marched through the town’s main thoroughfare, shouting slogans against “corruption, unaccountability, and the absence of the rule of law.”

The protesters also specifically demanded the resignation of Natshe, a long-time Arafat supporter, and the organization of new municipal elections to enable the people to choose their public servants. (The PA has been refusing to hold local elections apparently out of concern for a possible victory by the Islamist opposition.)

Earlier, Natshe sought to defend himself against a spate of vitriolic charges of criminal negligence, arguing that the Municipal Council didn’t know about the factory and that its proprietor, Walid Asali, had been granted a license to use the stall for cool-storing vegetables and fruits. Moreover, Natshe accused his accusers of “using the tragedy for political reasons.”

However, Natshe’s defense didn’t seem very genuine. According to neighbours, several formal complaints about the factory and the fact that it was operating in a residential area had been submitted to the Municipal Council, which obviously failed to move to close down the factory.

One Palestinian Authority official, Civil Defense Chief Abdul Hay Abdul Wahid, lashed out at Natshe, accusing him of tolerating violations of safety standards in order to levy taxes and fees from proprietors of illegal factories and workshops.

And in a telling gesture, Abdul Wahid also lambasted the PA leadership for relegating most of the money to security agencies and leaving very little funds for services dealing with people’s lives, such as his Civil Defense Department.

Lawlessness and chaos permeating PA enclaves

Undoubtedly, the tragedy raised serious questions about safety conditions in PA-run areas, which in turn triggered hitherto suppressed but fundamental questions about the state of lawlessness and chaos permeating throughout the PA enclaves.

Indeed, it is quite clear that the spontaneous angry reactions at the street level following the blaze reflected a deeper and wider sense of frustration and indignation at the many morbid phenomena afflicting the PA regime, such as Yasser Arafat’s political autocracy, the absence of the rule of law, the rampant graft, corruption, nepotism and cronyism, as well as a widespread feeling of collective powerlessness among many people.

One newspaper commentator, Ali Jarbawi, reflected the sentiment of most Palestinians when he wrote in his regular column in the semi-official daily al-Ayyam on 23 October that the Hebron tragedy only epitomized and encapsulated a general mess covering and overwhelming all aspects of Palestinians public life.

He pointed out the state of lawlessness, chaos and corruption, which he said were mere symptoms of a profound political morbidity plaguing the “Palestinian experiment”.

Jarbawi, a former Professor of Political Science at Beir Zeit University, said it was no secret that many of the lawbreakers in the Palestinian society were members of the security agencies, the very people who are supposed to uphold the rule of law.

Jarbawi asked in embittered and lamenting tones, “Is it not strange that many of our security cadres have actually become car thieves; is it not strange that our security officers routinely use their power to levy commissions, protection money and monthly stipends from the people?”

He further asked, “Why is no body speaking about our collapsing justice system? And why local elections have not been organized, despite the fact that six years passed since the establishment of the Palestinian Authority?”

Arafat’s reign of power

These daring criticisms, which get as close as possible to pointing an accusing finger at the “sacrosanct cow” (Yasser Arafat), are unlikely to convince the increasingly frail PA Chairman to change his ways.

Arafat, now entering his 70th year, never allowed any genuine sharing of power, neither in the PLO nor in the PA. He is most certainly to remain as such for the rest of his life, especially in light of the fact that he is solidly propped up by Israel, the United States and most Arab regimes in the Middle East.

This means that the Palestinian people, especially the democratic-minded forces in the Palestinian society, e.g. those believing in the idea of civil society and the rule of law, should probably start preparing for the post-Arafat era.

Palestinian Journalist Describes Harassment,
Restrictions on Free Movement

New York- The Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ), the New York-based independent press freedom group, is distributing the personal account of Palestinian journalist Taher Shriteh, a veteran Gaza-based reporter for The New York Times, the British Broadcasting Corporation, and the Japanese daily Yomiuri Shimbun, who has been banned by Israeli authorities from traveling to Jerusalem and the West Bank.
Shriteh has been denied Israeli permission to visit Jerusalem and the West Bank since March 1995, effectively barring him from gathering news outside Gaza and from meeting with current and prospective employers. CPJ believes that these measures have been taken in reprisal for Shriteh's years of reporting about the activities of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas)
in Gaza.
Shriteh has never been tried or convicted of any crime, and Israeli authorities have never provided any explanation for the harsh restrictions on his movements.
Last month, Shriteh learned that Israeli authorities had rejected his application for a permit to travel via the safe-passage route between Gaza and the West Bank which officially opened on 25 October. No reason was provided for the decision. He was also forbidden to use the Israeli
security-escorted shuttle bus service that operates between Gaza and the West Bank to transport people barred from entering Israel.
In a letter to Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak on October 21, CPJ's Executive Director Ann K. Cooper urged the Israeli leader to "ensure that current restrictions on Mr. Shriteh's freedom of movement are lifted immediately, so that he may carry out his professional work in Jerusalem and the West Bank without further interference from Israeli authorities."
Shriteh is one of the most accomplished and respected Palestinian journalists in the West Bank and Gaza. For over a decade, his reporting for major international newspapers and news agencies has earned him a reputation for uncompromising professionalism. In March 1993, he received
the prestigious Freedom of the Press award from the U.S.-based National Press Club in recognition of his contributions to press freedom and for his reporting on the Intifada despite "incarceration and harassment."

Muslims in Nazareth accept compromise
on building mosque near church

Occupied Jerusalem - Muslims in Israel’s biggest Arab town, Nazareth, accepted with some reservations a compromise presented by an Israeli ministerial committee on building a mosque on disputed Waqf land in the vicinity of the Church of Annunciation, one of the most important churches in Christendom.

Muslim sources said Suleiman Abu Ahmed, leader of the Islamist Nazareth United List, agreed in principle to the proposed compromise.

Abu Ahmed said in an interview with the Jerusalem-based Arabic daily newspaper, al Quds, that he accepted the compromise but rejected the proposed wall between the mosque and a plaza to be built nearby.

“We don’t want to see dividing walls that remind people that this place was a point of contention between Muslims and Christians.”

The Israeli ministerial committee proposed the building of a 700 square metre mosque while an additional 450 square metres of the Waqf lot would be converted to a plaza for tourists visiting the Church of Annunciation.

Civic Leaders in Nablus call on Arafat
to bridle his security men

Occupied Jerusalem - A hundred local and civic leaders in Nablus, north of the West Bank, urged Palestinian Authority leader Yasser Arafat to bridle his security men who “are implanting chaos and disorder in our town.”

In an open letter to Arafat published today, the petitioners pointed out that PA security officials, who are supposed to maintain law and order, were vying for “power and money” and indulging in Mafia-like activities.

The letter said that “violence and gangster-ism” were proliferating in Nablus and taking many forms such as car theft, car burning, shooting and attempted murder.

The letter said the perpetrators, who come from the PA apparatus and the society at large, were crossing the red lines and misusing their power.

Tension has been rife in Nablus for the past few months, mainly because of rivalry among the chiefs of Yasser Arafat’s numerous security agencies, who, Palestinian sources contend, have nothing in common except loyalty to Arafat.

Palestinian leaders warn Arafat against
compromising the refugees’ rights

Occupied Jerusalem - Palestinian refugee leaders unanimously warned Palestinian Authority leader Yasser Arafat against compromising on the refugees’ inalienable and inherent right to return to their homeland in implementation of United Nations resolution 194.

The warning came during a rally at the Beach refugee camp in solidarity with the refugees’ demands for repatriation.

Awad said any settlement not including the right to return for refugees, even if it bore the signature of Yasser Arafat, would be worthless.

Will Hawatmeh join Arafat's boat?

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak has decided to allow the leader of the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine (DFLP) Nayef Hawatmeh to return the PA self-rule areas. The decision, said an Israeli government
spokesperson, was taken in light of Hawatmeh's support for the peace process and his express inclination to take part in the final status talks between the PA and the Jewish state. Hawatmeh, considered the most pragmatic leader of the receding Palestinian left, made news earlier this year when he shook hands with Israeli President Ezer Weisman during King Hussein's funeral in Amman. And ever since, he has been seeking Yaser Arafat's good offices to convince the Israeli authorities to allow him to
return to the autonomous enclaves.
Predictably, Hawatmeh reacted favorably to the Israeli decision, which he termed "a correction of a historical mistake by Israel." He said he wouldn't pay any political price for his awarded return , arguing that "every expatriate Palestinian has the right to return home, this is not an Israeli favor."
Hawatmeh, whose DFLP enjoys little street popularity among Palestinians in the self-rule areas, has been advocating the "two-state solution" based on United Nations resolutions 242 and 338 and a settlement of the refugee problem based on UN resolution 194, which calls for repatriation or indemnification for those wishing not to return.
Observers in Palestine suggest that the main motive behind the Israeli decision to allow Hawatmeh to return was Israel's perpetual desire to strengthen the increasingly unpopular regime of Yaser Arafat and build as wide-ranging a consensus among Palestinians as possible in the run-up to the final status talks.
Hawatmeh, whose ideas about the shape which the final status settlement
should assume enjoy wide Palestinian support transcending factional politics, is unlikely though to be able to exert meaningful influence on Yaser Arafat, given the latter's authoritarian autocracy. Mandela: Peace requires full Israeli
withdrawal from occupied territories

Occupied Jerusalem- Nelson Mandela, the former President of South Africa urged the Zionist regime to demonstrate a genuine desire for peace with the Palestinian and the rest of Arab countries.

Mandela, who was on a visit to Israel and the autonomous enclaves last month, said in a joint press conference with Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy in West Jerusalem that peace couldn’t be achieved in the region without the realization of Palestinians’ legitimate rights, including the right of self-determination.

He said Israel would have to pull out of the West Bank and the Golan Heights and allow for the establishment of an independent Palestinian state.

Mandela’s remarks were not received well by Levy, who reiterated Israel’s refusal to return to the 1967 borders.

Meanwhile, an Israeli writer accused successive Israeli governments of adopting a policy of hypocrisy toward South Africa.

Yaron London, a leftist Israeli columnist, pointed out that successive Israeli governments developed extensive security relations with the defunct and semi-Nazi apartheid regime in South Africa.

He argued that it was hypocritical for Israel to threaten to sever relations with Austria if the Freedom Party, headed by Yorg Haidar, was allowed to join that country’s coalition government.

“We developed weapon systems and we helped the apartheid regime repress and torment the freedom fighters of the National African Council…it is no surprise then that Nelson Mandela’s heart is leaning toward the Palestinians,” wrote London in the Hebrew daily, Yedeot Ahranot.

London urged Israel to learn from Mandela and from the way he sought to bring about reconciliation to South Africa after he became President.

“The truth and reconciliation committee he created didn’t settle scores with the murders, some of whom didn’t differ much from the murderers of the Gestapo, but was a means to cleanse the national conscience of the grievances perpetrated by the whites against the blacks, and by the blacks against whites, and the by the blacks against the blacks.”

He added, “in contrast, Israel was arguing whether to admit responsibility and apologize for the massacre of Kufer Kasem,” which Israeli soldiers perpetrated against Palestinian civilians in 1956.

London concluded, “Israel has much to learn from Mandela.”
