Press Cuttings

The Washington Post, 18 October 1999

The Government of Prime Minister Ehud Barak in Israel has made clear that some of the 42 illegal hilltop settlements set up on the West Bank in the waning days of the Netanyahu government will be removed. At the same time, the government has approved tenders for up to 2,600 housing units within existing settlements. Palestinians and others who regard unilateral settlement activity as antithetical to the peace process have decried the new building approvals -- not to mention the fact that most of the hilltop settlements will remain -- and wonder whether Mr. Barak's approach differs more in style than in substance from that of his predecessor.

The New York Times, 20 October 1999

Jerusalem- It was a trip intended to heal old wounds, and as Nelson Mandela concluded his first visit to Israel Tuesday he took pains to praise "my friends" -- Prime Minister Ehud Barak and President Ezer Weizman -- for their regional peacemaking efforts and the "warmth" of their reception.

..Yet the visit was marked by continuing undercurrents of distrust between Mandela, a staunch champion of the Palestinian cause, and a country that once helped arm the apartheid regime that Mandela drove out of power.

The Boston Globe, 20 October 1999

Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza Strip figure as one of the most volatile issues dividing Israel and the Palestinians as they embark on the final phase of negotiations aimed at reaching a definitive solution to decades of conflict. The Palestinians, who want to cobble the West Bank and Gaza Strip into an independent state within a year, demand all settlements be closed.

Israel captured the West Bank from Jordan in the 1967 Six-Day War. Israel defends its settlement policies by saying the outlying communities add to the Jewish state's security. And many Jews believe the land was granted to them by God and forms part of the biblical land of Israel.

The Daily Telegraph, 22 October 1999

Supporters of Benjamin Netanyahu, the former Israeli prime minister, said yesterday he was the victim of a political "character assassination" after police accused him of taking official gifts that should have become state property when he lost power.

Mr Netanyahu and his wife, Sara, were taken from their home and spent the day, his 50th birthday, being interrogated by fraud squad officers on why dozens of expensive items belonging to the state were among boxes of his possessions.

A spokesman for the Right-wing Likud Party, which Mr Netanyahu used to lead, said: "The police maliciously invited the press and cameras in time to humiliate and defame a person and to pressure him before an investigation."

Ha'aretz, 25 October 1999

Clinton and Barak are united in their desire to make next year one of comprehensive peace in the region. U.S. National Security Adviser Sandy Berger spoke last week about the year to come until the fifth anniversary of the murder of Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin, which will be marked during the week that Clinton's successor will be elected. Berger announced that America and Clinton have no greater priority than to achieve peace in the region. Indyk, a week earlier, said that the administration "is working with Israel and Syria to prepare the ground not only for the renewal of the talks, but also to bring them to rapid closure. The coming year offers a very real opportunity to end the Israeli-Arab conflict in a comprehensive manner, once and for all."
