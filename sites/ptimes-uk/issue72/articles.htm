Ethnic, Religious Divisions Endanger Israeli Society

By: Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem- As Israel celebrated the 49th annual anniversary of its establishment in Palestine, hundreds of thousands of Haredi ultra-Orthodox Jews marked the "repugnant anniversary" by pelting passing cars with stones, blocking streets with trash cans and, more ominously, smashing tombstones at several military cemeteries in central Israel.

At Jerusalem's Mi'a Sha'arim neighbourhood, a traditional stronghold of the ultra-Orthodox community, scores of black-draped, brim-hatted Haredis clashed with the police along the Bar Illan Street, and shouted anti-Zionism slogans.

Other Haredis smashed tombstones at Holon's military cemetery on the outskirts of Tel Aviv on 11 May, just a few hours after Israeli political and military leaders participated in a solemn commemoration of the Jewish state's fallen soldiers.

According to Israeli sources, the Haredis scrawled anti-state and anti-Zionist slogans in several locations at the military cemetery, upsetting Israeli leaders.

Israeli President Ezer Weisman, sensing the Haredi disruption of "Independence Day" celebrations, spoke of a wide and deep gap between the secular and religious camps.

Referring to the smashing of tombstones in Holon, Weisman said "these shameless and despicable acts of vandalism are very dangerous."

Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, himself under fire from another religious camp (the Shas Party), said he was "shocked" by this "unbelievable act."

Three camps

The religious sector in Israel is made up of three main blocs. First, the so-called religious-Zionist camp, chiefly embodied in the National Religious Party (MIFDAL) and the Gush Emunim movement (Bloc of the Faithful). This camp harbours extreme racist, jingoistic and war-like attitudes toward the Arabs and believes Israel should take over the entire land of Israel, including the West Bank and Gaza Strip, as well as parts of Jordan, Lebanon, Syria and Egypt, following "biblical prophecy."

Interestingly, Gush Emunim produced such notorious extremists and murderers as Rabbi Meir Kahane, Rabbi Moshe Levinger and Baruch Goldstein, who murdered 29 Arab worshippers at the Ibrahimi Mosque in Hebron on 25 February 1994.

The second camp is the Shas Party, headed by Rabbi Ovadia Yossef, former Chief Rabbi of Israel. The movement encompasses mainly Safardi Jews who had immigrated from Arab and Muslim countries. Given their underprivileged and inferior position in the Israeli society, Safardi Jews view with suspicion and mistrust the political establishment in Israel, which has always been dominated by Ashkenazi Jews of European origin.

The Safardi community, which suffers a disproportionately high degree of poverty, often accuses the political establishment of racial discrimination against Jews of Middle Eastern origin, and of maintaining an apartheid-like regime.

During the first week of May, thousands of Shas supporters converged on the Attorney-General headquarters in Jerusalem, shouting slogans against the government and the judicial system. One rabbi went as far as calling the Attorney-General a "Nazi."

Moreover, hundreds of Shas supporters stormed the High Court building in West Jerusalem on 13 May to protest the trial of the movement's leader Arie Deri'e on charges of corruption, bribery and misuse of public money. They seemed to be convinced that the charges were concocted by the Ashkenazi establishment to discredit Deri'e and therefore undermine the entire Safardi community.

More to the point, the matter could have serious political ramifications for the Netanyahu government. Rabbi Yossef and Deri'e repeatedly threatened to withdraw their support of the government if Netanyahu did not take a pro-Deri'e stance in the upcoming trial.

The third and most controversial segment of Israel's religious sector is the ultra-Orthodox community, which follows an eschatological and rather monomaniacal belief that no state for the Jews ought to be established before the advent or appearance of the Messiah. Indeed, the Naturi Karta, the most vocal group of the ultra-Orthodox Haredi community, believe that the establishment of Israel in Palestine in 1948 was "an apostatic development" portending "doom and gloom" for the Jewish people.

Moreover, the rabbis of Naturi Karta argue that the state of Israel poses a grave threat to the spiritual salvation and the very existence of the Jewish people. Hence, followers of the movement, numbered in the hundreds of thousands, adamantly refuse to bear Israeli passports, to serve in the Israeli army, to salute the Israeli flag, or even to send their children to public schools.

PT interviews Abu Marzouk after his release

Dr. Abu Marzouk assails American justice and vows to continue

his legitimate struggle against Occupation

Amman-Mahmoud al Khatib

Dr. Mousa Abu Marzouk, the former political leader of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas), assailed the American judicial system saying he did not see any justice during his 22-month ordeal. The former Hamas leader emphasized that he would prefer facing baseless Israeli charges against him rather than staying in a US jail. PT interviewed Dr. Abu Marzouk in Amman after he was deported to Jordan.

Q. In view of the developments of your detention in New York, what do you think are the real reasons for your detention?

A. I have been asked this question hundreds of times but in fact I have no answer. My name was put on the Immigration and Naturalization Serviceís persona non grata list without telling me or anyone else why. Also, two months before my arrival in the United States, the US Justice Department issued my re-entry visa and at the same time added my name to the list. But, I believe that my name was added for the sole purpose of serving Israel's interests.

Q. As a leader of Hamas, didnít you expect to be detained upon your arrival in New York?

A. No, I did not because I was there in February, five months before the ordeal. I also had never committed any crime in or outside the United States, so I did not expect such an action from the US authorities. I had also never been questioned by any security officer in the United States. My comings and goings to and from the United States had never been impeded before for any reason prior to my detention. I was moving legally using my own name. I never used false or covert names.

Q. Did you think that the Israeli extradition request was based on evident charges against your person as the leader of the political body of Hamas?

A. It was a political step. The Israeli request for my extradition was completely political because it did not come through the Justice Ministry or the Public Prosecutor in Israel. It was sent by the Israeli cabinet to the US Justice Department, which ordered the start of the legal proceedings of my extradition to Israel.

The Israelis also justified their decision of requesting my extradition by saying that they wanted to maintain the peace process and to stop the escalation of violence. However, the legal advisor of the Israeli Foreign Ministry advised his Ministry that there was no legal evidence against me and hence they would not be able to introduce any charge against me before a civilian court in Israel. This proves that the Israeli request was political.

Q. How do you assess the reaction of the American public opinion and media to your cause?

A. I used to read most of the American newspapers in my cell, especially the articles that talked about my case. I think, in general, they were fair and neutral. When I was released, I wrote a letter thanking the American media for their fair and objective stand concerning my situation.

Q. After about 22 months in an American jail, how do you assess American justice?

A. I did not find any kind of justice in the United States. One of the reasons that pushed me to withdraw my appeal from the second court of appeal was that I despaired of receiving any justice in America. I am the first man in US history to be sentenced to extradition without a hearing session. I am also the first politician to be sentenced by an American court to be extradited to another country. I am also the first in US history to stop fighting the extradition sentence by withdrawing my appeal and asking to be extradited in order to face unfounded charges in Israel.

Q. If you had been extradited, were you ready to face an Israeli court, and why?

A. Yes. Because I could not stay in jail in the United States fighting forever in order to have a hearing before the court. I waited for 22 months for a hearing but to no avail. I realized that there was not any kind of justice for an Arab or a Muslim in the United States. As a Palestinian, nobody acknowledged my rights and I found myself in an endless dilemma. Also the charges against me were baseless and untrue, so I decided to face them before an Israeli court.

Q. Dr. Abu Marzouk, what are your future plans after the end of your ordeal?

A. I am committed to serve my Palestinian People and their cause anywhere and anytime until they restore their full rights in their homeland. This was my commitment in the past and it will remain so in the future.

Q. How does Dr. Abu Marzouk assess the present political situation in the area?

A. There were problems from the beginning. The Israeli-PLO negotiators delayed a number of serious problems to a later stage. From the beginning we knew that there would be no real solutions to them. The Palestinian People will be the only losers in the end because the Israelis are not anywhere close to being ready to fulfill their commitments to the provisions of the Oslo Accords.

An Introduction to Hamas

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) is now considered the most prominent movement representing the Islamic ideological line in the Palestinian scene. It adopts Islam as a programme from which it takes its ideas and conceptions. Shortly after its inception, the Movement defined itself as a "wing of Palestinian Muslim Brotherhood," making it necessary to examine the historical, ideological and organizational relationships between Hamas and the Muslim Brotherhood Movement.

The Organizational Structure of Hamas

The organizational structure of Hamas, like that of the Muslim Brotherhood, generally depends on the basis of educating both the individual and the family. Hamas has also adopted the approach of mobilizing people around the ideological line of the Brotherhood and toward resisting the Israeli occupation. The Movement has a unified leading Bureau that resorts to a higher authority which is the 'Shura Council', a unified consultant body elected directly by the cadres. The Leading Bureau forms various technical systems. Security and military systems are formed as parts of the technical systems.

For its membership, the Movement, however, does not stipulate membership in the Muslim Brotherhood.

Hamas sources are quoted in order to stress that the Movement adopts the principle of decentralization in its work and a policy of functional independence. This is especially so when it comes to the necessary separation between the political, informational and organizational structure on the one hand and the military and security structure on the other.

Therefore, it can be said that Hamas works through three defined systems: the Political Bureau, the Informational Bureau, and Ezzedin Al Qassam Battalion, the military wing of the Movement.

The Objectives of Hamas Movement

Apart from being a national liberation movement seeking the elimination of Israeli occupation, Hamas is also an Islamic Movement. Its outlook on all aspects of life is inspired by the principles of Islam, from which it draws its ideas and conceptions. However, this does not limit the Movement's ability to practice a considerable amount of pragmatism and flexibility without necessarily having to deviate from its basic principles.

The Palestinian Issue, confirms Hamas, is not the cause of the Palestinians alone: it is rather the cause of Arabs and Muslims in general, and solving this issue and making peace cannot be achieved unless occupation is eliminated. By studying its literature, closely examining its political activities and discussing its leaders, a general picture can be drawn as to the objectives of the Movement. These objectives can be summarized as follows:

First: The General objective/ establishing the Islamic State

Hamas believes that the establishment of the state is a result of liberation. It is the right of the Palestinian people, asserts Hamas, to live in a democratic political system that is built on the principles of Shura and political pluralism.

Second: The Strategic objective/ liberating the whole Palestinian land

Hamas believes that Palestine, from the Mediterranean Sea to the Jordan River and from north to south, is the land of the Palestinian people. Hamas also believes that the Zionist Movement, in collaboration with European colonialism, usurped a large part of the Palestinian land in 1947 and completed its usurpation of the whole country in 1967. Accordingly, Hamas considers the liberation of Palestine a strategic objective of its programmes.

Third: Interim objectives

In its endeavour to achieve its general strategic objective, Hamas has adopted several interim goals. The following are Hamas' main interim goals:

1- The Liberation of the West Bank and Gaza Strip

Even after the Oslo Agreement, Hamas still believes that about one third of Gaza Strip and more than half the West Bank are still occupied in spite of the self rule agreements.

2- The Islamization of the society

This is one of Hamas' goals as an Islamic movement working in a society the majority of which believes in Islam as a religion, a culture and a civilization. To achieve this goal, the Movement is building a network of organizations in different fields.

3- Establishing the legitimacy of Armed Struggle

Hamas believes that resisting the occupation is a religious and national duty which is approved by international law and UN charters. Therefore, the Movement has sought to mobilize Palestinian masses and the Arab, Islamic and international public opinion in such a way as to promote the legitimacy of its resistance to the Israeli occupation.

4- The continuation of the Intifada

Hamas considered the Intifada as a stage in the continuing resistance against Israeli occupation and as an expression of rejecting occupation hegemony and injustice. It is with this view in mind that Hamas was keen on keeping the Intifada beyond any political transactions.

5- Preserving the National Unity

Hamas has actively worked on the internal level to preserve national unity and confront the Israeli policy of "Divide and Rule." The Movement has always stressed its commitment to its humanitarian and tolerant attitude toward non-Muslims and its respect for other organizations. Its official statements and communiquÈs have always pointed out that it is a movement for all Palestinian masses, Muslims and Christians alike.

6- Releasing prisoners and stopping Israeli aggression against civilian Palestinians

The Movement has also aimed at releasing all prisoners and preventing aggressions against Palestinian civilians. It has even offered Israel a truce proposal by which both sides stop attacking civilian targets including Israeli settlers if they are properly disarmed.

7- Activating the Arab and Islamic backing

This activation for the Palestinian issue is the central cause of Arabs and Muslims. This backing is considered the 'strategic reservoir' of this cause.

Fourth: Circumstantial objectives

1- Neutralizing Israeli military power

Hamas thought, at the beginning of the Intifada, that adopting civil confrontation techniques such as stones and blockages would isolate and neutralize the occupation's huge military might and equipment.

2- Establishing political legitimacy

This political legitimacy has been achieved through the active participation of Hamas in the Intifada and the ensuing persecution, torture, deportation and martyrdom of Hamas members.

The Means of Hamas Movement

1- Mosque activities

As an Islamic ideological movement, Hamas had concerned itself with the primary place of worship; i.e. the mosque. It considers the mosque establishment one of the most important means for achieving its goals and promoting its principles. Mosques are fields for preaching, guidance, political mobilization for struggle and resistance because they offer the opportunity for gathering people five times a day.

2- Political and informational activity

In this respect, Hamas holds political symposiums, campaigns, and Islamic exhibitions as well as issuing communiquÈs, publications and booklets.

3- Networking through institutions

Due to the disintegration of existing institutions in the Occupied Territories after the outbreak of the Intifada in 1987 and the weakness of the Israeli Civil Administration, Hamas set about establishing what has comparatively been highly organized and effective institutions.

4- Civil Resistance

Various civil resistance forms (demonstrations, strikes, civil disobedience, protests, non-cooperation, etc..) have been central to the Movement's resistance programmes and techniques.

5- Military Action

Since the outbreak of the Intifada, the confrontation of Israeli occupation has gone through several stages. The following are the main ones:

a. Massive Civil Resistance

b. 'The Knives War"

With the escalation of the occupation's measures against the Palestinian people and the decline of the role of certain organizations in the Intifada, it looked as if the situation necessitated a move into some qualitative action which did not depend on massive presence. Consequently, "cold steel" resistance, later known as "knives war", was adopted.

c. Armed resistance

In January 1992, the Movement's military action started as a natural result of the occupation's terrorism against the Palestinian people during Palestinian civil protests. The Intifada came to a point where it was necessary to limit the amount of massive activities and to redefine and readjust such activities so as to ease the economic and social burden of the people. This actually paved the way for the Movement's military programme in a time when Israelis were intensively increasing the operations of their terrorist "Death Squads" to liquidate the strong nucleus of the Intifada activists and leaders.

On of the most important facts to be stressed here is that there have been subjective and objective factors which contributed to the excellence of Hamas' military performance. Remarkable among these factors has been the independence of the military wing from the political one.

d. Martyrs operations

These operations have indicated that readiness to sacrifice oneself among Ezzedin Al Qassam militant have made them tough opponents and ideological matches whose concepts of loss and gain are quite different from their enemy's. What is considered a loss by the enemy is mere martyrdom and consequent paradise for them.

Corruption: The Other Face of the Palestinian Authority

By: Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem: On 21 March, the British daily, The Guardian, published a shocking account of the rampant corruption engulfing virtually all aspects of PA administration in the Gaza Strip. In fact, David Hirst's audacious revelations were so shocking that some Gazan activists translated the article entitled "Shameless in Gaza" into Arabic and distributed it as a leaflet throughout the Strip and in some parts of the West Bank. Moreover, a finer translation of Hirst's investigation was aired several times by "al Quds," the Damascus-based radio station run by opponents of Yasser Arafat.

"Shameless in Gaza" was so candid in exposing the moral decadence, administrative nepotism, and the overwhelming financial corruption permeating the PA hierarchy from the very top to the very bottom. One Palestinian journalist remarked that Hirst exposed only one aspect of an entirely morbid body known as the PA. Another journalist envied Hirst "because he has a powerful country to protect him in case the establishment decides to break his fingers and silence his mouth." He rightly argued that had a Palestinian journalist indulged in this kind of "dangerous talk", he would have incurred the full wages of his folly, with punishments conceivably ranging from "condemnation to a dark cell in some notorious Gaza jail" to "accidental liquidation by some overzealous Force-17 members who didn't abide by the spirit of the law."

Hirst's presentation of the "Tunisians, the new profiteers of Gaza"ówho often "use their VIP cars to sail through Israeli checkpoints on their way to the flesh-pots of Tel Aviv...even as Israeli border closures rob day labourers of their menial wagesódid not go unnoticed by the Palestinian intellectual community.

On 12 May, the Palestinian Legislative Council (constantly bullied by Arafat) held a long-delayed hearing on "Corruption in the Palestinian Authority", during which counsellors listened to hair-raising stories that, in the words of one counsellor, transcended reality.

Nabil Amre, apparently alluding to Hirst's article, recognized during a seminar at Beir Zeit University on 15 May that "there is widespread administrative corruption within the PA."

Amre, a close confidant of Yasser Arafat, actually stopped short of saying that corruption was the modus operandi of the Palestinian Authority administration. However, the man who reportedly writes most of Arafatís public speeches opined that the PA would be corrupt only as much as the Palestinian people allow it to be.

Amre's ostensibly valid argument, however, bears all the features of a half-truth, since Arafat's Authority is not an exclusive Palestinian affair. One should not forget that the PA was established first and foremost by an Israeli-American decision, mainly to absolve Israel of the liabilities of its continuing occupation of Palestine and to make Zionist domination more viable and less costly. It was Israel, the United States, and the so-called donor countries, after all, that provided Arafat with "money-power" to enslave the Palestinians and that allowed him to "hold all the reins, control all the money, and make all the decisions" because it is "much easier to deal with a corrupt autocratic leadership that is not accountable to the people.."

"Silence Incriminates"

For its part, the PA was so reticent about the scandalous revelations that neither PA radio and television, nor indeed any of the Arabic language newspapers published in Jerusalem or within the autonomous enclave, mentioned anything about Hirst's article.

A lone exception was the Gaza-based Islamist weekly Al Risala, which published a short column entitled "The Essence of Authority." The author (this writer) argued that the basic requirements for a modern, healthy society (e.g. social justice, equal opportunities for all, and especially accountability and equality before the law) were becoming all too "conspicuous antitheses" of the ìSultaî (authority) of Yasser Arafat, and that the inescapable outcome of this mischief was all too predictable.

The Death of Abu Dayeh Versus the PA Detention of Kuttab

By Ruth Anderson

Recently, two seemingly unrelated incidents occurredóone in Ramallah and the other in occupied Jerusalem. In the West Bank city of Ramallah, Daoud Kuttab, a leading Palestinian journalist and a U.S. citizen, was arrested by Palestinian police. According to Palestinian Prosecutor General Khaled Al Kidra, Mr. Kuttab is being held pending police investigations concerning suspicion of violating journalism regulations.

What proves fascinating is the sudden interest of the White House and its demands that Kuttab be immediately released. White House spokesman Michael McCurry stated, "We are very concerned about the implications of his detention both for the rule of law and for freedom of the press."

It is also interesting to note that Kuttab owns a television studio funded by the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID) and that he had been broadcasting live sessions of the Palestinian Legislative Council in Ramallah. France is also outraged by the detention of Kuttab, who was the winner of the International Press Freedom Award.

The reason given for Kuttab's arrest by PA police was that he spread the word regarding Arafat's ever-expanding censorship which, by the way, is funded with American and Israeli money, and that the PA routinely jams radio and TV programmes.

Though his arrest was but another blunder on the part of the PA, Kuttab is not likely to be abused in prison and his speedy release is imminent. What is of concern here for those who speak out about the abuse of freedom and the rule of law is the continued torture of Palestinian detainees at the hands of Israeli interrogators.

There was no outcry about the death of one lone Palestinian man who was savagely beaten and died during the last week in May. There was no White House spokesman to protest the death of Khalil Abu Dayeh. There was no official protest to the Israeli authorities concerning the brutal murder of this man. And France, like the White House, so concerned over the arrest of Kuttab, remained silent.

The Israeli authorities arrested Abu Dayeh during the second week in May when he was in Jerusalem with his wife. He was allegedly trying to steal a weapon from an Israeli soldier in Jerusalem's Old City.

On 23 May 1997, Khalil Ali Abu Dayeh, 37 years old and married with 5 children, died from wounds received from severe beatings. He was first held in the Russian Compound Prison and Interrogation Centre in Jerusalem and later transferred to the Givat Sha'ul Psychiatric hospital, a move which Abu Dayeh resisted. He was again beaten on his hands, wrists and neck. When he complained of throat pains on 21 May, he was taken to Sharei Zedek hospital in Jerusalem. There he was tied by his feet and hands and when he tried to resist, he was beaten until he died.

The autopsy report conducted by the Autopsy Department of the Palestinian Authority confirmed that Abu Dayeh had been severely beaten, that his body had sustained blows by heavy instruments, that his feet and hands had been tied, that he had fractures in various parts of his body, that the bones on the right side of his face had been broken by a dull, heavy instrument, that there were deep lacerations on his right shoulder and that he had severe internal hemorrhaging.

Where was the world when this happened? Why were there no international protests for this crime against humanity? Had he been an animal, animal lovers the world over would have risen in outrage at the death of a helpless creature at the hands of madmen. Abu Dayeh was indeed helpless and his killers were indeed madmen; the only unfortunate thing is that though he was treated as less than human, he was not an animal and therefore his death did not stir the passions of animal lovers nor did it move the White House to speak out about the brutality of his death.

Who cares about the non-American or little-known Palestinians who are arrested daily and tortured by the Israeli police? Who cares if the indigenous inhabitants of Palestine are losing their land each day to new Jewish settlements or blatant Israeli confiscation of Palestinian property? Who protests the unjustified curfews and closures placed on the Gaza Strip and West Bank which really are two huge concentration camps? And how is it that Israel is called the only Democracy in the Middle East when Palestinians have no rights and no freedom; they are unjustly put under curfew, harassed, beaten and tortured; and their land and their dignity are taken from them just because they were born Palestinian?

There will never be peace unless and until the Palestinians are given back their freedom and dignity, the right to live in peace on their own land and are given the chance to survive economically as well as culturally without unjust interference.

Men like Kuttab will survive. It is men like Abu Dayeh that we must worry about

Zionism and the Future of Palestine

By: Robert W. McGee*

I read an interesting book the other day -- Zionism and the Future of Palestine. It was written by Morris Jastrow, Jr. and published by the Macmillan Company in 1919. It has been out of print for many years now and is almost impossible to find. The person who lent it to me told me that there has been a conscious effort to remove it from library bookshelves. It deserves to be reprinted.

Jastrow traces the origins of Zionism and discusses its three aspects -- religious, economic and political -- which must be sharply differentiated from each other. Religious zionism involves the belief that the Jews will return to Palestine. Economic zionism was aimed at ameliorating the pitiful conditions of the Jews living in countries like Russia and Romania without citizenship rights and subject to oppression and persecution. Political zionism had the aim of converting Palestine into a Jewish state, which Theodor Herzl, the founder of political zionism, claimed to be the solution to the Jewish question.

Jastrow spends some time discussing the origins of these three aspects of

zionism, but the most interesting part, and the part that is most likely to enrage ardent supporters of the Jewish state, is the section on political zionism. He spends much of the remainder of the book discussing the fallacies and dangers of political zionism.

Reform Judaism, along with the possibility of attaining full citizenship in the west, snapped the ties between religion and nationality. As Jews could become citizens of the western countries they moved to when they escaped oppression in Eastern Europe, they were able to practice Judaism while exercising their rights as citizens in a number of western states. Political zionism involves a misinterpretation of the trend of Jewish history over the last 2,000 years. Religion and nationality were no longer inextricably bound together. Thus, political zionism was based on a false premise, that religion and nationality were inseparable.

Even many zionists of the time conceded that there was no legitimate reason for having a Jewish state as long as the Jews who lived in Palestine were guaranteed the freedom and security of the other inhabitants of the region.

Jastrow correctly predicted that the intertwining of religion and nationality -- political zionism -- would have negative consequences. Whereas non-Jews have only one country and one loyalty -- Americans are American, the French are French, etc. -- Jews are seen as having split loyalties. They are both citizens of the country in which they live and also supporters of the Jewish state. Thus, Jews who live outside of Israel (occupied Palestine) are seen as being less than totally loyal to the country where they reside. This political difference adds to the anti-semitism that already exists, just as Jastrow predicted.

As time passes it is becoming increasingly clear that Britain did a great disservice to the inhabitants of Palestine by giving away land that it did not own. Present-day problems can be traced to this tragic violation of property rights, coupled with the false premise of political zionism, which Jastrow exposed in 1919.

* Prof. Robert W. McGee is the President of The Dumont Institute for Public Policy Research, Dumont, NJ-USA- http://www.dumontinst.com

Religious Zionism in Focus

Gush Emunim's popularity among Israelis grow

By: Israel Shahak*

(Part 1 of 2)

At the time when so many politicians, political scientists and media pundits in Western countries are busily discussing the real or supposed dangers of Islamic fundamentalism, hardly anybody speak of the Jewish fundamentalism, which not only exists, but also grows, in Israel and even more in the United States. I don't mean by that the negligible splinters of Kahanists, but the much more influential and dangerous Messianic ideology of the religious settlers in the Territories organizationally represented by the Gush Emunim. Unlike the Kahanist splinters, Gush Emunim is considered perfectly respectable. On some important issues, it is supported by the entire opposition camp in Israel (Labor and Meretz), the right-wing and religious as well as by some influential Jewish and Christian groups in the U.S. Although it has organized demonstrations against Yitshak Rabin, some of its leaders remained until his assassination on 4 November, 1995, his closest friends.

The prospects of Gush Emunim seizing power in Israel through a coup d'etat or heavily influencing Israeli policies through a complacent government (like the present government of Benjamin Netanyahu) is by no means unlikely: and the reminder that Israel possesses nuclear weapons and insists on keeping its nuclear monopoly in the Middle East is in this respect in place. Contrary to what is usually believed, the ideology of Gush Emunim is in my view even more extremist than the ideologies attributed to the most extremist Islamic fundamentalist organizations. It is high time that this ideology be more widely known than it has been so far.

Like other run-of-the-mill Israeli ideologies, the Gush Emunim ideology is in its essentials traceable to the 1920s. It was devised by Abraham Yitzhak Kook, the Chief Rabbi of Palestine, the most influential supporter of religious Zionism. His followers believe he was divinely inspired and infallible. His son and successor, Rabbi Tzvi Yehuda Kook, who died childless at the age of 91 in 1980s, was treated with similar deference. To all intents and purposes, his followers became a sect within Orthodox Judaism, and that sect eventually evolved into present Gush Emunim. Following T.Y. Kook's death, the spiritual leadership of the sect devolved into a "rabbinical council," with a membership selected by mysterious criteria from among his disciples. The innovative tenets of their theology are traceable to an interpretation of Jewish mysticism (the Cabala). Sine, however the writings of both Kooks are (possibly on purpose) even more obscure than the invariably obscure Cabbalistic literature and in modern interpretations of both. Unfortunately, the deeper implications of all those writings are theologically too intricate to lend themselves easily to a popular presentation to otherwise well-educated readers. This is why such presentations are lacking, even in Hebrew.

On Gush Emunim, (G.E.) however, several good books have appeared in Hebrew. The only on available in English is no more than fair. It is: "for the Land and the Lord, Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel," by Ian S. Lustick, Council on Foreign Relations, 1988. As the author informs us in his preface the book originated with a paper written for the U.S. Department of Defense. This may partly explain the book's excessive concentration on the political stances of G.E. and its relative neglect of invariant elements in G.E. doctrines. Contrary to what its title suggests, "Jewish Fundamentalism" is hardly explained in the book. Moreover, Lustick tends to present the more extremist G.E. dogmas in a way that wouldn't overly shock his American readers. Salama Before 1948

The village stood on flat terrain on the central coastal plain, on the north side of a highway leading to Jaffa. [Kana'na and Abd al-Hadi 1986:31] The inhabitants believed that their village was narned after Salama Abu Hashim, a companion of the Prophet Muhammad, who was buried in the village in A.D. 634. His tomb, on the northwest side of the village, became known as the shrine of Sayyiduna (our master) Salama. [ibid.:9, 311 In 1596, Salama was a village in the 7wahiya of Ramla (liwa' of Gaza) with a population of ninety-four. It paid taxes on a number of crops, including wheat and barley, as well as on other types of property such as goats and beehives. [Hut. and Abd.:1541 The Syrian Sufi traveller al-Bakri al-Siddiqi, who traveled in the area in the mid-eighteenth century, visited the above-mentioned shrine. [Al-Rihla, cited in al-Khalidi 1968:152] In the late nineteenth century, Salama was a village built of adobe brick and had a few gardens and wells. [SWP (1881) 11:254]

During the British Mandate, Salama was divided into quarters, each quarter inhabited by a hamula (clan) or sub-clan. The houses were at first clustered together, with the houses of each hamula or sub-hamula centered around a large courtyard (hawsh) that had a single common entrance. The courtyard provided private space for women to do their household chores, for children to play, and for families to gather in the evening or on special occasions. Some families also built houses in the orchards; these did not usually belong to hamulas. Although most houses were constructed of adobe brick, some people built stone houses and Baghdadi houses, which were made of wood and insulated with gravel and whitewash on the inside. [Kana'na and 'Abd al-Hadi 1986:30, 35] The population was comprised of 6,670 Muslims and 60 Christians. Salama had two elementary schools, one for boys and another for girls. The boys' school was opened in 1920 and the girls' in 1936; 504 boys and 121 girls were enrolled in 1941. The villagers also supported a soccer team.

There were numerous shops in the village, including five coffee shops. During the Mandate, a transportation company that owned cars and buses was established in Salama, with partners from the neighboring village of al-'Abbasiyya; the company was called the Salama-al-'Abbasiyya Automobile Company. [Kana'na and 'Abd al-Hadi 1986] The villagers worked primarily in agriculture and associated activities, and to a much lesser extent in commerce and the civil service. In 1944/45 a total of 2,853 dunums was devoted to citrus and bananas and 2,266 dunums were allocated to cereals; 370 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards. Agriculture was both rainfed and irrigated; irrigation water was drawn from some eighty-five artesian wells. [Kana'na and 'Abd al-Hadi 1986:32] The residents shipped their produce in Jaffa and sold some of it in nearby Zionist settlements. They also shipped milk to a dairy factory in Jaffa that was owned by two men from Salama.

Occupation and Depopulation

Salama was hemmed in by a number of Jewish settlements and came under almost continuous attack for a period of five months, beginning on 5 December 1947, a week after the passing of the United Nations Partition Resolution. The New York Times reported that members of the Haganah opened fire with machine-guns at Salama on that date and that Arab families were evacuating the area and moving towards al-Ramla and Lydda. The Palestinian newspaper Filastin reported a twopronged attack on the same date, which abated when British police arrived and later resumed during the night. Sniping and further attacks were reported over the following two days. The History of the Haganah states that in December 1947 "the Haganah leadership in Tel Aviv decided to attack the infamous village of Salama," adding that "this was the first attack on an Arab village." The assault was carried out at dawn on 19 December and was unsuccessful. Palestinian historian 'Arif al'Arif mentions another raid on 28 December and writes that it was preceded by a diversionary attack from the settlement of Petach Tiqwa. The raid originated at Ramat Gan, where the Zionists had assembled a large force drawn from the Jewish Settlement Police and the Irgun Zvai Leumi (IZL). The village's defenders managed not only to drive the attackers back, but mounted a counteroffensive against Petach Tiqwa, where they were joined by militiamen from Lydda and al-'Abbasiyya. [F:6/ 12/47, 7/12/47, 8/12/47; NYT:6/12/47, 11/1/48; R:274-79; S:1379]

In early January 1948, the villagers had set up various makeshift defenses around Salama. The New York Times reported on 11 January that British army units had used gunfire to clear four roadblocks around the village and had told the makhtar to make the people fill in a large ditch "presumably intended as a defense measure." Filastin described the same incident, adding that the army justified its actions by saying that it needed to move freely through the area. The defensive barriers were evidently being put to good use, since Filastin reported no less than ten separate attacks on the village in the month of January alone, sometimes more than one in a single night. [F:11/1/48; NYT:11/1/481

The Zionists believed that Salama provided shelter for nonlocal guerrillas, but al-'Arif writes that it was the villagers themselves who organized a militia of around 30 men, in the wake of the UN Partition Resolution in November 1947. After that, scarcely a day went by without a skirmish around the village, during which "bullets rained down" upon Salama. On 18 January 1948, Israeli historian Benny Morris records a major Zionist attack on the village, by the Third Battalion of the Alexandroni Brigade. The operational orders for the January operation stated: "The aim is . . . to attack the northern part of the village of Salama ... to cause deaths, blow up houses and to burn everything possible." A qualification was added: "Efforts should be made to avoid harming women and children." According to Morris, numerous houses were destroyed in the January attack.

The largest attacks that occurred in subsequent weeks and which were reported by various sources were carried out on 28 February and 15-16 April. The Arab Liberation Army sent 20 fighters to join in the defense of Salama during the first attack. Reporting on that attack, the New York Times indicated that the Haganah had "invaded" Salama, and the attack was only revealed the following day when British police discovered the bodies of 6 Jews killed during the attempted invasion. A communique issued by Arab militia forces based in the area said that a force of 250 Jewish troops participated in the first attack, including an advance party of 50. It added that the 6 Jews killed were part of the latter force, which was surrounded by the village's defenders. The communique, published in Filastin, said that 3 Arabs were also killed in the battle, including 1 woman. In the second attack, al-'Arif writes, at least 30 three-inch mortar shells rained down on the village from Jewish positions at Petach Tiqwa. [F:2/3/48; NYT:1/3/ 48, 29/4/48; R:274-79; S:1379, 1502; M:1571

The attacks persisted into the second half of April, but the village defenders soon ran out of ammunition, and the residents began to leave. The village was not occupied, however, until the end of April, during Operation Chametz ("Passover"; see Bayt Dajan, Jaffa District) which aimed at the encirclement and occupation of Jaffa. [M:157; R:274-79] Units of the Alexandroni Brigade occupied Salama on 29 April 1948. [S:1574-75] Morris quotes the Haganah radio as saying that the village was evacuated "at the first onslaught," but al-'Arif states that Jewish forces did not enter Salama until they had made sure that it had been evacuated by its residents. Al-'Arif writes that the village was empty by 30 April, and a Times dispatch indicates that Salama surrendered to the Haganah on that day. Later that day, Salama was visited by Jewish Agency chairman David Ben-Gurion, who wrote in his diary that he found "only one old blind woman." Morris states that the villagers were scattered among various locations, some going to the Ramallah and Nablus areas, others to Gaza and Jordan. [M:100; NYT:29/ 4/48, 1/5/48; R:274-79; S:1553]

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

The village and its lands have been engulfed by the expansion of Tel Aviv.

The Village Today

A number of village buildings remain: numerous houses, four coffee shops, the mosque, the shrine, one cemetery, and the two schools (see photos). The houses are deserted and in a state of disrepair, except for a few that are inhabited by .Jewish families. Most are made of concrete and exhibit a variety of architectural features. They are either one- or twostorey buildings and have rectangular doors and windows (except for one that combines both arched and rectangular windows). Four houses are identified as belonging to Ahmad Muhammad Salih, Mustafa Abu Najm, Abu Jarada, and Abu 'Amasha. The house of Abu Najm is a two-storey, concrete structure with rectangular doors and windows (some of which have grillwork and others shutters). It is sealed, and the outside staircase leading to its second floor is gone.

The four coffee shops were named after their owners: Muhammad al-Hawtari, Abu 'Asba, Sha'ban al-Naji, and al'Arbid. A Jewish family lives in the al-Hawtari coffeeshop. It has an enclosed front porch and a slanted roof covered with corrugated metal sheets; the bottom door panel has been marked with a star of David. The domed shrine is in a state of disrepair. One of the two village cemeteries ("the martyr's cemetery") is deserted and overgrown with wild vegetation, while the other has been turned into a small Israeli park. Fig, cypress, palm, and Christ's-thorn trees and cactuses grow on the site. The surrounding land is generally covered by construction.

"Robert Fisk: Reporting the Truth and Exposing Lies"

by: Nader Hashemi

Nader Hashemi is the co-ordinator of the Carleton University Middle East Discussion Group. He is an active member of several human rights and solidarity groups related to the Middle East and Bosnia-Hercegovina.

Veteran Middle East correspondent Robert Fisk spoke to a capacity crowd of 350 in Ottawa, Canada last month on the topic "Threats, Lies and Videotape: Reporting the Middle East." His lecture could have alternatively been titled: "Honesty and Moral Integrity: Reporting the Middle East." With a penetrating oratory style and flawless English diction, he spoke for one hour and 45 minutes drawing upon his 30 years as a professional journalist, 21 of which have been spent reporting from the Middle East and North Africa. His presentation was a blend of history, humour, personal anecdotes and solid analysis. Fisk who holds has a PhD in Politics from Trinity College, Dublin, is the author of the 60,000-selling and widely acclaimed book, PITY THE NATION: LEBANON AT WAR, and has the distinction of being the longest serving Western correspondent in the region. His talk was a reflection on both the changing nature of his profession and the restrictions placed on news reporting from the Middle East. It was interspersed with live video footage from his 1993 documentary "From Beirut to Bosnia" and never seen before raw film from the Israeli massacre in Qana, south Lebanon.

Theorizing about recent changes journalism, Fisk lamented the "geographical shrinkage" of his profession "firstly because it diminishes our world, turning ideas into government inspired certainties, locking us into the straight-jacket of think tanks and institutes that expound simple north-south, east-west perspectives. State Department correspondents now tell us . . how the international community -- formerly the 'Free World' -- should behave, how Russia is too big to be shunned over the bloodshed of Grozny, ... how Bosnia does not -- and later does -- represent a US national interest, how the Middle East must seize the unique, not-to-be-repeated offer of an American-Israeli peace. And secondly, I hate the way," Fisk continued, "in which this geographical shrinkage is flattening out history, smoothing out the creases of the historical record so that humiliation and political treachery are turned into 'age-old hatreds', so that reasoned opposition to a flawed peace treaty is turned into 'support for terrorism'."

A major part of his address was directed toward criticizing the impact of sound-bit technology. "How odd it is, these days," Fisk observed, "to find our perspectives narrowed rather than broadened by an increase of knowledge. How strange is our desire -- in the short, edible sound-bites of the news agency wire -- to put history into a box. How eccentric is our wish to avoid the connections, every bit as strong as the fibre optic cable, which bind history to us." Quoting from what Edward Said has termed "the passivity and prolonged distraction" of television viewers and what the Palestinian scholar calls the "rise of democratically- induced conformism." Fisk put forward Said's thesis that despite the diversity and heterogeneity of the United States, "it is easy to stir up feelings of endangered patriotism and national insecurity (Edward Said, 'American contrasts,' AL-AHRAM WEEKLY, 17-23 February 1994)." Political censorship for reasons of state, as in the 1990-1 Gulf War, where the mainstream media, instead of challenging the state accepted censorship as a condition of reporting, was cited as an example.

Robert Fisk listed three areas which have made reporting the news more difficult: 1) the physical danger due to an increase in the sophistication of modern weaponry, 2) the technical problems associated with television whose "technology" Fisk points out is "as restrictive as the cliches of many of its reporters," and 3) the increase power of lobby groups. Fisk spent time criticizing the intimidation and pressure tactics of such groups "who try to persuade our editors that we are liars or racists or that we should be fired from our jobs because we told truths which they wish to silence. Never have these organizations been as potentially effective as they are today," he added. "They do not write or telephone to elicit information; they do so in order to inhibit our telling of the truth." Fisk was speaking from extensive person experience in addressing this theme. Pro-Israel groups, whom for years have criticized his reporting, launched a frontal assault in 1994 against the US Discovery Channel after it aired "From Beirut to Bosnia," a three hour documentary where Dr. Fisk explores the reasons behind anti-Western sentiment in the Muslim World. Their pressure and intimidation tactics were successful in preventing a customary second showing of the documentary (see Robert Fisk, "From Beirut to Bosnia," INDEX ON CENSORSHIP, May/June 1995).

Fisk was also critical of media censorship in the Arab world. After investigating and reporting on Egypt's unfair elections and the systematic violations of human rights by Egyptian security police, he was attacked in the leading daily AL-AHRAM for "spreading lies and deceit" and likened to "a black crow pecking at the corpse of Egypt." He showed the audience a blown up cartoon from the Bahrain newspaper AKBAR AL-KHALEEJ, where he was depicted along with colleagues from the BBC and THE TIMES (London) as a rabid dog and menace to society; a clear threat against his life. Criticizing his Arab press colleagues, he stated: "They are quick to vilify Western journalists, sometimes with very good reason. . . . But I still remember how, during the Israeli siege of west Beirut in 1982 -- when thousands of civilians died, either in Israeli air-raids, bombardments from the land and sea or in the Sabra and Chatila camps at the hands of Israel's allies, not a single Arab journalist -- newspaper or television journalist -- came to Beirut from Egypt or Jordan or the Gulf, from Algeria, from Morocco or from Libya. . . Loud was the rhetoric in newspapers around the rest of the Arab world. Absent were their reporters, many of whom bowdlerized the reports that we sent out of the besieged city to print in their newspapers." In the Gulf War they were no better Fisk added. "They lined up, as usual, behind their leaders, wrote glowing articles about the New World Order, the perfection of American military power or, if they were Palestinian or Jordanian or Iraqi, the moral leadership of Saddam Hussein."

Fisk shared with his audience insight into the moral underpinnings that inform his unique brand of journalism. "Whether or not we journalists write the first draft of history," Fisk affirmed in his crisp English accent and scholarly demeanour, "we are often the first witnesses to history. Indeed, I believe that is what the job of a journalist should be: to be a witness to history, to discover the truth . .and to set it down in print so that no one can ever say: 'we didn't know, 'nobody told us'." Further axioms of the Fiskian code of conduct include a belief in the importance of challenging authority, investigating the powerful, and exposing the official line put by government and state.

And finally onto Qana. Robert Fisk was the first journalist into the UN compound of the Fijian battalion after it was hit by proximity shells during the height of Israel's bombardment of southern Lebanon last year. In a moving and graphic description which brought many in the audience to tears, he related the scene as he entered the camp. "Blood ran in streams from the gates of the United Nations' compound in which those poor people had taken refuge. They were the gates of hell. As I walked inside, I saw a young girl holding in her arms the body of a middle-aged man, rocking the corpse from side to side and weeping and crying over and over 'my father, my father.' There were babies without heads, women without arms. I will never forget what I saw. I reported it all in my paper.

He then proceeded to tell the entire story of the Qana massacre. The fact that he was speaking on the 18 April, exactly one-year after the Israeli attack, made his presentation all the more poignant. "For us reporters at the time -- and the United Nations -- the truth or otherwise of Israel's explanation -- that it never intended to hit the UN base and its protected Muslim civilians -- rested on its denial that it could not see where its shells were falling. But the survivors, UN soldiers and refugee alike, all told of seeing a pilotless Israeli photo- reconnaissance aircraft over the camp during the massacre. And if this was true, then the implication was obvious: the Israelis knew all to well what they were doing."

After extensive interviews with refugees and UN soldiers, Fisk heard repeated rumours that a UN soldier at a nearby base had coincidentally videotaped the bombing of Qana including the pilotless Israeli photo-reconnaissance aircraft. His search for the mysterious film was in vain. He was told that UN personnel were under strict orders not to discuss its existence with anyone. It was two days after the mass funeral for the massacre victims of Qana that Robert Fisk's telephone rang in his Beirut apartment. An anonymous voice gave him a map reference and added: "1300 hours." With the audience listening intently as he recounted the story, Fisk's moving description, which perhaps was the highlight of the evening, deserves to be quoted verbatim.

The map reference was a crossroads outside Qana. I have never driven so fast to southern Lebanon. And at 1300 hours, I saw in the rear-view mirror a UN jeep pull up behind me. A UN soldier in battledress and blue beret walked up to me, shook hands and said: 'I copied the tape before the UN took it. The plane is there. I have made a personal decision. I have two young children,' he said, 'the same age as the ones I carried dead in my own arms at Qana. This is for them.' And from his battledress blouse he pulled a video-cassette and threw it on the passenger seat of my car. It was, I think in retrospect, the most dramatic individual personal act I have ever seen a soldier take. The mighty powers may try to cover up but the little men can still sometimes win.

The unedited raw film clearly shows the UN base in Qana under bombardment with the Israeli reconnaissance drone flying over head. Fisk talked the audience through the screening pointing out the arc and direction of the in-coming artillery shells. An Israeli helicopter is also visible over Qana at the time of the attack, dropping phosphorous flares to avoid heat-seeking missiles. Qana is covered in smoke as the artillery shells rain down from the sky. At one point flames are clearly visible in the UN base. Fisk announces pointing to the screen, "There is the conference room on fire. There are about 50 people burning to death inside that room at the moment. This smoke," pointing to a nearby section of the screen, "is basically from the cremation of these people as the walls catch fire." The audience sits in stoned-silence like a jury in a courtroom, as Fisk presents his evidence with the meticulous precision and poise of a prosecuting lawyer who convincingly demolishes the defense team's main argument. After the video was turned off he returned to the podium and ended his talk with the following two sentences. "But here, I think, the work of journalism must end -- and the facts of history take over. For your kindness this evening, Ladies and Gentlemen, and for your kindness in inviting me to Ottawa to speak to you, I thank you very much." A thunderous applause and standing ovation ensued.

Reflecting on the evening's lecture and video presentation I am reminded of Noam Chomsky famous admonition that "it is the responsibility of intellectuals to speak the truth and to expose lies." No reporter lives up to that ideal better today than the Middle East correspondent for THE INDEPENDENT, Robert Fisk.
