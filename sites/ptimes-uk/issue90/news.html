Israel predetermines the fate of the West Bank as redeployment is carried out

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

After a series of delays and mini-crises, the Israeli army finally carried out the first phase of the Wye River agreement on 20 November by handing over to the Palestinian Authority as many as 33 villages and hamlets around the city of Jenin, the West Bank's northernmost town.
The redeployment process itself was brief, even symbolic, and involved little or no army movement of note in or around the vacated areas since cadres representing various PA security agencies had already been operating in these villages with Israeli knowledge and tacit approval.
The total area of these villages is around 500 square kilometres, four fifths of which became area (A) while the remaining 100 square kilometres (around 2% of area C where Israel maintains full administrative and security authority) were relegated to area B, which means the PA will exercise only civilian responsibilities while the Israelis will retain the security authority.
With the latest redeployment in the Jenin area, the PA will have under its full control 10% of the West Bank. Moreover, when the second and third phases of the Wye River memorandum are carried out, the PA will have full internal control over 18% of the West Bank and partial control over 28% of the territory.
However, these figures can not be taken for granted since Israel does not count East Jerusalem and the large confiscated areas annexed to it as part of the West Bank.

Ratification of the Wye River Memorandum
The ratification of the Wye River Memorandum by the Israeli Government on 19 November was adopted only after the PA issued a series of mostly imprecise and poorly worded decrees aimed at "safeguarding national unity and protecting national interests."
These included the "anti-incitement decree", the decree on the confiscation of illegal fire-arms, including fake weapons, and a ban on driving cars carrying Israeli license plates by Palestinians.
The decree, announced in Hebron on 19 November by Arafat's advisor Al Tayeb Abdul Rahim, was scoffed at by many Palestinians, who argued that "it suited Israel more than us".
However, most people, including the Islamist camp, didn't seem to take the decree seriously, as they realized that it was meant first and foremost to meet Israeli dictates and demands.
On 20 November, Arafat's police chief in Gaza, Ghazi al Jabali, issued the so-called "decommissioning of fire-arms decree", which urged all Palestinians in the autonomous enclaves to hand over to the PA all unlicensed fire-arms in their possessions in a period not exceeding two weeks. The decree proscribes stiff monetary fines on violators.
Finally, the same al Jabali issued a third decree against "driving or owning a vehicle without a proper Palestinian license plate."
This latest decree is ostensibly aimed at fighting the huge car-theft phenomenon. However, since the bulk of car thieves within the PA enclaves are connected with the security agencies (and the Israeli intelligence), there is not much al Jabali or any other PA official can do to stop this lucrative business.

Implementation of the Wye Memorandum
Besides the redeployment in the West Bank's northern parts, the implementation of the first phase of the Wye memorandum included two additional accords, the first on the opening of the Gaza airport on 24 November, and the release of 250 Palestinian prisoners.
The PA-controlled media celebrated the opening of "Yasser Arafat's International Airport", which will be the Palestinians' main outlet to the outside world, as a "great victory".
Yasser Arafat himself toured the facility on 22 November, saying the airport was a symbol of Palestinian Statehood.
However, apart from the many Palestinian symbols in the airport, e.g. Arafat's ubiquitous portraits, Palestinian flags, carts bearing the phrase "Palestine National Airline", etc., the Israeli army will exercise complete security responsibility at the airport for three months, by the end of which new negotiated arrangements will be reached.
The most scandalous flaw blemishing the airport agreement is the fact that Israel will have the final say in deciding who can and who can not reach the airport.
Israeli roadblocks will be erected on all routes to the airport, and those Palestinians deemed by the Israeli intelligence as "objectionable" will not be allowed to proceed either from Gaza to the airport, or from the airport to Gaza.
This is very significant since the Israeli authorities maintain long blacklists of Palestinians who are barred from travelling abroad, such as this writer, for "security reasons".
The list includes political activists, university professors, writers and journalists, in addition to college students, supporters of Islamist movements, prominent critics of the Oslo process and former prisoners.
The PA did not object much to the virtual Israeli control of passenger movement to and from the airport probably because Israel gave assurances that "only opposition figures would be turned back."
If the airport agreement was scandalous, the prisoners release was even more so.
The PA had been saying all along that the Wye memorandum stipulated the release of 750 political activists and freedom fighters.
However, when the first 250 prisoners were released on 20 November, it turned out that most of them (150) were actually common criminals, including car thieves.
Feeling cheated and embarrassed by the Israeli feat, which is not the first of its kind, the PA accused the Israeli Government of "deception and cheating".
Chief Palestinian negotiator, Dr. Saeb Ureikat, protested the Israeli act saying, "We didn't go to Wye Plantation to negotiate the release of car thieves and common criminals."
The refusal by Israel to release political prisoners and former freedom fighters infuriated many Palestinians, especially relatives who had been told by the PA that their loved ones would be released, and drew strong criticisms of "the sloppy and unprofessional performance of PA negotiators at Wye Plantation."
Interestingly, Arafat, who is enjoying the redeployment "victory", has not retorted to Netanyahu, saying only that the PA remained committed to freeing all Palestinian prisoners.

Underlying the Redeployment
The redeployment near Jenin was preceded with a vitriolic exchange of statements by Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, his Foreign Minister Ariel Sharon, and Arafat.
The public acrimony began when Netanyahu said the Wye River memorandum served to lower Palestinian expectations vis-à-vis the final settlement, suggesting the PA would eventually come to terms with "vital Israeli interests-Judea and Samaria". These interests, he pointed out, included the annexation of the Jordan Valley, the estimated 194 Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, Gush Itzion (the area extending from Bethlehem to Hebron) and a 3-kilometre-wide security zone along the former armistice line between the West Bank and Israel proper. This is, he added, in addition to keeping Jerusalem as Israel's eternal and undivided capital, the mantra routinely intoned by Israeli officials.
Moreover, Netanyahu repeated what he had been saying ever since he returned form Wye Plantation-the third phase of redeployment in the West Bank would cover no more than 1% of the West Bank. And in a gesture probably meant to frustrate Arafat, Netanyahu added that the "Palestinian leadership as well as the American administration understood the Israeli position to that effect."
Arafat did retort on 15 November saying, "we will accept nothing short of an independent Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital." In a speech marking the 10th anniversary of the Palestinian declaration of independence, which took place in Algiers in 1988, Arafat said, "we will soon have 40% of the West Bank under our control, and after the third phase, we will have the rest, and afterwards we will march to Jerusalem, our eternal capital, and be it as it may."
Furthermore, Arafat reaffirmed his intention to declare Statehood by 4 May 1999, saying before a cheering multitude of his supporters, "we will declare the State in May."
What angered the Israelis most, however, and gave Netanyahu a pretext to "postpone the implementation of the redeployment until further notice" was Arafat's speech to a Fatah congress in East Jerusalem in which the Chairman said, "We will brandish our guns in order to extract our right to pray in Jerusalem…because Jerusalem was, is and will continue to be our capital, irrespective of their whims."
The resurgence of the phraseology of defiance on Yasser Arafat's part proved too unacceptable and too inflammatory for Netanyahu, who did order the suspension of redeployment implementation "until Arafat retracted his statements and recommitted himself to a peaceful resolution of contentious issues, including final status subjects."
Meanwhile, Ariel Sharon, obviously fed up with Netanyahu's less than straightforward talk, decided to be himself, when he urged settlers to take over all hilltops adjacent to their settlements.
"Go and get the hilltops, whoever gets there first will have the hills, and that which you don't grab will end up in their hands", Sharon told a meeting of T'somet party supporters on 15 November.
Predictably, his spasmodic remarks were well-received by the settlers who, on 16 November, heeded his call and moved in to grab several hills in three locations near Nablus and in the Ramallah areas. This happened concomitantly with the pulverizing by Israeli army bulldozers of thousands of Palestinian olive groves in twelve locations throughout the West Bank for the purpose of opening bypass roads for the settlements.

The calm before the storm
Far from viewing it as a great achievement, most Palestinians seem to think that the redeployment of the Israeli army from 10%-13% of the West Bank is only the calm that precedes the storm. This probably explains the prevailing caution in peoples' reactions and the absence of public euphoria, as was the case four years ago on the eve of the signing of the Oslo Accords.
The unabated expansion of settlements, the planned annexation by Israel of 10% of the West Bank (the so-called Meri land), along with Sharon's provocative calls on settlers to grab as many hilltops as possible before Palestinian hands reach them, all suggest that soon after the Wye memorandum is implemented, the bitter struggle will be resumed, and guns from both sides will be brandished and blood from both sides will be shed.

PA cracks down on Hamas following martyrdom operation

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh
Underscoring its determination to implement the Wye River Agreement, the Palestinian Authority moved forcefully against Hamas following the 29 October car-bomb attack near Khan Younis, arresting hundreds of its rank and file and placing the movement's founder and spiritual leader under house arrest.
Though confined to Gaza, the crack-down, ostensibly precipitated by the botched suicide-bomb attack, was the most serious since Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu came to power more than two years ago.
In addition to taking unprecedented punitive measures against Sheikh Yassin, including severing his telephone line and preventing him from receiving visitors, Palestinian police arrested several top Hamas leaders, including Mahmoud al Zahar, Ismael Abu Shanab, Ismael Haniyya and Ahmed Baher.
Moreover, the spate of arrests was accompanied by the introduction of certain restrictions on freedom of speech and expression, particularly press freedom, prompting charges from civic leaders and liberal-minded politicians that the PA was not only fighting violence but also violating human rights and civil liberties.
More to the point, Gaza Police Chief Ghazi al Jabali issued a decree banning the public torching of the Israeli and American flags, arguing that the practice undermines Palestinian national interests and hurts PA relations with Israel and the United States.
Meanwhile, the "war of words" between Hamas and the PA escalated since the botched car-bomb attack at the Khan Younis intersections. Hamas leaders accused the PA of repressing Hamas in order to appease Israel and the United States irrespective of human rights considerations. For its part, PA officials attacked Hamas, accusing it of "subservience to Iran" and "playing into the hands of Jewish settlers and Israeli extremists who oppose the Wye River agreement."
Defending the stringent measures against Hamas, PA official al Tayeb Abdul Rahim said the timing of the 9 October operation was meant to corrode the Wye Plantation agreement and give the Israeli Government "further excuses not to withdraw from Palestinian land."
"How would the Palestinian position have looked if the suicide bomber had succeeded in killing 40 Israeli schoolboys and -girls?" said Abdul Rahim, justifying the crack-down on Hamas.
Hamas responded to this by arguing that the movement's political leadership is totally separated from the military wing, the Ezzul Deen al Qassam Brigades, and therefore it can't be held responsible for its actions.
Indeed, several Hamas officials privately voiced consternation at the Khan Younis car-bomb attack, saying it was politically inexpedient, and wrong in timing
"Those who planned and carried out the attack seemed to have extended an open invitation to Yasser Arafat's police to come and arrest us," said one Hamas leader in the Hebron area on condition of anonymity.
Despite PA charges to the contrary, all signs indicate that the Gaza leadership of Hamas played no part in planning, preparing or executing the 29 October attack.
This suggested that the outside leadership of Hamas might have ordered the attack, probably without the local leadership's approval.
This explained the virtual silence displayed by Islamist leaders in Gaza, who, while denying direct responsibility for the attack, refused to elaborate on who might have been directly responsible for its planning and excution.
Hamas didn't like to accentuate the outside-inside divide since this would undermine the projected unity of the movement in the face of strenuous and relentless efforts by the PA to "Palestinianize" Hamas by trying to ostracize and de-legitimize the movement's leaders based in such Arab and Islamic capitals as Amman, Damascus, Beirut and Tehran.
The PA didn't completely reject Hamas disavowals. PA officials, unable to produce hard evidence incriminating Islamist detainees, alleged that "an Iranian trend within Hamas" was responsible for the attack. The PA further alleged that the Iranian-backed group "was planning to assassinate PA officials."
Most Palestinians, however, didn't take seriously the talk about an Iranian-backed faction within Hamas for objective reasons having to do with Hamas's staunch ideological independence and reputed adamant refusal to be at any State's beck and call.
Moreover, the PA campaign to that effect seemed to be targeting Iran as much as, if not more than, Hamas, especially after the supreme Iranian leader Ali Khamenae castigated PA Chairman Yasser Arafat for "signing this treacherous agreement which compromises Muslim rights in Palestine and al Quds."
Then why hit Hamas local leadership, risking inter-Palestinian fighting and sagging popularity among ordinary Palestinians opposed to collective punishment reminiscent of Israeli occupation practices.?
The answer is clear: making Hamas leaders and supporters in Gaza suffer is the most effective way of deterring their colleagues abroad from carrying out further attacks against Israel, and, consequently, harming Palestinian interests and undermining Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority.

Speakers: Muslim unity is pre-condition for liberation of al Quds

Occupied Jerusalem (al Quds, 16 November) Muslim speakers in al Quds marked the occasion of "Isra and Miraj" anniversary, Prophet Muhammed's night journey from Mecca to Baitul Maqdes (Jerusalem) and his subsequent heavenly flight to the Throne of the Almighty, with calls on Muslim leaders and peoples the world-over to display unity in order to qualify for the liberation of Palestine and the delivery of al Masjidul Aqsa from Zionist hands.
Sheikh Muhammed Hussein, Imam of al Masjidul Aqsa, said Muslim unity was the key to the liberation of al Quds and the recovery of Muslim rights from Zionist hands.
"Muslim unity is essential for any meaningful endeavour to restore the Islamic holy places in Baitul Maqdes…the current state of fragmentation and division among Muslim States and peoples can only perpetuate Zionist hegemony and aggression," said Hussein before thousands of Muslims at al Masjidul Aqsa.
He added that Zionist Jews and their allies in the West could not have seized Palestine from Muslim hands had they found a strong and united Muslim Umma (community).
The Palestinian Islamist leader also called on Muslim regimes to try to deliver themselves from the shackles of Western powers, particularly the United States, which, he said, is the world's foremost evil power.
Ikrema Sabri, the Mufti of Jerusalem, pointed out that the Isra and Miraj anniversary was a reminder of the Islamic identity of Palestine.
He pointed out that the Prophet Muhammed (pbuh) brought about the moral conquest of Palestine long before Muslim armies set foot on the soil of al Quds.
This, Sabri said, underscores the "Islamicity" of Palestine, past, present and future until the Day of Judgement.
As in al Quds, Muslim speakers at al Khalil's (Hebron's) al Haram al Ibrahimi al Sharif celebrated the sacred anniversary in the shadow of the Zionist occupiers' guns.
Arif al Jabari, deputy-minister of Wakf in the Palestinian Authority, said sooner or later, Palestine and Jerusalem would revert to where they rightly belong, to the Muslim Umma.
He recited the first few verses of Sura Isra that foresee the ultimate demise of Zionist rule in Palestine at the hands of "true and dedicated servants of the Almighty."
Jabari said Muslims could form a superpower provided they abandon their old and un-Islamic ways such as despotism and disunity, as well as excessive worldliness and materialism.
Sheikh Fathi Amre, Head of Religious Orientation at al Khalil's Wakf department, said the Isra and Miraj anniversary reminded us all that al Quds and al Masjidul Aqsa were a trust in every Muslim's heart.
He appealed to Muslims saying, "Don't abandon al Masjidul Aqsa; don't give up, because if you do, Allah will abandon you and let you down."

Fatah Prisoners to Arafat: "You betrayed us"

PT (21 November)- "You have betrayed us, Mr President"; this is how Fatah prisoners in Israeli jails began their letter to PA chairman Yasser Arafat on 21 November following Israeli refusal to release them.
Palestinian sources said the letter described the Wye River agreement as "humiliating" and "disgraceful" and "filled with Israeli dictates and blackmail."
Most of the estimated 3,000 prisoners in Israeli jails belong to the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas). However, Fatah says that as many as 500 prisoners formerly associated with the movement were still languishing in Israeli jails because they were involved "in killing Jews."
Israel has always refused to release Palestinian prisoners who belonged to Hamas or "had blood on their hands", e.g. killed Israeli soldiers or settlers.

Arafat issues "anti-incitement" decree

PT (19 November)- In an obvious attempt to meet Israeli demands, PA Chairman Yasser Arafat issued on 19 November the so-called anti incitement decree.
The decree listed acts of incitement, including "racial discrimination", "incitement to violence", "degrading various religions", and the formation of "societies and associations that practice incitement and spoils life or agitates the masses for change through violence."
The poorly worded decree, say observers, is with no substance, since the violations stipulated in it are not common in Palestine.
For example, it is the Palestinians who are the primary victim of Israeli incitement, not the opposite.
Moreover, Palestinians, the majority of whom are Muslims, recognize other monotheistic religions and accord them ample veneration and respect.
This is unlike wide sectors of religious Jews who use extremely derogatory labels when referring to Islam and Christianity.
What is particularly scandalous about Arafat's decree is that it was not reciprocated by similar commitments on the Israeli side, especially with concern to incitement to murder Palestinians by Jewish rabbis throughout Jewish settlements.
On 21 November, Israeli officials expressed profound dissatisfaction at the failure by the PA "to stop incitement against Jews and Zionists."
Israeli officials cited two examples of continued PA incitement: the first was the Juma'a Friday sermon at al Masjidul Aqsa in which the speaker, Hayyan al Idrisi, called on the Muslim Umma to pay more attention to Zionist attempts to destroy the al Aqsa Mosque and his reaffirmation that Palestine was, is and will continue to be Islamic.
The third example was an article on the official PA Web site on the internet in which Zionism was referred to as a racist ideology.
In 1974, the United Nations adopted a resolution considering Zionism a form of racism.
Israel, the Zionist State, still views itself as "a State for the Jews" not a "state for its citizens."

PA seeks to mobilize support following redeployment

PT (20 November) - The Palestinian Authority has been trying to obtain public support after the conclusion of the Wye agreement and the implementation of the first redeployment of the Israeli forces around Jenin.
PA security and civilian organizations held several big rallies in Nablus, Ramallah and Hebron to mark the so-called "independence day" during which Yasser Arafat spoke of "the nearness of the fulfillment of our dream of a Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital."
However, it seemed that apart from Arafat and the close circle of advisors and hangers-on around him, and probably a small but vocal group of fanatic supporters, the majority of Palestinians wouldn't "buy it this time", probably out of awareness of the adage "bitten once, shy twice."
Indeed, most Palestinians seemed to realize that the current redeployment of the Israeli troops would probably be the last, and that instead of heralding the acquisition of more Palestinian rights and Statehood, the Wye agreement would only be a harbinger for the resumption of the bitter struggle with the Zionists over Palestine. Hence, the caution.

Jewish historian: The future of Jews lies in the re-establishment of Caliphate

A Jewish historian from Tel Aviv has called on Arabs and Muslims to re-establish the Islamic caliphate "in order to bring about a genuine, durable and just resolution of the conflict between Israelis and Palestinians. " Professor Meyer Kaplan said in an interview with Palestine Times that the future of Jews in Palestine would look doubtful and precarious if the current state of Zionism remained unchanged, which he said necessitated the establishment of an umbrella Islamic caliphate transcending geopolitical borders and covering Palestine. He said that Jews would accept in principle Islamic sovereignty over Palestine "provided that the caliphate rules according to the law of the Prophet." He cited the "law of Medina" as the most appropriate law of the land in a prospective Islamic State where "Jews, Muslims, Christians and others are considered citizens of the Islamic State." Kaplan lashed out at the current peace process, saying it is "nothing" from the historical point of view, and that, historically speaking, it is not worth the paper on which it is written. "Neither Jews nor Arabs are satisfied with it, so each party will wait until the time is ripe to leap on the other", he said.

PA's law of the jungle

Gaza- (19 November) Palestinian Authority police have kept Sheikh Ahmed Yassin under house-arrest for the third consecutive week, refusing to allow him to receive visitors and barring him from performing prayers at his neighbourhood mosque.
The PA has not yet given any official reason for the "house-arrest" and PA officials continued to issue conflicting statements on the matter. On 18 November, police chief Ghazi al Jabali attacked Sheikh Yassin saying, "placing him under house-arrest is the lesser of the two evils to protect him from his own evil and protect the Palestinian people as well." However, Muhammed Dahlan, head of the Preventive Security Force in Gaza, said, "the house-arrest of Sheikh Yassin came about to protect him from Israeli agents." Never the less, observers noticed that the real motive behind the house-arrest was to silence the sheikh and prevent him from talking to the media, especially Arab satellite television channels.

Israel reactivates "death squads"

Israeli sources said on 19 November the Israeli army reconstructed one of its death squads, which was active in murdering Palestinians during the intifadah (1987-1993).
According to the Israeli daily, Ma'ariv, a number of high-ranking Israeli officers took part in a special ceremony marking the reactivation of the undercover force known as "Samson" or Shimshon in Hebrew. The paper said that among the officers taking part in the ceremony were the Commander of the Southern Area, Yom Tov Samya, Commander of the Israeli army in Gaza, Yoab Glenet, and the Commander of the Samson unit Saghi Telmi. Israeli undercover units, whose soldiers disguise themselves as Palestinians, assassinated many ordinary Palestinians, including children, during the intifadah. Israeli and Palestinian sources estimated the number of Palestinians killed at the hands of these units at 120, most of them innocent bystanders.

Israel to confiscate "meri land"

On the eve of the partial implementation of the Wye River agreement, the Israeli Government announced plans to seize the West Bank's "meri land", e.g. State land whose ownership was with the Ottoman State before the First World War. Meri land makes up over 10% of the West Bank, and the Israeli decision to confiscate it makes it the biggest land grab by the Zionists since 1967. According to the Israeli State-run radio, Voice of Israel, the land would be used to expand existing Jewish settlements.
The seizure of the land, along with the opening of scores of new wide bypass roads, suggests, in no vague manner, that the Israeli Government is not interested in real peace with the Palestinians. It also shows that the current developments in the peace process are disingenuous and that a bitter, violent and bloody showdown with the Palestinians is coming, sooner rather than later.

Zionist occupiers destroy more Arab homes in Jerusalem

The Zionist occupiers have escalated the diabolical practice of destroying Arab homes in East Jerusalem in an effort to check Arab demographic growth. According to a recent bulletin published by the East Jerusalem-based "land research centre", the Israeli Government and municipal authorities have invented a new set of ways and legal tricks to prevent Arabs from building, thereby giving the Israelis the green light to destroy homes allegedly built without license. The bulletin pointed out that the number of Palestinian houses destroyed in Jerusalem reached (so far) 27 houses, while the average number of houses destroyed annually since 1990 was 28 houses. The bulletin said the Israeli goals behind the house-demolition policy included "narrowing Palestinians' horizons", "narrowing as much as possible land ownership by Arabs", "reducing Palestinian demographic as well as human presence", and classifying much of the Arab-owned land as "public gardens or green areas" in order to prevent Palestinians from building homes thereon.

Likud, Labour lose in Israeli local elections

Occupied Jerusalem (16 November) The two main political parties in Israel, the Likud and Labour, have voiced mounting anxiety about their dwindling popularity following last month's local elections in Israel, which many observers contend have carried a plain message of "no confidence" in Israeli party politics.
That message was conveyed throughout the Jewish State, as voters in one electoral circle after the other shunned Likud and Labor candidates, mainly in favour of independents and break-away faction leaders. In fact, in many cases, even those Labor and Likud candidates who managed to win did so by distancing themselves from their parties, which they came to view as a liability rather than an asset.
Undoubtedly, the biggest loser in the elections was the Likud, the party of Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, which lost the municipal councils of virtually all big towns with the exception of Jerusalem. Moreover, in addition to the Mayorships, the Likud also lost its representative majority in scores of important towns to independents, local and small parties and, to a lesser extent, to the Labor party.
Buffeted by the election results, Netanyahu initially sought to put on a brave face, arguing, rather rightly, that the voters rebuffed not just the Likud but Labor as well. The Israeli premier, however, admitted later that there was "something wrong in our system", adding that the big parties must draw the right conclusions from the election results.
Likewise, the Labor party, despite its limited successes in Tel Aviv, Beir al Saba'a and a few additional localities, could not conceal its anxiety about the overall trend of anti-party politics in Israel, as seen in the elections' outcome. This, observers suggest, is evidence that the mainstream public in Israel is moving away from the established political parties.
Indeed, Labor Party leader Ehud Barak recognized, somewhat begrudgingly, that "all was not right for Labor", saying that "the big political parties must reconsider their ways to prevent smaller parties and local groups from taking over national politics."
Barak, like many politicians from right and left, blamed the new electoral system of a direct election of Prime Minister for the looming crisis in the Israeli political system. He suggested that the system be scrapped in favour of the older proportional representation system. Barak, it is well known, was among the foremost supporters of the current system, unlike many in the Labor party, including former Israeli Prime Minister Shimon Peres. Hence, his vacillation to that effect could affect his election results negatively since it would project him as a leader with undecided political thoughts.
Underlying the crisis in the Israeli election system is what is referred to as the "Americanization of Israeli elections", both on the local and national levels, whereby the emphasis is put on "personality" rather than "ideology" or "party affiliation".
The main problem stemming from this "Americanization" is the fact that the mainstream Israeli parties are not yet ready for it, as seen in the latest elections. So far, the Israeli election system worked from top to bottom, which meant that voters voted essentially at their parties' call, and candidates were elected mostly for their party affiliation.
Now this formula, which has prevailed so far, is about to change, meaning that the voters are beginning to give less attention to ideology and more weight to performance and such personality-related characteristics as honesty, veracity, skill and public service record, which are considered a rare commodity in both the Likud and Labor parties.
Clearly, the big parties are not ready to succumb to the new phenomenon and are actually moving to reverse it before it becomes too overwhelming to stop.
In the last few days, a number of Israeli politicians and Knesset members have suggested several alternatives to the direct election of the Prime Minister. The Speaker of the Knesset, Dan Tichon, suggested the minimum representation rate (the so-called passage boundary) be raised from 2% to 5% of the overall vote, thus enabling the big parties such as Labor and Likud to actually arrogate (the lost) votes destined to smaller parties that won't make it to the Knesset.
Needless to say, this suggestion is likely to be rejected by the smaller parties, which see its implementation as a mortal threat to their very survival.
Meanwhile, some politicians, especially from the Likud, and also from Labor, have urged for a return to the old classical system of proportional representation as "the least of the two evils."
This system, its advocates argue, would reintroduce the lost balance to Israeli politics. However, critics argue that reverting to the old system would force the big parties to seek out the formation of fragile coalitions with smaller parties, thus making the big parties hostage and their coalitions hostage to whims and blackmail demands of smaller parties, the junior coalition partners.
A significant development in the Israeli election was the considerable rise in the "Haredim" (religious Jews) representation in Jerusalem's new municipal council. The Haredim won 15 seats of the town's 31 seats, enabling them to have virtual veto power on all issues. The vanishing power of secular forces, along with the Arab boycott of the election, shows that Jerusalem is becoming a "fundamentalist Jewish stronghold."
This, it goes without saying, will auger bad for the future of the divided city, especially the prospects of peace between Jews and Palestinians.

The Arab Sector
The election results in the Arab sector, which makes up 20% of the population, showed a similar trend, taking the form of the prevailing national issues, which hitherto united the collective Arab consciousness in Israel, at the expense of local, clan, sectarian and even personal considerations.
The most conspicuous feature, however, was the dwindling popularity of the communist party known as the Democratic Front for Peace and Equality.
The front lost several of its traditional strongholds, such as Dir Hanna and Kufr Yasive, where the communists had maintained their grip on local politics for over 20 years.
The front suffered its biggest loss in Nazereth, where the Arab Democratic List (a coalition of the Islamic Movement and the Arab Democratic Party) won 10 seats of the 19 contested seats forming the municipal board, thus denying the communists their majority, which they had maintained since 1976.
This means that Ramez Jarayseh, the town's communist mayor who narrowly defeated the Islamist candidate Suleiman Ahmed, will have to form a coalition with the Islamic majority in the Council, a task that is certainly not going to be easy.
The Islamic movement, the front's traditional rival, made certain successes in several towns and villages, particularly in Um al Fahem, where the incumbent Sheikh Raed Salah won a third term by a stunning 70% of the total votes.
Never the less, the general trend in the Arab sector was a shift in favour of the Hamula, the extended clan, and independents.
This represented a definite setback for national platforms that emphasized just peace for Palestinians in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip and equality for Israeli Arabs.
According to Abdul Malek Dahamshe, an Arab member of the Knesset, the Oslo Accords made the Arab community more inward-looking, since the Israeli-Palestinian conflict is perceived as "on its way to resolution."
This trend, Dahamshe opines, is negative and harmful to Arab interests, because the conflict is "far from over" and equality for Israeli Arabs "is still an illusive and distant dream."
