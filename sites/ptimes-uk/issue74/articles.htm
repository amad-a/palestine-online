Israel: The only country that uses torture openly

By Steve Sozby

In November, 1996, the Israeli Supreme Court lifted an injunction against the use of "moderate physical force" in the interrogation of Palestinian detainees. The injunction was in place since the death of Abd Samad Harizat, who was beaten to death during interrogation in April 1995. This past May, a United Nations committee on torture, an Israeli High Court ruling and another torture death of a Palestinian prisoner rekindled the issue of torture in Israel.

Israel is one of 102 nations that have agreed to outlaw torture by signing the non-binding Convention on Torture in 1987. Since the intifada began late that same year, over 20 Palestinian political prisoners have been tortured to death in Israel.

"Israel is the only Western country that openly uses torture," said Lea Tsemel, a veteran defense lawyer and a founder of the Public Committee Against Torture in Israel. "This is not some brute in the secret services beating up a prisoner. It's done in the open. There is a quiet legitimization by a high-ranking commission and government ministers."

In early May, a United Nations human rights committee determined that Israel's interrogation practices, such as shaking prisoners and subjecting them to long periods of loud music, cold air and being tied up, amounted to torture and should be halted immediately. The Committee Against Torture also denounced the use of death threats, beatings and other interrogation methods used by Israelis against Palestinian prisoners.

"This conclusion is particularly evident where such methods of interrogation are combined, which appears to be the standard case," committee member Peter Thomas Burns said. Burns, the United Nations committee member specializing in Israel, said the standards of the Convention Against Torture were absolute while Israel seems to think that treatment of detainees could be modified to fit the circumstances. He said the international agreement prohibits "exceptional circumstances" as excuses for torture and questioned how Israel could define prolonged interrogation, shaking, sleep deprivation and awkward and painful sitting positions as anything but torture.

Two weeks after the special United Nations Committee denounced Israel for torture, the Israeli High Court rejected a petition filed by a Palestinian human rights group to stop the torture of Omar Ghneimat, who claimed he had been tortured during 47 days of interrogation. Ghneimat said Israeli interrogators had forced him to lie on the ground while handcuffed and jumped on his body, kicked and beat him, dragged his body on the floor while handcuffed, tied him into painful positions so that he

could not sit or stand, forced him to stand in a squatting position for long periods of time, and put him in tight handcuffs which caused his fingers, arms and legs to swell. The petition to stop his torture was brought to the Israeli High Court by The Palestinian Society for the Protection of Human Rights and the Environment (LAW).

Omar Ghneimat was arrested in March as part of a crackdown on suspected Hamas members shortly after his brother Moussa died while carrying out a bombing attack on a Tel Aviv cafe that killed three Israelis. Another brother was also arrested, along with others from the West Bank village of Serif. They are suspected of being responsible for kidnapping and killing an Israeli soldier last September. In the High Court decision, the police were ordered to investigate the causes of Ghneimat's bruises and cuts since there were inconsistencies between Ghneimat's and the interrogator's testimonies. The Court decided to reject LAW's petition since the state attorney notified the Court that "at this stage" no more physical pressure was being used against Ghneimat.

LAW'S attorney argued that "physical pressure" was an ambiguous term which the Court could not rely upon and did not include the psychological torture typically utilized by the Shabak including sleep deprivation. She also argued that the state attorney had made no commitment to refrain from torturing Ghneimat in the future and that it was upon the Court to enforce the international prohibition against torture and order the Shabak to refrain from any future torture especially in the light of Ghneimat's bruises and cuts. The President of the High Court, Aaron Barak, rejected this argument and told LAW's attorney that she could always come back to the Court if the Shabak continues to torture Ghneimat.

Pictures of Ghneimat's bruised arms were printed in both Israeli and Arabic newspapers and caused an embarrassment to the Israeli security services.

On 16 May, shortly after the High Court ruling, Khaled Abu Daiyya was arrested while with his wife in Jerusalem. The father of three children from the Jerusalem village of Beit Safafa was taken to the Russian Compound jail and there, according to witness Mahmoud Abu Ter, was severely beaten. Abu Ter heard beatings on at least two occasions, and saw Abu Daiyya's bloodied body.

Police transferred Abu Daiyya to the Givat Sha'ul Psychiatric hospital, a move which he resisted. Abu Daiyya was then beaten again on his hands, wrists and neck. He complained of throat pains and on 21 May was taken to Sharei Zedek hospital in Jerusalem. There he was tied by his feet and hands and, when again he tried to resist, was beaten until he died. The exact time of death has been difficult to establish as the hospital and police reports differ. The autopsy, conducted by Dr. Jalal Jabri, the General Director of the Autopsy Department of the Palestinian Authority confirmed that the body was subjected to severe beating; the body sustained blows with heavy instruments; the hands and feet had been bound; there were fractures in various parts of the body; the bones of the right side of the face, on an area spanning 15-20 centimetres, had been broken by a dull, heavy instrument; there were deep lacerations on the right shoulder; the 5th, 6th, 11th and 12th ribs were broken; and there was severe internal hemorrhaging. Thousands of Palestinian political prisoners have been tortured in Israeli prisons over the past few years.

Despite the international effort as well as a movement in Israel to abolish the use of physical force against detainees, Israel continues to insist on employing such measures during interrogations. As Palestinians step up their resistance to Netanyahu's increased confiscation of West Bank lands, there is little doubt that many more Palestinians will suffer the same brutal fate as Ghneimat and Abu Daiyya.

Ethnic Cleansing Continues in Palestine

By Robert W. McGee

The government of occupied Palestine continues its ethnic cleansing policy. After declaring a temporary moratorium on demolishing Palestinian homes to keep the peace process alive, the Israeli military bulldozed nineteen more homes in the village of Bir Naballa in the West Bank. The homes were located near a settler bypass road. Forty-seven Palestinians, mostly children under 12 years old, were made homeless in this latest human rights abuse. The families could not remove their belongings before the demolitions and the soldiers prevented their neighbors from helping them.

This ethnic cleansing is only the latest in a series of concerted efforts to destroy whole Palestinian neighborhoods that lie near illegal Jewish settlements. According to the Israeli defense minister, 860 homes have been targeted for demolition. More than 2500 buildings in East Jerusalem are also vulnerable to destruction, according to some reports.

This policy of demolishing Palestinian homes and building settler homes and apartments on Palestinian land constitutes a major human rights abuse. Most of the world's nations have condemned this policy. Yet the United States continues to ignore these abuses, taking the position that the problem must be worked out between the Israelis and the Palestinians without outside interference. Yet the USA does not hesitate to interfere in every other part of the world where it declares itself to have a strategic interest. U.S. taxpayers are being forced by the pro-Zionist Clinton administration to pour billions of dollars into this corrupt regime to support these human rights abuses.

If the USA were really interested in seeing a resolution to this concerted policy of nazi-like ethnic cleansing, it would cut off aid to Israel and demand that the millions of Palestinians who have been driven out of their own country be allowed to return to their homeland and vote in elections. The U.S. government does not hesitate to supervise elections in other countries. Why not offer to supervise an election in occupied Palestine that includes Palestinians as well as Israelis? The U.S. government pays lip service to democratic principles, but when it comes to actually protecting human rights, it falls short.

Palestine Times interviews Hamas' spokesman in Hebron

Natshi: Hamas is one movement, one policy

Palestinians are convinced that resistance is the sole option

Interviewed by Mahmoud al Khatib

The spokesman of the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, in occupied Al Khalil (Hebron), Mr. Mohammed Jamal Natshi, stressed the popularity of his Movement among the Palestinians which is increasing day by day. He added that the Palestinians, including many of the Palestinian Authority officials, are convinced that the Islamic solution is the only means to restore the Palestinian rights. Mr. Natshi was one of the 400 Hamas leaders and activists deported by the Israeli Occupation authorities to Marjel Zuhour in South of Lebanon in the early 1990s. The following interview was conducted with Mr. Natshi by telephone.

 

Q. What are the latest developments in Hebron?

A. Hebron is the centre of the struggle. There are daily clashes and friction with the Occupation soldiers and Jewish settlers, especially because they occupy Hebron's heart. Hebron itself is surrounded by the Jewish settlers from all side. The Jewish settlement in the Old Town's centre makes Hebron similar to Jerusalem regarding the struggle over the religious places. The Jewish settlers in Hebron and round it are the worst kind. Because the downtown is over-populated by Hebronites, the confrontations and clashes with the 400 settlers among them and with the Israeli soldiers who protect them have become inevitable. The city has witnessed in recent years several massacres against Hebronites, most notably the one committed by the terrorist Baruch Goldstein, who killed 29 worshippers in Hebron in February 1994. There are continuous shootings by the settlers at the Palestinians who shop in the old market. The most recent infamous incident was the distribution of blasphemous anti-Islamic drawings which insulted Prophet Mohammed (peace be upon him) and the Virgin Mary. All these issues make Hebron different than other cities of the West Bank concerning the struggle against Occupation.

 

Q. Many analysts attribute the escalation of clashes and confrontations in Hebron to the strong presence of Hamas in the city. Do you agree?

A. Yes. There is no doubt that the Islamic Movement in general and Hamas in particular enjoy a strong and distinguished presence in Hebron. Elections in any institution or student council are guaranteed to be in favour of the Islamic Movement. The rally that was organized on 19 July, for instance, was attended by more than 25,000 citizens and reflected the real popularity and position of the Islamic Movement among the Hebronites. It also proved that the common Muslims in the city are aware of the reality that the input to the (peace) agreements did not ease the people's lives but made them worse and more difficult. They understand that the agreements did not bring any dividends at the security and economic levels. Added to this are the continuous confiscation of land, harassment by settlers against the citizens and the surrounding of Hebron's entrances and exits by the Occupation soldiers. It is also known that the Islamic awakening in Hebron has been deep-rooted and strong for a long time. Therefore, Hamas' markedly strong presence in the city also has deep roots. The continuous struggle with the Occupation soldiers and settlers made the Hebronites see the verses of the Holy Qur'an realized and coming true, especially those which describe the Israelis, their allegations, and their misconduct. All the aforesaid issues made the people strongly believe that the Islamic solution is the only solution to their cause and to the Palestinian question in general.

 

Q. Concerning the recent intifadah in Hebron, circles close to the self-rule authority allege that the authority's supporters ignited the new uprising and that Hamas' supporters were involved as of late..

A. In fact, Hamas was the first to call for the intifadah (uprising) and the first to start it in Hebron and Gaza Strip. This fact cannot be ignored. The first strike and different activities of escalation were staged by Hamas. But as for the clashes in the heart of Hebron, Hamas has a different viewpoint which is supported by all the citizens of the city: we support the escalation of clashes and confrontations against the occupation and settlers away from the city's centre. However, the centre of the Old Town should be full of life and normal life activities shown through the ordinary movement of citizens in order to siege the Jewish settlement there. Otherwise, the occupation authorities may use any escalation of clashes as a pretext to close the roads and markets in the old city. All Hebronites support the Hamas attitude that this place in particular is not suitable for resistance works. The citizens should carry on with their normal life in that part of the city because the Jewish settlers wish for it to become vacant in order to expand their settlement in the heart of Hebron.

 

Q. How do you assess the relation between Hamas and the Palestinian Authority in the light of the recent incidents that followed the Tel Aviv suicide bombing?

A. The facts have proved the reliability and credibility of the Islamic Movement's attitude. Many people who were supporting the self-rule authority and the peace process have become convinced of our views and opinions which oppose the Oslo agreements. Therefore, the PA is no longer able to defend its position and market these agreements among the Palestinians in the forums, public meetings and even the dialogues because every time the Occupation authorities do something, it reconfirms the Islamic perspective and refutes the allegations of the PA. No one accepts the accusations made by the PA that the Islamists' activities and work destroy the Palestinians' dreams and national interests. We have also many brothers in the occupation jails. There is security coordination between the PA and Israel. The PA's security men and informers watch and follow the movements of the Islamists. However, there is no direct harassment against the Islamists because the PA itself is no longer able to make harassment-like arrests, to deter Islamic establishments, or to prevent political activities like rallies and lectures.

 

Q. What is your view concerning the national dialogue in the light of the recent developments in the peace process?

A. It is clear that the PA by such dialogues wanted to show that it controlled the situation and that it was able to gather the different factions and powers. The dialogues were ceremonious. They were not serious. I think any Palestinian-Palestinian dialogue has become subject to the ceiling of the Oslo Accords between Israel and the PLO which will never make any political agreement among Palestinians a success. For example, Hamas representatives in the national dialogue held in Cairo in 1995 reached an agreement with the PLO representatives on certain points, at the top of which were the public freedoms. But when Hamas representatives returned to the West Bank and Gaza they were arrested by the PA. Even the participants in the dialogues were not safe from arrest! The reason is that the (peace) accords oblige the PA to impose mass punishment when our freedom fighters attack the settlers or the occupation soldiers. Therefore, I do not see that such a dialogue would be useful or that it would achieve serious objectives.

 

Q. If you are invited to a dialogue now, do you think it will enhance the PA's position ?

A. If a serious dialogue is held now, it will foster the Islamic Movement's position because the common people are convinced that the Oslo Accords are a complete failure and that they added to our woes. Therefore, they want an alternative. Many of the PA officials agree with the Islamic view that the resistance is the only option. They believe that the negotiation process according to their fragile perspective does not achieve even their simple objective which is the restoration of 17% of Palestine. They have become sure that even this little objective will never be achieved without force or resistance. All this makes the dialogue in every meeting, forum or gathering in the Islamic Movement's favour.

 

Q. How do you view the future of the PA after Chairman Arafat?

A. I think that the source of 70% of the PA's dictatorship is Mr. Arafat's person. There is no doubt that some dictatorship will remain because of the mentality that dominates the largest faction of the PLO - Fateh. The same autocratic mentality will continue but on a smaller scale.

 

Q. So you are optimistic of the future, although many analysts expect internal disputes and struggles among the PA's security bodies.

A. I think the long struggle 'refines' the people. The Palestinian People experienced a long period of continuous struggle which made them love freedom and hate oppression and jails. Therefore, we hear loud shouts against autocracy, individualism and dictatorship. I hope the future will carry freedom and openness with it. I think Arafat's person is that which clings to autocracy.

 

Q. When we talk about the relation inside Hamas, we come to the relation between its leaders in the occupied territories and in diaspora. There is also some kind of distinction between the political stands of Hamas leaders in the West Bank and of those in Gaza. What is the reality of this relation?

A. Disparity exists in the viewpoints but without divisions in the leadership. This disparity is an old-present phenomena in any intellectual or political movement. I do not see any special difference between our leaders in the occupied territories and in diaspora. The different views available here existed there outside the occupied territories.

But the media usually wants to make a fuss from nothing depending on a certain statement here or somewhat different statement there. There is one Movement and one policy. The decision is made after taking the views of all members of the leadership in and outside the occupied territories. Because the majority of the Palestinian People are living in diaspora, they should play a role in drawing the policy of the Islamic Movement. In short, the relation between the Hamas leaders inside and outside, in jails and out of jails, is integrated.

 

Q. How do you see the end of the final status talks between Israel and the PLO? What will be your response?

A. I do not think the final solution will be very different than the present one. The offered solution is autonomy. The Occupation troops may redeploy in the residential areas of the West Bank classified as areas B and C because the Zionists are not concerned with the people. They want the land, the borders, the river and the settlements. The people will be surrounded by Jewish settlements. The final settlement on the serious issues like Occupied Jerusalem, the refugees and the settlements will be dead and decided in Israel's favour. As for the response, I think the Islamic Movement will have its usual response. It will enhance its resistance. The struggle against the Zionists will end up to be dogmatic and God's promise will come true.

The rising star of Hamas

By Khalid Amayreh

 

Occupied Jerusalem - With the peace process looking increasingly precarious, the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) has been reasserting its strength and popularity in the occupied territories.

In the last few weeks, the Movement was able to mobilize tens of thousands of Palestinians throughout the West Bank and the Gaza Strip to reassert the "Islam-is-the-solution" slogan and also to renew Islamists' rejection of Israeli colonialism and American connivance with it.

On 20 July, as many as 20,000 Palestinians, mostly young people, took part in a spectacular march in downtown Hebron. Carrying green banners and large signs affirming the "Islamic choice," the huge multitude swore allegiance to the Prophet Muhammed and warned Jewish settlers and other enemies not to play with fire, an obvious reference to the blasphemous anti-Islamic posters pasted up by a Jewish female settler on Arab storefronts in Hebron last month.

Sheikh Nayef Rajoub, a charismatic Islamist leader from Dura, addressed the massive rally, stressing the "ideological nature of Jewish-Islamic enmity." Rajoub said it was within the normal order of things that the Jews chose to attack the Prophet of Islam because he, as did Jesus before him, exposed their morbid mentality and hostility to God.

Rajoub attacked the peace process, describing it as "explicitly futile" and calling on the Palestinian people, Arabs and Muslims all over the world to wash their hands of it once and for all.

"Under the rubric of this repugnant process, Israel seized more land, destroyed more homes and consolidated its occupation of our land," said Rajoub amid cheering approval from the huge audience.

Dr. Azzam Salhab of the Hebron University also castigated the peace process, saying that it was ironic that while Israel is doing everything conceivable to destroy it and eviscerate it of substance, the Palestinian Authority is holding fast to it. Moreover, Salhab called on the PA to disregard American and Israeli blackmail and to adopt Islam, respect human rights, political pluralism and freedom of the press and expression.

In Tulkarm, thousands of townspeople took part in a rally organized by the Islamic Movement on Monday, 22 July. The rally, which coincided with the Prophet Muhammed's birthday anniversary, was addressed by several Islamic speakers including Dr. Naji Subeiheh who urged Palestinians to "hold fast to the robe of God because if we don't then He won't grant us victory." Moreover, Subeiheh urged all Palestinians to be united because "disunity breeds division and division breeds defeat."

In Jabalya refugee camp in the Gaza Strip, the Islamic Movement organized a massive rally under the slogan "Allegiance to the Prophet" with the participation of thousands. Addressing the rally, Dr. Nezar Rayyan said the profane posters in Hebron were merely a "balloon test" by Jewish elements to see how Mulims would react if and when the Jews attack the Al Aqsa Mosque.

Speaking on behalf of Hamas, Ayman Taha said Jewish settlers would not have dared pasted the profane posters had it not been for the Arab regimes which themselves adopt anti-Islamic policies.

Ismael Hanniya, a prominent Hamas leader and co-founder, said it was imperative that Hamas increase its strength, adding that "this is a precondition for preventing the Palestinian arena from deteriorating further."

Also addressing the rally was Dr. Abdul Aziz al Rantisi, former spokesman of the Islamist deportees in Marj al Zuhur, who said that the Oslo agreement was already dead despite all artificial attempts to keep it alive.

Al Rantisi denied "hostile rumours" that Hamas was being divided against itself, saying the Movement was as united as ever.

Position Vindicated

Observers notice that more and more Palestinians are finding out that the views that Hamas has held all along vis-a-vis the peace process have now been vindicated. Ordinary Palestinians, who are not necessarily affiliated with any political group, now readily point to the "critical situation" in the occupied territories where Israeli occupation and domination have grown more sinister than ever, though indirectly and vicariously.

Indeed, there are many Palestinians today who openly lament the days of the "direct occupation" when the Palestinian cause was still very much alive in the consciousness and conscience of the world.

Moreover, there are many Palestinians, and their number is increasing, who have come to believe that the establishment of Yasser Arafat's autonomous Authority was part of an American-led international conspiracy to liquidate the Palestinian cause. Those who advocate this viewpoint that the United States, Israel and the donor countries are well aware of the extremely "morbid and corrupt nature" of the Palestinian Authority, adamantly refuse to do anything that might hurt the Chairman. This, they contend, shows that "the whole thing amounts to a grand conspiracy to liquidate the Palestinian cause by perpetuating the Israeli occupation in connivance with the Palestinian Authority itself."

American study

 

Demonizing Islamic Revivalism: The Jewish

Lobby's Impact on U.S. Foreign Policy

 

This invaluable study is authored by Dr. Ahmad Yousef, the Director of The United Association for Studies and Research (UASR); and Mr. Terry Rauch, the President of The Multicultural Alliance, Washington. The Palestine Times will publish it in parts.

 

Part 1

Introduction

The topic covered in this paper is finding an audience, not only among academicians and politicians, but also among everyday Americans who have maintained an interest in the processes of democracy.

Increasingly the American public is becoming sensitive to the power that money is exacting over American ideals, as well as to special interest dominance over the collective American interest.

The authors of this paper break new and thought provoking ground. We are all hesitant, (and rightly so) to identify phenomena by racial, class, or gender definitions but these authors have done exactly that, identifying AIPAC and the Near East Policy Institute as the guardians and protectors of Israeli interest in both the United States and Israel. The authors speak to the enormous impact that these institutions have on American domestic and foreign policy. They were not intimidated by the fear of being labeled 'anti-Semitic' and did a good job of handling delicate issues tastefully and honestly. Perhaps their courage will embolden others to do the same.

It should not be considered anti-Semitic or racist to subject AIPAC and the Near East Policy Institute to scrutiny, or to have and share an opinion as to how their activities are affecting America. The very existence of the term anti-Semitic suggest the serious nature of the political phenomenon that these authors seek to explore. Racism, which refers to the act of hating, discriminating against, or targeting an individual or group based on racial background or ethnicity, seems a sufficient word to identify the experience of any individual or group that may find itself subjected to such treatment. No form of racism need be accepted as more serious than any other, and neither should any type of racism be ignored.

It is important to point out that these institutions are not the focus of these authors analysis because they are Jewish, nor because their activities seem to be directed exclusively toward the good of Israel, even sometimes at American expense. These institutions are the subjects of analysis because they are effective. They are good at what they do.

 

The Effect on American Policy

Domestic politics in the United States can be seen as a continuous process of varying constituencies competing to persuade the formal institutions to reflect their interests and objectives in the policies of the nation. Lobbying organizations are often described as informal political institutions, because although they are not mentioned in the Constitution as official bodies, therefore their activities have profound affects on the lives of American citizens and others. An understanding of their role in the system is essential for the proper appreciation of policy making. Political lobbies are organized attempts to influence government policy. Their role, and particularly that of the leaders of the most important ones, continues to be an issue of intense debate among political scientists who seek to examine where power lies in the American system.

This subject is of special interest to those who wish to study U.S. foreign policy in the Middle East. Lobbying efforts in this area are overwhelmingly Jewish-based and pro-Israeli in orientation. Is this a problem? To some, it undoubtedly is not, but to others who see a grave danger in forming American Middle East policy so exclusively towards a particular objective, namely Israel right or wrong, this is most certainly a time of serious problems. This paper details a few of the obstacles faced by our government concerning its policy toward the Middle East and in particular toward the world's Islamic revival.

Before setting forth on this topic, it is important to note that anti-Semitic attitudes, statements, or literature can only be seen as wrong, pure and simple. Hatred of peoples, or discrimination against them on the basis of nationality, ethnicity, gender, race, or religion must be scorned, yet at the same time we must not shy away from the realities of the world in which we live. As Americans or individuals living legally in America, we have rights. One of those rights is the right of representation. When a country reaches a point where a small minority population with an iron solid stance has secured a hold on government policy, that country restricts itself to one of two outcomes. The first is the oppression, misrepresentation, or silencing of different peoples within that country who might disagree with them. The other outcome can, once recognized, evolve from the first. This outcome is the revolt or lashing out of the various other peoples in the country. America is currently deep within the first outcome.

The most powerful officially listed lobby on Capitol Hill is the A.I.P.A.C. (America-Israel Public Affairs Committee). Its parallel effort, directed at the executive branch of government and the Washington policy elite, is at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy. The Washington Institute, as it is often called, is a pro-Israeli institution that is something between a think tank and a sophisticated propaganda apparatus. Its most important function by far is its role as an elite influence apparatus for Israel.

The Washington Institute grew directly out of AIPAC, having been established initially as the organization's research branch. Since its early origins, the influence of the Washington Institute for Near East Policy has burgeoned uncontrollably, forming in essence what has become a training ground and revolving door for pro-Israeli figures to obtain some of the most powerful policy making positions in the national security apparatus of the United States government. Just as the AIPAC has managed to place its most extreme advocates in leading positions on congressional and senatorial committees responsible for foreign affairs and appropriations, so has its executive branch parallel, the Washington Institute, managed to propel, essentially overnight, its own officers - and other staunch advocates for whom Israel is top-priority into key positions in the Clinton Administration's foreign, defense, and intelligence sectors, especially offices with Middle East responsibilities. This influence at the executive level was virtually unknown before the advent of President Clinton's first administration. Now that it has been well-established, however, it will probably have to be taken for granted as a permanent fixture on the Washington political landscape.

It was Clinton, whom Israelis laud as their best friend ever to occupy the White House, who upon taking office appointed the head of the Washington Institute, Dr. Martin Indyk, to be the Director of Middle East Affairs at the National Security Council. Since Dr. Indyk, who is Jewish, was not a citizen of the United States, but rather was of Australian nationality, the citizenship process was speeded through so he could assume this post in time for the inauguration. As it turned out, Martin Indyk became a citizen of the United States of America a mere twelve days before taking office. And it is he who is responsible for formulating the doctrine of dual containment toward Iraq and Iran, with the more recent indirect additions of Libya and Sudan, and by extension, Islamist movements such as Hamas and the Islamist trend in general. Dual containment, although now coming under increasing criticism from the Europeans and from some of the non-Israeli sectors of the American foreign policy elite, remains official Clinton Administration Mideast policy.

Next to Martin Indyk, who has since been removed from his post and sent to Tel Aviv as Israel's U.S. Ambassador, is Dennis Ross, who is equal in power to what Indyk was before. Bypassing the normal State Department structure and its career officials, who were deemed not sufficiently committed to Israel's interests, the Administration appointed Ross, who is also Jewish, as the responsible official for America's entire involvement in the Middle East peace process. And despite repeated accusations by his Palestinian interlocutors that he is not an impartial mediator, he has remained in this important position. The impartiality of his deputy, Aaron Miller, who is also Jewish, is equally compromised when it comes to matters involving Israel.

Although it is true that Ross was held over from the Bush Administration, he was not the primary actor dealing with the Arab-Israeli negotiations; that honor fell to James Baker, the Secretary of State, who was normally responsible for all areas of American foreign policy. Baker was an impartial mediator, which is precisely why he was detested by the Israelis. Between administrations, before the appointment of Ross to head up Clinton's Middle East peace process team, Ross had been slated to take over as the head of the Washington Institute for Near East Policy.

Other friends of Israel in the Clinton Administration, some of whom are more extreme in their pro-Israeli views than others, have included Sandy Berger, a left-of-Labor type who is now the National Security Adviser, as well as the two previous CIA directors, James Woolsey and especially John Deutch, whose views are thought to be more extreme and who is thought to have close relatives in Israel. Now we have Madeleine Albright, the new Secretary of State, who has turned out to be also of Jewish origin and whose views are probably the most extreme of the three. It remains to be seen whether she will be taking over any major portion of the Middle East policy-setting responsibilities previously exercised almost exclusively by Dennis Ross. Also it is likely that Martin Indyk might be brought back from Tel Aviv to take up a new post as Assistant Secretary of State, probably with Middle East responsibilities.

Defending Our Honour

By Lena Winfrey Sayyed

America, Europe, Middle Eastern and other countries need to reevaluate the friendliness and treaties they have with Israel in light of how the Israelis have recently insulted their religions. How can you be friends with someone who ridicules your religion? Doesn't it make you angry? Where's our sense of honor for the love of God?

Recently in the West Bank city of Hebron, the Jewish soldiers have closed the city and they and the Jewish settlers have not only been fighting the Palestinians, but also they have issued leaflets to insult both Islam and Christianity. One was of a pig wearing a hata (scarf-like hat) with the name "Muhammad" written under it, and it is sitting across a book representing the Qur'an. They also took the representation of Mary, the Mother of Jesus, and placed her head onto the body of a cow. Then, they entered a building and ripped up copies of the Qur'an as well as painting insulting graffiti on the city's walls. Not only Palestinians should be angry, but also every Muslim and Christian throughout the whole world.

The Muslims received a double-slap because we also respect the Virgin Mary. Muslims consider her one of the five most pious and best women to have ever lived. Muslims also highly respect Jesus. One chapter in the Qur'an is named "Maryam".

Last Request

By E.Yaghi

Nadene didn't look like a heroine. She didn't even look like she possessed strong personality. She hadn't much courage nor did she own a drop of malice. She was slight of figure and her hazel eyes were enchanting and soothing, her face comforting and attractive. But she certainly did not fit the role of a typical heroine.

What makes then, a man, woman, or even a child a hero? True, at that certain moment that demands courage, adrenaline is pumped into the bloodstream in increased amounts, pushing a person to the limits of physical and/or mental capacity. But what sort of outside factors force an ordinary kind of person to dare the impossible?

As a young girl, Nadene had been timid and gentle. Her older brothers exercised their authority over her and she accepted any abuses with only a

shrug or smile and went about her work. She was compassionate to her younger brothers and sisters and was never known to speak a cruel work to anyone.

The obedient daughter and sister went on to become an obedient wife and had four handsome boys who symbolized her purpose of existence. Her sons grew older and then the Intifada began. One black night, Nadene's husband left without a word of good-bye and stole away to join a guerrilla group. Later, Nadene rec- eived word that he had been captured and was enchained in a dreaded underground Israeli prison. She never heard from him again and spent many silent nights fretting and wondering if he was even alive. She was forced to face life without a husband a father to guide her growing boys. To add to her heavy burden, two of her younger sons were killed by rubber bullets as they threw rocks at heavily armed Israeli soldiers. She now had only two out of her five males left. The meaning of courage had become branded in her heart.

One day, while she was sitting in her parents' house with her father, she said, "Papa, do you remember the time during an Israeli search when I was, so afraid of the soldiers that I hid in the chicken coop?" She smiled faintly and waited for his reaction.

"Yes, how could I forget, you were still a teenager then and tried to cram yourself into a place that a smaller child would not have thought of fitting into. No one would have believed it possible that you could be there, but when we went looking for you, that's where we found you."

He broke out in laughter and his gray eyes twinkled. She bathed in his mirth and for a few minutes they both forgot the present. But soon she remembered her missing husband and her two dead sons and her laughter died too.

Her father's warm eyes seemed to look deep into hers and penetrate her heart to comprehend her feelings. "Nadene, why don't you stay with us for a while? It would do you good. We'd love to have you and the boys with us for a change. You look so thin, so empty."

Her countenance grew pensive and she searched his eyes for reassurance and replied, "Perhaps, I will, Papa. Yes, I think that's a good idea. Maybe you could convince your grandsons to stay off the street and give me a break."

She returned to her home and packed some belongings, gathered her two sons and returned to her father's house where she found temporary peace and contentment. She began to wonder how she could bear to go back to her desolate house.

A week passed. Nadene sat in the kitchen with her mother. They were both stuffing zucchini when Lathe, the younger of her two surviving sons stumbled into the doorway. Blood streamed out of his nose and his head was badly bruised. His eyes were almost swollen shut. Nadene gasped when she saw him and her stomach turned. Her heart beat with painful throbs. Her body shook and she grew hysterical. Lathe pleaded with, "Mother, it's nothing! I'm all right but the Israelis took Hussein!"

Nadene's scream punctured the air. Great tears rolled down her stricken face and she became paralyzed with fear. Her elderly mother grabbed the boy's arm and sat him down. She went to get some cotton and alcohol to clean his wounds. She didn't hear her mother's comforting words, "You'll be OK soon. You'll have some more scars to add to those you already have. Something else to brag about to your friends."

His mother's face remained frozen and unresponsive as the grandmother continued, "I'll send for a doctor to come and see your mother. I think she's in shock. Tell me about your brother Hussein. What happened to him?"

"We were burning some tires and throwing rocks at the troops. Then a soldier caught me and started beating me with his rifle butt and kicked me hard with his boots. He hit me so much I stopped knowing what was going on, but some of the Shebab came after him with Hussein and he let go of me. I ran away with my rescuers and each of us hid in a different house. They didn't find me, but later some of the guys with us said that the soldiers found Hussein and took him prisoner. They last saw him in a police van. When it was safe, I came back here."

Nadene's tears continued to roll down her pale cheeks, but her eyes saw nothing and she responded to no one. The doctor came and gave her frozen form a sedative and at last she slept fitfully. Not much later, she got up, moaned and ranted and began to pace back and forth like a caged animal. As daylight slowly formed, she returned to a statue-like state. For days, no word came concerning her son. Her eyes grew hollow. Her thin body grew thinner and she became like a wild lioness whose cub was endangered. She couldn't look at Lathe's swollen eyes and bruised body. Anger glinted in her eyes replacing the softness that once had shone there. A harsh mask covered her benevolent face and her lips tautened into thin lines.

At last, one cold winter morning, she put on her only thobe and a faded decorated head covering and slipped out of her father's house. Purple storm clouds gathered over her village and strong winds seemed to push them to some other destination. A white pigeon beat the sky with the pumping of its wings on a solo flight. A hawk swept the area searching for food and the white pigeon disappeared in fright. The air was misty and apparently empathized with the mist in Nadene's eyes. She swallowed an icy breath, set her head straight and briskly walked down the dusty street. Eventually, she caught sight of her target. She touched the cold sharp metal huddled insider her thobe and moved on hypnotically.

She focused on her target of three soldiers who stood non-chalantly talking

and laughing. They had noticed her but she wasn't a threat. Their backs were turned as they searched the street for trouble. Quietly, as a cat ready to stalk its prey, she stole up behind the armed men, tearing her dagger from its place and quickly plunged it into the back of one soldier. He yelled. With an unknown strength, she withdrew the dagger and struck the soldier beside him before he could really grasp what was happening. The blood of the two soldiers penetrated their uniforms and they fell to the ground while the frantic woman approached yet the third soldier who fought her daggered hand. A shot rang through the confusion and struck the crazed woman in her head. She collapsed in death. Her crimson dagger quivered and lay beside her in stunned silence.

Somehow Nadene had reached down and found the courage she never had to have to strike out as few would dare. The gentle doe who was maddened by the deaths of her menfolk and the imprisonment of her son and husband, ceased to suffer. She was buried a martyr in her blood-soaked clothing. Now she lives a new free life for martyrs never die but live forever somewhere where there is no fear, hate or evil. Somewhere over the rainbow in the land of Heaven.

When Nadene died, she ascended to heaven and appeared as a torch and met her Great Creator and stood under his throne. She was asked, "What is your last request?"

Nadene answered, I have no request. I am satisfied with what I have." She was asked a second and third time the same question so Nadene bravely replied as a true heroine, "My only and last request is to go back to earth and fight and die again so I can be a martyr again and again!"

A Christian Perception of Islam*

The Struggle For Dialogue And Peace

Muslims are called by the Qura'an and the example of the Prophet of Islam to strive for peace through all available means. The very terms "Islam" derives from the root word whose basic meaning is "peace." This concept of peace defines the world view of Islam, but more importantly it permeates the daily lives of Muslims. Thus each time two Muslims meet, they greet each other with the words "Assalamu Alaikum" "peace be on you," and "Wa-alaikumu-salam" "peace be on you (also)." The idea to be at peace with God, oneself, our fellow human beings and the world of nature is the Islamic way of life.

In Qura'anic terms, peace does not only mean "absence of war", rather it is a positive state of security in which one is free from anxiety or fear. It is a state that entails both submission or "self surrender to God" (Islam) and the "true faith in God" (Iman). References are made to this concept, directly or indirectly, on every page of the Qura'an through the many derivatives of the root S-L-M and A-M-N from which Islam and Iman are derived respectively.

"Peace" is also an integral part of two of Allah's names As-Salam and Al Mumin mentioned in the Qura'an. According to the Holy Qura'an peace on earth (which is the precondition of peace in heaven) is the result of living according to the will of God. The Qura'anic ideal is not easy to achieve in the world we live in because it entails not simply the desire to end violence and war as means of conflict-resolution, but rather the commitment of "doing what God approves." However, from the Qura'anic perspective, achieving peace either here and now or in the hereafter is not meant to be easy.

 

Jihad

Peace is dependent upon Jihad Fi Sabil-Lah, or striving in the cause of God. It is most unfortunate that Jihad, which is the means whereby God's vision of a peaceful world can come to be, is often identified in the mind of many Muslims and non-Muslims with mere destruction. Jihad is often translated as "holy war," a characterization which does not however fully or deeply reflect the essence of the term. In my understanding the concept of Jihad is more meaningfully translated as "holy peace." the word Jihad comes from the verbal noun of the Arabic verb Jahada, meaning "to endeavor," "to strive" or "to struggle."

In a religious context, it can mean to struggle against one's evil inclination or toward the moral uplift of society or for the spread of Islam. Islam can be spread peacefully with the "Jihad of the pen," according to Surah 16-125 of the Qura'an.

"Call thou to the way of the Lord with wisdom and admonition, and dispute with them in the better way."

But the use of force is also possible namely with the "Jihad of the sword" according to the Surah 2:193 of the Qura'an.

"And fight them on until there is no more tumult or oppression, and there prevail justice and faith in God, but if they cease, let there be no hostility except to those who practice oppression."

 

Holy Peace

Unfortunately, this possibility of Jihad through the use of "sword" has been overemphasized by Muslims for political and material reasons, and by non-Muslims in order to generate a feeling of revulsion against Muslims for their own interests, which are far from religious in nature.

Both Muslims and non-Muslims easily forget the meaning of Jihad as the struggle against one's evil inclinations and as one's efforts towards the moral uplifting of society. Among pious Muslims and mystics, it is spiritual and moral Jihad which is emphasized. This is called greater Jihad in comparative terms, in relation to the lesser Jihad, the more popular concept of Jihad, which is the "Holy War," as stated in the following tradition (Haidth" of the Prophet Muhammed:

"Once, having returned from one of his campaigns, the Prophet said: 'We have now returned from the lesser Jihad' "

The tradition adds that the companions asked: "What is the greater Jihad?" and the Prophet answered: "The struggle against one's evil inclination." This is the deeper and more personal aspect of Jihad, which by its very is an inner struggle for "holy peace," as opposed to the outer physical struggle for "holy war." To attain real peace one first must undergo a a real struggle that begins from within.

 

Dialogue

On this level, dialogue can be a channel of peace if it encourages an internal struggle that purifies the hearts of men and women, preparing the spirit for the great dialogue with God, oneself and with people. This great dialogue cannot be possible without a great struggle. Through this process, we may achieve the "great conversion" of the heart. This is not related to the conversion from one's religion to another, but to the conversion of ourselves to God. As Rumi, one of the great sufi poets of Islam stated:

Jihad can only be waged by constant waging of the greater Jihad; without spiritual, ethical content, the jihad becomes an instrument for ego and rebellious masquerading in the hypocritical soul as fighters for the sake of God.

This emphasis on the spiritual aspect of Jihad is part of our continuous effort to read in Islam and Christianity elements of relevance to a deeper dialogue between Muslims and Christians. According to the Qura'an, Muslims have the right to defend themselves against injustice and the duty to protect the weak from injustice (Sura 2:190-193; 217-; 4:75-78; 22:39-40) But they are reminded over and over again that the "limits said by God" (Hudud Allah) are not to be transgressed at any time, and that injustice must be accorded to the enemy. Furthermore, any initiative toward peace taken by an enemy must be accepted and responded to in good faith and with good will.

The religion of Islam and Christianity teach moral fortitude and enjoin each member to exercise justice. It is within this commandment that Jihad or "holy war" has to be understood under the present circumstances.

It was a vote of 131 countries in favour, 3 countries against (including the U.S. and Israel) and 14 abstained regarding the resolution taken by the United Nations General Assembly urging the International Community to work effectively so as to stop the construction of Jewish settlements in the occupied territories, and requesting that Israel provide information about the goods being produced and manufactured in those illegal settlements, in an attempt, so it seems, to boycott those goods in the future. The resolution was mild in tone, compared to the draft, and contained no sanctions whatsoever so as to attract as many votes as possible. Secretary-General Mr. Kofi Anan which showed that Israel has not stopped building a new settlement at Jebal Abu Ghneim in East Jerusalem despite

the Assembly's previous demand. The report also stated that the settlement construction had a negative political, economic, demographic and geographic impact and had caused harm to the Middle East peace process. Further, Israel had revoked the residency rights of hundreds of Palestinian Jerusalemites in 1997 leaving them with the status of "resident immigrants" and subjecting them to discriminatory immigration controls.

While referring to the Israeli Prime Minister's promise (given at the same time as construction at Jebal Abu Ghneim proceeded) to build 3,500 housing units for Palestinians in East Jerusalem, the report said "those housing units were not to be built at Jebal Abu Ghneim but in 10 as-yet unspecified neighbourhoods in the Arab East Jerusalem. Further, it was also not clear whether the housing units would be government-funded, or if only building permits would be issued." As for the construction of settlements, the report went on to say that "expansion activities were recorded in more than 30 existing settlement areas."

As for the realization of the principle of territorial integrity, as stated in the Oslo Accords, this principle has been frustrated by Israeli restrictions on the movements of persons and goods between the so-called A, B and C areas of the West Bank and the Gaza Strip and between the occupied territories and the outside world. Moreover, safe passage arrangements have not been established, and arrangements for Gaza seaport and airport have not been agreed upon, the report stated.

Since the Israeli policy of general closure, which has been in effect since 30 March 1993, imposes explicit restrictions on the mobility of goods and persons, the general closure has been accompanied by periodic comprehensive closures. The report also recorded the continued administrative detention of almost 300 Palestinians in Israeli jails, held without charge or trial. Palestinian detainees in Israeli custody continue to be subjected to torture and other ill-treatment under security regulations officially endorsed by the Israeli Supreme Court and the government. It also mentioned that Israeli demolitions of Palestinian houses in Jerusalem and other parts of the occupied territories has continued.

While the report does serve as a valuable document providing proof of Israel's illegal activity, especially with regard to Jerusalem, it brings back to one's mind the report submitted by the previous U.N. Secretary-General Dr. Boutros Ghali on the event of the Israeli bombing last year in Qana, south of Lebanon, wherein dozens of civilians were killed and many more injured, including many U.N. peacekeeping troops, since the Israeli military operation "The Grapes of Wrath" attacked a U.N. base where civilians took shelter. The report blamed Israel and held her fully responsible for the attack. This report was one of the reasons that made Ghali the exception to the rule, since all U.N. Secretary-Generals served two consecutive terms. Dr. Ghali was replaced by Mr. Anan. Now the question that remains is will this report be Anan's mistake that will cause him to lose a second term in his post or will the promised administrative reforms cover up for him?

al-Lajjun Before 1948

The village stood on a slightly elevated hill on the south-western edge of the Marj ibn 'Amir Plain, on the two banks of Wadi al-Lajjun. Jinin as well as the plain were visible from al-Lajjun. It was bordered on the west and south by the Carmel Mountains [Kanaina and Mahamid 1987:44], by Tall al-Mutasallim (to the northeast), and by Tall al-Asmar (to the northwest). The village, which was linked by secondary roads to a highway between Jinin and Haifa and to another highway that led southwest to the town of Umm al-Fahm, lay close to the junction of the two highways.

After the Bar-Kochba Revolt had been suppressed in A.D. 130 the Roman emperor Hadrian ordered a second Roman legion, the Legio VI Ferrata ("Ironsides"), to be stationed in the north of the country. The place where it established its camp was known as Legio. When in the third century the army was removed, Legio became a city and its name was augmented with the adjectival Masimianupolis. This remained its name throughout the Byzantine period. It came under Arab control in the seventh century A.D. during the early days of the Islamic conquest. Al-Lajjun was the site of more than one military confrontation between Muslim factions, such as the one in A.D. 945 between the Hamdanids of Aleppo and the Ikhshidids of Egypt in which the renowned Sayf al-Dawla al-Hamadani was defeated. [D 3/2:164-71] Al-Lajjun was captured by the Crusaders but was taken back by Salah al-Din al-Ayyubi (Saladin) in 1187.

A number of Arab chroniclers and geographers mentioned al-Lajjun over the years, including Ibn al-Faqih (writing in A.D. 903), al-Maqdisi (writing in ca. A.D. 985), and Yaqut al-Hamawi (d. 1228). Al-Maqdisi described it as a pleasant town on the edge of Palestine (when the boundaries between Syria and Palestine were different from the present borders) and mentioned its sources of fresh water. Al-Maqdisi and al-Hamawi (in his Mu'jam) reported the presence of what the people of al-Lajjun called the mosque of Abraham, built over a round rock. But whereas Ibn al-Faqih said that the mosque lay outside al-Lajjun, al-Hamawi reported that it was in the center of the town. [Cited in al-Khalidi 1968:193-94 and in Le Strange 1965:492-93] A number of Muslim kings and prominent persons passed through the village, including al-Kamil, the sixth Ayyubid ruler, who there gave his daughter 'Ashura' in marriage to his nephew in 1231. The village was also the burial site of two Muslim scholars, Ali al-Shafi'i (d. 1310) and 'Ali ibn al- Jalal (d. 1400). [D 3/2:164-711]

In 1596, al-Lajjun was a village in the nahiya of Sha'ra (liwa' of Lajjun), with a population of 226. It paid taxes on a number of crops, including wheat and barley, as well as on other types of property, such as goats, beehives, and water buffalos. [Hut. and Abd.:159] Zahir al-'Umar, who became the effective ruler of northern Palestine for a short period during the second half of the eighteenth century, was reported to have used cannons against al-Lajjun in the course of his campaign (1771-73) to capture Nablus. [Abu Dayya 1986:51 ] It is unclear whether this attack led to the village's decline in the years that followed; when the British consul for Jerusalem and Palestine (1846-62), James Finn, visited the area, he did not see a village on the site. [Finn 1868:229-30] The authors of the Survey of Western Palestine noticed a khan, however, to the south of the ruins of al-Lajjun in the early 1880s. [SWP (1881) 11:64-66]

In the late nineteenth century, villagers from Umm al-Fahm moved to the site of al-Lajjun to make use of its farmland. Gradually, they settled in the village, building their houses around the springs, especially next to the khan. When the massive mound at nearby Tall al-Mutasallim (ancient Megiddo) was excavated by German archaeologists in 1903, some of the inhabitants of al-Lajjun reused stones from the ancient structures that had been unearthed to build new housing. [Fisher 1929:18] More people moved to al-Lajjun from Umm al-Fahm during the British Mandate, and particularly in the late thirties, due to the British government's persecution of the population for their activities in the 1936-39 revolt that had spread throughout Palestine. Al-Lajjun's economy grew rapidly as a result of the influx of additional population. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:7-9]

As the village expanded it was divided into three quarters, one to the east, one to the west, and another known as Khirbat al-Khan. Each quarter was inhabited by one or more hamulas (clans): the al-Mahajina al-Tahta and al-Ghubariyya clans, the al-Jabbarin and al-Mahamid clans, and the al-Mahajina al-Fawqa clan. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:44] In 1931 the population consisted of 829 Muslirns, 26 Christians, and 2 Jews; there is no exact breakdown for the year 1945. In 1943 one of the large landowners in the village financed the construction of a mosque, built of white stone, in the al-Ghubariyya (eastern) quarter. Another mosque was also established in the al-Mahamid quarter during the same period, and was financed by the residents of the quarter themselves. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:44,45] Al-Lajjun also had a school that was founded in 1937 and that had an enrollment of 83 in 1944. It was located in the quarter belonging to the al-Mahajina al-Fawqa clan, that is, in Khirbat al-Khan. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:50]

There was a small market place in the village, as well as six grain mills (powered by the numerous springs and wadis in the vicinity), and a health center. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:44] The various quarters of al-Lajjun had many shops. A bus company was established in al-Lajjun by a villager from Umm al-Fahm; the bus line served Umm al-Fahm, Haifa, and a number of villages, such as Zir'in. In 1937 the line had seven buses. Subsequently the company was licensed to serve Jinin also, and acquired the name of al-Lajjun Bus Company. [Kana'na and Mahamid 1987:48-49] The villagers cultivated grain, vegetables, and citrus fruits. al-Lajjun's archaeological heritage has totally disappeared with little record and no excavation. Most ruins have been bulldozed into a heap to allow planting on the fields.

 

Occupation and Depopulation

The official Israeli account states that al-Lajjun was occupied shortly before I June 1948, following the "clearing" of the Baysan Valley and prior to the (unsuccessful) Israeli attack on Jinin. At that time, the Golani Brigade captured a number of villages in the vicinity of Jinin, including al-Lajjun, which was entered at dawn on 30 May, according to a press report in the New York Times. Several Israeli columns moved towards the Triangle area at this time, taking a cluster of villages north of Jinin. It is possible that the Golani's Fourth Battalion expelled the villagers directly, in keeping with their practice during their previous operation in the Baysan Valley.

Earlier New York Times reports had stated that the village was first attacked and captured in mid-April, during the battle around the settlement of Mishmar ha-'Emeq (see Abu Shusha, Haifa District). The commander of the Arab Liberation Army (ALA) also reported an assault on 13 April, when Jewish forces attempted "to reach the crossroads at Lajjun in an outflanking operation." The attack apparently failed. The Times said that twelve people were killed and fifteen wounded during that offensive. The paper also said that al-Lajjun was occupied a few days later, on 17 April, twelve days after the attack on Mishmar ha-'Emeq had been launched from that village. The account read: "Lajjun is the most important place taken by the Jews, whose offensive has carried them through ten villages south and east of Mishmar Ha'emek." The report added that women and children had been removed from the village and that 27 buildings were blown up by the Haganah in al-Lajjun and other villages nearby.

But Arab military sources imply that the village was not occupied at that time, for ALA commander Fawzi al-Qawuqji states that attacks resumed the following month, on 6 May, when ALA positions in the area of al-Lajjun were attacked by Haganah forces. The ALA's Yarmuk Battalion and other units drove the attackers back, but two days later, the ALA commander reported that Haganah forces were "trying to cut off the Lajjun area from Tulkarm in preparation for seizing Lajjun and Jenin...." In summing up the military situation in Palestine on 8 May, al-Qawuqji reported "great activity" and "fully equipped and armed lHaganah] concentrations north of Jenin" and "heavy fighting in the 'Ara district," i.e., some 12 km southwest of al-Lajjun. It is not clear whether the village of al-Lajjun had come under Haganah control by this time or whether it held out for about three more weeks, as the History of the War of Independence states.

During the second truce, in early September, a United Nations official fixed the permanent truce line in the area at al-Lajjun, according to press reports. A 500-yard area was established on both sides of the line in which Arabs and Jews were allowed to harvest crops. [NYT:15/4/48, 18/4/48, 31/5/48, 1/6/48, 7/ 9148; Q:6-9, 15, 44; T:179]

 

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

In 1949 Israel established the settlement of Yosef Kaplan, which was later renamed Kibbutz Megiddo (167220), about 0.5 km to the northeast of the village site. At the time of its construction the traditional landholdings of a chain of Palestinian villages, stretching between the town of Umm al-Fahm and al-Lajjun, had been amalgamated to form a large bloc. The settlement's proximity to al-Lajjun suggests that it was built on land that had once belonged to al-Lajjun, but this land may have belonged to a neighboring village in earlier times.

The Village Today

Only the white stone mosque, one village mill, the village health center, and a few partially destroyed houses remain on the site. The mosque has been converted into a carpentry workshop and one of the houses has been made into a chicken coop. The health center and grain mill are deserted, and the school is gone. The cemetery remains, but it is in a neglected state; the tomb of Yusuf al-Hamdan, a prominent nationalist who fell in the 1936 revolt, is clearly visible. The surrounding lands are planted with almond trees, wheat, and barley; they also contain animal sheds, a fodder plant, and a pump installed on the spring of 'Ayn al-Haija. The site is tighUy fenced in and entry is blocked.
