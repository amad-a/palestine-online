Declare Oslo is dead!

Musil Shihadeh*

This is the best course of action that the Palestinian leadership should take under the present circumstances. The Palestinian-Israeli negotiations under the so-called Oslo terms is a farce, as the word negotiations should be replaced by dictations. How and in what manner could any Palestinian leader dream of getting a fair outcome when he is negotiating from such a pathetic position of weakness? As long as he thinks so? No one can deny the fact that Israel is doing more damage to the Palestinian aspirations for national objectives under the Oslo agreement than without it. Present settlements are being expanded while new ones are being established, while Palestinian land confiscation is going on at an increased rate. The Judaization of Jerusalem is a daily continuing process, while the prospects for the return of the refugees in conformity with so many United Nations resolutions, starting with resolution 194 in 1949, are being totally ignored and actually replaced by the old Israeli theme of settling them in their present host countries. Let us not talk about future borders of the proposed Palestinian State, because when the Palestinians used to own 93% of Palestine in 1947, they are desperately struggling to retrieve a maximum of 10%. In other words, Israel is still committing the same violations of international laws against the Palestinian rights, if not more, under the Oslo accords than without it. The only difference is that such violations are committed under the pretext of peace negotiations and thus extracting legality under the auspices of Palestinian-Israeli peace negotiations with the Palestinian leadership witnessing and blessing the demise of our national objectives.

Of course the Palestinian leadership would not waste any time in rationalizing their continuance to succumb to Israeli demands by declaring to the Palestinian masses that there are no other alternatives. Do we have other alternatives? Before one should try to answer such an important question, we should study carefully the final objectives of Israel in the area. Israel is determined to establish itself as a normal member of the Middle East community in order to gain economic benefits from its rich neighbours, while gaining a strategic position so as to become the vanguard of Western influence and the benefactor in the area, and finally achieve peace to actuate their theme of the new safe haven for Jews all over the world. In gaining such final legitimacy they need the Palestinian bridge to cross, without which Israel would remain an isolated armed castle with no place to go except within itself. In other words, they are desperate to conclude a peace treaty with the real protagonists, namely the Palestinians, before they proceed further to acquire different kinds of hegemony over their neighbours. This is the strong Palestinian card, which the present leadership is contemplating giving away to the greedy Zionists. From the Zionist point of view there would be no devastation to their objectives that can exceed the death of the Oslo accords. And why not, when they are getting more than what they were asking for through peaceful means than through the status quo that preceded Oslo. Such a route would ensure getting the implicit blessing of the entire Arab world, which would automatically follow the same steps that the Palestinians have followed. No Arab government would place itself in any compromising position when the real protagonists, the Palestinians, have signed a peace treaty with their enemy.

Therefore, our alternative is not to sign such a humiliating peace agreement until Israel realizes our determination to achieve our national objectives. Furthermore, the Palestinian leadership should realize that they cannot sign away the lands of our ancestors, which also belongs to our next generation, without the approval of a public referendum. The PA is definitely exceeding its jurisdiction in deciding our rights. The PA should immediately cease all talks with the Israelis, declare a Palestinian State without borders and leave the final struggle to our next generation. Israel will not annex the West Bank with more than two and half million Palestinians aboard, because it would defeat their own supreme goal of Jewish exclusivity. Annexation means giving the indigenous Palestinians equal human and civil rights not only in conformity with international laws but their own laws. Such annexation would become an Israeli nightmare when half of the Israeli parliament would have Arab representatives. There are those, of course, who claim that Israel might practice such annexation without the indigenous people, either through further mass evictions or disenfranchisement of those who remain. Such a horrible scheme is not to be taken seriously when we realize that such views are espoused by super minorities that follow the Kahana teachings and have failed drastically to gain any political influence.

It is time for the PA to declare that Oslo is dead and no more negotiations with Israel should be contemplated.

Israel’s Weapons of Mass Destruction:

When Will the World Start to Care?

 

 

By Ramzy Baroud

 

Israel’s wealth of nuclear weapons is certainly a known fact to many in the Middle East as well as around the world. Although such a finding was never declared as a result of an extensive UN investigation, such as the one conducted for years to track alleged Iraqi weapons, an abundance of evidence and signs give such a claim all that is needed. However, little if any enthusiasm was ever shown to confront Israel’s ceaseless pursuit of more weapons of mass destruction which have truly made it the real threat to the future and peace in the Middle East and probably the world.

The exposed Israeli secret is brought up now and then as a result of a leak of information or the courage of an Arab State daring to challenge the world and especially the West’s indifference and silence. Egypt was the first to revive recent criticism of the Israeli nuclear programme. Ossama al-Baz, an advisor to President Hosni Mubarak, predicted in an Egyptian television interview that “the double-standard policy of the U.S. regarding Israel’s nuclear arms will change after the implementation of peace with all the Arab parties.” He explained that “Israel will then be incapable of repeating that it is a small State surrounded by powerful and aggressive States.”

Al-Baz’s statement was timely, coinciding with Mubarak’s acceptance of his country’s participation in the multilateral Middle East peace talks held in Moscow in early February. Earlier talks were doomed to failure and were frozen for three and a half years as a result of Israel’s refusal to sign the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT). Later on, it arrogantly refused to submit its nuclear facilities to inspections by the International Atomic Energy Agency.

Egypt’s campaign was followed by an Israeli parliamentary debate regarding Israel’s top secret nuclear weapons wherein Arab MP Issam Makhoul warned the world of up to 300 nuclear warheads currently possessed by Israel. While right-wing Jewish members tried desperately to distract Makhoul from carrying out his speech, the determined Arab member managed to get his message through. “All the world knows that Israel has a vast warehouse of atomic, biological and chemical weapons which serve as the anchor for the Middle East arms race.”

It is Shimon Peres, the then-Prime Minister of Israel, who is credited with advancing Israel’s nuclear weapons programme, whose existence is no longer a secret, at least for those who bother to listen to Israel’s own leaders’ remarks once confronted with a bold question. The fact that those leaders are quite careless when speaking of such a subject shows the level of confidence which Israel has in terms of its “special status” in the eyes of the United States and thereafter the United Nations.

Last November, the High Israeli Court permitted the publishing and distribution of the closed-door trial of Mordechai Vanunu (the Israeli nuclear technician who challenged the programme’s secrecy and revealed astonishing findings to the London Sunday Times about Israel’s nuclear capabilities) held in Israel in 1986 after Israeli Mossad agents kidnapped him and shipped him to Israel.

Shimon Peres, who is now perceived as a promoter of peaceful reconciliation between Israel and its Arab neighbours, was angered by the November court decision. He told the court that his revelations “caused serious damage to Israel and their publication led certain Arab countries to harden their stance to our disadvantage.” It is evident from the prominent Israeli leader’s declaration that the revealing of such documents will make “certain Arabs” able to confront Israel with its long-ignored weapons of mass destruction.

When the Iraqi President’s son-in-law Hussein Kamal fled to Amman and uttered to the press that Iraq is still capable of manufacturing nuclear warheads, the United Nations was outraged (notice that Iraq was alleged to have the capability of producing weapons of mass destruction and has not yet obtained these weapons). An additional team was sent to Baghdad to investigate the findings, and the proud United States strengthened its arguments and therefore lengthened its sanctions. Everyone had a reason to believe that Iraq is truly a threat to its neighbours. In Vanunu’s case, however, the shock quickly faded and the fate of the former Israeli scientist who dared to speak is an occasional theme embraced by human rights groups every now and then.

In Israel’s case, speaking out against Israel’s violation of international laws and regulations and its threat to the safety of its neighbours is not enough to pressure the Jewish State into changing its policy. If you simply find those who have the courage to speak, will you find those with the courage to listen or to act? Despite all of the clear signs exposing Israel’s weapons programme with massive destruction capabilities, strangely enough, Israel is maintaining its image as the victim rather than the bully. It is yet another episode in a long series of Israel’s influence in a world that acts according to a previously set agenda despite the ethical values that most countries preach. And it is that influence that helped Israel avoid the consequences of its greatest crimes, while others are being punished for crimes for which they were never even indicted.

Peace without normalization

By Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Syria’s decision to attend the 1991 Madrid Conference marked the end of one chapter and the beginning of another in its thorny relations with the United States. The previous 35 years were overshadowed by an array of attitudes ranging from mistrust, in the best of times, to undisguised hostility in the worst. Reviled and blacklisted by America among the pariah States of the world, Damascus has, nonetheless, remained a key address on the road to Middle East peace. Thus, when the Shepherdstown negotiators failed to sign an agreement based on the Madrid formula—land for peace—the region seemed destined for yet another round of bloodletting.

Apart from the few set pieces choreographed for the cameras, nothing that transpired in Shepherdstown reflected movement on the Syrian-Israeli track. Worse still, there were no gestures of goodwill from any of the parties. While Washington refused even to consider the removal of Syria from its list of countries that “sponsor terrorism”, Israel, likewise, spurned the notion of discussing its withdrawal from the occupied Golan Heights. Yet, as is always the case, it was the Arab side which, for whatever reasons, chose to concede. For even while sticking to its own position that there could be no progress toward peace before Israel committed itself to a timetable of withdrawal, Syria, nevertheless, dropped one of its key demands—that Mr. Barak honour the commitments given by former Prime Minister Rabin in previous negotiations.

The full significance of this latest hot-and-cold in Syrian-Israeli negotiations can, perhaps, best be understood in the light of Israel’s final status talks with the Palestinian Authority. Coinciding as it did with the deadlocked Palestinian-Israeli talks, the Shepherdstown encounter inevitably fuelled much speculation. Many interpreted it as a tactical manoeuvre intended to force the hand of the Palestinian leadership. One view actually asserted that a major breakthrough with Syria will not only reduce the threat of war, but would also enable Israel to avoid conceding fully to the Palestinians. And, in the final analysis, that the Palestinian issue would become more of a local dispute rather than a regional or international problem.

As the secret initiatives and exchanges continue behind the scenes, tension is fast rising on the streets. Recurrent calls by senior Western officials for both sides, Israelis and Palestinians, to make hard decisions have further complicated matters, particularly at this point when the future of Jerusalem and 5 million refugees is being discussed. All things considered, neither the return of the Golan Heights, the West Bank or even a withdrawal from southern Lebanon would create the normalization that Israel so desperately needs. This is because the rights of the people who suffered most throughout the conflict are still not recognized.

Peace between rulers
Speaking at the recently concluded meeting of Foreign Ministers of the Multilateral Steering Group of the Middle East Peace Process in Moscow (1 February 2000), America’s Secretary of State tried to gloss over affairs and give the most promising assessment of the current situation. To illustrate her point, she announced that the “Palestinian refugees are being trained in advanced computer technologies.” How many refugees are being trained and where was this important education conducted still remain a mystery. For while the suave Ms. Albright was speaking, Palestinian officials in the West Bank were sounding the alarm of an imminent eruption in the camps. The immediate, though not the only cause poised to shatter their patience is the excessive cuts by UNRWA of workers’ wages as well as social and relief services. Under the circumstances, one wonders what good would advanced computer technology serve after the refugees were overtaken by chronic illnesses and disease caused by rampant malnutrition and the conversion of their camps into veritable garbage dumps following the walk-off by sanitation workers whose wages were cut by the Agency.

Ephraim Halivi, head of the Israeli external intelligence agency, Mossad, significantly, presented a more realistic portrait of the current situation. He bluntly told a meeting of Israeli diplomats stationed in Arab countries that a comprehensive peace will not be attained at this present stage because all the agreements signed to date are simply arrangements between individual Arab rulers and the Israelis. This growing realization within Israel has, consequently, led the country’s foreign ministry to take a step backward and terminate the use of the term ‘normalization’ in its internal documents which describe relations with Arab States. Since its adoption after the Camp David Treaty, the term has failed to mirror the true nature of relations between Israel and its neighbours. As a result, an internal memo recently circulated to Israeli foreign ministry personnel called for the term ‘normalization’ to be replaced by the phrase ‘good neighbourliness.’

Despite the outward pretenses of change, the State Department has also come to the same realization, albeit privately, that the term ‘normalization’ has proven to be utterly obsolete and meaningless in the context of Middle East peace. Hence the working paper presented to the Israelis and Syrians in Shepherdstown spoke only of ‘good neighbourliness’ and not ‘normalization’. After 20 years of self-deception, the White House, it appears, has finally recognized that to the vast majority of Arabs, its magic word ‘normalization’ is seen as synonymous with Israeli greed, expansion and hegemony.

Syria and the refugees
Far away from Shepherdstown, the Syrian-Israeli conflict continues to wreak havoc not on the Golan Heights but in neighbouring Lebanon. There, the innocent victims are Lebanese civilians and Palestinian refugees. Ever since the slaughter of 3,000 refugees in Tel el-Za’atar in 1976, the Palestinians have suffered untold human losses in Lebanon. For their part, Israeli leaders have generally held the view that if Syria is weakened militarily and even expelled from Lebanon, they would be able to complete the imposition of a regional peace based on their terms. Itamar Rabinovich, Israel’s former Ambassador to Washington—now chief negotiator with Syria—confirmed this thinking when he was asked by IDF radio whether the main address for the 1996 Qana massacre was Lebanon. He replied, “I think the effective address is Syria.”

With respect to the refugee question, Syria has from the beginning and throughout the Madrid process maintained its support for Palestinian repatriation. Today, the Palestinians in Syria (465,662) constitute 10.7% of the total number of refugees registered with UNRWA. Since 1956, the authorities in Damascus have legislated a number of laws that facilitated their absorption into the economic and social life of the country. Hence, with the exception of the right to vote in parliamentary elections, they have enjoyed the same civil rights as their Syrian counterparts.

Quite bizarrely though, the absence of social discrimination against the Palestinians in Syria has prompted certain elements within the Israeli establishment to suggest that Syria would be a good option to resettle some of the refugees. To preempt such an eventuality, Syria enforced a number of administrative measures. Foremost among these has been the introduction of entry restrictions on Palestinians with Egyptian, Jordanian and Iraqi travel documents. In Lebanon, the Syrian response was further accentuated by the enforcement of a cordon sanitaire around the camps, notably Ain el-Hilweh, the largest in southern Lebanon. The previous overtures by President Lahud and Prime Minister Hoss to accommodate a limited renewal of Fatah influence in the camps were suddenly suppressed after the Barak administration stepped up its diplomatic campaigns in the region.

Though agreeing to attend the Shepherdstown talks, Syria has yet refused to participate in the Multilateral Working Group. In explaining their boycott of the Moscow meeting—the first in eight years—the Syrians argued that participation would only bring the Jewish State closer to its objective of normalization and acceptance within the regional system. And this would, furthermore, amount to an undeserved reward for the State that has relentlessly pushed the region closer to war by its occupation of Arab lands and attacks upon their civilian populations. Accordingly, the Syrian position remains one of denying Israel the strategic, political and economic advantages it seeks from peace until such time that it withdraws completely from Arab territories and restores the usurped rights of their peoples. On the question of Palestinian national rights, observers note with deep misgivings that Prime Minister Barak still refuses to speak of a “Palestinian State”, choosing instead to describe it always as the “Palestinian entity.”

Negotiating peace in the arms bazaar
As it appears, Zionist leaders tend very often to ignore causes and emphasize only effects in their relations with non-Jews. Mr. Barak is no exception. He believes theirs is a conflict with the Arab countries more than it is with the Palestinians. Hence, in the run up to Shepherdstown, he declared that United Nations resolution 242 applied only to the Arab States and not to the Palestinians. Rightly or wrongly, the truth is the Jewish State has spent the entire last 50 years confronting its neighbours. Ruled as it is by retired and serving army officers since its creation, Israel cannot, therefore, continue much longer along this course if it is to develop and achieve genuine economic independence.

For the moment, though, it must carry on milking the West. The peace process, like the holocaust, has proven to be an exceptionally lucrative enterprise. Negotiating venues have thus become bazaars where the merchants of peace sell state-of-the-art weapons. All in the name of security, diplomats are urged to devise plans to subjugate the weak and empower the strong. Mossad’s chief, Ephraim Halivi, confirmed this when he informed Israeli diplomats that as long as a broad peace between the peoples of the region is not attained, the basic guarantee of peace would rest in the strengthening of Israel’s military power.

Having lost at the negotiating table more than it ever did on the battlefield, the PLO must now double its efforts to safeguard the rights of the refugees. Increased cooperation with the host countries is both the natural way and necessary means toward this end. Their adoption has become all the more imperative in the wake of Israel’s strategy to localize and diffuse the refugee problem through separate agreements with Syria, Jordan and Lebanon and thereby escapes its international obligations. By design, all the conventions on refugee issues codified since 1940 emphasize the principle of repatriation. They have been used to resolve refugee problems in Vietnam, El Salvador, Guatemala, Rwanda, Bosnia and Kosovo. On what basis, therefore, must the Palestinians be denied and coerced to relinquish their right of return?!

*The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and editor of its Return Review. The PRC’s website is at www.prc.org.uk

On Resolution 181 and 194

Such rights are not negotiable since their legality stems from all international conferences, including the very Charter of the UN, which has accepted Israel’s membership into the World Body based on its commitment to abide by all the items of the UN Charter.

The Palestinian sacred right to return then is not subject to public polls, or negotiations, but should be immediately implemented in conformity with international law. Israel’s rejection to abide by international law should prompt the Palestinian leadership to immediately cease all contacts and negotiations with the Zionist regime and seek other alternatives that would force Israel to accept international legality.

The Palestinian storm that erupted over French Prime Minister Lionel Jospin's anti-Hezbollah comments caught the Palestinian Authority's leadership by surprise. They had not anticipated such a mass outburst of anger.What did Jospin actually do to warrant such a lashing, be it the stone-pelting at Bir Zeit University, or the raging demonstrations at other universities on the West Bank? Jospin has never been considered an enemy of the Palestinians. For years he has supported the Palestinian right to statehood, and he reiterated this commitment during a news conference in Jerusalem. When the French prime minister arrived in Gaza, he spoke to Yasser Arafat about how he had supported the Palestinian leader during the Lebanon War, and throughout the siege on Beirut. Speaking to reporters in Gaza, Jospin recalled, "I was a friend of Arafat's, and I visited him when nobody wanted him as a friend."

Yet Jospin and his Palestinian hosts both made the same fatal mistake. They did not grasp the degree to which support for Hezbollah has soared recently among Arab masses in general, and among Palestinians in particular. In retrospect, it appears that by uttering two sentences characterizing Hezbollah fighters as terrorists, Jospin trampled on sacred ground.

Palestinian admiration for Hezbollah today can be compared to Palestinian veneration a decade ago for Iraq President Saddam Hussein. As in Hussein's case ten years ago, Hezbollah has emerged today as the only force in the Arab World which is brazenly defying Israel. Hezbollah Shi'ite fighters in South Lebanon have come to symbolize Arab pride, their status enhanced when compared to the hapless PA leadership or the images of humiliated Arab leaders being compelled to submit to Washington's bidding. Hezbollah has shown the world that a willingness to sacrifice, determination, courage and belief are potent antidotes to past failure.

This week, the principal of a Ramallah high school described how students worship Hezbollah fighters and consider them quasi-divine. As the students see it, a few dozen devout Muslim warriors have been making a laughing-stock out of the Israel Defense Forces. Raising the standard of Jihad, Hezbollah has foiled the Middle East's strongest army, whose frightened soldiers are howling with fear, begging to be taken out of Lebanon. And, as the Palestinians see it, Hezbollah has displayed high moral scruples, abiding by standards of "purity of arms." Hezbollah confines its attacks to enemy soldiers, documenting its strikes with television footage which inspires awe. This is a perception of heroism commanding respect.

Then, along comes Jospin, branding Hezbollah fighters as terrorists. How could he say such a thing? This question has been posed throughout the West Bank and the Gaza Strip this week. The Bir Zeit outburst against Jospin was spontaneous, as were mass demonstrations staged in Hebron, Bethlehem, Nablus and Ramallah. Similar protests, which featured the burning of pictures of Jospin and of Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy, flared as well in Lebanon.

Meanwhile, Arab television networks showed Hezbollah leaders saluting the students at Bir Zeit University.

Aware that his remarks at the Jerusalem press conference last Thursday had unwittingly prompted a surge of rage, Jospin arrived at Bir Zeit two days later and tried to control the damage in his lecture at the law faculty. He told his listeners that France denounces the Israeli conquest in southern Lebanon. Such backtracking proved to be of little avail.

Students waited for the prime minister outside the lecture hall, distributing circulars accusing Jospin of "spilling the blood of Lebanese children" to advance his own political ambitions in France. Similarly, Palestinian newspapers hinted that Jospin had directed his anti-Hezbollah remarks at powerful world Jewish lobbies, with the aim of raising his electoral prospects in France.

After the stone-throwing incident, Arafat launched some damage-control of his own to try and patch up things with Jospin. The Palestinian leader summoned Bir Zeit University President Hanna Nasser and the two, together, apologized to the French prime minister. Other Palestinian spokesmen denounced the rock-throwing episode at Bir Zeit, but they used cautious language. Being too vehement would have been impolitic.

It is easy to see which way the winds are blowing in Gaza and the West Bank. Few Palestinians seemed particularly upset about the attack against Jospin, many among the Palestinian public even praised the student attackers. PA council members passed a resolution condemning Jospin's comments, and upholding Lebanese resistance to the Israeli conquest as being legitimate.

Arafat struck the same chord. Jibril Rajoub, the head of the PA preventive security service on the West Bank, rejected Jospin's remarks openly, deriding them for failing to draw a distinction between conquerors and victims in South Lebanon.

Yet more strident were spokesmen from Hamas and left-wing Palestinian opposition groups. "We are certain that the way to Palestine, and the way to liberate Lebanon and the Golan, is to use Hezbollah's method, the method of resistance," declared a defiant petition circulated two weeks ago by prominent left-wing Palestinians, including former Nablus Mayor Bassam Al-Shaka, and Dr. Khaider Abad Al-Shafi.

Despite this torrent against Jospin's statements, Arafat had no choice but to respond severely to the Bir Zeit demonstrators. Student protests in recent days, along with a series of other incidents, have reinforced the perception that events on the ground in the PA territories are spinning out of control.

A tumultuous demonstration held a week ago by striking school teachers in Hebron furnished evidence of mounting chaos. The same could be said of local disturbances which have flared up recently in Bethlehem, Ramallah, and elsewhere.

There is no exact information about the number of Bir Zeit students detained as a result of the stone-throwing incident. PA security officials allude to a figure of 30, but university officials talk of a much larger number, saying more than 100 students have been arrested.

Studies at Bir Zeit were suspended officially by the university's administration for three days. This three-day period has come and gone, but as of Wednesday classes were still not in session there.

In the meantime, police have beefed up their presence on campus out of concerns over the possibility of renewed riots. Lecturers and students have vowed not to return to the classroom until detained students are released from custody. Their vow has stirred more ferment on campuses throughout the West Bank leading concerned Palestinian university presidents to meet with PA Minister of Higher Education Munzer Salah to express their worries.

Campus unrest prompted Brigadier General Ghazi Jabali, head of the Palestinian police, to release a public announcement on Tuesday stating that any public assembly or demonstration would require a special permit. Anyone who holds a protest without permission and violates this order, is subject to a harsh punishment of two months imprisonment, or fines of 50 Jordanian dinars. Asked by foreign correspondents whether he had published this statement to intimidate the students, Jabali replied, "No, I just wanted to refresh the public's memory."

Yesterday, a Bir Zeit student who lives in East Jerusalem stated that some of his friends have received a notice saying that they have been expelled from the university, due to their participation in Saturday's riots and their attempt to physically harm the French prime minister. They were also informed that they would not be allowed to study at any other Palestinian university.

This student mentioned rumors that his expelled peers have already received phone calls from institutions of higher education in Damascus and Beirut, inviting them to enroll, and even offering them attractive scholarships. With hindsight, it appears that Arafat and his aides should have been able to anticipate the sequence of events, perceiving the upsurge in Hezbollah's popularity among the Palestinian public as a result of the Shi'ite organization's recent military successes against Israel.

Switching historical analogies, one Ramallah-based Palestinian journalist compared current admiration for Hezbollah not to the veneration Saddam Hussein commanded during the Persian Gulf crisis, but rather to the respect won by insurgent Palestinian resistance forces after the 1967 war. After the national armies of the Arab states were routed humiliatingly by Israel in a mere six days, it was these Palestinian guerrillas who restored some measure of Arab pride.

Arafat, more than anyone, can grasp this analogy. With a virtuoso public relations twist, he managed during that post-Six Day War period to convert one day of fighting in March 1968 in the Jordan Valley into the "Palestinian Stalingrad." Masses in vanquished Arab states, and the despondent Palestinian public in 1967, came to revere the Arafat-led Palestinian resistance movement, just as they admire the Lebanese resistance today.

Unable to measure the mood of the Palestinian public by such historical standards, France's prime minister paid a steep price, becoming the butt of violent, vituperative criticism in the Arab world, and in his country. For the Palestinian leadership, a lesson might be extracted from Jospin's Middle East misadventure. The Palestinian public, it appears, is inclined to favor a determined, violent struggle of the sort waged by Hezbollah, losing patience with what it increasingly perceives as a policy of accommodating humiliating compromises dictated by Israel.

Clash of Civilizations: Remaking of World Order

Samuel Huntington

Review by Dr. Sulayman S. Nyang

Since the end of the Cold War, Western strategists have spent a great deal of their time on the nature of the emerging world order. Francis Fukuyama’s End of History, Paul Kennedy’s The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers, and several other seminal works have attracted the attention of scholars, politicians, and members of the reading public around the world. Each of these texts is a sampling of intellectual fruits by one strategist or another who is preoccupied with the present state of affairs in the world. The collapse of the former Soviet Union and the fragmentation of the old coalition against that ideological empire have combined to heighten the need for a new world order. This book by Professor Samuel Huntington is the latest addition to the growing list of works trying to decipher the future course of international relations. It is an elaboration of an earlier article on the same subject published in the Summer, 1993, issue of Foreign Affairs, an important forum for debates on U.S. foreign policy.

Before we go to the discussion of how significant this work is in the universe of American strategic thought, let us examine the author’s rationale, given in the first chapter, for writing this book. In that part of the book Professor Huntington argues for a new paradigm in international relations theory. Contending that the post-Cold-War period begs for a new way of looking at and explaining politics among nations, and convinced that he has worked out a formula for those who engage in world politics, especially his Western leaders, he points to the inadequacies of the rival theories, while acknowledging their explanatory value. The rest of this review will be directed to the examination of his ideas and how they can be understood in terms of four categories of significance.

Huntington’s book is significant in four important respects. First, the book has changed the nature of political definition of conflicts in the international system. It has also introduced a new way of looking at and interpreting political affairs between states. Instead of looking at international relations through the lens of nation-states, he now urges us to begin a new way of looking at conflicts in the world system. For him the national state is still an actor of note, but its behavior and activity could best be interpreted and analyzed from the perspective of inter-civilizational relations. That is to say, according to Huntington, the global system is now going to be driven more by civilizational motives and interests than by the traditional factor of national interest only. Or to put his points in a more positive way, one can say that, in Huntington’s view, the old idea of national interest should be expanded a bit to embrace the collective interest of fellow members of a given civilization. This widening of the political net to include all partners within a given civilization, has serious consequences for world politics and for the big and small powers that are jockeying for influence around the world. Using his markers to demarcate the new configuration of political forces in the world, Professor Samuel Huntington has interestingly carved the world into eight possible centers of civilization. These are the Chinese, Islamic, the Hindu, the Japanese, the Latin American, the Orthodox-Christian, the Western, and the African.

This division of the world, if accepted by the political leaders of the planet, could transform significantly the way we all view the international system. The implication of such an approach to understanding and dealing with global affairs is that men and women in the post-Cold-War world are likely to fight each other not only because of race, language, ethnicity, and religion, but also primarily because they belong to different civilizations. That is to say, our civilizations are cultural wombs that fashion us so that we cannot rise to the level of self-transcendence individually and collectively. And because we cannot transcend ourselves individually and collectively, we end life and die without ever crossing the cultural and civilizational lines separating us from the other members of humanity, who are trapped forever within the solid walls of their own civilizations. By conceptualizing the world system in this manner, Professor Huntington makes it categorically clear to the students of international relations that henceforth Mankind would suffer more by inter-civilizational rivalry and conflict than by the age-old wrangles over national interest. It is this perspective that makes the work of Huntington significant and interesting. What he proposes as a new and viable paradigm, if accepted by the powers that be in the West, would implicitly or explicitly warrant the creation of a Security Council for Human Inter-Civilizational Affairs. Such a political arrangement would resurrect the old notion of collective security and the politics of the balance of power.

The second significance of this book lies in its understanding and interpretation of world affairs. The perspective is both Euro-centric and tempocentric. That is to say, the author apparently seeks the best ways to guarantee the continued dominance of the Western countries in the affairs of men. His world view is built on the assumption that the peoples of the Euro-American world have dominated world affairs for several centuries, and that they should develop new and changing strategies to protect themselves from their current rivals and potential enemies tomorrow. The Euro-centric bias in the book, when combined with its tempocentricity, leads to a view of the world that is a mirror image of the old American society, where the non-Whites found themselves at the bottom of the social ladder. Such a world order creates a caste system in which the conflation of race, religion, and culture decides your standing in the scale of social significance. This point does not register somehow in the mind of Professor Huntington. Even though he notes the fact that the international system of the European societies of the medieval and post-medieval period was a mirror image of the pluralism of the feudal order, he still fails to realize that the structuring of the world according to his paradigm would create a world order that is a replication of his own society. In a world where the Western man and woman are outnumbered by the Chinese and the Muslim, or by the Latin American and the Indian Hindu, it would be dangerous and unwise to deny these forces the opportunity to realize fully their right as humans to create and to compete for the recognition of fellow humans. The Eurocentrism of his analysis apparently leads the author to include “disingenuous” behavior as an important element in the strategic calculus of the West. That is to say, according to Professor Huntington, the Western countries should remember that their civilizational interest may sometimes be served not by adherence to morality but by flirtation with political opportunism.

The third significance of this book is its conscious decision to bracket Judaism and Israel with the West. All other world religions that have played any meaningful role in human history are identified with a civilization. Why lump Judaism with Christianity when other forms of Christianity are somehow segregated from the West? How do you go about separating one form of Christianity from the others? What is it that warrants the inclusion of Jews in the Western category? These and other related questions are somehow consciously avoided by the author. One can speculate about why this line of reasoning is pursued in his book. A thought that comes to mind is the fact that Judiasm has not created, and did not create in the past, any major civilization that currently embraces other human groups outside the people who now claim to be descendants of the ancient Hebrews. Another argument could be that the Jews have somehow transformed themselves significantly into full fledged Westerners and their destiny is now inextricably linked to that of the United States and Western Europe. The second point of speculation on my part might be the correct answer to the unanswered question in Professor Huntington’s book. No matter how one sees the question, the fact remains that Jews in the West are today an important group in the Euro-American World. Though their numbers are small, their current contributions in almost all areas of life in the West are definitely unprecedented and unbeatable.

The fourth significance of this book under review lies in the author’s desperate attempt to fashion out a new coalition of enemies for the Euro-American World. Having witnessed the collapse and disappearance of the old Communist empire under Russia, and determined to maintain a high level of military spending in the Pentagon and elsewhere in the West, men like Professor Huntington are now in search of enemies. To them, the Green Threat now replaces the Red Menace. Muhammad, Confucius, and their followers have now replaced Karl Marx and Chairman Mao on the enemy list of Professor Huntington and his Cold War cohorts. To reify his theory and paradigm, he advises Western leaders to note his reasons for bracketing these two civilizations as chief enemies. The followers of Islam constitute a perennial threat because they have a sense of cultural superiority that now suffers from their sense of humiliation under Western domination. The Chinese people, on the other hand, are demographically challenging and their continued economic success poses a threat because economic success does translate into political and military power. Professor Huntington not only identifies the sources of conflict between the West and other civilizations, but formulates strategies that, if successfully followed by the planners in the West, would lead to their continued dominance. According to the professor, the planners and strategists from the West should apply the balance of power theory to contain and neutralize the rising powers such as China in East Asia and India in South Asia. His reading of the literature on East Asian history leads him to the conclusion that the East Asian peoples have in the past accepted a hierarchical order dominated by China. He fears a return to this historical past and that rising China could well be the benefactor of a bandwagon effect. This bandwagoning strategy has been evident in East Asian history before, and Huntington fears its return if the East Asian peoples perceive the United States as retreating from a global role as a primary balancing force to a rising China.

This book deserves some attention from the Muslim World largely because the author is influential in certain circles in which Western policies are made. Although his ideas are being challenged globally and within policy-making circles of the West, there is still need for concerted Muslim efforts to expose the fallacies of the argument presented in this work. Muslim countries, however, should avoid the problem of overexposing the Huntington thesis as a self-fulfilling prophecy. Many Muslim and non-Muslim countries in the Middle East and South East Asia have organized conferences and seminars to deal with part or all of the ideas contained in this volume. Because of its provocative nature, and because of the intended and unintended consequences such a scholarly work could bring to the life of Muslims individually and collectively, it would be an investment in prudential intellectualism for English-speaking Muslims, especially those living and working in the West, to counter Professor Huntington’s fallacies.

Living Gold

Erik Paul

Hani was a quilt maker who lived in a small village somewhere in the Jordan Valley. When young, he spent a great deal of his free time with his best friend Elias. Together, on warm days, they would go out into the fields and sit and discuss life, religion and the future. The worst thing about Hani was that he could see no faults in his friend who remained his ideal until one day some money was stolen from Elias’ house. It was then Elias signed a complaint that he suspected Hani, for as he stated, “He was the only one who knew where I hid my money.”

Try as he would to no avail, Hani denied the accusations and endeavoured to assert his innocence, but no one believed him, not even his fianc. He was detained in a local prison during the investigations of the reported theft. No evidence was found against him, so the police soon released him.

It was early evening in spring when Hani walked out of jail free, but though he had been behind bars, he felt freer when he was imprisoned. Slowly, he trudged home in the still of the night where the only sound he could hear was the whoop of an owl and some crickets squeaking in wee voices. He could only think of his friend’s double betrayal and his fianc breaking off their engagement. Once home, he lit an oil lamp and sat alone until the small hours of the night, thinking hard about all that had happened.

For days he refused to step outside his house and sweated while sleeping. He woke up in the middle of the night screaming, “No, I did not steal anything. I’m innocent.”

He suddenly jumped up when he saw the stern countenance of his friend Elias in his nightmare and then paced back and forth in his cottage asking himself, “Why me? Why me? I trusted Elias. He was my idol!”

He decided the only face-saving thing he could do would be to move to another village. So he gathered his equipment, which wasn’t much for a quilt maker, paid his rent and left without telling a soul where he was going or saying good-bye to anyone. To further his confusion and increase his delusions abut friendship, he was told that Elias planned to marry his former fianc.

After he settled in a different village, he shunned his faith and fellow man, and kept much to himself except when someone wanted him to make a new quilt or refurbish an old one. At times, he felt like visiting his brother who was living in Amman, but at other moments shutting out the world seemed better. He continued his trade for several years having as little contact with others as possible. Since there was no one to spend his money on and he cared little for the extravagances of life, he saved his earnings and exchanged them for gold. At last, his greatest pleasure became his treasure and every night after he finished working, he would take out his gold and spend hours worshipping it. Then he returned it to his leather bag and hid it on his closet shelf. One day, when he went to the village to buy some more wool for his quilts, a scavenger raided his home, turning it upside down until at last he found and confiscated the leather bag and its contents. He stuffed the gold pieces in his pockets, threw the bag on the floor, and rushed out of Hani’s house and the village forever.

When Hani returned home, he discovered his empty leather bag trampled on the floor covered with dirt. With disbelief, he slowly picked it up, turned it upside down and shook it even though he knew it was empty. He slumped down on his bed, sunk his head in his hands and cried, “This is the second time I have been betrayed by other human beings and now I have lost the only thing that was dear to me!”

For two weeks he stopped making quilts and after alerting his neighbour and the village of the theft, he secluded himself in his home, making it a veritable prison. Yet, the reaction of the people around him stunned him. They seemed concerned, yet their sympathy hardly touched his hardened heart. Then one day at the beginning of summer, a man from Amman knocked on his door. In his arms he carried a small child with golden curly hair. Hani opened the door and stared at the child who had a dirty face and large brown eyes, wide and wondering, looking back at him. The man said, “I’m sorry to bother you, sir, but I’m a friend of your brother. He told me that if anything every happened to him to deliver his only child to you. A week ago, your brother and his wife were killed in a car accident. The baby survived and you are the next of kin and the only one who can take care of the child. I’ve been searching for you for days.”

Hani didn’t know what to be more shocked about, his brother’s death or the sudden responsibility of this young life thrust into his arms. The bundle cuddled next to him. He felt another heart beating beside his own in a warm body. All he could say to the stranger who brought the baby was, “What is its name?”

“His name is Yahya. Good day, sir, and forgive me for having to inform you of your brother’s death in this manner.”

Hani looked down at the small form and exclaimed to it, “I’ve found some living gold to replace the gold I lost!”

It was little Yahya who taught Hani to rediscover the world again and to have faith once more in his fellow man. He raised the boy as a conscientious adult and together they prayed and believed in God, for Hani, as a good uncle, could not raise the boy as an atheist. And the circle of love, once non-existent, grew into a realm of wonder. Hani gained friends who praised and admired him for dedicating his life to the orphaned child. The child grew into a man, got married and now lives with his wife and their children in Uncle Hani’s house. The day Hani’s gold was stolen from him became the turning point of his life, for he gained unthought-of riches in the golden-haired tot who found his uncle’s heart and gave him back his trust in human nature.

If she could have picked a day for dying in the cold of a desolate, damp, forsaken winter, she would not have picked a better day. Outside the sky was blue and clear and soft puffs of creamy clouds inched their way across the lazy winter sky that hung like a protective canopy over the world that Fatima was finally leaving.

Propped by pillows on her bed beside a window with a view, she drifted off and on between the here of this world to the there of the unknown. Her attentive, dutiful daughter, heaven-sent, planted gentle kisses on her mother’s forehead and kept straightening pillows and covers as a doting parent might arrange the apparel of a precious infant.

Outside, the sound of a cement mixer reminded the dying Fatima of the automatic water pump of days of yore in her beloved homeland where droves of orange groves, lemon trees and honeybees once reigned supreme.

Skateboards rolling down the paved street and children’s laughter in her host country took her back again to Palestine to the childhood laughter of her own children running barefoot through the green, green sprinklers of youth and innocence. It was a time of fresh milk, yogurt, cheese, honey and home-baked bread. A time of long glorious days lived to the fullest with the sweet sweat of hard work and summer suns falling like the sound of angels singing and rainbows of promise stretching from one horizon to another.

Quilted patches of her life now flashed before her as she drifted in and out of the here and the tangible to the darkness and the unknown quantity awaiting her. And in one of her flashbacks, she remembered when, in the midst of all the songs, the dance and land spread out against a paradise sky that she possessed, she one day had gathered her young children as a hen would gather her chicks and stated in a strong, determined voice to her husband, “I am leaving and taking the kids with me. There have been massacres of Palestinian women and children reported in places like Deir Yassin carried out by Jewish soldiers come to take our land away from us. If you wish to stay and die on our land, you are free to do so. But we are leaving. I will not watch my children be butchered in front of me.”

She did not bother to look twice into his face for she did not want to encounter his bewildered, clouded expression. She bundled up some pots and pans, some blankets, some clothing for her youngsters and piled goods and kids on a wagon and prepared to drive off to safety. She had told herself that it would only be a few days and they could all return safely home again. Knowing somehow that her husband would not remain behind with all that he loved loaded onto the wagon, she locked the door of her cherished home and placed the keys to her house and barn in the hidden pocket of her thobe.

It was the last time she saw her home, her orange groves, the lemon trees, the birds that sang everywhere, and her beloved village. For some reason, she noted, her children, not understanding what was going on, were excited about the journey they were embarking on. No one knew then that they had embarked on a difficult and dangerous expedition into homelessness, Statelessness and abject poverty. It was an expedition that would force her older children to work hard in the fields of a neighbouring country just so all the family could eat one meal.

But Fatima’s black coffee eyes were keen and sharp. Long used to tending the fields, giving childbirth and then returning to work a few hours later, her strength matched that of most men, and it was this very strength that secured the well-being of her children. In spite of the hard times, lack of money, loss of wealth and estate and a once-life in paradise, Fatima raised her sons as men and her daughters as ladies. She was the envy of all her relatives and no one could outwit her or surpass her in the battle for survival.

She and her husband, who was once a great hero in his own way, and their children came to settle East of Gideon in a God-forsaken place where no grass grew, no trees were found and where no birds sang. In this barren land, the sun was not young and there were no rainbows that stretched glorious and colourful from one horizon to another. Yet Fatima and her family survived and made do with very little. There were rations of flour full of weevils. There were rations of rice filled with worms. The sugar rations were filled with dirt and the grains were so coarse that one could count them. And all the donated food and clothing from international charitable organizations never reached the refugees as free commodities, but were sold for those who could afford to purchase them. Sometimes a meal consisted of hot tea and bread and nothing else. Still, there was much to be thankful for. Fatima had grabbed on to that precious gift of life and never let go until long after she had raised her children and seen them all successful.

She and her family lived in a barren, grey, desolate place as second-class citizens and were never allowed to forget by the native inhabitants that they were not equal to the rest of the indigenous population.

So she planted a part of Palestine wherever she could. In tin cans, in clay pots, between the rocks and in windowsills. And right over her door, jasmine spread its fragrance to all who entered Fatima’s little kingdom.

She prayed and waited for the day that would take her and her family back to her homeland. Back to the golden days of summer suns, sweet sweat, back to the time when her children ran free as the nobility of apple blossoms and mint. Back to her earthly paradise, on to the fields of praise, where she and her family could reign once more. But those days never came.

Her keys that she kept in a wooden jewellery box began to rust. But every once in a dreamy while, she would open the box and gently touch them. And on that day she thought she might die, when day turned to night and wild pigeons were fast asleep and after all her relatives who came to pay their last respects had departed, she whispered softly to her daughter, “Please bring me my jewellery box.”

Her daughter quickly swept across the room, to the treasured corner of a forgotten closet, and opened a creaking door. She then took the jewellery box and placed it on her mother’s lap.

“Open it,” the mother demanded in a barely audible voice.

Her daughter opened the box only to find some rusty old keys lying on faded silk cushions. Fatima put her hand slowly in the box, picked up the keys, kissed them and placed them over her heart. Today definitely was not a day to die. Who knew what the night might bring. Then she slept, clasping the keys in her hand, resting her hand over her heart. Visions danced through her mind of her former Garden of Eden when she sang songs of spinning and living carefree, of threshing and lightning flashes and thundering rain in her kingdom that stretched further than any eye could ever hope to see.

Zaki al-Milad

Globalization and Identity: Development or Meltdown?

Davidor Rothkopf, professor of international relations at Columbia University, states: "It is true that globalization has economic roots and political implications, but it has also shed light on the power of culture in the global environment. Cultural power both gathers and disperses, at a time when tensions between wholism and separation arise in every issue and are linked to international relations. The reality is that the effect of globalization on culture, and the effect of culture on globalization, deserve attention."3 Globalization has opened up the issue of identity on a global scale. The discussion of globalization and culture has revealed both their mutual dependencies and contradictions.
Perhaps Rothkopf's discussion is one of the few instances in the 20th century in which a large scale discussion is initiated over the issue of identity and what threatens it through the two alternatives of "annihilation and negation" and "meltdown and integration." A third alternative is for culture to be revitalized and expanded.
Before there was great interest in globalization, discussions of identity were greeted with skepticism and indifference. Concern about identity was presented as a type of thinking that deserved to be criticized. The opposite is taking place today when there is widespread serious interest, as well as uncertainty, about globalization. Globalization entails the imposition of one way of thinking, and the popularization of one type of culture, through control of the tools of globalization, especially worldwide satellite delivery of pictures and meanings and symbols and principles, TV programs, computer software, and the internet. The directors of globalization today are improving the means and systems of international transmission, and are inventing technologies and new revolutionary services in the field of communication. They dominate the world market of ideas and services, which, in turn, affects the way of life, beliefs, languages, and other components of culture worldwide.
Many have written about the role of technology and information services in this process. Today 15 large American companies in the area of communications, including giant companies such as Motorola, Loral Space and Communications, and Teledesic, are pursuing competing projects. These will allow the individual anywhere in the world to have instant communication with any other individual no matter where he or she is without needing to build any basic infrastructure underground near the sender or receiver. Loral has estimated the cost of such a call at $3.00 per minute.
Technology not only creates change, but creates its own metaphorical world.4
Samuel Huntington has criticized those who he believes have called for one world culture, based on the West, by saying: "Many in the West think that the world is moving towards one united world culture, essentially Western culture. This thinking is arrogant, false, and dangerous. The spread of Western consumer items does not mean the spread of Western culture."5
This clarity in Huntington's ideas, even though he is critical, underlines the fears of nations and civilizations for their identities, cultures, and civilizational models. The West will soon discover that it is a reason for the renewal and upliftment of identities and cultures worldwide, even those cultures and identities that were almost forgotten and left behind by history and on their way to annihilation. This is due to the West's provocations, the unraveling of its culture, and its efforts to dominate and control. The confrontation of the West with these identities has led to their revival and renewed dynamism, including those cultures displaced by the West, such as some of the original cultures in Europe, North America, and Australia. These cultures have regained their strength, and history is being rewritten in North America. The travel diaries of Christopher Columbus, who was portrayed by the Europeans as a discoverer of the New World, are giving rise to accusations that he was a pirate and destroyer and that he was not an explorer but a colonizer engaged in murder and theft. As some modern history books in Europe are now admitting, the history of North America does not begin with Columbus's voyage in 1492.
The reality that must be understood by the West is that the non-Arab nations and civilizations, and some cultures in the West itself, no longer view the West as a dream and model of progress to be followed. In its own enlightened self-interest the West should reduce its encroachment on the world, and stop its adventures, and rethink its behavior patterns that have instigated other nations and civilizations to be anti-West.
It has become evidently clear that identities cannot be removed easily, or through a crushing blow, and in a final way. The hand of time might stop for some cultures, but it may move again at any time. Some cultures might be outside of history for a time, and they might return in another period. History has its cycles and its variations, and God is more knowledgable of its movements than any human. The Holy Qur'an states: "We rearrange the days among people."6 Days are apportioned among people according to patterns and laws that govern life, history, and society. The secret that gives identities this everlasting quality is the fact that they manifest themselves at very deep levels inseparable from the nation's soul and general being. Identities bring together and affect all behavior patterns, the entire system of morals and rights and responsibilities, the varieties of norms and habits, including the ways of eating, and the institutions of marriage, contract, language, and religious services. This interconnectedness and interaction continues for a long time and under a variety of conditions and circumstances, which causes the identity to be deeply engrained to the extent that participation in the philosophy and practice of communal life is not conceivable without it.
Even though it is logical that nations and civilizations should fear for their identities, it is still impossible for globalization to compartmentalize and de-establish the identities of nations. There may be major breaches, and the West may take advantage of deep holes, but it is still very hard to verify the loss and complete meltdown of identities. How is it possible to explain the continuing presence of native peoples in North America and Australia, who are living their cultures and identities and their old moral and social systems at a time when the West, especially the United States, has achieved the highest forms of progress and modernization?
Huntington argues that when all the nations were exposed to modernization, they may have become Westernized superficially, but on the most important levels of culture this did not affect their language, religion, and values. In fact, when nations modernize, they resort to their cultures and traditional religions to run way from the modern world."7
Globalization is a challenge, a challenge from which identities and cultures may benefit. Cultures have become more prominent on the world political stage at the level of relations among nations and civilizations. Perhaps the West was a cause for this increased presence. This issue received special attention by Labed and Kratoshgel in their book, The Return of Identity and Culture to International Relations Theory.8 They raise the question of the long absence of interest in culture and identity in the field of International Relations, and they present a framework for rethinking the modern theoretical extent of the concepts of culture and identity.
In the recent past there have been some seminars and intellectual activities that have emphasized the problematic nature of the relation between globalization and identity. These include:

• Seminar on Globalization and Identity, Rabat, Kingdom of Morocco Academy, May 1997;

• The Arabs and Globalization, Beirut, Center for Arab Unity Studies, December 1997;

• Globalization and the Preservation of Cultural Identities, Creativity, and Political Cultures, Paris, French National Academy for Education, Training, and Culture, January 1998;

• Political Cultures for the Sake of Development, Stockholm, UNESCO, March 1997;

• Globalization and Issues of Cultural Identity, Cairo, Egyptian High Council for Culture, April 1998; and

• Francophonism and Globalization, Beirut, April 1998.
In all these seminars, globalization was criticized as a promoter of unified culture and a derailer of cultural pluralism.

Arab Literature and Globalization
Globalization has attracted widespread attention in Arabic literature, economic, cultural, political, educational, media, and technological. This attention will multiply in the 21st century as people become more aware of its most glaring aspects and react to its characteristics and features.
The West is taken up by globalization because it is the founder of globalization and the moving force behind it, living it as a reality, and organizing its capabilities and resources and systems on the basis of globalization. Thus the West is aware of the nature of globalization and its philosophy and its future and its actions, as well as its effect on its choices and directions and strategies. We in the Arab and Islamic world, on the other hand, are involved with globalization but we are not an active or effective element in its movement and direction. We still have not finalized our reading on the nature and philosophy of globalization. We are enchanted by it rather than acting upon it; we are affected without being effectors; and we are fearful and wary of it, because we do not know our place in globalization nor our future.
Whoever looks at Arabic literature will find little understanding and analysis of globalization, because much writing is designed to demonize it, as if describing a science-fiction monster coming from a strange world beyond our solar system. Arab writers do not know how to describe globalization, or identify it, or disentangle its features and signs. What is said is simply exaggeration and fear-mongering, or assumptions and metaphoric illustration.
Sadeq Jalal al Azm states that globalization is, "a feature of the capitalistic transformation of humanity, under the imperialism of the core nations and their leaders, and under their control, within a global system of unequal exchange."9
Muhammad Abid al-Jabiri states, "Globalization is neo-colonialism, and what is neo in this term does not mean a clean break from what is prior, but rather a continuation in a new form."10 Elsewhere he adds, "Globalization is not simply a tool of capitalistic progress; it is also, in the first degree, an ideology that reflects the desire to control the world."11
Al-Sayyid Yaseen sees that, "We are still in the stage of understanding the phenomenon of globalization, and teasing out the hidden laws that govern its progress and are involved in its formation. It is actually a phenomenon incomplete in its features and traits; we can actually say that globalization is a continuing project, revealing each day a new side of its many faces."12

Ali Harb states, "It is dangerous to trivialize and oversimplify our reading of the phenomenon of globalization. We are facing an event that is complex and dense, which is opening up without meaning or direction, and is involved in many arenas, and which unleashes new social forces and alternative cultural forms. The reality is that whoever follows what is written and said about globalization, whether in the magazines or intellectual seminars, will find contradictory positions, reflecting

differences in reading and analysis."13

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

By Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 6

The Rise of the Haredim in Israel

Although expanding steadily from the early 1970s, Jewish religious fundamentalism in Israel attracted relatively little interest in the dominant secularly oriented Israeli society until 1988. Members of the various Haredi sects, generally self-contained in residentially segregated areas of Israeli cities, led lives absorbed by concerns and preoccupations that appeared exotic at best to outsiders. Although some members of these sects clashed sharply over specific issues with the secular part of Israeli society and at those times acquired a bit of public attention, they were mostly ignored. The sensational Haredi political success in the Israeli parliamentary elections of 1988, predicted by none of the professional pollsters, surprised many people. Because of their continued political successes in succeeding elections through the 1990s, the Haredim put themselves into a position at various times to be able to dictate to the Israeli secular majority.

The Haredi political successes not only caused many Israeli Jews to look more closely at and to be more concerned with the Haredim but also sparked increased attention abroad, especially in the United States. The interest generated in the United States prompted the writing and publication of many new books and articles in English that focused upon the folkloristic aspects of the Haredim, but unfortunately largely ignored their basic ideology and world outlook. The following discussion will attempt to analyze, particularly for those readers who are not literate in Hebrew, the political importance of the Haredi upsurge. A crucial part of this analysis is the acceptance of the well-documented proposition that an understanding of the entire Israeli political right is to some extent dependent upon an understanding of the basic elements of Heredi politics, apart from the disagreements, splits and reunification efforts of many Heredi individuals and sects. The two major questions to be analyzed are:

How have the Haredi parties secured their political influence?

What organizational structure have the Haredi employed for maximum political success?

Concern with education has provided the major answer to both questions. The Haredi have on balance successfully educated their own children and other Jewish children, over whom they have obtained custody, in a manner guaranteeing maximum continuity. The Haredi have influenced many Israeli Jews in addition to their own by acquiring direct authority over several school networks and by indirectly influencing numbers of other schools.

Throughout the twentieth century, the Haredim have attempted to continue Jewish education as it had mostly existed in the diaspora before the Enlightenment influenced Jewish society. The governments in the countries in which the Haredim lived, however, have at times insisted upon some modernized curricular content that was inconsistent with and in opposition to what had previously been taught in Jewish schools. This was the case in Israel until 1980. Since 1980, helped by generous Israeli governmental subsidies, the Haredim have attempted with some success to reimpose the earlier type of Jewish education and the earlier school networking system in many poorer provincial Israeli towns and in slum areas of larger Israeli cities. The Haredi goal has obviously been to perpetuate their educational influence upon an increasing segment of younger-generation Israelis.

Historically, Jewish schooling began with the heder for Jewish male children aged three or four. (The heder, a word meaning 'room' in Hebrew, was the name of the traditional Jewish elementary school as it existed from talmudic times in the earliest centuries of the Common Era until the formation of the first modern nation-states at which time many Jews strove to modify or abolish the heder.) The heder was previously for males only. According to the Talmud and the Halacha, females do not need education and are explicitly forbidden from some forms of study. Until modern times, most Jewish women received no formal education and were mostly illiterate. This stood in striking contrast to Jewish males Faced with governments of modern nation states and with many Jews themselves reacting against and abolishing the exclusion of females from formal education, the Haredim established special institutions to train, more precisely to indoctrinate, young Haredi girls to accept and to agree to inferior education. Heder education consists only of sacred, Jewish studies. Secular subjects, including arithmetic, foreign languages, science, literature and Hebrew grammar are excluded. Most of the Bible is included among subjects not taught. After studying the Pentateuch with the help of a commentary by Rashi (Rabbi Shlomo Yitzhaki who died in 1099), the students proceed directly to study of the easier parts of the Talmud. After studying about eight years, the less capable students are sent to various places to learn a craft, trade or some other occupation; the more capable are admitted to an institution of higher learning called a yeshiva. (Yeshiva in Hebrew means sitting or meeting.) Usually, several levels of "yeshivot" (plural) exist. The weeding-out process of students continues at each level. Those students who are found to be less capable are directed to moneymaking pursuits and somewhat later to involvement in religious services as minor rabbis or as supervisors of religious kashrut rules in restaurants, hospitals, the army and other institutions. The more capable students proceed in their learning by going from one yeshiva level to another. After graduating from the highest yeshiva and marrying, the best of the students spend their lives in an institution called a kollel (a term derived from the word meaning "entire") and spend their time studying only talmudic literature. A few of the most capable are later appointed to high rabbinic positions or become heads of yeshivot or kollels.

As mentioned previously, traditional Jewish education, described above, does not include any secular or humanistic studies. It is worth re-emphasizing that this exclusion of secular subjects includes not only mathematics, all sciences and foreign languages but also Hebrew literature, which includes poetry dealing with religious subjects, grammar and Jewish history. It is thus no surprise that Hebrew religious poetry, even the medieval masterpieces, are unknown to the Haredim. Only the sacred studies (a pre-modern term in Judaism) are taught with the greatest possible intensity. The sacred studies consist mostly of the Talmud and some subsequent talmudic literature. At the highest yeshiva level, one out of twelve to fourteen hours per day of sacred studies may be devoted to the study of morality, which primarily consists of lurid descriptions of the punishment, inflicted by God either in the life of this world or in hell, for even the smallest deviations from religious commandments. The teachings of the biblical prophets, the books of Job and Ecclesiastes and numerous other parts of the Bible are studied neither in the heders nor the yeshivot and are therefore unknown to the Haredim. Except for the Pentateuch, Haredim know only those parts of the Bible quoted in the Talmud and then only within the context of talmudic interpretation. Haredim generally lack knowledge of major parts of the Bible; this lack of knowledge constitutes one source of the differences between the Haredim and some other religious as well as most secular Israeli Jews. Yeshiva students are often deprived of sleep. After reaching the age of sixteen, Yeshiva students devote at least twelve to fourteen hours per day to study. The classes are noisy, because the students shout about what they are studying. Studying in silence is considered to be a sin. Chaos is often the result in the classroom; different students often shout about different passages of texts. Students may ask questions about the internal matters of what is being studied but never about the assumptions upon which interpretations are made or about the external world. Students are most often isolated from the outside world, especially from the secular world. Students are prohibited from contact with unbelievers. The teacher's authority is extensive and almost absolute. The main teacher or the head of the yeshiva usually will select the wives for students.

The type of education described above has shaped human character. It also inevitably has produced dissenters. The first Jewish dissenters from Judaism in modern times rebelled against this type of education and became principled opponents of the religion that from their perspectives tried to subject them to such totalitarian controls. Other individuals, schooled in the Haredi tradition, have ultimately yielded to temptations of modernity, such as watching television and attending movies This usually has resulted in a weakening of commitment to Haredi Judaism but seldom to its renunciation. In Israel such persons have been and still are called "traditional" or "Mesorati." These people have usually remained -and still are- outwardly uncritical of what they learned; they have continued to worship the charismatic rabbis without paying any price for renunciating the prohibition of forbidden secular pleasures. Others who have strayed but have not undergone self-emancipation have after a temporary break returned to sacred studies to be again indoctrinated by their education.

The Haredim emphasize the sanctity and predominant importance of the sacred studies; they believe that the virtue emanating from those engaged in sacred studies is responsible for all good happenings for Jews. For that reason those who engage in sacred studies are not required to make their own livings, are granted numerous privileges and are exempted from communal duties. All of this originated and became universal among Jews in talmudic times. Living in autonomous communities, in which they retained local rule, Jews could and did determine that individuals engaged in sacred studies be exempted from paying taxes and from most other obligations and burdens for which members of the community were responsible. Additionally, the disciples of the sages, those who reached a specified high degree of proficiency in the sacred studies, were granted special privileges in many areas of life over which the Jewish community had control. During talmudic times (c. AD 200-500) in Iraq, for example, the disciples of the sages, who also were merchants, were granted the privilege of selling their merchandise before ordinary Jews were allowed to do so in the markets of Jewish towns. That meant that these disciples of the sages had no competition.

A burning issue in Jewish history, and in Israeli politics, is how rabbis and rabbinical students earn their livelihoods. In Israel the constantly increasing burden of support weighs heavily upon taxpayers, most of whom are not religious. This has provoked and continues to provoke resentment, especially when combined with the fact that a majority of rabbinical students do not have to serve in the army. Most Israeli religious Jews, especially the Haredim, attempt to justify state support and freedom from army service by arguing that the Jews and the Jewish state of Israel exist by virtue of their support of talmudic study. Their support is supposedly responsible in turn for God's support, which includes God's allowing Israel to win its wars. This argument, similar to arguments made by clergy of other religions and frequently emphasized in the Israeli media, alleges that God's help not soldiers win wars. This argument specifies that God provides other benefits as well. He, for example, grants good weather because of rabbis and students who spend most of their time studying Talmud. Engaging in such study is the best way, better than reciting prayers, giving charity or performing other good deeds, to gain entrance into paradise. Those who engage in talmudic study make it possible for themselves, their families, their financial supporters and, to some extent, other Jews to enter paradise.
