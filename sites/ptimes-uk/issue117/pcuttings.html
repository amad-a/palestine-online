Press Cuttings

  • The Boston Globe 23 February 2001
    Israeli police officers said in court yesterday that they had been ordered to fire live bullets without warning at Arab Israeli demonstrators armed only with slingshots during the early stages of the current Palestinian uprising.

    The testimony, during Israel's first public review of its forces' actions during the uprising, probed one of the most explosive issues of the nearly five-month conflict, the deaths last October of 13 Palestinians who were Israeli citizens.

    Nearly 400 people in all have died so far in the ongoing uprising, the great majority of them Palestinians.

    In a packed room at Israel's Supreme Court, five Border Patrol police officers testified anonymously from behind a makeshift wooden screen erected to shield them from the enraged audience of grieving relatives. In a sign of the intense sensitivity over the hearings, 15 security guards stood across the front of the courtroom scanning the spectators.

  • The Washington Post, 23 February 2001
    When Palestinian security men came to arrest a suspected bomb maker in Gaza's Jabalya refugee camp recently, they met a defiant response: Scores of young men poured out of the Al-Nour Mosque, surged at the police and pelted them with stones.

    After firing into the air, the police finally arrested their suspect, Hamdi Khalil Sakani, a 17-year-old barber allied with the militant Islamic Resistance Movement, or Hamas. But the swelling crowd then marched on a nearby police station and stoned it until Sakani was released, local residents said. The siege continued until a Hamas activist arrived to help calm the crowd.

    Five months into the Palestinian uprising against Israel, incidents like the one at Jabalya, in which angry crowds challenge the security forces of Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority, are said to be occurring more frequently.

  • The Daily Telegraph, 21 February 2001
    The Israeli army has used "excessive, random or negligent" force against Palestinians during the four-month-old uprising and encouraged a "culture of impunity" among its soldiers, according to Amnesty International.

    In a report released today, the human rights group condemns the policy of assassinating Palestinians suspected of attacking Israelis as contrary to international law.

    At least 13 Palestinians have been killed under the policy since November. The latest was on Monday in the West Bank city of Nablus, where an activist of the extremist group Hamas was shot dead as he walked away from a mosque.

  • The Guardian, Wednesday February 21, 200
    "I feel there is no use in telling anyone what has been happening here tonight. Since 4.30 we have been under heavy shelling. We tried to hide my nieces behind a cupboard but they are babies and cannot sit still. Guess what - we could see our houses being shelled on Israeli TV." Marina Barham of Inad Theatre in Beit Jala on the West Bank was sending emails to friends about what was happening to her family.

  • The New York Times, 20 February 2001
    An Israeli Arab couple lost control today as a border police officer suspected of fatally shooting their son took the witness stand a few feet from them.

    Attending the first public hearing into police killings of 13 people during a wave of violent protests by Israeli Arabs in October, the grief- stricken parents, Hatem and Rushdiyeh Gara, erupted.

    "Murderer!" Mrs. Gara shouted in Arabic at the police officer, Rashed Murshid, before she collapsed. "You murdered my son!"

    ..For many Israeli Arabs, the police shootings, during protests in sympathy with the Palestinian uprising, were the climax of decades of discrimination — inadequate government funding for their towns and villages, inferior roads and schools, unemployment and poverty.

  • The Guardian, 19 February 2001
    Israel has just elected a prime minister who, brought before the bar of international justice, would surely be judged a war criminal in the class of, say, Ratko Mladic, the Bosnian Serb commander as firmly associated with the Srebrenica massacre as General Sharon was with that of Sabra and Shatila during the 1982 Israeli invasion of Lebanon.

    Sharon calls Yasser Arafat "a murderer and a liar" but, in the run-up to the elections, the liberal Israeli press copiously illustrated the deceit and sanguinary brutality which have been the twin pillars of his own career. One of his likely coalition partners, Avigdor Lieberman, has spoken of burning Beirut and bombing Tehran. Sharon's ideas on the furtherance of the peace process make a mockery of it. If any Israeli leader ever had the makings of a western villain, the destroyer of US interests in the region, it is surely he.

    (From Beirut, David Hirst sees the US accused of double standards on Saddam and Sharon)
