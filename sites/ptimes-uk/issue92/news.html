Political Prisoners in PA custody go on hunger strike

Occupied Jerusalem: Scores of political prisoners in PA jails and detention centres began an open-ended hunger strike last month, according to the Ramallah-based human rights organization, the LAW. The organization said in a statement published in the Palestinian press that 64 prisoners detained in the Jeneid Prison, located on the western outskirts of Nablus, went on a hunger strike to protest their continued incarceration without charge or trial.

The LAW pointed out that the prisoners were particularly embittered with what they described as the “total apathy” displayed by the PA towards them, and the Palestinian leadership’s failure to heed repeated recommendations by the Ministry of Justice and the Attorney-General to release them.

The LAW pointed out that political arrests and imprisonment are a characteristic of an unhealthy society.

Palestinian sources said the continued incarceration of hundreds of political, mostly Islamist, prisoners was, for the most part, due to promises PA Chairman Yasser Arafat had made to CIA officials whereby political Islamist activists would remain behind bars for prolonged periods of time as a punishment for their political and ideological views.

Dirty campaign mars Israeli election campaign

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

With the general Israeli elections nearing, the two main political parties in Israel—Likud and Labor—continued to experience internal divisions, which observers contend makes the outcome of the elections all too unpredictable.

The Likud, which has been in a state of turmoil since the Knesset decided to hold early elections a month ago, suffered a new set-back this week when former defense minister Moshe Arens decided to run against Prime Minister Netanyahu in the Likud primary election, slated to be held on 25 January.

It is not certain how popular Arens is in the Likud camp, but recent opinion polls indicate that up to 25% of Likud delegates support him.

Arens is the second Likudist leader to decide to challenge the increasingly beleaguered Netanyahu.

Earlier, Uzi Landou, an extremely hawkish figure advocating the annexation of the West Bank and the termination of the Oslo Accords, announced his intention to challenge Netanyahu for party leadership.

While both Arens and Landou have been quite critical of Netanyahu’s performance as Prime Minister, Arens seems to display a whiff of pragmatism, especially vis-à-vis the Palestinians.

In contrast, Landou is viewed as particularly dogmatic, even racist, given his extremely racist views on Palestinian rights.

Nevertheless, both Arens and Landou are expected to exert a radicalizing influence on Netanyahu.

Landou pointed out that if the Likud emerged the winner in the coming election, the Wye River agreement would be scrapped.

On 15 January, Landou decided to leave the race for the Likud leadership, but didn’t say if he would support Netanyahu or Arens.

Meanwhile, Netanyahu continued unabated to make vitriolic statements reminiscent of his election in the spring of 1996.

For example, the Israeli premier warned on 10 January that he would annex the bulk of the West Bank if Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat decided to go ahead with his plans to declare Statehood in May.

Using a twisted language that exudes fear among Israelis, Netanyahu said he would not allow Arafat to determine Israel’s boundaries or re-divide Jerusalem.

This demagoguery on Netanyahu’s part coincided with earlier decisions by the Israeli Government to expand Jewish settlements throughout the West Bank, particularly in East Jerusalem and Jabal Abu Ghneim on the town’s southern outskirts.

Meanwhile, the Likud election steering committee, headed by Justice Minister Tzahi Hanegbi, decided to concentrate campaign attacks on the person of Labor Party leader Ehud Barak, while effectively ignoring the ‘centrist’ candidates Yitzhak Mordechai, former defence minister and Amnon Shahak.

This tactic, reportedly adopted on the advice of American public relations experts who were brought from the United States to advise the Likud on how to win, manifested itself last week in a series of slogans and bumper stickers throughout Israel.

Some of the stickers read ‘Barak barach’, meaning ‘Barak escaped’, an allusion to a training incident that took place several years ago in the Negev desert where Barak, then Chief of Staff, allegedly left the field, leaving behind several Israeli soldiers bleeding.

An inquiry into the incident later cleared Barak of any ‘wrong-doing’, but this did not stop the Likud from using the incident as additional ammunition to besmirch his image.

Furthermore, the Likud is expected to highlight some of Barak’s ‘slips of the tongue’, such as his infamous statement last year that “if I were born a Palestinian, I would probably join a terrorist organization”, to undermine his image among the Zionist core of the Israeli population who grew up to believe that the Palestinian struggle for freedom and liberation was ‘sheer terrorism’.

For its part, the Labor Party will stress “the flaws in Netanyahu’s personality and his utter lack of statesmanship.”

One Labor campaign jingle said “Netanyahu is not good for the Jews; he is good for Sheikh Yassin,” an allusion to the release of the Hamas leader from Israeli detention following the botched assassination attempt in Amman on Khaled Misha’al, the chief of Hamas political bureau, in late 1997.

Moreover, the Labor campaign will try to expose “inconsistencies and contradictions” in Netanyahu’s policies and stances on various issues, especially the peace process, where Netanyahu achieved “neither security nor peace”.

Everybody is waiting for the Israeli election
With a vociferous election campaign in Israel heating up, the Palestinians, the United States and other key players in the Middle East peace process seem to have resigned themselves to the increasingly manifest fact that very little, if anything, can be done to advance or even activate the stalled peace process, at least until after the Israeli election, slated for 17 May.

This week, U.S. envoy Dennis Ross paid another routine visit to the region where he sought, unsuccessfully, to convince Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu to resume the implementation of the Wye River memorandum, signed at an impressive White House ceremony on 23 October.

Netanyahu, beleaguered by internal opposition and wrangling within his party (the Likud), reportedly told Ross that he could not carry out any further redeployment of the Israeli army in the West Bank even if he wanted to, since any step as such would be a grave election blunder, even a sort of political suicide.

For his part, Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat urged the American administration, which is also fighting for its survival, to find a mechanism to force Israel to implement the Wye River agreement before the Israeli elections.

Arafat argued in a rather embittered tone that “it is illogical that we all be held hostage to the Israeli elections and that agreements must be carried out irrespective of what political developments take place within the Jewish State.”

However, Ross had no words of consolation for Arafat aside from reiterating the usual platitudinous remarks about the good merits of the peace process, prompting PA minister of Planning and International Cooperation, Nabil Sha’ath, to declare that “we hadn’t expected much from his (Ross’s) visit in the first place.”

Palestinian disappointment surrounding Ross’s ineffective visit was in fact over more than just his failure to get Netanyahu to implement the Wye agreement, a task no serious observer of the Palestinian-Israeli arena had expected Ross to achieve.

In his speech before the Tel Aviv-based Peres Peace Centre, an institute ostensibly aimed at promoting peace between Jews and Arabs, Ross indicated that the United States would minimize its involvement in the final status talks between Israel and the Palestinians, due to the complexity and sensitivity of the issues to be discussed.

The statement, while receiving profound satisfaction from the Israeli Government, nearly stunned the Palestinian Authority, which interpreted it as “an effective betrayal and let-down to the Palestinians.”

Arafat’s spokesman, Marwan Kanafani, called Ross’s statement “a naked manifestation of American conspiracy and connivance with Israel.”

“What the Americans are telling us is, in effect, that they will leave us to be vanquished and devoured by Israeli insolence,” said Kanafani.

In fact, Ross’s remarks regarding the final status talks are perfectly compatible with Netanyahu’s strategy of “lowering Palestinian expectations”, a theme he is expected to repeat many times over in the four months leading up to the Israeli elections.

The undeclared implication inherent in all of this is very significant, namely that the Palestinians would effectively rely on Israeli “magnanimity” rather than “international legitimacy” to restore their rights.

Tough Policy
The Israeli premier has actually embarked on a spate of measures in the West Bank suggesting that he is still faithful to the “greater Israel” ideas of the far right.

His “getting tough” policy toward the Palestinians, as labeled by his aides, seems to have two main goals: the first is the need for an objective election in order to woo the far-right-wing voters, who vehemently oppose any “hand-over of Jewish land” to Goyem, e.g. to the Palestinians.

After all, it was the Israeli redeployment in the northern part of the West Bank two months ago that triggered the National Religious Party (NRP), an erstwhile junior-coalition partner in the Netanyahu Government, to abandon Netanyahu and, consequently, expedite the downfall of his Government.

The NRP, known as the Mifdal by its Hebrew acronym, enjoys the support of hundreds of thousands of messianic Jews who believe in the “integrity of the land of Israel”, including some 130,000 Jewish settlers living in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip wishing to see the peace process come to a speedy end.

Predictably, Netanyahu wants to receive their votes and the votes of their supporters in Israel proper. In order to be able to get their support, he has done what they like best, particularly endorsing more settlement expansion throughout the West Bank.
And this is what he precisely did, flying in the face of everybody else.

Consequently, he gave the go-ahead last week to build several high-rise buildings at Ras Al Amoud, in the heart of Arab East Jerusalem, as declared by the American Jewish financier Irvin Moscovitch, who reportedly will pay the bill.

Moscovitch, who toured the streets of East Jerusalem and Hebron on Tuesday, 11 January, pointed out that “we have the rights, we have the money and we have the permission, and the building will start right away.”

In addition, Netanyahu has ordered the opening of 12 new bypass roads in various parts of the West Bank to enable Jewish settlers to travel to and from their settlements without having to pass through Arab population centres.

Palestinian law-makers: PA frees thieves,
keeps political prisoners behind bars

Occupied Jerusalem: Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat ordered the release of an undisclosed number of political detainees who have been incarcerated for varied periods of time without charge or trial, PA sources said.

A PA police source in Gaza disclosed that an additional group of detainees would be freed on orders from Arafat. The police source didn’t mention any names, but said that “scores of prisoners were released.”

According to Nabil Amre, PA minister for Parliamentarian affairs, was quoted as saying recently that Arafat had promised to free “prisoners”.

However, Amre said that “those with serious charges will remain in custody.” The PA has in detention as many as 700 mostly Islamist activists who are critical of the so-called peace process. The vast majority of the detainees have not been accused of any wrongdoing, which explains the fact that the PA has not leveled any charges against them or brought them before a court of law.

Relatives of political detainees demonstrated outside the Legislative Council headquarters in Ramallah, demanding the release of the prisoners and accusing Arafat of “keeping them behind bars solely to satisfy Israel and the United States Central Intelligence Agency.”

Meanwhile, the Palestinian Legislative Council accused the Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat of deceiving the Palestinian public opinion by falsely claiming that political prisoners were released on the occasion of Eid-ul-Fitr while in reality the people who were released were mostly thieves and common criminals.

Several councilors pointed out during a Legislative Council session in Ramallah that the Palestinian Authority was imitating the Israeli occupation authorities in refraining from releasing political detainees while releasing car thieves and other common criminals.

The only difference between the two authorities, said the councilors, is that the Zionists charge and try Palestinian detainees whereas the PA keeps them incarcerated for prolonged periods of time without charge or trial.

Last week, the Legislative Council “warned” the PA against continuing to ignore the Council’s recommendations and decisions concerning political detainees in PA jails.

The Council recently renewed its appeals to Arafat to release the political prisoners, as they were on their fifth day of a general hunger strike to protest their illegal incarceration.

The Council said in a statement read on the “Voice of Palestine”, the official PA organ, that “political arrests must be terminated” and that “the dignity and authority of the law must be restored.”

Some councilors, however, doubted the ability of the Council to make any difference, due to “Arafat’s autocracy” and his “utter indifference to and even contempt of the Council and its decisions.”

In other developments reflecting the chaos permeating through the PA political polity, Legislative Council Speaker Ahmed Qrey declared that the term of the Council is open-ended.

Qrey said during a radio interview, “the Council will continue to function until the establishment of a Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital.”

According to the Oslo accords, under which the Legislative Council was elected, the term of the Council ends on 4 May 1999, when the interim agreement between the Zionist regime and the PLO expires.

Arafat and the Legislative Council were thus elected to a four-year term expiring on 4 May.

Observers note that PA Chairman Yasser Arafat is likely to “freeze until further notice” the political process, particularly the election process, within the autonomous enclaves until a true popularity-booster can be achieved, e.g. a tangible Israeli evacuation from the West Bank.

Arafat has canceled all municipal and local elections in the West Bank and the

Gaza Strip, mainly to forestall a victory by the Islamist opposition.

Sheikh Yassin: PA informs Israel about us

Occupied Jerusalem: Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin strongly lambasted Yasser Arafat’s Palestinian Authority for “acting at Israel’s beck and call” and for “arresting, torturing and extracting information from our activists, which is then passed on to the Zionists and the American Central Intelligence Agency.”

Speaking in an interview with the London-based al Wasat Arabic weekly, Yassin pointed out that “the PA is not serious about dialogue with the opposition; it uses this matter as a propaganda tactic and occasionally as a means of pressure on Israel.”

Yassin said the PA was blocking “our attacks against Zionist targets.”

“Whatever reaches the PA is passed on to Israel and the CIA”, said Sheikh Yassin. The Palestinian Islamist leader said Hamas was not interested in who wins the upcoming Israeli elections. “The Labor and the Likud differ only in appearances, but they are in full agreement on basic matters, especially on Palestinian rights.”

Finally, Yassin accused the PA of lacking any credible strategy to restore Palestinian rights, saying “they [the PA] thinks that the Zionists will become suddenly magnanimous and grant Palestinians their rights.”

“We must extract our rights”, he said, “because rights are not granted, they are extracted.”

Meanwhile, An Islamist leader in the Gaza Strip has said Hamas is likely to support a prospective declaration of Palestinian Statehood “provided that it be genuine, not just symbolic.”

Ismael Abu Shanab, a close aide to Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, said in an article published in the academic journal “Palestinian Politics”, that “a genuine Statehood declaration must aim at establishing Palestinian sovereignty on all PA-administered territory while considering the rest of the Palestinian land occupied territory to be liberated in due time.”

Abu Shanab pointed out that there ought to be real coordination with neighbouring Arab and Muslim countries, which he said was essential for the restoration of Palestinian rights. In addition, Abu Shanab called for “the re-formulation of the Palestinian polity, including the restructuring of Palestinian institutions on the basis of equality, equity and collective non-partisan participation in the decision-making process.”

Finally, Abu Shanab called on the PA to “dislodge itself from the shackles of Oslo, since the other side is already doing that.”

Palestinians to the PA: Release political prisoners now

Occupied Jerusalem- Several demonstrations, protests and sit-ins took place throughout the West Bank Sunday in protest against the continued incarceration of hundreds of Islamist activists by Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority police.
Palestinian sources said protests took place in Ramallah, Nablus and Bethlehem, where thousand of protesters shouted anti Arafat slogans and demanded the release of political prisoners.
In Nablus, scores of detainees' relatives held a sit-in outside the PA headquarters, former headquarters of the Israeli occupation army, protesting PA failure to release their beloved ones.
On Saturday, five thousand Hamas supporters held a huge rally at the Najah National University for the same purpose.
Sheikh Jamil Salim, who addressed the rally, lamented the fact that the "real patriots are behind bars and Zionist collaborators and spies are out in the street."
"What is the proper attributes and descriptions of an authority that imprisons true patriots and true fighters for freedom and at the same time let drug addicts, whoremongers, and Israeli collaborators and informers lead a normal life?" said Salim.
He pointed out that the PA has almost totally succumbed to CIA and Zionist dictates and demands, adding that "the authority of law in our county is the first and foremost victim of submission to the CIA will."
On Sunday, scores of Islamist political prisoners in Jericho joined their colleagues in Nablus in their hunger strike.
A spokesman of the Jericho prisoners said in a telephone interview from his detention center that senior PA security officials told the prisoners that "their release or non-release was contingent on Yasser Arafat…because this is a political not legal matter."
Earlier the prisoners wrote to Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub, General Intelligence chief Tawfik Tirawi and Police Chief Haj Ismael Jaber, demanding that they either be released or tried in a court of law.
Palestinian sources close to the PA suggested that the PA was increasingly embarrassed by this "scandal" (the outcry of political detainees).
However, the same sources added that there was little the PA could do, given Arafat's inability to resist American and Israeli dictates.
One source, who asked for anonymity said "Arafat is utterly weak before the Americans, and he constantly seeks a certificate of good conduct from them."
Faced with this situation, Hamas is expected to organize further demonstrations in the West Bank, which, the movement's leadership, hope would pressure the PA to release the prisoners and refrain from detaining Palestinians without charge or trial.
More to the point, the international commission of jurists, in cooperation with the Palestinian human rights organization al Qanun (the LAW) issued a statement Sunday criticizing the PA for "continuing to detain Palestinian citizens without charge or trial in violation the ninth paragraph of the Geneva Convention and the International Declaration of Human Rights.
The statement urged the PA leadership to "desist from flagrant detention and to immediately free all political prisoners."

Hamas: “All prisoners must be released”

Occupied Jerusalem: The Palestinian Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, has criticized the continued detention by PA police of several top Islamist leaders for voicing critical views of the peace process and PA violations of human rights. In a statement published in the West Bank, Hamas dismissed the release of a few detainees by the PA on the eve of Eid-ul-Fitr as “sheer propaganda”.

The four are Dr. Abdul Aziz al-Rantisi, Jamal Mansur, Dr. Ibrahim Makadmeh and Muhammed Jamal al Natshe. Arafat has repeatedly refused to release the four, claiming that “their release contravenes our international commitments.”

Occupation authorities demolish Arab home
in Occupied Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem: Israeli occupation authorities demolished an Arab home in Eleisawiya neighbourhood in East Jerusalem and seriously injured a Palestinian who protested the brutal act.

Palestinian sources said Israeli bulldozers demolished the 150 square metre one-storey building, belonging to Mahmud Issa Derbas, on the pretext of having been constructed without a license.

Israeli occupation soldiers opened fire and threw tear gas at Palestinian protesters, seriously injuring 38-year-old Zaki Nureddin, who was taken to Hadassa (Ein Karem) hospital in serious condition.

Palestinian sources said a bullet hit Nureddin in the head, causing him to lose consciousness.

Another person, a middle-aged woman, was also moderately injured by Zionist bullets.

Israel demolishes hundreds of residential Arab homes every year, as a reprisal for political or military resistance, or, as is often the case, to check Arab demographic expansion, particularly in the Jerusalem area.

Besides demolishing Arab homes, the occupation authorities resort to withdrawing residency rights from Palestinians in Jerusalem.

So far, as many 3,000 Palestinians in occupied Jerusalem were banished from their town as a result of this racist Zionist policy of ethnic cleansing.

Begin founds new Israeli party

Occupied Jerusalem: Benny Begin, an erstwhile Likud leader and son of Likud founder Menachem Begin, announced the formation of a new right-wing political party known as “Hirut (the original)”.

Begin, an extremely jingoistic figure, hoped the new party would once again reunite the right-wing camp in the upcoming Israeli elections. Begin vehemently opposed the Camp David accords, the Madrid Peace Conference, the Oslo accords and the Wye agreement. Indeed, the former minister opposes, as a matter of principle, any arrangements with the Palestinians or other Arabs involving Israeli withdrawal from any part of Palestine, which he considers “Israel’s biblical land”.

In fact, Begin openly advocates the undoing of the Oslo process, the dismantling of the Palestinian Authority and the reoccupation of the Palestinian towns and villages handed over to Yasser Arafat under the terms of the Oslo accords. Begin is expected to enjoy the support of the extremist right camp in Israel, particularly the settlers, their supporters and messianic Jews believing in the so-called “integrity of the land of Israel.”

American Jewish millionaire Irving Moskowitz reportedly promised Begin a million dollars if he succeeded in reuniting the “nationalist camp”. Begin is not expected to succeed as a prime ministerial candidate, but his party could win a number of seats in the next Israeli Knesset.

With Begin forming a new party, the Israeli political arena has become crowded with scores of political parties vying for the 120-seat Knesset.

On the other hand, the Palestinian Authority accused the American millionaire, Irving Moskowitz of leading settlement activities in the occupied territories. In a meeting it held in Ramallah last month, the Palestinian cabinet accused Moskowitz and Israeli extremist forces of intensifying settlement activities “under the guise” of the Israeli election campaign.

Moskowitz, a multi-millionaire from Miami of the United States, is a major sponsor of settlement activity in occupied East Jerusalem and gives money to settlers in Hebron. “This settlement onslaught is led by the American Moskowitz in an obvious transgression on the rights of the Palestinian people”, the cabinet said in a statement distributed by the Palestinian news agency “Wafa”.

Israel’s Chief Rabbi: Reformed Jews are worse than Nazis

Occupied Jerusalem: Yossi Sarid, leader of the leftist Israeli party Meretz, strongly criticized Israel’s chief Rabbi Elyahu Bakshi Doron for equating reformed Jews to Nazis, describing the statement as “detestable and despicable”.

Sarid urged Doron to withdraw the statement, which he said “put to shame all Jews.”

Doron was quoted as saying “reformists [reformed Jews] are doing to the Jewish people today what the holocaust did to us 50 years ago.”

Doron accused reformed Judaism of seeking to redefine Jewish identity in ways that could result in the assimilation of remaining Jews and obliteration of Judaism.

He cited reformists’ support for mixed marriages, saying this alone could end up in a colossal disaster for the Jewish people.

The vast majority of the world’s 13 million Jews are affiliated with reformed and conservative sects.

The population of Orthodox Jews the world over does not exceed one million. However, the Orthodox Jews control, to a very large extent, religious life in the Zionist regime, including the influential High Rabbinate, which oversees all matters relating to marriage, conversion and burial.

So far, Israeli lawmakers have not been able to agree on a definition of “who is a Jew.” The High Rabbinate, however, is still insisting on a biological definition of “Jewishness”, arguing that according to Halacha (Jewish religious law), a Jew is one whose mother is Jewish, irrespective of the religion or race of the father.

Arafat weighs options on Statehood

Hebron- From Khalid Amayreh (25 January): Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat has been weighing his options for the next few months which will witness two important dates: the expiration of the term of the interim agreement with Israel, and the Israeli general elections slated for 17 May.

Although Arafat has not made a final decision on the vital subject of declaring Statehood, he has indicated on several occasions that he will be eventually ready to postpone the declaration of Statehood if he receives certain guarantees, or, more correctly, assurances, from Israel and the international communities, particularly the United States, supporting the Palestinians’ right to Statehood.

Observers noted that in his speeches of late, Arafat began to refer to “4 May” as merely “an important date” or “a date that can’t be ignored”, suggesting that he might be willing to let it pass quietly without making the pivotal decision he had been expected to make.

Palestinian sources close to Arafat suggested on 24 January that Arafat would be willing to postpone indefinitely the Statehood declaration if Israel met two essential Palestinian demands: First, a complete freeze on Jewish settlement expansion in the West Bank; and second, the implementation of the hapless Wye River agreement, frozen by Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu ostensibly for election considerations.

Arafat reportedly made his demands to EU officials, who told him that it was next to impossible to get the current Israeli Government to agree to these demands while in the middle of a turbulent election campaign.

European officials, Palestinian sources said, told Arafat that a declaration of Statehood on 4 May would spell disaster for the peace process and push the Israeli Prime Minister to take drastic action, including the possible annexation of much of the West Bank.

Arafat probably knows that he has virtually no chance of getting the Europeans, or for that matter the Americans, to convince Netanyahu to accede , even vaguely, to demands that even Yitzhak Rabin had never truly accepted, namely the freezing of Jewish settlement building. If so, then why would Arafat make demands he knew well they had no chance of being accepted by the Israelis?

The answer is simple, Arafat wants “a reward” compensating and warranting the postponement and that can only be obtained from the United States and the EU.

Indeed, this is what the Palestinian leader will be looking for during his current visit to the United States and Europe.

Arafat will be arguing , rightly, that the fourth of May 1999 is not a date that the Palestinians chose or willed and that, in any case, the interim agreement will come to an end by that date, necessitating measures to be taken.

It is perceived that the Europeans and the Americans will just tell Arafat to “swallow his pride” and unceremoniously postpone the Statehood declaration until after the Israeli elections.

After all, the Americans and the EU countries are well aware of Arafat’s limited options, so the general feeling is that he won’t put up much of a resistance.

However, Arafat must have “something” with which to maintain his credibility and convince the Palestinian public opinion to accept the postponement.

This “something” could take the form of “a promise” from the American administration to support the Palestinian declaration date at a later but undesignated date.

According to the latest opinion polls in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, roughly 50% of the Palestinians believe that a postponement of Statehood was necessary in order to deprive Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu of the opportunity to use the issue to woo Jewish voters to re-elect him as Prime Minister.

This should make it easier for Arafat to “bite the bullet”, at least until after 17 May, when the Israelis would decide either to elect a new Prime Minister or to keep Benyamin Netanyahu in office.

PA furious with BBC
Finally, the PA has been furious with the BBC for its recent investigative documentary entitled “Arafat’s Authority: the Real Story”. Yasser Abed Rabbu, the PA Information Minister, attacked the television documentary, saying “it besmirched the image of the PA and my own”, adding that “most of what it contained was baseless.” He said that “my part of the story was never presented”, and that “the BBC never phoned him to get his opinion and his response to the allegations mentioned in the programme.”

The BBC documentary sought to expose the financial and administrative corruption, which many observers and journalists contend is rampant throughout Yasser Arafat’s administration. The documentary noted the Legislative Council report on the embezzlement and squandering of hundreds of million of dollars, pointing out that among the accused was Abed Rabbu, hence his fury at the programme.

Abed Rabbu, ostensibly one of the most liberal-minded members of Arafat’s Government, demanded that the dissemination and distribution of the documentary be stopped immediately. He also demanded that the BBC apologize to him personally and to his boss, Yasser Arafat.

Yasser Abed Rabbu (meaning Yasser the servant of God) is often referred to by many Palestinians as “Yasser Abed Yasser”, meaning “Yasser the servant of Yasser” (Arafat), connoting his subservience to the PA Chairman.

PA is committed to Democracy, but to a certain extent

Occupied Jerusalem: A Palestinian Authority official, who oversees Palestinian media compliance with censorship rules, said in a lecture in the Palestinian town of al-Nasira that “we are committed to democracy, but to a certain extent.” Speaking during a seminar on “Freedom of the press within the autonomous enclaves”, Ziad Abdul Fattah said, “democracy is a relative value, and we cannot deal with it as an absolute matter.”

Abdul Fattah, President of the official PA “Wafa news agency”, admitted that “there are mistakes and excesses”, adding that “these things happen everywhere.” He accused human rights groups and foreign media of “seeking to besmirch our image…and that is why they are constantly putting us under the microscope.”

Abdul Fattah, however, sought to evade a question on “the incarceration of political critics” within the autonomous enclaves. He said he knew that political detention, torture and human rights violations were inconsistent and incompatible with democracy, but added that “our situation is still not conducive to the establishment of full democracy.”

Abdul Fattah (some call him de facto minister of information) reportedly issued orders to close several private television stations in the West Bank for “over-covering anti-American demonstrations in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip.”

Israel to build 400 new flats in East Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem: Israeli sources said a Jewish contractor was planning to build as many as 400 flats in East Jerusalem. The sources said the contractor, Yehuda Levy, proprietor of the construction company Degel Investments, would build the flats in the Silwan neighbourhood.

In the last few years, the Zionist regime intensified its efforts to seize Arab land in the Silwan area, which some Jews call the “City of David”. In this context, several Jewish societies put their hands on as many as 30 Arab homes, which have been used as “focal points” of further Judaization of the Palestinian neighbourhood.

There are an estimated 180,000 Palestinians currently living in East Jerusalem and nearly as many living in neighbourhoods that have not been annexed by the Zionist occupation authorities.

Jewish murderer receives “rehabilitation
for a year” for murdering a Palestinian

Occupied Jerusalem: Consistent with the racist judicial system in the Zionist regime, a Zionist court has sentenced a Jewish murderer of a Palestinian to “one year in a rehabilitation centre.” The murderer, a settler from the small Jewish settlement of Beit Hagay near al Khalil, was convicted of premeditated murder in 1998 for his responsibility in the killing of Majid Abu Turki, 50, by way of smacking an iron rod against his head. The settler claimed that he killed Turki unintentionally and that he was “entertaining”.

Normally, the Zionist regime adopts two sets of laws in the Occupied West Bank and the Gaza Strip: one harsh and sinister for the Arabs and another benign and liberal for Jewish settlers. This double-faced “legal polity”, say human rights organizations, allows Jewish terrorists to murder Palestinians virtually with impunity.
