Albright fails to bridge gaps between Israel and PA

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khaled Amayreh

In her thirty-third visit to Palestine on 28 June, U.S. Secretary of State Madeline Albright failed again to bridge the wide gaps between Israel and the Palestinian Authority on the paramount final status issues such as the fate of Jerusalem, the refugees and the borders.

Albright met separately with Israeli and Palestinian officials on 28 June but without making any breakthroughs.

Following her first meeting with Barak, the Israeli Prime Minister reiterated his refusal to pull out of the West Bank. “We will not return to the borders of 1967; the Palestinians want 100% of the territory, and this is unacceptable to us.”

In Ramallah, Albright urged PA Chairman Yasser Arafat to “make sacrifices for peace,” which observers surmise as a tacit pressure on the Palestinian leader for further concessions to the Zionist regime.

However, Arafat this time refused to budge.

“They carried out UN resolutions 242 on the Egyptian and Jordanian fronts, and there is a similar agreement on the Syrian front; they carried out UN resolution 425 on the Lebanese front, and we demand they do the same on the Palestinian front,” said Arafat following his meeting with Albright.

The main thrust of Albright’s visit to Palestine was to weigh the prospects of holding a make-or-break U.S.-Israeli-PA marathon summit to hammer out a framework agreement on final status issues, a penultimate step before the signing of a peace treaty between Israel and the Palestinians.

However, it was apparent from the beginning that the gaps separating the two sides on such fundamental issues as Jerusalem, the refugees and future borders between Israel and a prospective Palestinian State were too wide to be bridged.

Even before Albright’s arrival in Palestine, U.S. Envoy Dennis Ross, who has been shuttling between West Jerusalem and Ramallah in an effort to narrow these gaps, admitted on Tuesday that the two sides were still far apart and that the gaps “were still very wide.”

Earlier, Israel appeared to be pushing hard for a summit in Washington ostensibly in the hope that the U.S. would wrest further concessions from Chairman Arafat, given President Clinton’s diminishing ability to pressure Israel as a result of domestic American considerations.

The Palestinian leadership, nonetheless, has thus far remained opposed to holding such a summit, especially in the absence of real progress on the outstanding fundamental issues.

“Summits are held when most issues are threshed out and when only small differences need to be ironed out by the leaders. In our case, there are still very wide gaps separating the two sides and these need to be bridged before a summit can be held,” said PA negotiator Ahmed Qrei.

Moreover, PA presidential spokesman Nabil Abu Rudaineh questioned Israeli motives behind the aggressive pushing for a summit meeting in Washington.

“The Israelis want the summit in order to pressure the Palestinian leadership into making concessions and signing an agreement. We know their game–each time they don’t get anywhere with our negotiators, they try to deal directly with Chairman Arafat.”

The Palestinians insist that Israel carry out the outstanding issues of the interim agreement, including the paramount third phase of redeployment and the release of Palestinian prisoners.

Israel refuses to do so, insisting on merging the implementation of the remaining interim issues with the final settlement. The reason is to keep the PA in an inferior negotiating position vis-à-vis Israel and to minimize Israeli “concessions” to the Palestinians in the context of the final settlement.

The Palestinians are aware of Israeli tactics to that effect.

Palestinian negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat scolded “these Israeli manouevres, which will go nowhere.”

“Israel must realize that without full Israeli withdrawal to the 4 June 1967 borders in fulfillment of UN resolutions 242 and 338 as well as the resolution of the refugees’ cause in accordance with UN resolution 194, there can be no agreement and there can be no settlement.”

Clearly, the Israeli government rejects this Palestinian position and refuses to recognize the said UN resolutions as the basis for a final settlement of the conflict. Instead, it seeks aggressively to cajole the Palestinian Authority into agreeing to a “compromise” whereby the Jewish State would retain control of Jerusalem and large chunks of the West Bank while the PA would effectively cede the Palestinian refugees’ right to return to their former towns and villages from which they were expelled when Israel was created in 1948.

Needless to say, a deal as such would be rejected outright by the Palestinian people. In fact, Arafat himself reportedly intimated to President Clinton that should he sign a peace agreement excluding Jerusalem, his own life would be in danger.

Meanwhile, Barak, having labouriously succeeded in patching up his government following the recent crisis with the ultra-orthodox Shas movement, found temporary solace when the Knesset voted on Tuesday, 27 June, to rescind a bill that would have dissolved the Knesset and led to early general elections in Israel.

Nevertheless, new coalition rifts were looming again, underscoring the fragility of Barak’s government and its inability to take tough decisions on the peace process.

Shas itself, and despite the excessive concessions it extracted from Barak as a price for staying in the government, has again threatened to quit the coalition if the transfer of promised funds to its corrupt-ridden school system is not made immediately.

Moreover, Israeli Interior Minister Natan Sharansky also threatened to quit the government in case Barak decides to attend a Washington summit with Arafat without illustrating Israel’s red lines beforehand.

“Based on what I heard today, if a decision is made to hold a summit and the Prime Minister goes (to the summit), I will leave the government,” Sharansky reportedly said at a meeting of the Yisrael Ba’liya bureau Tuesday evening.

Even Foreign Minister David Levy indicated that his alliance with Barak could be terminated if the Israeli premier offered the Palestinians “far-reaching concessions.”

“We have offered the maximum without getting even the minimum from the Palestinians. The question is ‘has Arafat understood the need to make concessions?’ I don’t think so, and I am doubtful there will be a summit,” said Levy during an Israeli television show Tuesday night.

Albright’s visit was preceded by a plethora of hard-line, even vitriolic statements from both sides.

Responding to statements made a couple of weeks earlier by Israeli chief of staff General Shaul Mofaz in which the Israeli general threatened to use tanks and helicopter gunships to quell possible Palestinian protests in case the peace process collapsed, Arafat retorted saying, “We will not be scared by these threats. I remind those who forget of the Karama battle, Beirut and the intifadah.”

Several officials from both sides joined the verbal duel, which underscored the potential volatility of Israeli-Palestinian relations, with Levy vowing to “defend Israel’s red lines,” and PA official Ahmed Abdul Rahman urging Jewish settlers to “leave our land with your bulldozers because this is what you will have to do sooner or later.”

Hamas, too, stepped into the fray, vowing to “set Israel ablaze if the Israeli army attacked our people.”

The war of words, needless to say, underscores the volatility of Palestinian-Israeli relations and the potential for an outbreak of violence in case a breakthrough is not reached.

And with the ostensible American failure to get Israel to abide by UN resolutions pertaining to the Palestinian question, and with the Palestinian leadership seemingly determined to declare Palestinian Statehood in September–unilaterally or otherwise–it would be quite safe to presume that the region’s summer will be very hot, indeed.

PA continues onslaught of free media

Ramallah (West Bank)- Special to PT

In an effort to stifle further criticisms against secret talks with Israel, the Palestinian Authority has stepped up its campaign against independent and non-conformist media in the West Bank.

The latest measures included the closure of several radio and television stations in the West Bank as well as the arrest of a number of Palestinian journalists for “incitement and speaking ill of the Palestinian leadership” and also “for endangering Palestinian national interests.”

The clamp-down mainly targeted the media outlets that “didn’t go with the flow” and that refused to practice the crippling self-censorship which has virtually completely emasculated the Palestinian press and made it innocuous at best.

The new spate of suppression started on 21 May when agents from the Criminal Investigation Department (CID), one of Yasser Arafat’s estimated 13 security agencies, ordered Omar Nazzal, director of the Ramallah-based private television station (al-Watan), to cease broadcasting immediately.

The PA gave no reasons for the closure, but the proprietor suggested it had to do with comments voiced by a viewer during a phone-in show in which the viewer scoffed at remarks made earlier by PA Chairman Yasser Arafat discounting the victory of Hizbullah in South Lebanon.

Arafat had said in an interview with Israeli channel 2 television that the Israeli withdrawal from South Lebanon was prompted by Israel’s desire to carry out United Nations resolution 425 and had nothing to do with Hizbullah resistance.

The remarks infuriated most Palestinians who used words such as “stupid” and “sycophantic” to describe them, prompting the PA-controlled media to reassert Palestinian solidarity with “the Lebanese national resistance.”

On 23 May, as it was clear that no laws were violated, the PA Ministry of Information allowed Nazzal to resume broadcasting. However, when he did, CID agents stormed al-Watan’s studio in downtown Ramallah, ordering all employees to leave the building or else they would be arrested.

When Nazzal sought to tell the heavily armed force that the Ministry of Information had given him permission to broadcast, the police simply replied that they did not recognize the Ministry’s authority.

The station was eventually allowed to reopen on 24 May, apparently after Marwan al-Barghouthi, head of Fatah’s Supreme Council in the West Bank, interceded with some influential PA officials on Nazzal’s behalf.

The closure of al-Watan marked the fifth of its kind since it began broadcasting in 1996.

The clamp-down continued on 30 May when the same CID agents raided the studios and offices of the private television station al-Nasser and its sister radio station, al-Manara in Ramallah, again for allowing criticisms of the secret talks between Israel and the PA in Stockholm.

The criticisms were reportedly made during a talk show when a Palestinian Legislative Council member suggested that a new Palestinian leadership might be necessary for the attainment of Palestinian rights and avoiding a sell-out of these rights to Israel by the current Palestinian leadership, a prospective that is increasingly dreaded by many Palestinians, intellectuals and commoners alike.

“This is a grave violation of basic press freedom; they (the PA) are trying to replicate Arab dictatorships here by fostering an atmosphere of fear and terror whereby the citizens refrain from criticizing the government,” said al-Nasser’s director, Ammar Ammar, in reaction to the closure.

The closure of al-Nasser was widely criticized by democratic-minded groups and human rights organizations as well as by many Palestinian journalists.

However, the PA seemed utterly unmoved by these criticisms.

This was demonstrated on 1 June, when CDI agents closed down the al-Mahed television station in Bethlehem and arrested its owner, Samir Qumsiyeh.

Qumsiyeh, who is also the director of the Union of Private TV and Radio Station owners, had earlier circulated a memo asking member stations to replace their regular programmes for half an hour with a message protesting PA suppression of the press.

The arrest of Qumsiyeh and the closure of his television outlet carried the following message to the Palestinian public, namely that not only critics of Arafat and his policies will be suppressed, but also anybody else protesting this suppression, whoever he might be.

PA police had earlier arrested Fathi Barqawi, Chief News Editor at the Voice of Palestine, the official PA radio station, for “incitement and speaking ill of the Palestinian leadership.”

Barqawi was arrested in his hometown of Tulkarm reportedly after voicing criticisms of the Stockholm talks and alluding that Arafat was about to embark on making “deep concessions” to Israel on paramount issues such as the future of Jerusalem and the fate of some 4 million Palestinian refugees expelled from their homes when Israel was created in 1948.

On 6 June, as many as ten Palestinian political organizations, including Fatah and Hamas, circulated a leaflet in the West Bank criticizing “transgressions against press freedom in violation of the law.”

The leaflet urged the Palestinian leadership to rescind all measures against media outlets and demanded that the security agencies be reined-in in order to facilitate the establishment of civil society in Palestine.

On 17 June, hundreds of Palestinians from various political factions, including Hamas, marched through the streets of Ramallah to protest the continued onslaught on the media and political dissent.

The marchers carried placards reading “We didn’t struggle for a police state” and “Where is the rule of law?”

Moreover, the protesters demanded the immediate release of all political prisoners who are being detained without charge or trial in PA jails for criticizing the Palestinian leadership and the behaviour of its numerous security agencies.

Suppression of media seen as forewarning

Indeed, many Palestinians seem now convinced that the latest spate of suppression against the non-conformist media is merely a pre-warning to the Palestinian people at large by Arafat that any active opposition to a prospective unpopular deal with Israel will be crushed mercilessly by security agencies.

But this is not the end of the story.

For if a scenario as such came about, the PA security agencies would not be up against just the traditional opposition groups such as the Islamic movements and some still-defiant leftist factions, but would have also to face many followers of Fatah who would view a deal as such as an outright treason and betrayal of the Palestinian national struggle.

And as the recent unrest in the occupied territories demonstrated, Arafat can take nothing for granted, including Fatah’s unconditional loyalty.

Palestinians ambivalent about Assad’s death

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

Most Palestinians reacted with marked ambivalence and apathy to the death on 10 June of Syrian President Hafez al Assad at age 70.

At the official level, PA President Yasser Arafat, who is also 70 years old, issued a terse statement, eulogizing the Syrian leader and offering “our deep condolences to the Syrian people and the Arab nation.”

Moreover, Arafat declared a token three-day period of mourning throughout the autonomous enclaves, and the PA official radio station, the Voice of Palestine, broadcast recitations from the Holy Qur’an.

Arafat did attend Assad’s funeral, reportedly after Egyptian President Husni Mubarak interceded with the Syrians to allow Arafat to go to Damascus.

However, the clearly lukewarm reception that Arafat found upon his arrival suggested that the Syrians are not going to change their mind about Arafat anytime soon.

Assad looked down on Arafat with disdain and even contempt. He never wasted a chance to scold the Palestiniann leader for his “capitulatory agreements” with Israel, often arguing that Israel’s humiliating treatment of the PA vindicated his own uncompromising stance on the Golan Heights as well as his stubborn rejection of Oslo-styled deals with Israel.

Arafat had been trying hard to patch relations with Assad but to no avail. His self-humiliating sycophantic approach to Damascus as well as his numerous emissaries failed to get “the Lion of Damascus” to relent.

In fact, the Syrian-PA relations deteriorated considerably in the few months preceding Assad’s death.

A few months ago, Syrian Defense Minister Mustafa Tlass publicly scolded Arafat for “selling-out Jerusalem to the Zionists,” calling him the “son of 60,000 bitches.”

However, it is unlikely that Arafat’s participation in Assad’s funeral will result in an early thaw in Syrian-PA relations.

The would-be Syrian leader, Bashar, will be busy for months to come consolidating his power base and dealing with other more pressing priorities, and relations with Arafat are unlikely to become a real preoccupation for the new Assad of Damascus.

At the popular level, most ordinary Palestinians did not seem to be greatly saddened by Assad’s death.

“He will face the people he had killed in Hama and Lebanon,” said a Hebron taxi driver, alluding to the thousands of Syrian Islamists and their relatives crushed by the Syrian army in the city of Hama in 1982 following a brief rebellion.

“Of course, we are not really mourning after all that Assad did to the Palestinians,” a Fatah activist remarked.

In fact, most ordinary Palestinians felt that no tears ought to be shed for leaders such as Assad, who came to symbolize brutal despotism in the Arab world.

Still, other Palestinians were amazed by the speed in which Assad’s son, Bashar, was being groomed to succeed his father.

A columnist who writes regularly for the semi-official daily Al-Ayyam remarked on 11 June that “the kingdom-republic of Syria will have a new King-President.”

Aside from that, however, many Palestinians seemed to harbour certain hopes that the new leadership in Damascus will make drastic reforms, particularly in the field of upholding civil liberties and human rights.

Such hopes, which are not unrealistic given Bashar’s ongoing campaign against corruption, might be justified to a certain degree given that any democratic inroads in any Arab country would eventually spread to the rest of the Arab world, sooner or later.

Palestinians also seemed to hope that the new Assad of Damascus (Assad means lion) will insist on recovering in full the Syrian Golan Heights, seized by Israel in 1967, when his father was then Defense Minister.

The logic here is that if Syria insisted on recovering all its territories, in accordance with United Nations resolution 242, and Israel complied, the Palestinians would be in a stronger position to demand parity with Syria, namely full Israeli withdrawal from the West Bank and Gaza Strip pursuant to the same resolution.

Indeed, Assad’s uncompromising stance vis-à-vis the Golan, and his persistent resistance to Israel’s dictates are among the few things of Assad’s legacy that most Palestinians now remember with admiration, especially when put in contrast with Yasser Arafat’s often obsequious approach vis-à-vis Israel.

Hamas expresses sorrow

Hamas has expressed profound sorrow at the death of Syrian President Hafez al Assad.

The movement’s leadership issued a statement today, offering condolences to Assad’s son, Bashar, his family, the Syrian people and the Arab nation.

The statement said Hamas was shocked by the news of Assad’s death, calling the deceased Syrian leader “a great Arab leader.”

The statement also highlighted Assad’s “unceasing support for the Palestinian cause and solid determination to recover the entirety of the Golan Heights.”

“On this occasion, we remember the deceased’s enduring steadfastness in the face of Zionist and American pressure, as well as his support for the Islamic resistance in southern Lebanon which was instrumental in enabling the Lebanese people to liberate their land.”

Hamas maintains an office in Damascus, and one of its top officials, Musa Abu Marzuq, resides also in the Syrian capital, having been deported from Jordan.

PA escalates rhetoric

Gaza- PT

Given the peace process with Israel facing a virtual deadlock, if not a dead end, Palestinian Authority officials have been seeking to galvanize the public mood in the West Bank and Gaza Strip in advance of the seemingly likely declaration of Palestinian Statehood on 13 September.

On 25 June, while addressing thousands of his Fatah supporters in the town of Nablus, Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat declared that the “next few weeks are going to be the most sensitive and crucial for the Palestinian people because we will declare our Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital and whoever doesn’t like it, let him drink from the Dead Sea.”

Arafat added, amid a crescendo of applause, that “our people will not be scared–not by tanks, not by aircrafts–and we remind those who doubt our resolve of the battle of al-Karama and the intifadah.”

Arafat’s remarks were in response to recent statements by the Israeli Chief of Staff, Shaul Mofaz, in which he–the Israeli general–warned that the Israeli army would use tanks and helicopter gun ships to quell possible Palestinian protests in case the peace process collapsed.

Moreover, it is also believed that the verbal escalation on Arafat’s part, which coincided with the arrival of U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright in Palestine-Israel on 27 June, is aimed mainly at enhancing the PA negotiating position by demonstrating to the Israelis, and the Americans as well, that the PA has other options and that Arafat couldn’t sign a dismal final agreement with Israel that would betray Palestinian aspirations and rights.

However, this tactic of arousing the masses’ expectations could be somewhat risky for Arafat, especially if he decides at one point that he would accept less than a 100% Israeli withdrawal from the territories Israel seized in 1967 and only a symbolic implementation of the refugees’ right of return.

Actually, there are many signs that he would accept a compromise, though he insists that it be as favourable to him as possible.

In his Nablus speech, the Palestinian leader made a passing but significant remark which his aroused audience apparently failed to comprehend its real significance. Arafat said he would not accept the Israeli offer of receiving “just 75% of the West Bank,” which suggested that he might, even would, accept a compromise somewhere between 75% and 100%.

Needless to say, a compromise as such, which would also exclude Jerusalem and the right of return, would be utterly rejected by the bulk of the Palestinian nationalist camp, e.g. Fatah, in addition, of course, to the Islamist and leftist camps. And it would be difficult for Arafat to contain this rejection and keep it in the verbal sphere.

Even Arafat himself, who appealed to the Americans not to pressure him further into making additional concessions to Israel, reportedly intimated to President Clinton during his last visit to Washington that his life would be in danger if he crossed the Palestinian national red lines.

If true, this suggests that Arafat may be willing eventually to accommodate at least some of the Israeli demands, particularly on the refugee issue, but that he is worried about a strong, unfavourable popular reaction from people that could corrode his leadership.

Last week, Arafat ordered his police to arrest his own advisor on refugees, Abdul Fattah Ghanem, upon his return from Amman.

Ghanem reportedly leaked to Palestinian opposition groups information suggesting that the Palestinian leadership was coming to terms with Israel’s rejectionist stance on the refugees’ right of return.

Last month, Arafat’s security chief in Gaza, Muhammed Dahlan told a prominent refugee committee in Gaza that the refugee cause was no longer a problem since most of them didn’t want to return to their homes in what is now Israel.

Arafat later distanced himself from these statements by merely saying that “the Palestinian position on the refugees remains unchanged.”

All in all, the conflicting signals Arafat is giving to the masses publicly, and the Israelis and the Americans privately, underscores the dilemma he is facing, namely the fact that the requirements of the “peace process” as understood by the Americans and the Israelis are incompatible, even irreconcilable, with the most basic Palestinian aspirations.

Hamas warns Israel and U.S.

Meanwhile, Hamas has warned Israel and the United States against imposing any kind of agreement on the Palestinians which fails to fulfill their legitimate rights.

Hamas founder and leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin told reporters in Gaza on 26 June that the Palestinian people would reject any such agreement even if Yasser Arafat put his signature to it.

Responding to Israeli threats to repress further Palestinian protests, Yassin said, “We will set Israel ablaze if the Israeli army attacks Palestinians.”

Yassin argued that Israel’s rejection of Palestinian rights and insistence on ignoring the refugees’ right of return and retaining occupied East Jerusalem vindicated Hamas’ opposition to the peace process.

He added that “the only language Israel understands is the language of force.”

More to the point, Yassin tacitly warned Arafat against giving in to Israeli and American pressure, saying the issue of Jerusalem was an Islamic cause and that the Palestinian leadership had no right to make compromises on the fate of the town.

Interestingly, Hamas of late has collaborated with Fatah and some leftist groups in urging the PA to release political prisoners who are interned without charge or trial, often for criticizing PA political despotism and corruption.

This cooperation yielded positive results last week when Arafat ordered the release of Omar Assaf, a teacher unionist affiliated with the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine.

Assaf was arrested nearly two months ago for accusing the PA of embezzling funds which the donor countries had allocated for the teachers.

Sheikh Yassin: Palestinians will not
accept a capitulatory agreement

Gaza - Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin warned Israel and the United States against imposing a “capitulatory agreement” on the Palestinian people, saying Palestinians will not accept any agreement that would fail to meet their aspirations and just demands.

Yassin, who was speaking to reporters in Gaza on Monday, 26 June, said while the U.S. and the Zionist regimes might succeed in cajoling PA Chairman Yasser Arafat into accepting such an agreement, the Palestinian people would reject it out of hand and consider it null and void.

Yassin said Jerusalem was not a Palestinian or an Arab issue, but an Islamic issue since the holy Islamic places are located there. He argued that no Arab or Islamic leader had the right to cede Muslim rights to Jerusalem.

“Even all the Palestinians and all the Arabs don’t own the right to give up Jerusalem, the third holiest Islamic place in the world.” Yassin pointed out that Israel’s rejection of Palestinian rights vindicated Hamas’ opposition to the peace process.

He said that only a peace based on justice can survive, adding that Israel will not remain strong forever and Muslims won’t remain weak forever. He pointed out that “we don’t fight Jews for being Jews, but rather because they happen to be our oppressor.”

“I would fight my own brother if he took over my home,” he said. Meanwhile, Sheikh Yassin said Hamas would not participate in the meeting of the PLO Central Council scheduled to take place in Gaza earlier in the month of July.

“We are not participating in a meeting that is based on the Oslo Accords.” Yassin said, as a matter of principle, Hamas won’t take part in the Council’s meetings “because all these meetings are based on the Oslo Agreement.”

He added that if the Council decided to rid the Palestinian people of Oslo, then Hamas would take part in it meetings. Yassin said the problem facing the Palestinian people was not the absence of Statehood but the presence of the occupation. “The PLO had declared Statehood several times….the problem is that this Statehood lacks sovereignty and substance.”

German embassy in Amman opens
the door for Palestinian immigration

Gaza- An official in the Palestinian refugees’ popular committees in Gaza Strip disclosed that the German embassy in Amman had recently opened the door for immigration of Palestinian refugees.

Abdullah Al-Arabeed said that 35 Palestinian families had immigrated to Germany last May. He also warned against the purchase of refugees’ status cards by certain parties.

Arabeed, head of the popular committees executive bureau in the Gaza Strip, said that such practice fell in line with attempts to liquidate the Palestinian refugee’s issue, which is one of the most important topics in the final status talks along with Jerusalem, Jewish settlements, sovereignty, borders and the Palestinian state.

He criticized the Palestinian Authority’s stand vis-à-vis the refugees question, describing it as “ambiguous”. His statements were voiced during a seminar organized by Kana’an developmental institute at its premises in Gaza city.

Arabeed accused PA officials of openly declaring insistence on the refugees right of return while in secret or open talks (with the Zionists) they discuss ending the refugees’ problem through depriving them from the right of returning to their homeland based on different scenarios and projects.

He stressed importance of the role of the refugees’ popular committees in combating attempts to surrender the right of return that was included in the international resolution 194.

For his part, Abdullah Al-Horani, head of the national center for studies and documentation has considered the right of return as a sacred right that did not need any persuasion for it was a legitimate right guaranteed by international laws.

He warned the Palestinian negotiator against signing any agreement that would not include the national stable rights and that would not ensure a dignified return and deserved compensation to all refugees.

Horani warned against rumours that attempts to spread the illusion among the refugees and the public opinion on the impossibility of the refugees’ return to the Palestinian lands occupied in 1948. He charged that such a matter would deprive more than a million refugees in the West Bank and Gaza Strip from returning to their homes along with another quarter of a million refugees scattered in the Negev desert and the Galilee.

He also cautioned against propaganda statements that were intentionally leaked every now and then on the re-settlement of refugees, and added that the Palestinian people might understand the Palestinian negotiator’s inability to secure the right of return and all national rights but would never allow surrendering them altogether.

1,000 Palestinian refugees at the
Lebanese borders demand their return

Beirut - Around 1,000 Palestinian refugees have affirmed their insistence on returning to Palestine and their rejection of any concession of their rights and property.

The declaration was made during the “return trip” organized by the Islamic League for Palestine students for 1,000 Palestinian refugees from refugee camps in Lebanon.

The refugees, who gathered at the Fatima gate, offered their prayers then listened to the head of the Islamic League’s council who expressed pleasure over the great Lebanese victory against the Zionist occupation.

He asserted the refugees’ insistence on their right of return to Palestine, and underscored that the refugee camps in Lebanon did not assign any party to speak, negotiate or surrender on their behalf.

He emphasized that the refugees in Lebanon affirm that they would grab their rights using any means, announcing that the Palestinians opt for struggle to regain their usurped rights.

Water crisis in West Bank, Gaza exacerbates

Occupied Jerusalem - The water crisis in the Palestinian autonomous enclaves has exacerbated further since May forcing Palestinian citizens in large cities and small hamlets alike to seek water from old contaminated wells dating back to the Ottoman era.

The severity of the crisis has expressed itself in daily scenes of Palestinian citizens, young and old, walking the streets seeking to fill their empty bottles and other plastic containers.

Even the main hospital in Ramallah, the unofficial West Bank capital of the PA, was without water for several hours during mid-June having had its water supply severed owing to reduced pumping by the Israeli authorities who control water supplies to the PA enclaves.

Israel arrogates the lion’s share of water in the West Bank and leaves negligible amounts for the Palestinians.

According to human rights organizations, the average Palestinian citizen in the West Bank and Gaza receives an annual water share of 140 cubic metres while the average Israeli receives 560 cubic metres. The water shortage in the West Bank is particularly acute in the southern region where the vast majority of Palestinians purchase water from the black market.

Israeli interior minister warns of growing
Islamic trends among the Negev Bedouins

Occupied Jerusalem - Israeli interior minister Nathan Sharansky has warned against growing Islamic trends among the Negev desert Bedouins, especially in the area surrounding Beer Sheba.

Sharansky, who was speaking after a meeting with members of the parliament’s interior committee, said that there was reason for concern over activities of the young men in that area.

He charged the leadership of the Islamic Movement within the Green Line of inciting the Bedouins into rejecting Israeli laws. He described the victory of an Islamist with the chairmanship of Rahet town’s municipality as a big turning point that should be seriously considered by the Hebrew State.

The minister elaborated that the Bedouins, who were until recently serving in the Israeli army, were now being categorized as the most fanatic Arab groups among the Palestinians.

Sharansky called on Zionist premier Ehud Barak and his ministers to adopt a new policy towards those Bedouins by means of encouraging the secular trends within them and boosting their status to restore the masses’ confidence.

Settlers pump sewer water into Palestinian groves

Bethlehem - Many farmers in the Khadder village have reported that large quantities of their summer crops were damaged because settlers from the nearby Efrat settlement had pumped their sewerage water into the Palestinian groves.

Farmers said that hundreds of dunums planted with various kinds of vegetables were damaged, noting that the practice was a yearly habit.

However, they said that this year’s losses were doubled and estimated their losses at around $5,000. They appealed to international agencies to interfere and put an end to such practices, which aimed at forcing the farmers to evacuate their lands so that those settlers would annex them.
