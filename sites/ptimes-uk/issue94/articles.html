University elections put Hamas among the avant-garde

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khaled Amayreh

Contrary to many expectations and despite the combined Israeli-PA clamp-down, Hamas succeeded remarkably in defeating Fatah, the backbone of Yasser Arafat’s Palestinian Authority (PA), in the college elections which took place at the campuses of three major Palestinian universities—Bir Zeit, the Islamic University of Gaza and the Hebron University in Hebron—on 23 and 24 of March.

In the Hebron University elections on 23 March, Hamas and its junior sister the Islamic Jihad defeated a broad coalition consisting of Fatah and other PLO factions, including the PFLP, DFLP and the People’s Party.

Hamas and the Islamic Jihad received 20 and 2 seats respectively, while Fatah and the left obtained 19 seats out of the 41 contested seats of the Student Council.

The results were significant in many respects. The PA and its security apparatus worked very hard, using the stick and carrot method, to intimidate Islamic-oriented students and dissuade undecided students from voting for Hamas.

This scandalous interference in a university election by an authority claiming to represent all Palestinians took many brazen forms, including offering financial bribes, putting up a show of force at the Hebron University campus on the election day itself, and arresting a number of key Islamic student activists prior to the election in order to undermine the chances of Hamas winning.

According to neutral sources in Hebron, the PA paid the Fatah Bloc $51,000 to buy off voters. It is not clear where the money went, but there are already rumours that the money ended up in a few Fatah activists’ pockets.

Furthermore, the Shabiba used dirty tricks to scare students off the Islamic Bloc by disseminating false information to the effect that “those students voting for Hamas will not be given jobs after graduation.”

According to Abdul Khaleq al Natshe, an Islamist leader in Hebron, the Islamic Bloc was not facing another similar student group, but rather a “State with enormous means.”

“We are not merely competing against a group of students with a different platform; we are facing the entire PA apparatus which is using all means under their disposal to beat us.”

At the Islamic University of Gaza, Hamas received 75% of the students’ votes, the Islamic Jihad 5% and Fatah 19%.

The results sent a clear message to Arafat and his police and intelligence chiefs in Gaza, who never missed an opportunity to undermine the University.

In fact, in 1996, Gaza Police Chief Ghazi Jabali ordered his men to raid the buildings of the University, smashing scientific instruments in the chemistry labs, worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Fatah loses traditional stronghold

But the biggest disappointment for Fatah and the PA was what happened at Bir Zeit University, a traditional stronghold for the secular nationalist Palestinian movement.

Hamas emerged the premier winner, again in the face of overwhelming interference by the PA security establishment.

According to election results, Hamas won 23 seats, whereas Fatah won only 19, with the remaining 9 seats going to the leftist coalition.

This means that Hamas has won two additional seats compared to last year’s election, a noted achievement at a university that avowedly claims to be the main bastion of secularism in Palestine.

Future elections on hold

The election results are expected to sound an alarm bell in Fatah and the PA.

There was talk last month of possible local and municipal elections to take place this coming summer in the self-rule areas.

However, in light of Arafat’s plummeting popularity as expressly demonstrated at the educational institutes, it is very unlikely that Arafat will allow a bigger and more representative election to be held “under present circumstances.”

Indeed, “inadequate circumstances” were the reason why he froze the local elections, which were supposed to take place two years ago.

As to when conducive circumstances will come about, nobody knows for sure, but they don’t seem to be near, at least in light of the results of the university elections.

Arafat’s Aerial Manoeuvres

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

Perhaps Yasser Arafat deserves to be in the Guinness Book of Records: not for his 180-degree turn from ‘terrorist’ to ‘peace-maker’—to use American and Israeli terminology—nor for having survived so miraculously against all odds and outlived all his old comrades who were almost all liquidated along the zigzag road to Palestine. Rather, it is for spending most of his time in the past few months flying in the air that he qualifies to be a record-breaker.

Using simple arithmetic, one finds no difficulty coming to the conclusion that ‘Mr. Chairman’ hardly spends any time in Gaza, his capital city. One wonders whether it is his extremely busy schedule of essential tasks that keeps him off the ground or the desire to stay away from an increasingly unbearable environment—one of his own making—that keeps reminding him of his failings. In the morning he is in Riyadh visiting ailing King Fahd; at lunch-time he is in Cairo explaining himself and reporting to Egypt’s Mubarak; and in the evening he dines in Paris. The next morning he is in Gaza inspecting a guard of honour only to inspect it once more, prior to departing, hours later on another tour that may take him to the Gulf, or to Europe and eventually to his newly acquired friends in Washington. No businessman may ever contemplate competing with Arafat.

But what is it he seeks to sell? It is the Palestinian State. In the midst of utter desperation and profound frustration, the Palestinian State seems to be the lifeboat that may carry Arafat and his doomed Palestinian Authority to safety. Especially if recognized and encouraged by his Arab and European friends, the State, Arafat believes, may provide an exit, or deliverance, out of the predicament of Oslo’s legacy. On the other hand, he seems to think that playing his last card—the declaration of Statehood—especially when in the company of foreign dignitaries, may change his fortunes, or at least improve his chances of snatching something—anything—from his current Israeli ‘peace partners’.

Had it not been for pure electoral calculations, the Israeli government would have welcomed Arafat’s plan. Evidently, the Israeli Labour Party finds the ‘Palestinian State’ manoeuvre plausible. After all, the emergent Palestinian entity would have acquired nothing more than what it already has: a ‘President’, a ‘flag’, a ‘national anthem’, a ‘police force’, an efficient ‘prison service’, a powerless ‘parliament’ and a ‘local government’ in charge of administering the domestic affairs of the public. Such an entity, even if called a ‘State’, would have no sovereignty, would continue to be dependent in terms of economy and security on Israel and would have no control over its borders. In fact, a unilateral declaration of a Palestinian State could be the golden opportunity the Israelis have been waiting for.

With most of the 1967-occupied territory still under full Israeli control, the status quo created by such a declaration would enable Israel to formally annex what it controls. What remain afterwards, and especially so in the West Bank, will be separate and distant islets of Palestinian-controlled patches of heavily populated land surrounded by Jewish settlements, Israeli army barracks and highway bypasses for the exclusive use of Israel’s own ‘public’.

The reason why the Likud Party is adamant on rejecting this seemingly favourable step is purely technical. After all, this is a party that was brought to power by the votes of the extreme right, which would not swallow the idea of a Palestinian State even if it had zero sovereignty. Likud’s main priority now is to be re-elected and therefore it cannot be seen as leaning in any way toward Arafat’s State, even if it were more beneficial for the Israelis than for the Palestinians. Indeed, the time may come when the Likud may change its position. This will happen when the idea of a sovereignty-less Palestinian State becomes less detestable to right-wing Jewish voters, just as is the case now with the Labour Party and its left-wing supporters.

The disastrous repercussions of a unilateral declaration of a Palestinian State have become so obvious, especially to former secular PLO supporters inside and outside Palestine, that an alternative solution has been proposed. Realizing that a Palestinian State under the present circumstances is no State at all, intellectuals such as Dr. Azmi Bishara, Dr. Edward Said and Dr. Ghada El-Karmi have advocated a bi-national State solution.

The bitterness of Arafat’s plan has prompted such intellectuals to promote what they deem to be a less bitter, less evil, solution. However, for most Palestinians, inside as well as outside, neither proposal is acceptable, most obviously for failing to guarantee the Palestinians’ right to return to their homes.

When he returns from his Washington trip (started on 23 March), Arafat may cease to mention the State for a while. His ‘dear friend’ Bill Clinton will have advised him to ‘keep his mouth shut’ until the Israeli elections are over. Clinton is in a position to succeed in what others have failed, namely to persuade Arafat that it will do him no good to keep hoisting the State’s card in the face of Israel at this extremely crucial time. In the meantime, life in Palestine will proceed as usual; more Palestinian homes will be demolished every day; and more land will be confiscated to allow for the expansion of existing settlements, the building of new ones or the construction of additional highway bypasses. The Palestinians will continue to be imprisoned in their own towns and villages under the guard of both Israeli troops and Palestinian police. Palestinian prisoners will continue to languish in Israeli jails. Economic difficulties will continue to make life increasingly unbearable for hundreds of thousands of Palestinians. Various Palestinian security bodies will compete in proving to their ‘colleagues’ in the CIA and the Israeli intelligence services that they are trustworthy and reliable.

And Yasser Arafat will continue to inspect the guards of honour either upon arrival from a tour or in preparation for departure to embark on a new one.

* Director of the London-based Liberty for the Muslim World

The Process of Peace and War

By Ramzy Baroud

The Arab and Muslim worlds are listed in American foreign policy as a part of a “process”.

The term “process” in American politics was effectively used by its inventor Henry Kissinger during his adventures in “shuttle diplomacy” to resolve the Egyptian-Israeli conflict in the mid-1970s.

The use of this term has been widely associated with the word “peace”. Most of us, regardless of our stand on many of the political issues in the Middle East, repeat the term “peace process” with confidence. Since the establishment of civil societies, people are accustomed to resolving disputes or at least identifying them with simple yet precise definitions. So it has been said, Peace vs. War, Right vs. Wrong, and Victim vs. Aggressor.

The “peace process”, however, came as a saving device and an excuse for those who refuse to accept the moral implications of their actions. Because within the “peace process” there is no guilty or innocent, there is no victim or victimizer.

The process of peace simply denies all of that and resorts only to dividing the disputes into categories, starting with the trivial issues. In the case of the Israeli-Palestinian talks, Gaza-Jericho was agreed upon as a start, and all the real issues were left till the end of the process.

The moral dilemma here is: how can you engage two parties in peace talks while one holds all the cards and the other is denied holding even an identity card?

The concept of the “peace process” is an American invention created to get around the direct and clear resolutions of the United Nations without appearing as an imperialistic monster.

Once a weak nation gets involved in the so-called “process”, the process will be the only hope that it has to achieve anything, perhaps even mere survival.

Take the case of Israel and the Palestinians again. Israel could at any point reject any further involvement in this process. In fact, Israel did and will always threaten its withdrawal. For the Palestinians, it is not as easy an option as it sounds.

In the case of a poor and weak nation like that of the Palestinians, history is hard to reverse. No one will expect Arafat to take his men and women and search for a new front in Syria, Lebanon or the Moon Islands, perhaps to go back to the old “hit and run” strategy to fight the Israeli Occupation.
Who’s responsible for the abuse of weak nations? It’s “The Process”, of course.

In the old days, Palestinians had very specific demands: the implementation of UN resolution such and such…end of story.

Nowadays, however, even Palestinians don’t know what their legal rights are. Some argue Oslo I, while others carry on about Oslo II, the Cairo accords, the Washington accords, Wye River, etc…
And with each argument presented, Israel has a hundred counter-arguments to oppose it.

Reality has changed for Palestinians without any serious gains. All of a sudden, the victimized Palestinians found themselves partners in a dispute where no one was held accountable, thanks to Kissinger and his “process”.

The term “process”, which now has become a concept, is associated with other words besides peace. The “peace process” was declared because it has a positive sound, even though it carries a hidden agenda that cannot be felt until later on in the “process”, which may be too late. “War” is also a process.

The United States found itself unable to escape a serious war and, at the same time, found it impossible to handle and protect its interests in a civil manner.

As a middle ground, it chose to use the “middle war” or “the war process” to achieve its goals.

Americans don’t behave as if their army is engaging in a bloody war with Iraq, and that is the whole idea.

Through that process, the U.S. government can achieve its goals without being pressured and without being held accountable to its own people, as in the case of Vietnam.

At the same time, the sanctions and the continuous bombing in Iraq is enough to protect U.S. interests in the Middle East, including the security of Israel.

As attractive as it may sound, neither the process of peace nor war is going to have long-lasting effects. The two techniques are based on tricking certain groups into believing in things that are not true.

Such a trick can only go so far, but not far enough because nations, even poor ones, have a given capacity to tolerate injustice.

One day, however, once they find that the process is not taking them anywhere, and even if they have no cards left to play, they could tip the whole table over onto the heads of the players.

For the poor, it will not be a loss, because they never gained anything to begin with. The loss will come to those who had no respect for people’s rights.

For the fans of the “peace process”, I have only one thing to say: You can process food or a hard math problem, but you cannot process the people’s right to be free.

What would it take to heal the wounds?

By Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Under normal circumstances, time can be a natural healer of wounds. The atrocities committed at Deir Yassin on 9 April 1948 were, however, an exception to this rule. Instead of alleviating their pain, time increased the agony of Deir Yassin’s survivors. To these victims, justice was never done, nor seen to be done, even though the massacre constituted an appalling crime not only against themselves but also against humanity. In any event, neither Ben Gurion’s letter of apology to King Abdullah I of Jordan nor the change of its name to Kafr Sha’ul was enough to cover up the crime or erase the memory of Deir Yassin.

While the massacre remains a permanent symbol of Israeli brutality and inhumanity, it was by no means an isolated event. Similar acts of barbarity against civilians were executed with equal impunity throughout the last 50 years. If nothing else changed during the period, the methods of mass murder certainly became more deadly and devastating. The April 1996 bombing of the United Nations camp in Qana, southern Lebanon, was, indeed, one of the more recent and staggering examples. There, over 100 old men, women and children perished.

Crimes and atrocities

With regard to Deir Yassin itself, the murder of 254 defenceless civilians was, without doubt, grossly reprehensible. Notwithstanding, the manner in which this was done was itself more revolting. Here was a village whose inhabitants had entered into an agreement of mutual non-aggression with the Jews from the neighbouring Givat Shaul and Montefore settlements. In spite of that, the attack, it appeared, had become absolutely necessary by April 1948 in order to induce the birth of Israel and make Palestine as Jewish as England is English.

The Zionists were, of course, not the only ones who were guilty of bad faith. Having solemnly promised in the Balfour Declaration that “nothing shall be done which may prejudice the civil and religious rights of the existing non-Jewish communities in Palestine”, the British mandate failed to prevent the massacre. Indeed, when the then-High Commissioner Sir Alan Cunningham heard of the killings, he ordered his commander of ground forces, Lt. Gen. Gordon MacMillan, to send troops to Deir Yassin. The latter refused, claiming that such an intervention was not in the pursuit of British interests.

As a result, the International Red Cross was the first party to reach the village after the atrocity. Undeterred by Zionist threats to his personal safety, Jacques de Reynier, its Swiss representative, found 150 bodies thrown into a cistern. Of the total 254 which he counted, 145 were women, of whom 35 were pregnant. With the memories of Nazi war crimes still fresh in his mind, de Reynier poignantly recalled; “All I could think of was the SS troops I had seen in Athens.”

Perpetrators and accomplices

The killings at Deir Yassin were evidently consistent with the concept of an exclusive Jewish State in Palestine. It accomplished the aim of creating fear among the Palestinian population and accelerated their flight from the country after they were attacked. Count Folke Bernadotte, the UN mediator, corroborated this when he wrote in his report, “The exodus of Palestinian Arabs resulted from panic created by fighting in their communities, by remorse concerning real or alleged acts of terrorism, or expulsion.”

As it were, the violent “transfer” of the Palestinians was the translation of a policy that was rooted in Zionist thought and espoused by the Mandatory authority. Commenting on the partition proposal recommended by the Royal Peel Commision, Ben Gurion wrote into his diary on 12 July 1937, “The compulsory transfer of the Arabs from the valleys of the proposed Jewish State could give us something which we never had…” In the same manner, the British Government in its response to the report issued a ‘Statement of Policy’ on 20 July 1937 which read, in part, “It was assumed that provision would be made for the transfer of the greater part of the Arab population in the Jewish State, if necessary by compulsion…”

Dogged resistance from the Palestinians, however, obstructed the plan in the late 1930s. Later, when the UN adopted its partition scheme in November 1947, the Zionist transfer plan received a great boost. They were, in fact, encouraged and emboldened by the Organization’s decision to grant them 56% of Palestine, even though they constituted a mere 32% of the population. This resolution, No. 181, was, in effect, the green light for Haganah terrorists to begin as early as December 1947 the expulsion of the Arabs from the core area of the designated Jewish State. By establishing the Conciliation Committee for Palestine in December 1948 and the United Nations Relief and Works Agency [UNRWA] the following year, the world body had, effectually, confirmed the profound depths of its responsibility for the refugee problem.

Repatriation and justice

Despite the cold indifference of the international community, Deir Yassin failed to secure an exclusive State for the Jews in Palestine. True, it created a mass exodus of Palestinian refugees. Yet, it failed to extirpate their national aspirations. Hence, five decades after the event, the pain and memory of Deir Yassin remains as clear-cut in their minds as if it occurred yesterday. And, because of their conviction in the justice of their cause, they have, moreover, refused to surrender their right to repatriation, having no doubt that this would, in due course, be the first and not the last step toward the healing of their wound.

In reality, the return of the dispersed Palestinians to their homes and property is not a privilege or favour that is subject to Israeli or American goodwill. Neither is it a matter that depends upon Israel’s “demographic security” needs as Mr. Netanyahu suggests. Born as it were out of a violation of Palestinian rights, international law obliges Israel and all those responsible for the expulsion to restore the status quo ante. For this reason, the General Assembly reaffirmed in resolution No. 3236, “the inalienable right of the Palestinians to return to their homes and property from which they have been displaced and uprooted, and calls for their return.” In equally strong and explicit language, the UN Committee on the Exercise of the Inalienable Rights of the Palestinian People upheld that the Palestinian right of return “should be absolute for every Palestinian and must have priority over any other form of substitute arrangements, such as compensation.” That being the case, the recent “compromise” plan drawn up by the academic forum sponsored by Harvard University was dismissed as a non-starter in refugee circles. Its suggestion that Israel would pay some compensation and share the practical, but not moral or legal, responsibility for the Nakba (catastrophe) was condemned as a halfway measure that fell far short of the basic requirements of justice.

Whatever the case, the fact remains that the forced expulsion of the Palestinians was a crime so fundamental and unjust that no political “compromise” would amend its damage. Apart from being a violation of the human rights of the Palestinians, it also constituted a breach of international humanitarian law as well as the law of nationality. In the first instance, mass expulsion is so irreconcilable with respect for human rights that the Nuremberg Tribunal viewed it as a “crime against humanity”. Thus, it was on this basis that when Israel tried Adolf Eichman in 1961, it included charges of expulsion, declaring them “war crimes” and “crimes against humanity”. Be that as it may, the displaced Palestinians may not have powerful friends to champion their cause today, but should the rule of law be restored to the international system tomorrow, they would definitely have the full weight of history and the law on their side. There is, therefore, absolutely no reason why they should under the present circumstances accept a “compromise” solution that does not secure their fundamental rights or deliver justice.

Current attempts by the Clinton administration to transform the Palestinian refugee problem into a local affair confirms the extent to which Washington has deviated from the principles of its founding fathers. In 1891, when Czarist Russia expelled the Jews, President Benjamin Harrison protested that “the banishment, whether by direct decree or by no less certain indirect methods, of so large a number of men and women is not a local question.” Similar protests were also made to Romania in 1902: again concerning its treatment of Jews who were forced by their government “upon the charity of the United States.” With all this evidence on the table, it is inconceivable that the United States should today seek to disregard the international character of the Palestinian refugee problem. Throughout the past 50 years, all the neighbouring host countries have suffered tremendous losses, militarily, territorially and economically, as a result of this tragic and avoidable problem. Israel’s excuse that it has no capacity to absorb the dispersed Palestinians is both farcical and bizarre because it is still importing Jewish settlers from the four corners of the world.

With the birth of Jewish Israel in Palestine, Deir Yassin, like 530 other Arab villages, was erased from the map. Its tragic fate was naturally told in Israeli history books as a spectacular military victory. On their part, the survivors of Deir Yassin preserved its memory in the collective psyche of 7.5 million Palestinians, of whom 4,942,121 are now refugees. To all of them, Deir Yassin is symbolic of the bitter conflict and enormous opposition they are up against. One which in its organization, methods, political philosophy and social appeal is very much akin to the oppressive totalitarian ideologies of the early twentieth century.

If the atrocities committed at Deir Yassin will ever be assuaged, the remedy must include a settlement of the Palestinian refugee problem on the basis of international law. Whether Israel is inconvenienced or disturbed is irrelevant in this matter. For justice must be done and seen to be done. The Palestinian demand is by no means as extraordinary as their adversaries make it out to be. The Crimean Tartars, who were deported internally by the Communist authorities for collaborating with the Germans during the Second World War, were repatriated after the collapse of the Soviet Union. Is it that the Palestinians should also await for the collapse of the other “superpower” before they are repatriated?! They are prepared to wait and resist for as long as it takes because the right to return is non-negotiable.

* The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and Editor of its Return Review.

Mini Intifadah against Yasser Arafat in Rafah

Khalid Amayreh

Rafah- A popular mini intifadah against Yasser Arafat’s Palestinian Authority (PA) engulfed much of the town of Rafah following the passing by a special military court in Gaza of a death penalty by a firing squad on one of the residents of a nearby refugee camp.

The trouble began on 10 March, when the PA “State Security Court” (originally founded at Yitshak Rabin’s insistence to carry out speedy trials of militant activists accused of carrying out attacks against Israeli targets) sentenced Ra’ed al Attar to death by a firing squad for his role in the fatal shooting of Rifa’at Judeh, a Preventive Security Officer, during a chase in Khan Younis on 1 February.

Two of Attar’s colleagues, Muhammed abu Shammala and Osama Ali Taha, were also sentenced to life and 15 years imprisonment with hard labour respectively for their “indirect role” in the incident.

The trial itself, described invariably by human rights organizations as “farcical and theatrical”, was conducted on the basis of a PLO revolutionary law dating back to 1979, which critics say has more to do with revolution than with law, whereby a defendant is granted no real chance to defend himself.

Lack of evidence
When the five police officers comprising the prosecution board announced their verdict on 10 March, they utterly failed to cite unequivocal evidence proving that Judeh was actually killed by Attar, as the defense demanded all along.

Indeed, far from establishing a definite causative relationship between Attar and Judeh’s death, the five officers, who have little or no genuine legal training, argued repeatedly that since the bullet that killed Judeh was lost (!!), it was impossible to produce tangible “material evidence” to condemn Judeh.

In other words, the court had to rely on “circumstantial evidence”, effectively ignoring a mass of counter-evidence, including witness’s testimonies that made it clear that Attar was not seen firing at, on or toward Judeh.

For his part, Attar, who insisted throughout the trial that he was innocent, accused the police of concealing the fatal bullet that killed Judeh in order to avoid an internal PA scandal in case forensic tests proved the bullet was fired from a weapon carried by one of Judeh’s Preventive Security Force (PSF) colleagues.

Indeed, the prosecution refused to give a real hearing to Attar’s above-mentioned accusation, arguing that police testimony to that effect was indisputable evidence against the defendant.

Predictably, this infuriated Attar, who shouted in anger after hearing the verdict, “this is proof that justice does not exist here.”

Interestingly, many, probably most, Palestinians, along with human rights activists, lawyers and jurists, seem to have come to the same conclusion.

Police open fire on Rafah protesters
No sooner had the military court handed down its verdict than hundreds of the defendants’ relatives took to the streets in Rafah and the tiny refugee camp of Yibna nearby, shouting slogans against PA security chiefs and demanding the dissolution of the State security court.

The ostensibly spontaneous protest quickly gathered momentum, as angry youths began setting tyres on fire, turning garbage containers upside-down and hurling stones at the police.

The Warrior Poet

By: E. Yaghi

"I am a warrior-poet...

I fight battles with words

Which fly as strange birds

Into the darkness."

I am a Palestinian writer and poet who is being held in an Israeli prison somewhere in the desert. I have been told that my “crime” flows from the ink of my pen and if you have ever heard the expression, “the pen is mightier than the sword,” it must be true, for the Israelis apparently think my words are a threat to all their power, their money, their weapons and the backing of their greatest friends, the Americans.

My first name is Taha but in order to make me nothing more than a statistic, I have been given the number 39. My fellow inmates are writers too. We all share the guilt of trying to form a link with our Israeli counterparts who also write what they believe in.

Under the blistering sun of the desert, the burning sand is my bed and the prison camp I dwell in is an extermination camp, which exterminates all that is human in Man. If Hell exists, this prison would be its main gate of entry.

Let me make it clear that I am not a terrorist. I have never bombed anyone nor in any way taken a human life. I have never even struck anyone in anger. I only write for the freedom of my country and for the human rights entitled to all mankind.

My inmates and I live on crumbs of bread, bits of butter and jam and tiny portions of beans and warm water. Worms, snakes and scorpions share our foul beds with us. For over 60 days we have not been allowed to change our clothes. Whenever we suffer from intense pain, we go to the doctor. He only prescribes for us water and an herb that eases nothing. Since my only crime is being a freedom writer and hence, a warrior poet, I somehow derive strength from this charge.

My words have more influence than the Uzi submachine gun. Some of my Israeli counterparts who write the truth understand the need to reach a comprehensive peace agreement, one that will ensure the future of both Palestinian and Israeli children and one that will ensure the future of the region as a whole. So far, the Oslo Accords and the Wye Agreement have not ensured anything at all. It is obvious that any progressive culture whether Israeli or Western, cannot flourish in the shadow of violence and the violation of human rights. There are about 15 of us, writers and poets living in tragic conditions which run counter to basic human values.

Though I am imprisoned in the desert and in spite of the blistering heat, the ink in my pen will not dry up. Though I have been beaten for my words, wounded and placed in solitary confinement, my faith in God lives on and one day the freedom I seek will find me, either in this life or in death. I am a Warrior Poet and with my pen I fight for the freedom of my people.
