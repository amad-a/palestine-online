Palestinian anger at American double standards

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Like many in the Arab world, Palestinians breathed a sigh of relief following the conclusion of the last-minute agreement between UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and the Iraqi leadership. PA Chairman Yasser Arafat referred to the agreement as "the good news from Baghdad," and expressed the hope that the world, particularly the United States, would now pay equal attention to the Palestinian issue. Arafat's almost "wishful thinking" reflected the overall Palestinian indignation at what was described as the "American's obsessive desire to attack Iraq." That indignation was manifest throughout the autonomous enclaves and took the form of vociferous protests organized mostly by Yasser Arafat's own PLO faction, Fatah, and the pro-Iraqi Arab Liberation Front. So, in every Palestinian locality, the public mood was decidedly anti-American if only because of the "the scandalous double standards" governing American policy toward Iraq and Israel.

So the words were on everybody's mind and tongue: "Why don't they (the U.S.) force Israel to comply with UN resolutions as they are doing with Iraq? Why?" For most Palestinians, the answer is all too clear: The U.S. doesn't possess the will nor the inclination to put an end to Israeli defiance of UN resolutions as manifested in the ongoing building of Jewish settlements in the West Bank.

For many ordinary Palestinians, the new American-led crusade against Iraq has nothing to do in reality with the alleged hiding of weapons of mass destruction in the so-called presidential sites. In fact, some Palestinians have even come to think that the Americans were not after Saddam Hussein after all. Their rationale is that the Iraqi leader is serving the strategic and geopolitical interests of the United States in the region since the continuous crises he instigates provide the Americans with the pretext to maintain substantial American presence in the oil-rich Gulf area, in essence establishing and consolidating direct American military hegemony in the region.

Such words, however, sound too academic and too sophisticated to be understood, much less believed, by the rabble. Indeed, they had virtually no bearing on the several thousand Palestinians who took to the streets in support of the "Iraqi people" and against "American bellicosity and arrogance of power." The demonstrators shouted anti-American and anti-Israeli slogans, and held Saddam Hussein's portrait aloft, along with that of Yasser Arafat.

"We cannot remain silent when the cowboys are about to rain death on Baghdad. Men, women and children will be the victims," protested a middle-aged demonstrator outside the Dheishe refugee camp near Bethlehem.

The pro-Iraqi, anti-American protests in much of the West Bank (the Gaza Strip witnessed fewer protests due to stringent enforcement of the PA ban on pro-Iraqi demonstrations) proved a sensational item for the Israeli media and a definite propagandistic food-for-thought for the Israeli premier Benyamin Netanyahu who charged that Palestinian support for Iraq was proof that the Palestinians were seeking Israel's eradication.

Netanyahu's remarks along with reported American criticisms of the pro-Iraqi demonstrations and rallies within the autonomous enclaves eventually prompted Yasser Arafat to order his obsequious Gaza Police Chief Ghazi al Jabali to issue an "absolute ban on all demonstrations."

To appease the Israelis and impress Arafat, Jabali (who led the PA campaign against Hamas in Gaza in the spring of 1996) actually appeared on Israeli television ordering his subordinate police commanders to "break the necks of demonstrators." The remarks were too unpalatable for the Palestinian public opinion. Legislative Council members called his remarks "venomous and scandalous," and urged Arafat to fire him immediately. In the streets of Ramallah, hundreds of protesters, including leading Fatah activists, shouted "Ya Jabali Ya Amil" (Jabali collaborator."

The escalation of pro-Iraqi protests proved very embarrassing for Arafat who came to find himself unenviably pressed between the American-Israeli hammer and the anvil of the Palestinian public opinion, prompting PA police to move against private radio and television stations which devoted detailed coverage of the protests.

On 16 February, a huge PA police contingent made up of 120 policemen surrounded and stormed the "shepherds" local TV station in Bethlehem, serving the station a written order to close down its transmission for having shown footage containing the burning of the American flag and for hosting a phone-in programme during which viewers made calls expressing support for Iraq.

On 17 February, a local radio station in Ramallah, "Sawt al Hub wa Salam" (Voice of Love and Peace) was forced by the PA to sign off early also for broadcasting news of pro-Iraqi protests.

The following day, the PA Minister of Information, Yasser Abed Rabbu, issued a decree ordering the closure of five more TV stations and three radio stations in the West Bank "in observance of order and in compliance with laws and in order to organize the work of audio-visual broadcasters."

The closed stations included Qasre al Nil TV in Nablus, al Fajr al Jadid in Tulkarm, Hebron Radio (FM) in Hebron, Watani Atlas TV in Nablus, Tulkarm Central TV in Tulkarm, al Bilad Radio also in Tulkarm, al Awael TV in Jenin, and Naghem Radio in Qalqilia.

Earlier, the Ministry of Information delivered a memo to radio and TV stations throughout the West Bank ordering them to avoid transmitting Palestinian views on the Iraqi standoff with the United States.

The attack on press freedom, say human rights organizations, proves once again that the PA values the "legitimacy" that comes from Israeli and American acceptance more than that which comes from the Palestinian people.

Netanyahu seeks to restore "past glory" to the Mossad

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

In light of the Ciechanover Committee report that looked into the Mossad's bungled attempt on Hamas political chief in Amman, Khalid Misha'al, in September of 1997, it seems that the Israeli premier Netanyahu not only succeeded in overcoming the effects of the botched operation, but is actually planning to use the affair to restructure the Mossad in the hope of restoring some of its traditional notoriety.

The Ciechanover Committee, an innocuous body made up of Netanyahu's friends and allies, didn't blame Netanyahu for ordering the assassination attempt in the first place.

Instead, the Committee report criticized the various stages of the operation, including the "planning and execution" and the "unprofessional manner" in which it was carried out.

Indeed, the report gave every impression that the "concept of the operation" was not a bad idea, regardless of various considerations, including "the relations with Jordan."

Now, it is amply safe to assume that Netanyahu is waiting for the opportune time to "clean out the Mossad," including the ultimate firing of Mossad's present chief, Danny Yatom.

Netanyahu, though, is not expected to carry out the "clean-up" in a dramatic way lest he appear as a vindictive premier who sacrifices parochial party considerations at the expense of national considerations and interests.

But Yatom should be viewed as a virtual "lame duck" for two reasons:

First, his continued presence at the helm of the Mossad is likely to prevent an effective resumption of the vital intelligence and security coordination and cooperation with Jordan, particularly in the field of "monitoring Islamist elements on both sides of the River Jordan."

Needless to say, successive Israeli governments have always attached "paramount significance" to intelligence cooperation with Jordan, which explains why the Netanyahu government is making strenuous efforts to persuade King Hussein to allow the reopening of the Mossad office in the Jordanian capital.

Interestingly, the Mossad office in Amman, an annex to the Israeli embassy, played a key role in providing the logistics for the eight Mossad agents who took part in the failed attempt on Misha'al's life, which ostensibly infuriated King Hussein and made him insist on the removal of Yatom from the Mossad before any resumption of intelligence cooperation between Jordan and Israel would take place.

The second reason behind the contemplated clean-up of the Mossad is the reported obsessive desire on the part of the Israeli Prime Minister to restore the "past glory" to an organization that had acquired an unmatched reputation through the execution of spectacular operations such as the abduction from South America in the early 1960s of Adolph Eichman and the implanting of Eli Cohen in the top Syrian government echelon in the mid-1960s, as well as the subsequent assassination of numerous PLO leaders in Europe and the Arab world.

According to an Israeli source, Netanyahu's desire to "purify" the "ideological blemish" stems from his conviction that the political views of people like Yatom influence the kind of recommendations they make on the political level.

However, this is not "news" in the intelligence world, the Mossad included. Last December, a right-wing Mossad official, Yehuda Gill, confessed to having forwarded "concocted information" about alleged Syrian intentions to attack Israel.

In light of this, it seems that Netanyahu doesn't really aim to "curb the politicization" of the Mossad as much as he wants to give it free rein to "act wherever and whenever necessary for the security interests of Israel."

Indeed, Netanyahu made no secret of his peculiar understanding of the Ciechanover Committee report, e.g. that it gives him a green light to carry out assassination attempts on the soil of friendly and unfriendly countries alike.

Last week, following the release of the report, Netanyahu told Israeli TV that "we will continue to chase the terrorists wherever and whenever needed."

In fact, what Netanyahu seemed to be saying to people like King Hussein of Jordan is something like this: "Look! We will continue to act as we have always done, but next time we will make sure it won't be bungled."

Israelis' behaviour during crisis underscores

psychological vulnerability

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

The scene was all too familiar. Thousands of phobic-like, panic-stricken Israelis stood in long columns, queuing up for gas masks, antibiotics and other protective means to shield themselves against a possible Iraqi attack on Israel. The rowdy crowds squabbled, quarreled and cursed, vying for the "life-savers" as if death was imminent. The hysteria was also evident at supermarkets and grocery stores throughout pre-1967 Palestine where Israeli Jews of all ages queued up for canned food, especially tuna fish, but also for plastic sheets and paste tape in order to hermetically seal their rooms in case of an attack with chemical or biological agents.

Meanwhile, others were booking the first available flights to Europe and North America where they perceived they would be safe from a nightmare that is a psychotic state of mind rather than a factual reality. Ehud Olmert, the Israeli mayor of Occupied Jerusalem, thought the hysteria was an affront, was disgraceful and showed "the utter psychological vulnerability of Israelis." He said in a radio interview that the collective panic and phobia showed the Israelis as one who conceded defeat even before the beginning of the battle.

Israeli writers and journalists were more frank. A columnist in the mass circulation daily Yediot Ahronot exhorted Israelis to learn from the Palestinians who are almost totally exposed and possess no protection whatsoever against a chemical or biological attack. Israeli television, over-imbibing the collective hysteria, apparently couldn't impress upon their viewers the fact that the Palestinians were behaving not only normally but also with serenity and calm.

An Israeli TV reporter expressed surprise at the "business-as-usual manner" in Palestinian streets, fields and work places. One elderly Palestinian, plowing his land, was asked by the Israeli reporter if he was concerned about an Iraqi attack with chemical gas. The Palestinian said laconically "Allah Byerhamna" (God will protect us).

The reporter, unmoved by the simplicity and innocence of the Palestinian's response, opined to the mostly Hebrew-speaking audience that "sweeping fatalism and stupidity" characterized Palestinian behaviour in the face of a possible Iraqi attack. Unsure of the accuracy of his words and thoughts, the reporter then asked "why wouldn't the heavily protected Israelis, who have the IDF and the United States at their beck and call, behave 'naturally' like the Palestinians?"

It is not clear why Israelis behave as such during crises, often seeking double and triple protection. Could it be the exaggerated Jewish fear of death as mentioned in the Qur'an: "And thou will find them greediest of mankind for life and even greedier than the idolaters. Each one of them would like to be allowed to live a thousand years. And to live (a thousand years) would by no means remove him from the doom. For God is All-Seer of what they do." (Sura: II, v. 96).

US committee expresses concern over PA's closing of Palestinian broadcast

The US Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) expressed concern about recent efforts by the Palestinian Authority (PA) to silence private broadcasting in the West Bank, which, the committee said, violates international standards for free expression.

In a directive issued to the Palestinian media on February 15, the Ministry of Information banned all broadcasting of opinion and analysis about the current standoff between United Nations weapons inspectors and Iraq. Journalists and observers in the West Bank have interpreted the move as an attempt by the PA to curtail media coverage of pro-Iraqi sentiment among Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza. According to Reuters, the ministry said that its decision to impose the ban was to safeguard "higher Palestinian national interests and for the sake of protecting Palestinian security."

One day after the ban was implemented, more than 100 police surrounded the offices Al-Roa', a private television station in Bethlehem, and forced the station to suspend broadcasting for violating the ban. Al-Roa' had earlier broadcast news about pro-Iraqi demonstrations in the West Bank and had aired a program where viewers were able to phone in to express solidarity with Iraq.

In an apparent effort to further ensure compliance with the ministry's ban, Palestinian police chief Ghazi Jebali on February 18 asked television and radio stations to sign pledges that they would refrain from broadcasting "any news or footage of mass demonstrations or any other sensitive materials," or face closure, the Associated Press reported yesterday.

In a letter to Chairman Arafat of the PA signed by the CPJ's Executive Director, William A. Orme, Jr., the committee also expressed its deeply concern about the February 17 decision by police, requiring broadcasters to obtain new licenses from the Ministry of Interior. "Stations have been given one week to comply with the new regulation or face closure. Palestinian officials have yet to specify the requirements for obtaining the license. CPJ fears that this new licensing procedure may be used by authorities as a pretext to rein in independent broadcasters. CPJ has learned that on February 18, authorities closed down eight unlicensed television and radio stations which had been broadcasting for several months. Some of the stations had reportedly applied for licensing and were awaiting formal decisions on their applications," the CPJ pointed out.

"As a nonpartisan organization of journalists dedicated to the defense of our colleagues' rights throughout the world, CPJ views the PA's implementation of coverage bans and the closure of Al-Roa' as flagrant violations of the right to "seek, receive, and impart information and ideas through any media and regardless of frontiers," as guaranteed by Article 19 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights."

The committee urged Mr. Arafat to consider several recommendations aimed at bringing the PA's practices in accordance with international standards for a free press. These recommendations included:

"Rescind the Ministry of Information's ban on media coverage and permit Al-Roa' to resume broadcasting immediately; State publicly that the Palestinian National Authority accepts the principle that it has a duty under internationally recognized norms of free expression to allow journalists to disseminate a diversity of views, even if these views are opposed to or critical of prevailing policies; Ensure that any licensing or regulation of the broadcast media be carried out by an independent body to ensure non-discrimination in the awarding of licenses, and ensure that stations applying for permits be allowed to broadcast freely while their applications are under review."

Demonstration for the people of Iraq in Bethlehem

Bethlehem- From Steve Sozby

The combination of Israeli intransigence in the so-called "peace process" and of the U.S. pressing for another violent showdown with Iraq, tension in the West Bank is at a boiling point. I recently drove on the main Hebron-Jerusalem road through Bethlehem on a Saturday morning while school was getting out. Young Palestinian kids from the nearby Aida refugee camp were wrapping shirts around their faces while gathering stones not far from Israeli troops guarding Rachel's Tomb. Just two months ago, a 9-year-old boy was shot in the head and killed at the same location. I was killing time, not children, so I stopped to see how far the kids would press the Israeli enclave a mile south of their military checkpoint cutting off the West Bank from Jerusalem.

The Israelis sent a message when they shot dead the 9-year-old, as few of the kids would get close enough to the battle-clad IDF troops to even hit one on a bounce. Kids were using slingshots and the old Biblical slings to hurl projectiles, but none of the 8- and 9-year-olds wanted to get shot, so they kept a safe distance. Eventually the bored IDF commander gave the order to close the main road from Palestinian traffic and to shoot tear gas at the kids. Five of the six canisters of C-1 were fired at where we were standing, and a few brave youths threw them back at the soldiers.

An IDF sniper team was sent out to set up an ambush on the kids near Bint Jibreel refugee camp, which overlooks the main road where the kids were throwing stones. Before they could start shooting at the children, a larger demonstration emerged from the centre of Bethlehem. This one included older youths wearing Keffiyas to cover their face. They also had members of the Palestinian Authority police among them trying to encourage them not to march on the Israeli occupation forces. There was a large Palestinian flag and an Iraqi flag. Two youths emerged with an American and an Israeli flag, which were set on fire while the crowd chanted in Arabic: "Clinton, you pimp, leave Iraq alone." The IDF snipers started to shoot at the demonstrators and the PA police, which set off a clash that resulted in 14 Palestinians being shot and much more tear gas being fired into the area.

Palestinians are not demonstrating in favour of Saddam as a great leader. There is much sympathy on the streets of the West Bank and Gaza for the great suffering that the people of Iraq are enduring, as well as anger at the double standard of the Americans. "Israel defies U.N. resolutions at will, occupying our land, building settlements and using the Fourth Geneva Convention as toilet paper," said Mohammed Al-Akeel, one of the organizers of the demonstration in support for Iraq. "Meanwhile, Iraq has been starved and bombed back to the 13th century for suspected transgressions."

There is no doubt that the arrogance of the U.S. and Britain in the Middle East will have very negative long-term political consequences should they choose to use military force again against Iraq. There is not the same level of support for such actions even among the Gulf States, who traditionally do not oppose U.S. policy in the region. Clinton, of course, is seeking ways to turn attention away from his scandal at home and is not as concerned about the reaction of the Arab world to the deep hypocrisy of the U.S. It is this mixture of scandal and arrogance that may lead the U.S. to wreak further havoc and deep suffering on the already miserable people of Iraq.

Palestinian Islamists condemn U.S. "arrogance of power"

Occupied Jerusalem (16 February)

Palestinian Islamists have urged Arab heads of State to hasten to save the Iraqi people from "the imminent American aggression," a leaflet signed by the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) said on 16 February.

The leaflet pointed out that the United States was "going too far in its insolence and arrogance of power by preparing to strike at the Iraqi people, who have been debilitated by seven years of oppressive sanctions."

The leaflet went on: "With a cowboy mentality, America is launching a blind and evil crusade to protect its strategic interests...without ethics, save the ethics of naked military force, and without principles and humanitarian considerations."

The leaflet exposed the "stark double standards" adopted by the American administration vis-a-vis Iraq and "Israeli-Zionist brutal oppression and torment of the Palestinian people."

Moreover, the leaflet expressed appreciation for the Saudi Arabian refusal to allow American war planes to use Saudi soil as "a launching pad" for aggression against Iraq.

On Friday, 13 February, Islamist speakers in mosques throughout the occupied territories called on the Muslim Umma to resist American hegemony, saying the U.S. was not only after Iraq but after any and every country that refuses to budge to American imperialism.

Never the less, one speaker, Sheikh Nayef Rajoub of the Grand Mosque in Dura, opined that the "fault lies not in the United States but in the Muslim Umma itself."

"The fault is not with America, which seeks to subjugate and humiliate us, but in our Umma and its leaders, who readily accept American hegemony."

Rajoub said it was a foregone conclusion that Saddam Hussein is a brutal and ruthless despot, adding, however, that the United States was not after Saddam, who, he said, was serving American interests in the region, but was rather after "Iraq's recovery potentials."

The Friday imam at al Masjidul Aqsa essentially repeated the same theme, urging Muslim leaders "to awaken from their deadly silence and cowardly posture."

"When Saddam Hussein struck Tehran with long-range rockets, the U.S. and the West in general actually helped him, but when there was even a prospect of his missiles reaching the "State of oppression" (Israel), the world wasted not a moment in attacking and destroying him," the imam said during the Juma'a sermon on Friday.

Israel unhappy about agreement

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (23 February)

Israeli officials reacted with a combination of consternation and ambivalence to the agreement reached in Baghdad on Monday between U.N. Secretary-General Kofi Annan and the Iraqi leadership.

Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu told reporters that Saddam Hussein gave in to American demands, adding that there was no reason why the United States shouldn't go ahead with plans to attack Iraq if it emerged that the Iraqi leader didn't fully succumb to American conditions.

Netanyahu, who came under severe criticisms from the leftist opposition for showing poor brinkmanship, said the Israeli position throughout the crisis was right.

He added that "our enemies knew our force of deterrence before and after the crisis."

However, Netanyahu critics described his words as "verbal juggling."

Yossi Sarid, leader of the leftist party Merets, described Netanyahu as a big impostor who shams statesmanship.

Sarid accused Netanyahu of creating false hysteria in the Israeli society, arguing that Israel was the main loser in the Iraqi crisis.

Government ministers didn't hesitate to express outright dismay at the agreement.

Health Minister Joshua Matsa said he was not happy nor sad at the agreement, adding that a new crisis would unfold sooner or later.

He urged the United States to keep its armada of warships and aircraft carriers in the Gulf on a permanent basis.

The same call was also made by Avigodor Kahlani, Minister of Internal Security, who expressed ambivalence with the Baghdad agreement.

Kahlani told the Israeli radio "Voice of Israel" that Saddam Hussein would continue to pose a threat to Israel.

He also alluded to Iran as "an important threat to Israel and American interests in the region."

Jewish settlers warn government against

removing terrorist's tomb

Occupied Jerusalem - PT

Jewish extremists warned the Israeli government against removing the tomb of Baruch Goldstein from Kiryat Arba'a, threatening to "turn the world upside down in case the tomb was tinkered with."

Goldstein is the Jewish terrorist who on 25 February 1994 massacred 29 Palestinian worshippers at the Ibrahimi Mosque in Hebron before he was killed by survivors.

Ever since his death on the day of the massacre, Goldstein's tomb has become a pilgrimage site for Jews from places as far away as California.

Baruch Marzel, a friend of Goldstein, said in a radio interview last month that he, along with his friends, would "turn the world upside down" if the remains of Goldstein were removed from Kiryat Arba'a.

Marzel, a leading member of the terrorist Jewish movement "Kach," added that any tinkering with "the sacred tomb of the saint Baruch Goldstein" would spark off unpredictable reactions from many Jews.

Marzel's vitriolic threats came in reaction to a Knesset proposal tabled by the Israeli parliamentarian Dudy Tsuker calling for the removal of a memorial plaque atop Goldstein's tomb and transferring his remains to Tel Aviv or Jerusalem.

Moreover, the motion calls for the incrimination of activities and actions proscribing sanctity to terrorists and their places of burial.

Tsuker's motion was labelled as "evil and repulsive" by Kiryat Arba'a settlers.

The settlement chief rabbi, Dov Leor, described Tsuker's proposal as "inimical to Jewish traditions."

Leor is considered among the leading advocates of "Goldsteinism", the Jewish equivalent of Nazism.

Last year, he published a book glorifying and lionizing the terrorist Goldstein. He described him as "a great saint" whose action would herald the age of redemption for the Jewish people.

Moreover, Leor teaches his many disciples that the blood of Jews is redder than that of non-Jews, suggesting that God prefers Jews to non-Jews.

Jordan, Amnesty flays report on Misha'al affair

Amman (Star) - Jordan condemned conclusions of an official inquiry into the Mossad's botched assassination attempt of Hamas leader Khaled Misha'al for endorsing political killings by the Israeli government. Several Jordanian officials considered the outcome of the investigation as posing new Israeli threats to strike again at Palestinian activists in Amman.

Lower House Speaker, Saad Hayel Al Srour described the report issued by the Israeli committee set to inquire the bungled assassination attempt as a bid for further violations on Jordanian territories.

In London, Amnesty International released a statement criticizing the outcome of the Israeli official inquiry. "As we predicted, the committee of inquiry has concentrated on the reasons why the attempted assassination failed rather than the illegality of the botched killing. Now it has given the green light to State-sanctioned murder," Amnesty International said. It demanded the Israeli government to abide by its commitments under international law and to respect the right to life and immediately cease the use of extrajudicial executions, whoever may be the target.

Palestinian Authority commemorates 50th

anniversary of usurping Palestine

Ramallah (West Bank)- QP

The Palestinian Authority has reached an agreement with the Islamic Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (ISESCO) to organize an international conference on Palestine in mid-1998. The conference will be one of the activities to be organized by the PA commemorating the 1948 usurping of Palestine by the Zionist State. The agreement, which was signed by the PA Minister of Information Yasser Abed Rabbu and the ISESCO Director-General Abdel Aziz Tuwaijre, also included the establishment of a Palestinian museum in Ramallah, and the production of a film documenting the sufferings of the Palestinian national struggle over the past 50 years.

$300 million losses of Israeli tourism because of tension in the region

Occupied Jerusalem (QP) - An Israeli report estimated the losses of the Israeli tourist sector during 1997 at $300 million. The Israeli tourism officials attributed the losses to the tension in the region and mainly the bombings in the occupied territories that resulted in the reduced number of foreign tourists. The report pointed out that about 2 million tourists visited Israel in 1997 compared to 2.2 million in 1996.

Meanwhile, another Israeli report speculated that the Israeli losses were a result of car thefts, estimated at $2.2 billion during the past five years. The report published by the Yediot Aharonot daily said that about 166,000 cars were stolen from the 1948-occupied Palestine in the past five years, including about 46,000 cars stolen in 1997 alone. The report issued by the Union of Israeli insurance companies pointed out that 71% of the stolen cars were not found.

19 Palestinians died in the Palestinian Authority jails

Gaza (QP) - A report issued by the Gaza Centre for Rights and Laws said that 19 Palestinian citizens have died in the Palestinian Authority jails since the PA's arrival in Gaza in June 1994. The report added that one citizen died in 1994, 7 in 1995, 5 in 1996 and the same number in 1997, and one citizen died since the beginning of 1998. It pointed out that six of the dead citizens had been detained after being accused of collaborating with Israeli authorities, two had been accused of selling land for Jews while others had been detained for different reasons.

Palestinian juvenile detainees call to alleviate their suffering

Nablus (West Bank) QP - A number of Palestinian detainees (under 18 years of age) in the Israeli Talmund jail called on the human rights organizations to pressure Israeli occupation authorities to alleviate their suffering in the jail. They asserted that their families have not been allowed to visit them for the past seven months. The prisoners pointed out that a Palestinian woman from the Gaza Strip was prevented from visiting her son as well as her husband, who was sentenced to life imprisonment and who is dying at the hospital of the Israeli Ramleh jail. The report said that 73% of the 560 Palestinian prisoners in the jail are supporters of the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas.
