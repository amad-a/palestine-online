Time to Challenge American Piracy

Despite the 23 February agreement between UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and Iraqi President Saddam Hussein, the United States is continuing to beat the drums of war.

American officials, from Bill Clinton downward, are making no secret of their dissatisfaction with the last-minute accords which fortunately forestalled a blanket bombing of Baghdad that would have left countless of innocent Iraqis either dead, incinerated or mutilated.

The fact that Saddam Hussein caved in to American dictates and international demands, if only to save his tormented people from further suffering, was apparently bad news for the White House, and especially for the evil but influential trio of Madeleine Albright, William Cohen and Sandy Burger. The three Zionist Jews were reportedly hell-bent to bomb-out Baghdad "once and for all" in order to "establish American hegemony in the Gulf."

That was the declared goal of the American-led build-up, unprecedented since the second Gulf War in 1991. The other undeclared goal, however, has to do with Israel (Bill Clinton's organ grinder) and is aimed at preventing "weapons of mass destruction" from falling into the "wrong hands."

But then who would decide which hands are "right and which are wrong?" And if the "irresponsible use" of weapons of mass destruction is the "red line," what can we make then of the destruction of Hiroshima and Nagasaki by atomic bombs dropped on the two Japanese towns by the Americans in 1945; and the spraying of Vietnamese jungles with chemical agents which destroyed life and habitats?

Truly, the American-led crusade in the Gulf demonstrated in a stark manner the oppressive arrogance of power of a nation claiming to be an exemplary model for other nations.

That arrogance would have easily turned into "genocidal murder on a colossal scale" had not Saddam Hussein decided to be "wise."

One need not be a great military expert to imagine the widespread death and destruction in Baghdad if the trigger-happy Americans decided to go ahead with bombing the Iraqi capital, especially the "controversial sites" where they alleged the Iraqi leadership is hiding chemical and bacterial weapons.

Never the less, the Arab-Muslim peoples in the area and in far-outlying lands must start to speak out against their autocratic and authoritarian regimes' subservience to American hegemony.

The peoples and the elite must realize that the real problem does not lie in American hegemony, but in our vulnerability to accepting, even welcoming, our grave-diggers.

The masses must learn how to make their voices heard, first and foremost, by Arab leaders, who in times of crises act as American high-commissioners responsible to the White House irrespective of their peoples' feelings.

The anti-American protests, which took place in several Arab countries, were an encouraging sign upon which more popular involvement can be built.

As Arabs and Muslims, we must take our fate into our own hands and immediately do away with inaction and passivity.

We must take to the streets whenever needed in order to convey an unmistakable message to our dictators and dynastic despots who drive herds over us that the old days are over for good.

Indeed, the evil cowboys would have treated us lightly had they thought that our leaders were accountable to us more than to them.
