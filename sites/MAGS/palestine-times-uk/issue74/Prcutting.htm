The Israeli Defense Force has warned the government that retaking Palestinian-run cities is not a realistic option after army war-games showed that it would cost hundreds of soldiers' lives, press reports said yesterday. The IDF carried out exercises last month to rehearse Operation Thorn Field, a contingency plan under which Israeli troops would re-enter some or all of the seven cities handed over to the Palestinian Authority under the Oslo peace accords. According to Yediot Aharnot newspaper, IDF generals concluded: "Israel has nothing to gain from a violent confrontation with the Palestinians. They can only lose. Neither will the Palestinians gain anything." The Guardian, July 21, 1997.

 

Yasser Arafat's Palestinian police yesterday revealed details of what was claimed as "the largest Hamas factory of explosive devices" ever uncovered, found in an apartment building close to the self-rule Arab town of Bethlehem. The haul, whose size and deadly potential has underlined the seriousness of the uneasy security situation, included nine huge explosive devices ready for use and clothing and uniform intended to disguise the would-be bombers as Jews. According to the Palestinian police, who have been under pressure from Israel to crack down on Islamic militants vowing a new campaign of revenge against posters depicting Prophet Muhammad as a pig, the bomb factory contained Jewish prayer shawls and skull caps, wigs and Israeli army uniforms. The Times, July 22, 1997.

 

In a recently-published report, the United Nations estimated that the unemployment rate (in Gaza and the West Bank) increased from 19 per cent to nearly 30 per cent, and per capita GNP in the Palestinian territories dropped by more than 8 per cent. The average Palestinian wage fell by a tenth over the same period, from $411 per month to $370, sufficient to cover 64 per cent of basic family needs. All the statistics reflect the fact that the Oslo peace process has so far been economically damaging for the Palestinians. By demarcating Palestinian territory, it has solidified the inter-ethnic boundaries and partially unhitched the Palestinians from the much stronger Israeli economy. Since 1993, their income as a group has shrunk by about a third. The Guardian, July 23, 1997.

 

One quiet day last month in typically tumultuous Hebron, 74-year-old Munir Muhammad Mahmud Karaja slowly hobbled off to buy some vegetables at the market. But the peace suddenly evaporated, and a shot fired by Israeli soldiers, coping with stone-throwing Arabs, struck the elderly, silver-haired Karaja in the head. "I have had five operations since then," Karaja slowly explained. "My life has been turned upside down. My eyes hurt. My head hurts. My hands hurt. I was a writer and I cannot do that anymore." Israeli and Palestinian human rights activists offer Karaja as an example of innocent Arab victims who would suffer if a new law is passed to limit the Israeli government's liability to those accidentally injured in the ongoing Arab-Israeli strife. The Chicago Tribune, July 23, 1997.

 

Christians in the Holy Land seethed when an Israeli scientific journal recently illustrated an article on cloning by depicting the Madonna and Child with a cow's head superimposed on the face of the Virgin Mary. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu apologized publicly for the insult to Christianity. The editors of the publication, Galileo, expressed deep regret to anyone who was offended, insisting they hadn't known the Renaissance picture of the Madonna had any religious significance. The Chicago Tribune, July 23, 1997.
