1500 Figures Participate in Popular Jerusalem Conference

By Lena Winfrey Sayyed

1,500 people attended the opening of the two-day Popular Jerusalem Conference that began in Amman, Jordan on 20 August 1997. Participants hailed from 20 different countries to show support for Jerusalem.

The Conference began with a recitation from the Qurían and the singing of the ìJerusalem Nasheedî by the band Farqat Al-Rawaby. Speeches were then given by the President of the Preparatory Committee of the Conference Dr. Ishaq Al-Farhan, who is the General Secretary of the Islamic Action Front Party, and by Suleyman Arar, the General Secretary of the Arab Parties Secretariat. There were other speeches and then elections for the president, vice-president and secretaries. Dr. Ishaq Al-Farhan was elected to be the president of the Conference and nine deputies were appointed as vice-presidents. Suleyman Arar was one of the deputies. The eight remaining deputies were chosen from other parties from Syria, Lebanon, Egypt, Iraq, Malaysia, Libya and Palestine. Three sub-committees were elected: (1) Committee for the Strategic Popular Act to Defend Jerusalem; (2) Committee for the Jerusalem Charter; and (3) Drafting Committee.

The second day was spent discussing the charter and recommendations, which had to be agreed upon before the dayís end. Other participants in the Conference included 11 Jordanian opposition parties, the Future Party, the Muslim Brotherhood and independent political parties. The Conference was composed of mostly those participants living outside of Palestine.

Why is Palestine, and Jerusalem in particular, so important to these people? Abdul-Hady Awang, the deputy of the Islamic Party in Malaysia and a member of the Malaysian Parliament, states: ìPalestine is the land of the Prophets, and Muslims believe in all the Prophets....Jerusalem was the first Qibla (direction of prayer) that Muslims used to pray towards, and it is the third holiest place in Islam. Jerusalem was where the Prophet visited during Al-Israí and Al-Miraj (The Night Journey and the Ascent). Muslims recognize all Prophets, including Israelite Prophets. Muslims accept Christians and their holy sites and their beliefs are respected as well as the Jews being allowed to hold prayers. In the beginning of Islam, history shows Muslims were tolerant until the Crusaders came. When the Jews ruled Palestine, they did not have the same tolerance.î

Hamad Al-Farhan, a member of the Jordanian Upper House of Representatives and a member of the Preparatory Committee of the conference, also stated his view of the importance of Jerusalem. He gave two reasons. ìFirst, the Arabs around Palestine consider the losing of Jerusalem as a sign of losing all of Palestine. Jerusalem is sacred to Muslims. It is more sacred than the Vatican is to Christians. Jerusalemís sacredness is connected to the Prophet Mohammed {may the peace and blessings of God be upon him) while the Vatican is not connected to Jesus but to his followers.ì

Many people view the moving of the American embassy as a disaster. Ibrahim Ghosheh, spokesman of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas), said this will have ìa bad effect because it means the U.S. was a big liar for all the previous periods when it put itself as a mediator and then it recognizes both East and West Jerusalem as the Israeli capital.î Hamad Al-Farhan states that this decision made people feel defenceless. ìWe are not strong enough to challenge America. Why??...America as a world leader said its policy would not allow Jerusalem to be Israelís centre until the conflict is resolved. Then suddenly it did. People were repulsed. How can America support a state that is aggressive to the principles of Western Civilization?... It is an insult to humanity that a big leader of politics commits such a cheap act.ì

When asked how the status of Jerusalem affects the peace process, Ibrahim Ghosheh replied, ìI didnít use the words ëpeace processí. Islamists donít believe in the peace processóit is an artificial one. The battle of Jerusalem is coming. Whatís happening now is against the just and the fair. America and the West believe in their hearts that theyíre supporting the wrong side, which is not just and who are not the owner of Jerusalem and Palestine.î

When asked to evaluate the Conference, Ibrahim Ghosheh stated that ìThis is the first time the real policy of the Palestinian, Arab and Muslim people put their beliefs clearly in rejecting Zionism and the Israeli state and requesting liberation of both East and West Jerusalem and reinforcing that Jerusalem is the capital for Palestine.î

Abdel-Aziz Al-Sayyed, the General Coordinator of the Conference and the Secretary of the Arab Parties Secretariat, states that ìWhat made the Conference so successful were the documents it issued.î One is the Jerusalem Charter, which is expected to be signed by millions of Muslims. People have already begun to sign it. Also, a strategy was made for defending Jerusalem and occupied Palestinian land. He continued: ìWe hope the plans will be put into action politically and educationally. There are also plans in dealing with the media. These plans will be carried out by the parties and personalities who shared in the Conference. It will be carried on in all Arab and Muslim countries by Palestine and Jerusalem popular committees which exist in all these countries.î He also stated the fact that ìJerusalem and Palestine need regular conferences until Jerusalem and Palestine and all other occupied Arab lands are liberated from Zionist aggression and oppression.î

It was decided that conferences would be held every year in a different country. The Jerusalem Charter would be translated into English, French and Spanish for other Muslims to sign. The cooperative spirit of the members of the Conference surely must bear successful fruits in the near future.

Home Demolitions, Israel's Continuous Policy

by Steve Sozby

While the Clinton Administration sends envoys to Jerusalem to denounce "Islamic terrorism", the Netanyahu government has stepped up its campaign of provoking already frustrated Palestinians using a variety of excessive measures. The most graphic of these is the demolition of Palestinian homes in the West Bank. In the past week alone, over thirty "unlicensed" Arab homes have been bulldozed and dynamited by Israeli military forces. This comes at a time when the U.S. and Israel are pressuring a weary PA President Yasser Arafat to do more to crackdown on militants in the areas under his "control."

"We are in a very difficult situation here," says Mohammed Ibrahim, a political advisory to Arafat in Gaza. "On one hand, we are told to smash any opposition to Israel from our own people, while at the same time the Israelis are provoking our people by using collective measures. There is a very great level of frustration here that no leader can control in the absence of political accommodation."

In the wake of the July 30 suicide bombings in Jerusalem, which left

over a dozen Israelis dead, the Netanyahu government imposed a total closure on the West Bank and Gaza, making it impossible to move even between villages and towns. Workers were idle, produce did not move to markets and millions of dollars were further lost in an illegal form of collective punishment imposed on over two million people. In addition, the Israeli government openly violated the Oslo Accords and international law by withholding to the PA tax money taken from Palestinian workers, as outlined in the "peace" agreement. Even more distressing, however, is the increase in home demolitions, which threatens, according to former Netanyahu advisor Ret.-Gen. Oren Shahour, to "spark greater violence in the territories."

In an open disregard for the presence of U.S. envoy Dennis Ross, who seemingly cares little about the rights of the Palestinians anyway, ten Palestinian homes were destroyed in Jerusalem and the West Bank on August 13. This came at the same time that Ross was twisting Arafats arm to further control Hamas and Islamic Jihad. "The PA is becoming the soldiers for Israel. They are not here anymore to protect our people but to protect the security of the settlers," says an exasperated merchant George Barbour in Bethlehem. "People are losing their faith in our authority because they are not doing anything to stop the Israelis from taking our land and destroying our homes."

The Israelis claim house demolitions are legal because they were built without permits. It is a well-known fact that the Israeli military authority and the Jerusalem municipality rarely issue building permits to Arabs. Palestinians, faced with a huge housing crisis in Jerusalem and its surrounding villages and refugee camps, must build without Israeli compliance.

"We do not have the same low mortgages or tax breaks like the settlers to build homes here," explained Mazen Al Mimi, whose home was destroyed after he built seeking relief from the crowded Shou'fat refugee camp . "We invested our life-savings in this home and they came and took that all away from me in one day."

As Ross and the CIA tried to impose PA security cooperation with Israel, five homes were destroyed in Shou'fat camp, which faces the illegal Israeli settlement of Pisgat Zeev, where 30,000 Jewish residents live on confiscated Arab land. While Palestinian homes are destroyed, Pisgat Zeev expands onto the land of Shou'fat and Anata villages. The nearby refugee camp houses 8,000 Palestinian Jerusalemites who are still waiting for justice following their expulsion from their villages in 1948.

West Bank settlements are currently seeing a great housing boom, with construction taking place all over the territories. It is this mixture of home demolitions at the same time of settlement expansion that results in the outbreak of violence among an increasingly frustrated Palestinian population.

According to human rights monitoring groups in Palestine, since the Oslo Accords were signed and the era of "peace and reconciliation" began, 270 Palestinian homes have been destroyed by Israel. Ninety-two of those were under the Rabin/Peres governments, suggesting that this policy is not just that of the anti-Oslo Netanyahu government. It is clear, however, that the Likud government employs these measures at an even greater rate than the previous Labor government.

On May 18, 1997, Israeli Defense Ministry officials stated that 500 Palestinian homes have been targeted for demolition. Since the election of Prime Minister Netanyahu on May 29, 1996, nearly 180 homes have already been destroyed.

"It seems quite clear that the Israeli military authorities attempt to cleanse areas of the West Bank and Jerusalem of a Palestinian presence is to establish favorable population facts prior to the final status peace negotiations," says PA Minister Faisal Husseini. "The home demolitions are part of the same strategy that we see at Jebal Abu- Ghneim and Ras Al-Amud settlements. They are talking about peace but actually supporting policies of war."

Husseini is right: The Israeli authorities rely on "legal" pretexts concerning the lack of building permits in order to legitimize their current policy of demolishing homes and removing a Palestinian presence from Areas C near Jewish settlements, bypass roads and military installations. Should Netanyahu be forced to actually participate in the final-status talks, which seem pretty unlikely, he hopes by then that there will be little for the Palestinians to actually negotiate about, especially in East Jerusalem. It then seems pretty clear then that more Palestinians will lose their homes in the coming months from an Israeli government seeking to reduce the number of Arabs in areas that they plan to keep.

Everyday a new generation of Palestinian refugees are created by Israeli home demolitions in the West Bank. This comes while the refugees from 1948 and 1967 still wait in squalid camps for some form of compensation and justice. It is easy then to see where the motivations for suicide bombers comes from. One just has to look at the face of hungry Palestinian children sitting on the rubble of what was their home to understand that this so-called peace process cannot outlast efforts by the Israeli government to provoke Palestinians to react in frustration to the loss of their homes and land.

Palestinians hail "National Unity talks"

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

Palestinians from various political orientations have hailed the "National Unity Conference," held in Gaza on 20 August, describing it as "our response to Israeli insolence and morbid efforts to fan the flames of war amongst our people."

"National unity should be a fixed Palestinian strategy in the face of (Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin) Netanyahu's sinister efforts to strangle our people and narrow our horizons," said Muhammed Khalid, a prominent Hamas leader in the Jenin area.

"It is clear that Netanyahu wants to see Palestinians shed their own blood in order to appease his own cannibalistic instincts. We must not allow him to realize his diabolic dream," added Khalid.

Jamal Shubaki, a Legislative Council member and refugee from pre-1967 Palestine, concurred, saying "that not under any circumstances should the Palestinian Authority give deference to Israeli demands to repress a significant sector of our citizens."

"Our strategy should be to cooperate with, rather than fight, the Islamist camp. This policy shouldn't be a seasonal reaction to Israeli insolence and oppression, but a principled strategy independent of all other considerations," said Shubaki in a radio interview recently.

In Gaza, the National Unity Conference was opened at the Rashad al Shawwa Centre, under the chairmanship of Palestine Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat.

Hamas representative, Abdul Aziz al Rantisi called on Arafat "to abandon the Oslo Agreement because it utterly failed to restore our rights and dealt a painful blow to national unity."

"This agreement has forced us into a dark tunnel at the end of which there is no glimmer of hope or flicker of light," said Rantisi before a speechless Yasser Arafat.

Furthermore, Rantisi called on the PA to see to it that human rights are respected and the authority of law and order be made to prevail.

For his part, Sheikh Nafez Azzam of the Islamic Jihad organization reiterated his movement's implacable rejection of the Oslo Accords.

"This agreement has given Israel all the assets and us all the liabilities," said Azzam.

The Islamist leader also called on the PA to boycott a regional economic conference scheduled to be held in Qatar in November.

Azzam labelled the conference "the economic normalization conference," exhorting the PA not to play the role of "a normalization middle-man between Israeli and the Arab world."

 

Low Incentives

While Arafat is not expected to heed Hamas and Islamic Jihad calls for "terminating Oslo," he does seem to be trying to reach a certain common ground with the Islamist camp.

Indeed, Arafat's costly refusal to succumb completely to Israeli dictates is likely to be vindicated and could pay off in terms of enhancing his position in the occupied territories.

The fact that Hamas decided, somewhat hastily and without any reservations, to participate in the Gaza talks suggests that the Movement is showing a certain gratitude for Arafat's reluctance to clamp down on its activists and infrastructure as incessantly demanded by the Israelis and the Americans.

But Arafat is not being altruistic toward Hamas. The Chairman seems to realize that Netanyahu is after him just as much as Hamas, and that Netanyahu is interested in weakening him by making him fight Hamas on behalf of Israel in return for nothing.

"In the past Arafat fought Hamas with a vigour because he thought the peace process would pay off eventually. Now, the Chairman nearly lost this glimmer of hope, hence he has no incentives to fight Hamas," said an "insider" close to the Chairman.

A modus vivendi with Hamas?

Occupied Jerusalem- by Khalid Amayreh

In a step meant as a hint to Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu that the Palestinians can resort to "other options if necessary," Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat convened and chaired the so-termed "National Unity Forum." The conference, held in Gaza on 20 August and a day later in Ramallah, was apparently aimed at reaching a modicum of national Palestinian concordance in the face of the unprecedentedly draconian Israeli measures imposed on the occupied territories in the aftermath of the 30 July suicide bombing in West Jerusalem. Unlike previous dialogue sessions which were largely boycotted by Hamas and the Islamic Jihad on the grounds that they lacked substance, the Islamist participation was conspicuous this time, drawing angry Israeli reactions and vociferous accusations that "Arafat was colluding with terrorists rather than fighting them". The participation of Hamas in the two-day conference was meant, at least in part, to show gratitude to the PA leadership for not succumbing to Israeli dictates to clamp down on the Islamists under the elusive rubric of "fighting terrorism." However, Hamas representatives at the conference were careful enough to draw a definitive line between "gratitude" and political stance.

"Mr. president, I call on you to abandon the Oslo Agreement because it utterly failed to restore our rights and dealt a painful blow to our national unity," Hamas senior representative Abdul Aziz al Rantisi said in an unfaltering tone, addressing Chairman Arafat. Rantisi, once the spokesman of the 400 Islamist activists deported by the late Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin in December 1991, went on telling Arafat that "this Agreement has forced us into a dark tunnel at the end of which there is no flicker of light or glimmer of hope." The Islamist leader continued, saying "therefore, Mr. President, resistance of every kind is the right way to confront the occupation...we must leave the Oslo plan, which Israel uses as a weapon against us."

Rantisi's remarks were essentially echoed by the representative of Hamas' more radical sister, the Islamic Jihad. The group's representative, Dr. Nafez Azzam described the Oslo Accords as "the original sin," saying the ìAccords gave Israel all the assets and left to us all the liabilities...Israel will never alter its behaviour toward us, whether we are for or against the peace process." Moreover, Azzam exhorted Arafat to boycott the regional economic conference slated to be held in Qatar in November. "This is not a conference for economic development but one for economic normalization; we should not allow ourselves to serve as economic middlemen between the Zionist entity and the Arab world," said Azzam, as Arafat looked on speechless.

While Arafat is not expected to heed the Islamists call for "terminating Oslo" (because that would mean terminating the PA itself, as well), he does seem to be trying to reach a certain common ground with Hamas and the Islamic Jihad, purely for his own expediency. Arafat's and the PA's popularity had reached an all-time low among Palestinians even before the 30 July suicide bombing in West Jerusalem. The revelation of the massive corruption scam in the PA seriously undermined the president's image, projecting him as one presiding over a corrupt entity made up of officials and ministers who are essentially preoccupied with embezzling public money rather than serving their country. Added to that is the crippling, ongoing Israeli siege on the West Bank and Gaza Strip, which effectively paralysed the originally fragile Palestinian economy and exposed the vulnerability and inherent weakness of the Palestinian Authority.

Faced with a serious credibility crisis at home (stemming from his increasingly manifest inability to justify continuing to walk along the Oslo path) and throttling Israeli pressures that would, in case he submitted to them, remove the remaining "fig leaf" by exposing him completely naked as an Israeli security tool not unlike the Israeli-backed militia in Southern Lebanon, Arafat must have reasoned that he cannot afford to be "too responsive" to Israeli dictates. More to the point, the Palestinian leader may have realized that the current Israeli government was "after him as much as it is after Hamas," a theory corroborated by the recent revelation in Foreign Affairs that Israel was preparing a hit team to assassinate Yasser Arafat and other leading PA officials.

Still more, Arafat feels immensely "betrayed and deceived" by Netanyahu whom Palestinian sources contend "wants to see Arafat act at Israel's beck and call in return for nothing." This point was reportedly related to Arafat by his more militant Fatah leaders.

"In the past, Arafat fought Hamas with vigour and some determination because he thought the peace process would eventually pay off. Now the Chairman has nearly lost this glimmer of hope, thus losing the incentive to fight Hamas," intimated an "insider" close the Chairman.

Nevertheless, Arafat doesn't seem about to embrace Hamas, let alone meet its "unrealistic demands." The most he seems to aspire to achieve is a form of modus vivendi with the Islamist movement that would give him greater maneuverability and ward off Israeli charges and pressures. The Palestinian leader must have realized by now that the "stick-policy" alone is utterly inadequate in dealing with a movement as big and as influential as Hamas and that it is wiser to try to convince rather than coerce Hamas to see the overall advantage of "suspending attacks on Israel for the time being."

However, in the absence of any Palestinian strategy to counter the Israeli "arrogance of power," as manifested in the frantic settlement expansion onslaught in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, any modus vivendi with Hamas would be proven untenable.

Indeed, even if Hamas decided to exercise a degree of "national responsibility," e.g. by refraining from carrying out attacks on Israeli targets, the "terrorism problem" would not disappear as other Islamist elements (which are not necessarily affiliated with Hamas or vulnerable to Arafat's stick) would continue the struggle.

Israel demolishes more Arab Homes in Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Israeli occupation bulldozers destroyed three additional Palestinian homes at the Ras al Amud neighborhood in East Jerusalem.

Palestinian sources in Jerusalem said two Israeli bulldozers destroyed the three houses shortly before noon Wednesday, rendering three families homeless.

A spokesman for the Israeli mayor of Jerusalem, Ehud Olmert, justified the brutal act on the ground that the three premises were built without license.

However, Palestinians point out that licensing is only a pretext, arguing that demolition of Arab houses in East Jerusalem, as it is in the West Bank, is an established Israeli policy aimed at checking Arab demographic growth.

"House demolition is consistent with the undeclared Israeli policy of preventing the Arab population in the city from exceeding the 27% -line," said Faisal Husseini, member of the PLO executive committee.

Husseini pointed out that Israeli municipal authorities in East Jerusalem refuse to issue building licenses for Palestinians inside the city for racist reasons pertaining to the ethnic cleansing policy.

"They don't want to see more Palestinians here," said Husseini

On Tuesday, a prominent Israeli journalist and writer, Uri Avneri, disclosed that the successive Israeli governments adopted an undeclared policy against Arab demographic expansion and growth in East Jerusalem soon after the Israeli occupation in June 1967.

Writing in the Hebrew daily Ma'ariv, Avneiri pointed out that Labour and Likud governments alike committed themselves to maintain the proportion of non-Jews (the Arabs) in Jerusalem at the 27 % point, the same percentage in 1967.

Faced with a crushing housing shortage stemming from natural growth in their population and the scarcity of building licenses, many East Jerusalemite Palestinians are forced to seek shelter in surrounding areas such as Bethlehem and Ram, thus risking withdrawal of their residency rights by the Israeli authorities.

"They are launching a demographic genocide against us in the full view of the world...they don't allow us to build shelters for our children, and when we go elsewhere to seek one, they tell us "you no longer have the right to live in Jerusalem," said Abdul Hamid Nasser Addin, a Jerusalemite Arab who lost his residency right in Jerusalem because he moved to a new house in the Ram neighborhood.

In other development, Israeli soldiers prevented a group of Christian clergymen from reaching their church in Jerusalem.

The clergymen, along with civic leaders and Legislative Council members from Bethlehem, walked to the Israeli army roadblock near the settlement of Gilo north of Bethlehem, hoping to be allowed to proceed to Jerusalem.

However, the soldiers turned them back, threatening to shoot them.

Bethlehem has been under a tight siege for a month, sparking off violent protests by townspeople.

On Tuesday, 26 August, as many as 40 school girls fainted after their school was bombarded with tear gas shells by Israeli soldiers.

The Israeli government has so far refused to lift the siege on Bethlehem allegedly for security reasons.

On Wednesday, the Israeli War minister, Yitzhak Mordachai, said the siege on the Palestinian town would continue for the foreseeable future.

The Israeli government has been demanding that the Palestinian Authority destroy the social, economic and educational infrastructure of Hamas, an allusion to hospitals, schools, universities and charitable institutions run by Islamists.

So far, the Palestinian Authority has refused to the Zionist dictates.

Israeli soldiers tread on the Qur'an in Dura

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

Israeli soldiers reportedly raided several mosques in the Hebron area on 11 August, desecrating the holy places and, in a clear provocation, treading on copies of the Holy Qur'an.

Palestinian sources in Dura, 10 miles south-west of Hebron, said scores of Israeli soldiers raided the Grand Mosque in Dura at 2:30 a.m. allegedly to "look for inciteful materials".

The mosque muezzin, Subhi al Masalmeh, said soldiers smashed glass inside the mosque. One soldier, he said, trod on copies of the Holy Qur'an in his full view probably to test his reaction.

Al Masalmeh said the soldiers desisted from the sacrilegious act only when al Masalmeh complained to his superior.

Meanwhile, Israeli troops raided Masjid al Mujahed three blocks away, smashing furniture and glass.

Afterwards, the soldiers raided the Al Anwar Athletic and Cultural Club to carry out "security searches."

Ahmed Qazzaz, who supervises cultural activities at the Club, said "the soldiers turned everything upside down, but found nothing that would incriminate us."

"They came looking for inciteful material and perhaps more than that, but found bags and pencils for school children," Qazzaz said, referring to a school paraphernalia sale at the Club for low income families in the area.

According to Palestinian sources, Israeli occupation troops have raided as many as 50 mosques throughout the West Bank since the 30 July double suicide bombing in West Jerusalem.

At least 20 mosques were closed down for varying periods of time and as many as 50 imams were rounded up as an act of reprisal against the Islamic movement which Israel blames for responsibility for the bombing.

Israeli sources said that the number of Palestinians rounded up since the bombing reached 300, half of which were reportedly sent to detention and concentration camps inside Israel.

Israel treats Palestinian detainees as hostages and makes their release conditional on receiving political concessions from Yasser Arafat's political Authority.

So far, Israel and the PA have not established the identity of the perpetrators of the bombing.

Following the suicide bombings.. Israel imposes

closure on West Bank and Gaza

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

In retaliation for the 30 July suicide bombing in West Jerusalem, the Israeli government imposed a "total closure" on the West Bank and the Gaza Strip, cutting off Arab towns and villages individually from each other and collectively from the outside world. Consequently, many vital supplies and services, including those usually taken for granted, were stopped due to IDF blockades of Palestinian population centres. In several instances, patients died and women had to give birth at military roadblocks, having been denied access to nearby hospitals. Moreover, the collective "town-arrest" seems to have inflicted incalculable damage to the originally fragile Palestinian economy.

Palestinian Authority sources described the draconian Israeli measures as "unprecedented in harshness" and "aimed at subjugating and humiliating our people." PA Chairman Yasser Arafat termed the Israeli reprisals as a "criminal act of genocidal proportions" and amounting to a "declaration of war," a description repeatedly echoed by his aides and ministers.

Speaking during a press conference in Gaza on 4 August, the Palestinian leader accused Israel of "tightening the noose on the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian people instead of fighting terrorism." Arafat's desperate words did carry an air of veracity. The Israeli measures, probably the harshest ever, virtually paralysed the PA as it forced Palestinian officials "to hibernate, each in his immediate place of residence," as one official put it. For some officials, the "claustrophobia" was so acute, that media celebrities such as Nabil Sha'ath publicly complained on the air to a Voice of Israel reporter "to help me if you can because I'm stranded in Gaza due to the closure."

Hafez al Barghouthi, Editor-in-Chief of the pro-PA daily, al Hayatul Jadida, marvelled at the effects of the Israeli measures in preventing terrorism."

"I don't understand anymoreî, he sarcastically wrote in his daily column on 5 August, "how the dumping of a truck-load of earth at village entrances would prevent a would-be terrorist from proceeding to his ultimate destination...Nor how barring newspapers from reaching Gaza would contribute to fighting terror...unless one takes into account the plausible possibility that a terrorist could very well fold himself in paper form or be resurrected from one of the obituaries."

Ostensibly, the collective punishment is meant to induce the PA to crack down on Hamas on behalf of Israel and also to "further narrow Palestinians' horizons" in the hope that they would come to terms with the Israeli fait accompli. However, it is seemingly unlikely that the Israeli policy would achieve the desired goal on both levels. The PA, which has never been weaker among Palestinians, is not in a position to openly and unashamedly act at Benyamin Netanyahu's beck and call as this would quicken its demise in the eyes of the Palestinians and decisively vindicate the often-voiced charges that it is merely an Israeli puppet militia aimed at tormenting the Palestinians and ravaging their collective psychological immunity vis-¦-vis Israel.

Hence, a wholesale crackdown on Hamas and the Islamic Jihad under Israel's incessant dictates would conceivably strengthen the Islamists in Palestinian opinion. It could also corrode the already frail political constituencies on which Arafat has based his leadership and orchestrated his support for the Oslo Accords.

Indeed, this seems to explain why the PA has not yet embarked on "an all-out campaign of mass arrests" of Islamist elements as was done following the spate of suicide bomb attacks in Israel in the spring of 1996. But there are other important reasons militating against a decision to that effect. The PA has come to feel that Hamas is no longer the sole sought-after enemy by Israel and that Fateh, the PA itself and, indeed, the "collective Palestinian interest" would eventually be, more or less, ultimate targets for Israeli insolence.

This view was recently echoed by Abbas Zaki, a prominent Fateh leader and member of the Palestinian Legislative Council. Speaking in Hebron last week (before the suicidal bombing), Zaki said Hamas was no longer the only bad guys in the sight of Israel, adding that "every patriotic Palestinian acting on his patriotism is now viewed as a terrorist by Israel."

Zaki's remarks were vindicated a day later when the """Israeli army issued a warrant to arrest Arafat's top police officer Brig. Gen. Ghazi al Jabali on terrorism-related charges. Needless to say, the arrest warrant against al Jabali, who had led the crackdown on Hamas and Islamic Jihad last year, served as an "eye-opener" for many PA officials and ordinary Palestinians, who had previously been told that "Hamas was the enemyî.

Zaki's views, however, do not seem to reflect "Yasser Arafat's thinking and that of his deputy, Mahmud Abbasî. Arafat reportedly expressed willingness to "resume full security and intelligence coordination" with Israel. For his part, Abbas, whose increasingly focal obsequiousness vis-¦-vis Israel and who has come to be viewed with growing repugnance and simmering discontent, said the West Jerusalem suicidal bombing necessitated the immediate resumption of the security coordination with Israel. However, it seems that Arafat's and Abbas's reconciliatory approach toward Israel won't do the Palestinians any good in the long run. The reason largely stems from the reasonably sound premise that "Palestinian terrorism is not the main factor behind Israeli intransigence, and that Israel accentuates this factor purely for political and propagandistic reasons," a premise the Palestinian leadership has not really understood, much less acted on.

Is it too late for peace?

Most ordinary Palestinians reacted to the latest suicidal bombing with stark ambivalence. However, this time it seems that their "indignation at Netanyahu" and "disenchantment with Arafat" markedly outweighed their innate reservations and objections to wanton killing. Indeed, a sizable majority of Palestinians seemed to squarely blame the Israeli government for "pushing Palestinians to the brink of despair."

"This man (Netanyahu) is offering us either of two choices: surrender or terrorism," said one PA minister from the Hebron area, adding that "many more people would ultimately become potential and actual suicide bombers if the Israeli government continued its current policies in the West Bank."

The minister's pessimism will likely gain added significance in light of continued mutual violence. On 4 August, an Israeli settler murdered an elderly Palestinian shepherd outside the village of Yatta, 15 miles south of Hebron, apparently in retaliation for the West Jerusalem bombing. Interestingly, members of the bereaved family reportedly shouted "death to the Jews" and "Ayyash, Ayyash," a reference to the Hamas master bomber who Israel says stood behind the wave of suicidal bomb attacks in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem last year.

More significant, however, is the fact that an increasing number of Palestinians, Arabs and Muslims, have come to believe that it is already late to reach an equitable, lasting settlement between Israel and the Palestinians in light of the facts in Jerusalem and the West Bank. Unfortunately, this view, however gloomy it may be, is being vindicated.

UNRWA to cut services to Palestine refugees

Gaza- The United Nations Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees (UNRWA) decided to cut services it provides to the Palestinian refugees in a bid to reduce its budgetary deficit that is estimated at $20 million for the 1997 fiscal year. In a letter that UNRWA Commissioner-General Mr. Peter Hansen addressed to about 22,500 employees Agency-wide, he said he was compelled to reduce the international staff working with UNRWA by 15%, to freeze the recruitment of 249 additional teachers needed by the Agency schools in the new scholastic year, to cease the Agency contribution to the university scholarships, and to freeze the referral of refugee patients to hospitals and the hospitalization reimbursements for November and December 1997. Mr. Hansen added that UNRWA is considering imposing school fees similar to those imposed by the countries that host Palestinian refugees. Despite these measures, Mr. Hansen said, they will reduce the estimated budgetary deficit of 1997 by only one third. He added that UNRWA will continue its efforts to urge the donors to increase their contributions to the Agency.
