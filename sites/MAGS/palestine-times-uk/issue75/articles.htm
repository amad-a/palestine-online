Interview with Hamas Leader:

Khalid Mish'al: Occupation should not feel like stability

Following is an extensive interview with the head of the political bureau of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) Mr. Khalid Mish'al. Mr. Mish'al was commenting on the latest developments that followed the 30 July double-suicide bombing in West Jerusalem.

 

Q. Until the most recent suicide bombing in West Jerusalem, the Palestinian occupied territories had witnessed a comparative calm since the coming to power of Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu. Many observers wondered the whereabouts of Hamas and its military wing during that period. Some had predicted that there was an implicit agreement between Hamas and the Palestinian Authority in order to calm down the situation.

A. Hamas is a movement to resist the Israeli occupation regardless of who undertakes this occupation: the Labour party or the Likud. The enemy is one and the occupation is the occupation. Therefore, Hamas is concerned with resisting the occupation and restoring all Palestinian rights. As for the lengthy time period between military operations against the occupation, it depends on the field circumstances of the military wing, Al Qassam Brigades, which is an independent body. This prediction could have been true before the last March bombing in Tel Aviv, but after that and after the July double-suicide bombings, it could not be so. The resistance is practically continuous. Hamas acts according to Palestinian interests; this is what determines the policies of Hamas.

 

Q. Under the present power imbalance, what could the military do?

A. Hamas reads the situation objectively. It is aware of the power imbalance and of the superior Zionist military against an inferior Arab military. But this should not lead to the elimination of the Resistance option or the military action against the occupation. Although the Resistance, under the current balance of power, is not able to remove the occupation of Palestine and to eventually end the struggle against the Zionist Enemy, it is necessary in the interest of our people. The occupation should not be rewarded by giving him the virtue of stability. This Enemy that occupies our land, desecrates our holy places and injures our religious feelings should not be given a chance to enjoy stability. If we are not able to defeat the Enemy today, we can make them pay the cost of its occupation, aggression and atrocities. This is the main objective of the heroic Resistance and Jihad; it is to exert continuous pressure on the Enemy and to deny it any sort of stability. This continuity and these accumulated efforts of resistance are those which drive the Enemy backward. They are the correct means to liberate our land and extract the legitimate rights of our people.

Sunday, 3 August, which was the deadline set in the communiquÈ that adopted the Jerusalem bombing in order to achieve some demands to be met by Israel, could be an example of the anxiety, worry and horror generated among the Zionists. That day, about 1,000 telephone calls were recorded of Zionists giving information about suspect persons and imagined explosives. This is the worry and instability that the Zionist occupiers should always feel. Why do the legitimate owners of the land have to suffer homelessness, oppression, suppression, killings and sieges while this Enemy that occupies their land enjoys stability?

We in Hamas perform this duty to liberate the occupied land and restore the Palestinian rights on the one hand and to stop and impede the Zionist plans and projects of expansionism, hegemony and domination over the Arab and Islamic worlds on the other. Also, those who direct this question should ask themselves what did the other options do for them? The peace settlement was given a four-year period after the signing of the Oslo Accords. What did it achieve? It was proven that Oslo did not achieve anything for our people. The settlements have not stopped; the confiscation of land continues; bypass roads are being constructed on our land; and the efforts to Judaize and annex Jerusalem continues. The 5,000 Palestinian detainees in the Occupation jails have not been released. Add to this the fact that Oslo has not achieved the promised economic stability or luxury for Palestinians. The present situation in the region made the Zionist Enemy insist on its plans to annex the Golan Heights and to continue its atrocities in South Lebanon. Therefore, we assert with full confidence that the only real option is Resistance.

 

Q. But you are accused of targeting the peace settlement not the Occupation by your bombings.

A. Hamas' resistance of the occupation began before the peace settlement through the popular resistance, intifadah, and the military operations. Moreover, the peace settlement is stalemated and lacks success and continuity. This stalemate was not caused by Hamas' resistance but because our Zionist Enemy does not want real peace. Even during the former Labour government, this Enemy did not honour the Oslo agreements. As for the present leadership of the Enemy, they are not convinced of the Oslo Accords and have their own agenda. The Enemy, therefore, is the party that impedes the peace settlement and that does not want to give real relinquishments. The Enemy, by its very nature, does not respect any agreements or treaties with others. Another point is that the current agreements are unjust for our people and therefore any settlement that is based on oppression, aggression and honouring the hostile party will never succeed or continue. Occupation has not come to an end, and the settlers continue their crimes against our people and continue provoking our religious feelings. Hence our people did not lament the stalemate peace settlement. Hamas is trusted by our people because it is more keen to their interests and it exerts efforts to alleviate their sufferings.

 

Q. Why didnít Hamas declare responsibility for the 30 July bombings?

A. The declaration of military operations is the responsibility of the Hamas military wing, Ezzul Deen Al Qassam Brigades. They are the ones who decided whether or not to adopt these operations. We, the politicians, do not intervene in such issues.

 

Q. How do you see your relation with the Palestinian Authority?

A. We hoped that our relation with the (Palestinian) Authority would be bilateral. In other words, the Zionist party would not affect this relation. The Zionist Occupation is a common enemy for all the Palestinian people. We and the Authority are included in the Palestinian people. We have always emphasized that there should not be any outside influencing factors that may trouble this relation. Hamas' policy has not changed since the intifadah or after the signing of the Oslo Accords until this moment. Although we significantly differ from the Authority regarding the political vision and programme and although we strongly oppose the Oslo Accords and their appendixes which strengthened the Occupation, we have a firm stand towards the internal relation which is to prohibit bloodshed among Palestinians and to avoid any clash with the Authority as this would only serve the interests of the Zionist Enemy. Our awareness of our people's rights and interests and our keenness not to engage our people in side battles, away from their real struggle against the Zionist Enemy, are the principles that govern the Hamas relation towards the other Palestinian forces, including the Palestinian Authority.

As for the Authority, its stand towards Hamas was affected by the continuous Zionist pressure under the pretext of the implementation of the commitments stated in the Oslo Accords. This led to the appearance of negative practices that did not serve our people. However, these practices were not enough to satisfy the Enemy with the Authority or to make him respect the agreements. Despite the harm that affected Hamas by the Zionist Enemy and the Authority, Hamas remained loyal to the Palestinian interests and emerged from the challenges stronger and more popular among the Palestinian people.

 

Q. But lately the Authority has shown positive signs towards Hamas.

A. We have noticed after the Jerusalem double-martyrdom bombing that the PA did not launch arrest campaigns as was the usual practice in the past. We appreciate this stand because we believe that there should never be arrest campaigns by the Authority affecting any of our people. The killings, arrests and arbitrary measures our people suffer from the Zionist Occupation are enough. Therefore, the Authority should rush to release the detainees in its jails. We emphasize that the Authority should not respond to pressures exerted on it by Netanyahu and the United States Administration through its envoy Dennis Ross, both urging the Authority to crack down on Hamas and its infrastructures. The security coordination with the Zionist security services is also not acceptable because it targets our people and their interests.

What we want today is to enhance the national unity and unify our people's efforts in the face of the Enemy and its crimes and atrocities against the Palestinians.

 

Q. Some PA officials say that your military operations harmed the Palestinians and brought Netanyahu to power at which point he suspended the peace settlement and accelerated the building of settlements.

A. The present policies of Netanyahu are not caused by the Hamas military operations. They are simply policies to implement his platform. Hamas did not bring Netanyahu to power; the Zionists who side with extremism and aggressive behaviour were behind his rise to power. Netanyahu came as a natural secretion of the Zionist Street. When Peres tried to attract the Zionist votes in the last public election, he did not adopt moderate or peaceful policies but instead he showed extremism by committing the Qana massacre in South Lebanon and by suspending the redeployment of the Israeli troops in Hebron. Peres knew that the Zionist popular taste was extremist. As for the Jewish settlement, it was not started by Netanyahu but by the Zionist Labour governments. Settlement policy is being agreed upon by the two main Zionist parties: the Labour and the Likud.

Also the Zionist crimes against our people were present even before the existence of Hamas. Those who say that times have changed should understand that it is they who changed. The Zionist crimes and aggressions did not change which obliges our people and their active forces to continue their resistance and confrontation.

 

Q. How do you see the attempts to involve Hamas in the case of the Arab suspects who were arrested in New York on charges of planning to attack American and Jewish targets?

A. The American story about the apprehension of the three Arabs in New York was fabricated by influential powers in the U.S. Administration that we believe to be Zionists who work to serve the Zionist interests more than the American ones. The timing of this claim is suspicious and the use of the name Hamas was not done innocently. The false involvement of the name Hamas in this story aimed to attract the world's attention away from what was happening in the occupied territories and to deform the image of Hamas among the American people in order to give the U.S. Administration an excuse to make Hamas a new enemy for it. This is despite the fact, known to all, that Hamas' struggle is exclusively against the Zionist Occupation.

 

Q. Can you predict the costs that Hamas may pay because of Washington's pressures concerning Hamas relations with European and Arab countries?

A. Hamas had faced various pressures by the Zionists and the U.S. Administration. They instigated states of the region against Hamas. But Hamas defeated all these attempts because it reflects the conscience of Palestinian people and the Arab and Islamic nation. Hamas also enjoys high credibility among many Arab and Islamic countries. We have good and respectable relations with these countries. Hamas is a deep-rooted movement in the Palestinian, Arab and Islamic Street. We believe that the U.S. Administration's interest is to refuse to be the object of the Zionist pressures exerted on it. America has no interest in making enemies of more than 1 billion Arabs and Muslims in the world by becoming involved in a war against Hamas in favour of the Zionist State.

The undoing of Oslo

By: Khalid Amayreh

Finally, as always, PA Chairman Yasser Arafat caved in to Israeli dictates and American blackmail, agreeing to "full security coordination" with Israeli security and intelligence organs. Arafat's latest capitulation to insolent Israeli-American dictates was as widely expected as it was humiliating. The Chairman, it is manifestly clear to all those who know what the Oslo Agreement is all about, is inherently powerless to say "No," not to even the most provocative and humiliating of Israeli demands. The reason is simple: the Oslo Agreement made the very survival of the Palestinian Authority almost wholly dependent on its inextricable subservience to Israeli security interests. In other words, Arafat (the pivotal figure of the PA) has no choice but to "heed Israeli security dictates" willy nilly for his own and his Authority's sake. So, doing Israel's dirty work in the occupied territories becomes in effect a "paramount Palestinian national interest," using the words of al Tayeb Abdul Rahim, one of Arafat's numerous advisers.

Doing Israel's will was relatively easy and politically less costly during the Peres and Rabin administrations. At least both gave Arafat something (usually trivial and insignificant in substance but big in the Chairman's eyes), often enabling him to claim before the easy-come, easy-go rabble that Palestinians were making gains and that those who resisted the Israeli occupation targets, e.g. Hamas and the Islamic Jihad, were actually undermining Palestinian achievements and, therefore, deserved to be called traitors and collaborators with the enemy.

The false logic worked somewhat effectively for some time until Benyamin Netanyahu came to power a year ago. Indeed, from the very inception, the right-wing premier demonstrated amply his contempt for the Oslo Accords and indicated that he would try to corrode the agreement and eviscerate it of substance. And so he did and has been doing. The opening of the subterranean tunnel underneath the Al Aqsa Mosque, the on-going building of new Jewish settlements at Jabal Abu Ghneim as well as throughout the West Bank, and the daily demolition of Arab homes, just to mention a few things, all testify to Netanyahu's recalcitrance and arrogance of power.

With Netanyahu's insolence flying in the face of the international community, as expressed in his frantic colonial settlement expansion drive in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, and with his gang-like government tightening the noose around Arafat and his PA despite the latter's submission to Israeli demands, Arafat's horizons became narrower as never before. Furthermore, American connivance with Netanyahu, effective international apathy and Arab paralysis seemingly largely demoralized the Palestinian leadership so much that Arafat threatened to "resume the intifadah if Netanyahu refuses to revert to common sense."

But Arafat is not going to resume the intifadah, even if he could, for anything like that would spell the end of Oslo, and with it, that of the Palestinian Authority, which the Chairman considers an important national achievement that ought to be maintained at any price. Moreover, it is highly unlikely that the PA leadership would be able to convince significant numbers of Palestinians to take to the streets and risk being shot dead by Israeli soldiers for illusive tactical goals centring around improving Arafat's negotiating position. Indeed, it may be sufficiently safe to assume, given the mounting frustration and disenchantment with the PA in the occupied territories, that any new intifadah would be directed this time against both the Israeli occupation authorities and the PA combined. The prospect must be nightmarish for Arafat and may explain his reluctance to heed Israel's incessant demands to carry out massive arrests among Islamist elements within area "A".

On 16 August, Arafat told a group of carefully chosen journalists and intellectuals that he would not budge with respect to Israeli dictates to fight Hamas and destroy its infrastructure in the West Bank. The Chairman was also quoted as saying that "we reject the Israeli demands that we act against our brethren and do not intend to accept dictates on this from anyone." Surprisingly, the ostensibly defiant remarks did not draw angry reactions from the Israeli government. According to the Jerusalem Post, the remarks were passed off by the government as mere rhetoric and unworthy of a formal response.

So, if Arafat's threats are rhetorical, as they indeed seem to be, what choices is he left with? Maintaining the status quo (Netanyahu's preference), if at all possible, would perpetuate an apartheid arrangement whereby mounting Palestinian indignation would eventually explode into a real intifadah unlike anything the Arab-Israeli conflict has ever witnessed. Conceivably, the eruption of violence would be on a wide scale and would spread to neighbouring countries such as Lebanon, Jordan and Egypt. On the other hand, continuing to monkey for the Israeli organ grinder would only vindicate the long-held views, increasingly rife among ordinary Palestinians, that the PA is but an internationally recognized Palestinian version of the SLA, the Israeli puppet militia in South Lebanon.

Arafat, nevertheless, does have an alternative that would be honourable for himself and for the just Palestinian cause, provided that he shows leadership, courage and historical responsibility. That alternative must necessarily be the undoing of the Oslo arrangement by placing the Palestinian issue back in its proper context. Indeed, Arafat could rightly claim that the Oslo Agreement was meant to implement United Nations resolutions 242 and 338 which call for the Israeli pull-out from the West Bank and Gaza. However, given Israel's adamant refusal to abide by these resolutions, and her constant efforts to make them unrealistic by making them largely irrelevant (given the settlements), the Palestinians, the PA Chairman could argue, are no longer bound by a vague agreement that could only have one result: the liquidation of the Palestinian question. An audacious feat as such on Arafat's part would undoubtedly spell the end of the PA. But so what? An inherently weak, inept, and corrupt Authority that is totally dependent for its life and continued survival on the occupying power is not worthwhile or worth maintaining. It is therefore better, much better, for the Palestinians and their enduring, just cause to reappear before themselves and before the world as they really are, a colonized and tormented people aspiring for freedom and deliverance from the shackles of Zionist occupation and domination.

Would Arafat be courageous enough to do it? It is hard to say, and many would not give him the benefit of the doubt. But if he didn't, and soon (because time is running out for a just and durable settlement in light of the demographic changes in the West Bank), he would only go down in history as the Palestinian leader who saw over the liquidation of his people's cause.

Clinton's Health Improves While Palestinian Health Declines

By Robert W. McGee*

The occupying Israeli government continues its ethnic cleansing policy in Palestine while President Clinton takes his daily jogs for exercise. A recent New York Times photo of President Clinton and his daughter Chelsea shows them jogging while on vacation in Martha's Vineyard. The two and a half mile jog was the longest since his knee operation. The T-shirt he was wearing said "I Met Yoni Dotan." Dotan is the son of an Israeli entertainer. The shirt was given to him in connection with the Make a Wish Foundation, a group that grants wishes to severely ill children. Chelsea's T-shirt had the inscription "Live Free or Die," a popular phrase during the American Revolution.

While Clinton jogged to improve his health, the health of the Palestinians in the occupied territories was growing worse with each passing day. The occupying Israeli forces continue their systematic policy of preventing Palestinians from entering hospitals for treatment, going to work or to the market to buy needed food and medical supplies. They continue to demolish Palestinian homes, leaving dozens of Palestinian families homeless. They bulldoze roads, making it difficult or impossible for Palestinians to travel or transport goods. They turn away tourist buses, preventing them from entering Bethlehem during the peak of the tourist season, thus further weakening the Palestinian economy. Vegetables are rotting in the fields and new crops cannot be planted because farmers are prevented from getting to their fields. The fragile Palestinian economy is losing $6 million a day during the siege, according to some estimates, and Palestinians face hunger if the siege continues.

Zahya Harb, a 19 year-old pregnant Bedouin woman was denied permission to pass through the checkpoint in Beitunia, a village near Ramallah located 1.5 kilometers from the hospital. After waiting two hours, she was forced to give birth in a car. Sleiman Alhamouz was brutally beaten while on his way to a hospital in Hebron. Twelve year-old Raed Faris was severely beaten by soldiers as he was walking on his family's property. Israeli soldiers randomly selected a large number of men and children from the village of Azawiya and severely beat them.

The occupying forces have placed a number of restrictions on the movement of medical personnel and their mobile medical clinics. Mobile clinics and physicians have been denied passage into or through several cities and villages, including Tulkarem and Nablus. They could not pass through the checkpoint at Beitunia to reach villages in Northern Jerusalem. They could not leave Beit Sahour to go to near-by villages. There has been a 50 percent decrease in employee attendance at Al-Makassad Hospital in Jerusalem because employees were prevented from getting to work. St. John's Hospital in Jerusalem has seen a 60 percent decline in services. In the Red Crescent Hospital in Jerusalem, half the physicians and nurses have not been able to reach the hospital for at least two weeks.

Palestinian patients have been systematically forced to leave hospitals before finishing their treatments. Randa Hassouna was kicked out of intensive care at Hadassah Ein Karem Hospital after suffering head trauma and a concussion following a car accident. Issa Al Makhamra was expelled even though she had a severe spinal infection. In fact, the hospital expelled all Palestinian patients after the bombings in West Jerusalem. Such collective punishment tactics are reminiscent of the nazis during the 1940s.

Israeli settlers, with the backing of the occupying Israeli military, have stepped up their attacks on Palestinians. Palestinians have been randomly shot at as they try to go to work or transfer the wounded to nearby hospitals. Soldiers have invaded more than 40 villages to spread terror among the residents. Even Palestinians who have permits have been refused entry into Jerusalem or Israel (occupied Palestine). About 33,000 students and 15,000 teachers have been denied access to schools and universities. But the real question is why Palestinians should need permits to travel about in their own country? Palestinians have fewer rights than the Jews had under the nazis. Yet the Clinton administration, like so many administrations before him, continue to support such human rights abuses by giving the occupying Israeli government billions of dollars in foreign aid.

I have a suggestion for President Clinton's next vacation. Why not spend a few weeks in the West Bank or Gaza to see how the "other half" lives under Israeli occupation? A few T-shirt suggestions -- how about "I Met Zahya Harb" or "Stop the Ethnic Cleansing" or Cut off Aid to Israel"? And for Chelsea, how about "Palestine for the Palestinians," printed in Arabic, Hebrew and English? For gift suggestions -- why not give "Live Free or Die" T-shirts to members of the U.S. Embassy staff. Maybe that will give them a different perspective on the "peace" process.

 

* Prof. Robert W. McGee is the President of The Dumont Institute for Public Policy Research, Dumont, NJ- USA-

Another American Perception of Israeli Occupation

By David McLau*

The Palestinians are justified to strike back against their Israeli occupiers by any means necessary, including what we saw last month. We should remember that they do not have modern weapons. They fight with what they have. We should not deny them the right to self-defence while their homeland is being forcefully taken from them. There are some people who condemn the violence. But, the right of self-defence is just as basic as the right to live free from violence. In fact, that is why we supposedly have the Fourth Geneva Accords, to provide a means of avoiding the violence of ethnic cleansing.

The Fourth Geneva Accords suposedly prevents exactly the kind of colonization that Israel has done to Palestine. But , the Palestinians do not have the benefit of that law that the rest of us have , because of the veto of the United States in the United Nations.

The Accords clearly state that it is impermissible to acquire land by means of force which Israel did in 1967 and also 1948. Furthermore, it states that it is illegal to remove the indigenous population and colonize any territory with one's own population. Are we going to live by the law? Remember, there was a time when we had no laws and the Jews paid a terrible price. Now we have laws, and it is the Jews who do not want to abide by them!

The United States has used its veto power in the United Nations to prevent the world body from stopping the continuous land confiscation and ethnic cleansing in the Occupied Territories, time and again, over the past 30 years.

The Palestinians now have no other legal recourse but to resort to violence, and it will be in self-defence. Their violence will be justified both by history and by international law since they are defending their homeland from this illegal occupation and ethnic cleansing. It is exactly the way the French and Russian resistance movements fought their occupiers during the Second World War. Some may argue that the French resistance movement never killed German civilians during their struggle. However, one must remember that Germany never tried to colonize France with its civilians during that war the way that Palestine has now been colonized by the Israelis. And it is for that reason that civilian casualties in France were fewer as a result. One must only remember the intentional fire bombing of Dresden by the allies at the end of the War when it was common knowledge that the city was full of refugees, of which 200,000 died as a result of that firestorm, to see that war and self-defence can, at times, be very cruel.

I have come to the above-stated conclusion with regard to the Palestiniansí right to self-defence only after becoming well informed as to the daily confiscation of land, demolition of houses, torture of civilians, collective punishments, detentions and so on suffered by Palestinians. I see no other alternative for them.

* American writer

Palestinians defend their Aqsa Mosque

By Steve Sozby

 

East Jerusalem- Nearly everyday for the past several weeks, Israeli police have detained Jewish fanatics trying to enter Al-Haram Al-Sherif to pray. Located just above the Wailing Wall, the most sacred site for Jews, Al-Haram includes Al-Aqsa Mosque, the third holiest site in Islam, and the striking Dome of the Rock, which covers the location where Mohammed ascended to heaven.

To most outside observers, these acts of religious provocation against Palestinian Moslems are easily dismissed as lunatic behavior of a few mentally unstable zealots. A closer look, however, suggests a deeper trend in the religious nationalist Zionists movement that seeks to eventually take control of Al-Haram for the purpose of rebuilding the Third Jewish Temple. "Many rabbis and religious people in Israel hold that the messiah cannot come until the temple is rebuilt," explains Yaariv Ehud, an orthodox resident of the Jewish Quarter of Jerusalem. "The temple can only be rebuilt where it once stood, which is where the Moslems now have their mosque."

Threats to al-Aqsa and the Dome of the Rock began when Israel captured the Old City in 1967. A debate began then among government and religious leaders concerning the issue of a new temple. Though the Labor Government was then careful not to openly threaten any of the Arab churches or mosques in Jerusalem, others in Israel began acts of provocation that greatly increased tension in Palestine.

In 1969, an Australian tourist set Al-Aqsa ablaze. Israeli authorities denounced the act of arson and deported him back home, but for Moslems, who slowly heard talk of rebuilding a Jewish temple on the Temple Mount, suspicions grew. Israeli authorities began excavation activities around the outside walls of Al-Aqsa in the early 1970s, seeking the City of David, which was the King David's capital in Palestine thousands of years ago. Al-Aqsa and the Al-Haram compound started to suffer structural damage as a result of the diggings and Palestinians began to complain to the Israelis, who slowed down, but did not stop, their efforts to find past archeological glory.

In 1977 a new Likud government under former Argon terrorist Menachem Begin was elected in Israel. He formed a right-wing government that was ideologically devoted to the settlement of "Eretz" or greater Israel. Groups that were formerly on the fringe of Israeli politics had now one of their own in power and took the chance to further their efforts to undermine Arab control over places they sought for their own. The West Bank saw an explosion in religious settlements, as opposed to the "security" type that Labor created. Begin spoke of a "Divine duty" to settle "Judea and Samaria" (the West Bank) and found no shortage of willing citizens, spurred on by tax breaks and economic incentives to live on land taken by force from local Palestinians.

There was an alarming increase in provocative acts by young yeshiva students trying to enter Al-haram to pray. In 1978, bombs were found near Al-Aqsa in the possession of such groups. There were many violent confrontations between them and the Waqf guards, who prevented extremist Israelis from entering Al-haram to pray.

A settler underground was formed in 1980 to employ terror and violence against innocent Arabs in the West Bank. Composed mostly of settlers around Hebron and Nablus, the underground called itself "Terror Against Terror" or TNT in Hebrew. Explosive they were. In addition to putting bombs in the cars of three West Bank mayors, planting bombs in crowded stadiums and markets in Hebron, injuring dozens of people, and carrying out a machine gun attack on Hebron University, which left three students dead and 40 more injured, the underground also looked to the Moslem shrines in Jerusalem as a target of their zeal.

In 1984, a cell broke off of the TNT mainstream group because they felt that the other Jewish terrorists were neglecting their duty to destroy Al-Aqsa for the sake of a new temple. This cell was called the Lifta Group, named after the empty Palestinian village in West Jerusalem that was attacked by Israeli troops in 1948 and where the terrorists hid their weapons for the attack. Fortunately, Waqf guards saw three men climbing down into Al-haram one foggy morning and alerted Israeli police, who arrested them. The men were armed with assault rifles, a dozen hand grenades and other explosives.

The other members of TNT hesitated in their actual attack of Al-Aqsa, waiting to get official religious approval for their attack on the shrines. Leading rabbis in Israel have always called for the right of Jews to pray on "the Temple Mount" but have come short in calling for the elimination of the Islamic presence there. By the time that TNT was ready to carry out the attack, the Israeli intelligence had penetrated the group. Over 40 settlers were arrested, including army officers with access to heavy weapons. Despite murdering and maiming many innocent Arabs, all members of TNT have since been released from prison.

The Intifada years saw more mainstream Israeli groups established to address the issue of rebuilding the Temple on Al-Haram. Gershon Solomon, a Jerusalem politician, is the leader of "The Temple Mount Faithful." The regularly lead marches to the gates of Al-haram calling for the rebuilding of the temple there. It is this group's effort to lay a cornerstone near al-Aqsa in 1990 that sparked the massacre of 19 Palestinian civilians in al-Haram.

While the Israeli government claims to endorse a tolerance for all faiths in Jerusalem, the Israeli courts continue to grant prayer rights to extremists like Yehuda Etzion, a former TNT leader, who now heads a group called Hai Vakayoom. Their main aim is to regain the Temple Mount for Isreal and the Jews. "I will not rest until the Temple Mount is restored to its rightful owners, the Jews," Etzion recently told a group of journalists in Jerusalem. "Let the Moslems take the mosque to Saudi Arabia and pray there."

The Jebal Abu-Ghneim settlement, the new effort to build Jewish housing at Ras Al-Amud, the opening of a tunnel near al-Aqsa and the confiscation of thousands of Jerusalem ID cards all suggests a pattern of Israeli behavior in Arab Jerusalem that leads to further confrontation and violence. There is no greater risk of large-scale bloodshed in the Holy Land, however, than the threat to Al-haram. "We will give every drop of our blood to protect our mosques," says Ahmed Ibrahim in Ramallah. "The youth of Palestine have died in the past protecting our sacred Al-Aqsa and we will do so again and again until the enemies of Islam know that we will not be destroyed."

Are rabbis serious about dialogue with Muslim

religious leaders?

by Khalid Amayreh

Following the 30 July suicidal bombing in West Jerusalem, some Jewish religious leaders renewed earlier calls for a Jewish-Muslim dialogue to discuss particular issues, especially, the relatively new phenomenon (in Islam but not in Judaism) of suicidal bombings or "martyrdom operations" as often labelled by many Muslims. The latest call made earlier this month by Israel's Chief Rabbi Yisrael Meir Lau was not the first of its kind. On 29 January 1995, a week after the Beit Lid bombing, Lau made a similar appeal in which he criticized Muslim religious leaders for not condemning suicidal bombings by Palestinians against Jews.

Lau's calls, it is noted, were all made in the aftermath of suicidal bombings in which Israelis lost their lives. However, no such calls were ever made after similar, even far more deadly atrocities were committed against Muslim civilians by Israeli settlers (e.g. the Ibrahimi Mosque Massacre on 25 February 1994) or by recurrent IDF action (e.g. the Qana carnage on 18 April 1996). In fact, in the first example, some rabbis from Gush Emunim openly eulogized the murderer, Baruch Goldstein, as "a great saint and hero," and actually made his tomb in Kiryat Arba, on the outskirts of Hebron, a pilgrimage site. Others (including some Knesset members) gleefully celebrated the slaughter in the spirit of the Jewish holiday called the Purim. In the second example (the Qana massacre), which was perpetrated premeditatedly as ascertained by the United Nations, Israeli political and religious leaders alike adamantly refused to say "sorry" for the killing of over 100 defenseless Lebanese men, women and children.

Al-Mujaydil Before 1948

The village stood on the gently-sloping, southern slopes of Wadi al-Mujaydil, on the Nazareth-Haifa highway. In 1596, al-Mujaydil was in the nahtya of Tabariyya (ltwa' of Safad) with a population of twenty-two. It paid taxes on wheat, barley, fruit trees, goats, and beehives. [Hut. and Abd.:1871 In the late nineteenth century al-Mujaydil was described as a "flourishing" village built of stone and mud. It was on the northern side of a small plateau, and olive groves were cultivated to the east and south. The number of its residents was estimated at 800 (in 1859); they cultivated 100 faddans (1 faddan = 100-250 dunums; see Glossary). [SWP (1881) 1:275] Several springs flowed in the vicinity of the village, mainly in the southwest. Al-Mujaydil had a triangular outline; its houses were clustered closely together, and were made of stone and mud, stone and cement, or concrete. In 1945 it was the third largest village in the district in terms of population; it had 1,640 Muslims and 260 Christian inhabitants. There were two elementary schools in al-Mujaydil, one for boys and one for girls. The village also had a Roman Catholic church and a monastery, and a local council that administered its affairs.

A Survey of Palestine [(1946)1:139] estimates the council's expenditures at 36 Palestinian pounds (Pi) in 1929 but offers no figures for subsequent years. The Palestine Gazette [no. 1630, cited in D 7/2:138] shows that these expenditures amounted to 80 Palestinian pounds in 1937 and then fell to 34 Palestinian pounds in 1942, although the revenues continued to grow. Agriculture was the backbone of the economy; the most important crop was grain. In 1944/45 a total of 14,982 dunums was allotted to cereals; 1,685 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards, the bulk of which (1,600 dunums) was planted with olive trees. Al-Mujaydil was the second largest olive producer in the district and had two mechanical olive presses in operation. Traces of a Roman road were found close to the village, which may indicate that the region was opened to intensive settlement as early as Roman times.

 

Occupation and Depopulation

Like neighboring Ma'lul, al-Mujaydil was occupied by a unit of the Golani Brigade, according to the History of the Haganah. That occurred on 14 or 15 July 1948, during the second stage of Operation Dekel (see 'Amqa, Acre District), as the Israeli army closed in on Nazareth. The attack on this village misled the defenders of Nazareth into believing that the city would be attacked from the south. The History of the Haganah states: "It seems that the Arabs of Nazareth were confident of their ability to repulse any attack from that direction [i.e. the south]," but it goes on to describe how the assault on Nazareth proceeded from the northwest, by Israeli units concentrated in the previously occupied town of Shafa 'Amr.

Al-Mujaydil was one of the villages in Lower Galilee that was completely emptied of its inhabitants and then razed to the ground. [M:xv, 199-200; T:249] About 1,200 refugees from al-Mujaydil had reportedly reached Nazareth by the end of July 1948, but it is not clear whether they remained there. [M:343, n.6]

 

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

Jewish immigrants from Iran established the settlement of Migdal ha-'Emeq (172231) in 1952 on village land, less than 1 km southwest of the village site. The older settlement of Yif'at (171231), established in 1926 on what were traditionally village lands, is 2 km west of the site.

 

The Village Today

Most of the site is covered with a pine forest that serves as an Israeli park. The monastery and parts of the (destroyed) church are the only remaining buildings on the site; monks still live in the monastery (see photos). Remnants of destroyed houses and the walls of a cemetery are visible. Cactuses and pomegranate, olive, and fig trees grow around the site, which is dotted with wells.

GARDEN OF EDEN

BY E. YAGHI

The noise of the city thundered in Yahya's ears and blotted out the songs of the early morning sparrows. The aroma of jasmine and roses drifted up to greet him as he reluctantly climbed into a private taxi and headed for work.

Cars honked relentlessly and the taxi driver swore at various offenders. The driver spat out of his half opened window and then flipped out a cigarette from a box beside him, pushed the car lighter into its socket and nervously lit his cigarette. Yahya sat in the back of the car to avoid the driver's company and to meditate in the few minutes he had before reaching his job as engineer at a multinational company somewhere in Suburbia. He choked on the gray smoke that clouded even the back seat and stared with watery eyes at the madness about him. His dark reddish brown hair curled in ringlets around his face while his brows wrinkled in frustration.

When he reached his destination, he gave the driver a quick, "Stop here, please," and jumped out of the car, reached in his pockets and pulled out some money with "how much?" He paid the required amount, grabbed his briefcase and told the driver, "thank you."

Slowly he entered the huge glass facade building, shuffling his feet hesitantly. After he entered the office, he passed an array of secretaries and with amber eyes and mustached mouth, greeted them with a "good morning," pushing on into his own chambers where he flopped his briefcase down on his large desk and sunk into his blue swivel chair. A few hours later, he had immersed himself in paperwork, frequently chewing on the lid of his pen and scratching his head in thought.

One of the secretaries entered his office and said, "Sorry to disturb you Yahya, but Mr. Dawwas would like to see you.

He ignored the attractive face of the secretary and disregarded her pleasant smile. He normally would stop for a chat or a joke or two, but this time he trudged to his boss's office, dreading another encounter. He knocked on the brown door and somewhere deep inside, a gruff "come in" commanded him to enter. He cautiously shoved open the door and entered, his expression questioning his superior.

"Well, come closer! Don't stand so far away," Mr. Dawwas growled. "I've got a few issues to discuss with you. I consider you intelligent, but, I don't like your honest attitude. Too much honesty is a hindrance. I didn't get where I am just being a nice guy. Furthermore, I must insist you be more assertive towards those under you. Now, I've got a job I need done. If you refuse to perform it, then I'll have to consider your dismissal. If you comply, there will be certain benefits including perhaps an increase in pay as well as promotion."

Yahya felt the rough yet magnetic glare of his boss. He cast his own eyes downward, breaking the lock. "Sir, if it's about taking advantage of the poor or lying or cheating as you suggested before, then I'm sorry. I can't fulfill your request. It's not my nature to be deceitful. Not for money or power!"

He felt his eyes being pulled hypnotically to those of his employer. The fiery glint that had been there before extinguished and Mr. Dawwas's eyes grew cold and his face hard as he bellowed, "Very well. As you please. But don't say I didn't try to help you. I will consider your words and think about what to do with you. No one is indispensable, no matter how clever. Please leave!" and he motioned to his door with a dismissing hand.

Yahya had expected something like this to happen. He turned on his heels and thrust himself out of the office entrance, his face red with embarrassment. After he returned to his own office, he thought about Mr. Dawwas' demands as he toyed with his pen. At length he grabbed his briefcase and stormed out of the building. That was it. He couldn't work for a man he hated. A man he thought unjust and evil. But nevertheless, Yahya thought of himself as a failure and hung his had in self-accusations as he returned to his home where he lived with some of his friends.

He changed from his stiff business suit to some more causal trousers, scratched a quick note on a piece of paper and left. He took a bus out of Suburbia away from the noise and confusion. Seemingly in the middle of nowhere, he stopped the bus and descended to freedom.

He struck out for a small clearing where he whiffed the fresh mountain air. His chest expanded and a smile crept across a face that not long before had frowned in resentment. His bag landed on the ground with a thump and Yahya threw himself beside it and leaned against a fir tree. The odors of the forest filled his nostrils and the scolding of nearby birds delighted him. He reached for his bag and pulled out a flute on which he played a melancholy tune to the lavender wild flowers and evergreen trees. A small breeze tossed his curls, giving him a boyish look.

He quit playing his flute, sighed and spoke to the unseen residents of the forest. "Why is it that some people grow rich from the poverty of others? Why must the rich rule the poor and lies govern truth? I thought I could change the world with my degree and hopes but I can't even change one company or even the ideas of one man. I must be a misfit. Ideas of love, charity, sharing and giving seem to be fashions of the past. Is chivalry dead? I am tired of competition, tired of adulating masses who worship power, money and glory. I want to be simple and honest."

In despair he rose, brushed himself off, clutched his bag and began to walk. The mountain air filled his lungs as he plowed through thorn scrub with his shoes. At times he bent down and pulled a thistle from his leg and then continued on down a winding path towards a green valley.

As he sauntered with refreshed spirits, he thought he heard someone crying. He stopped to listen as a small voice moaned, "Oh, please, somebody help me!"

He waited to determine the direction of the cry and upon hearing it again, made his way to the sound.

He searched for the voice and then in surprise discovered a young boy hugging a distorted foot. The boy's face was stained with tears and twisted in pain. When he caught sight of Yahya, the boy's countenance brightened. "Hello there," Yahya called. "What's wrong with you boy? Did you hurt your foot?"

"Yes, sir. I was on my way back to my village when I caught my foot on some bramble bushes and tripped. When I tried to get up and walk, my foot hurt me so much that I fell down again. Please, can you help me?"

So, the compassionate Yahya bundled the boy on his back, struggled with his bag and limped to the village. He was greeted as a hero and the boy was carefully lifted off Yayah's back and taken away to have his foot mended.

Yahya never left the small village. Instead he fell in love with it and considered it to be a safe haven apart from the din, confusion and greed of

the world. He applied his education to the needs of the humble honest folk in a place he considered to be his Garden of Eden. On quiet nights if you pass Yayah's village, you may still hear him playing a sweet tune on his silver flute in the midst of the ringing laughter of his happy wife and children.

As for his greedy boss, God does not forget the bad deeds a person performs. The boss became severely paralyzed and was unable to continue his life of crime and evil. He now sits in a lonely wheelchair attended by one faithful servant and is shut away from the honest and pure pleasures of life.

Book Review

Citizen and the State

A comparative study of citizenship legislation in Israel, Jordan, Palestine, Syria and Lebanon

Author: Uri Davis

Published 28 August 1997

224pp , 235 x 155 mm, Price: 30 Sterling Pounds

In this analysis of the relationship between the individual and the state and the ideas of citizenship, sovereignty and national self-determination as played out in the Levant, Dr. Uri Davis introduces a new conceptual framework for the Middle East. In doing so, he unlocks many of the critical issues which confront the region today: democratization, the role of religion in the state, human rights and the prospects of political stability.

Taking the idea of citizenship as the point of departure, the book critically examines the call for a "new Middle East", which figures centrally in the regional and international political narrative that has emerged since the Gulf War and the launch of the Middle East regional peace process in Madrid in 1991. The subsequent Israel-PLO negotiations concluded in Oslo and signed in Washington in 1993 (Oslo I) and 1995 (Oslo II) are likewise subjected to critical examination.

Five detailed case-studies follow, wherein Uri Davis examines the citizenship legislation of the four states of the LevantóIsrael, Jordan, Syria and Lebanon-and of the Palestinian Authority, particularly in the light of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and relevant United Nations covenants. Much of this material is made available to the English readership for the first time here.

Addressing some of the most intractable problems of the Levant, Dr. Davis's important study breaks new ground for change in the Arab world. He presents an argument for a federal Middle East modelled on the European Union, and his conclusions have a direct bearing on the Palestine question and the implications of the peace process for Palestinian sovereignty. In the light of the case-studies, he also makes recommendations that are directed to local, regional and international non-governmental organizations active in campaigning for the defence of human rights and for democratization in the region.

Dr. Uri Davis is Chairman of Al-Beit (Association for the Defence of Human Rights in Israel) and one of Israel's leading human rights activists. His publications on democracy and human rights in Israel and Palestine are numerous.
