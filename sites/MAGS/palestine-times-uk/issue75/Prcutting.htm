Despite the arrival on Saturday of the American mediator Dennis Ross, the Israeli government has sworn to keep the remaining closures in place until the Palestinian leader, Yasser Arafat, arrests more than 150 activists from the Islamist group Hamas, suspected by the Israelis of planning the suicide attack. Palestinian officials say they have arrested some suspects but are not prepared to carry out a mass internment on Israel's orders. Despite their present hardship, Mr. Hadah and his fellow traders say Hamas members should not be detained without proper evidence.

"Hamas people have children too. Who's going to feed them when they're in prison? And why should they go to prison anyway?" asked Yusef Showamri, selling watermelons at a nearby stall.

As the traders were talking about 50 teenage boys and young men, clutching small rocks and tyres, walked past them toward the checkpoint, chanting Hamas slogans.

A Hamas banner held aloft by the crowd proclaimed: "We salute and are proud of the martyrs." Julian Borger writes from Ramallah, West Bank. The Guardian, August 12, 1997.

 

But those Palestinians bulldozed off their land will feel differently. Mr. Arafat is being asked to take tougher measures against Hamas in a climate where Israel holds the PA under siege, starves it of funds which it is owed, frustrates implementation of deals on essential infrastructural projects, and continues to pre-empt a final agreement by creating new facts-new settlements-on the map. This is not only unrealistic but - if he complies - bound to weaken his authority further... If there is a slim chance of persuading Mr. Arafat and the Palestinian people to take the risk, then the impartiality of the White House must be established above suspicion. From the editorial of The Guardian, August 12, 1997.

AS A court sentenced two Israeli border policemen yesterday to eight months in jail for beating and humiliating six Palestinians at a military checkpoint, more evidence emerged of similar assaults. Tashi Shamaya, 19, and David Ben Abu, 20, were given additional 12-month suspended sentences. The assaults were filmed by an amateur Palestinian cameraman last October. The videotape, showing the two policemen kicking some of the Palestinians in the stomach and forcing others to do push-ups, was broadcast round the world and was used as evidence in the trial. Shamaya and Ben Abu were charged with aggravated assault and abuse of authority for their treatment of the Palestinians, who had been caught entering Israel without permits. Ross Dunn writes from Jerusalem, The Times, August 15, 1997.

 

The Israeli prime minister demands that Mr. Arafat crush the extremists and deliver their leaders to Israel. The Palestinian leader is incapable of doing that, and would be destroyed if he tried, since Mr. Netanyahu insists that whatever Mr. Arafat does, Israel will make no substantive political concessions and will permit no Palestinian state to exist. If we are to believe what President Clinton and Mrs. Albright have most recently said about the American role, Mr. Arafat's hopes are illusions. They still insist that the Israelis and Palestinians must settle all this between them. William Pfaff writes, The International Herald Tribune, August 15, 1997.

 

At the top of the list of targets in the crackdown are leaders of the military wing of the Islamic Resistance Movement, known as Hamas. The militant group has claimed responsibility for several "terrorist" bombings in Israel and is widely suspected of masterminding the attack at the Jerusalem market. Israeli security sources said the names of prime suspects were passed to Palestinian security officials right after the bombing -- names that included Mohammed Deif, chief of operations for the military wing of Hamas, and his deputy, Muhi Adin Sharif. Deif is the successor to Mohammed Yayha Ayyash, nicknamed "The Engineer" for his alleged bomb-making prowess. Ayyash was killed 18 months ago when a booby-trapped cellular telephone exploded in his hand - an assassination widely believed to have been carried out by Israeli agents. William Drozdiak writes,The Washington Post, August 19, 1997.

 

The time provided by the Oslo formula for incremental steps to build confidence for a final compromise has gone by. It has not brought any confidence. On the contrary, there is much less confidence than five years ago. But that does not mean that plunging straight into ''final negotiations'' on the most contested issues would bring any better results than edging up to them has done. Judging by his recent decisions, Prime Minister Netanyahu's plan for Palestinian self-government appears to be a disconnected set of enclaves on a minimum of territory which can be disrupted, surrounded, denied any chance of viability at the sole discretion of Israeli authority. Flora Lewis writes, The International Herald Tribune, August 22, 1997.
