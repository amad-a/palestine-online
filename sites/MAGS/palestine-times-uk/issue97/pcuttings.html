Press Cuttings

Ha'aretz, 21 June 1999

Palestinian police in Gaza took an unusual step last week, trying to stop Palestinian laborers from traveling to their workplaces in the Gaza Strip's Jewish settlements. Palestinian spokesmen explained that this was an official move on behalf of the authorities, intended to demonstrate the PA's position, which places the struggle against the settlements at the top of its agenda. Later, at its weekly meeting in Ramallah, the Palestinian leadership decided to demand that the laborers stop working in the settlements.

The interesting question in this context is not why the Palestinian Authority took this step now, but rather why the Palestinian institutions and PLO did not do so sooner. Palestinian laborers have been building the settlements for more than 30 years. The number of Palestinians currently working in the settlements is some 11,000.

 

The Sunday Telegraph, 20 June 1999

Benjamin Netanyahu has turned on fellow Israelis with the fury of a rejected leader, accusing them of "lowering the flag of Zionism" in the face of the enemy.

The former prime minister's bitterness at his crushing electoral defeat last month was unconcealed as he blamed the nation for abandoning its ideals. Mr Netanyahu also criticised cabinet colleagues who abandoned him before the vote and complained angrily about the meagre pension he will receive for his service to the state.

For good measure, he warned Israelis that the peace talks with the Palestinians, stymied for much of his term in office, was a recipe for disaster. The gist of the message from "Bibi" speaking freely for the first time since he was dumped, to the newspaper Ma'ariv, was that Israel did not deserve him.

The Los Angeles Times, Saturday, June 19, 1999

Eager to avoid upsetting Middle East peace hopes, President Clinton on Friday ordered a delay of at least six months in moving the U.S. Embassy in Israel from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem.

Clinton's decision runs contrary to the wishes of many members of Congress, who favor relocating the embassy as a powerful sign of support for Israel's claim to sovereignty over Jerusalem.

But the relocation would no doubt infuriate the nations that have resisted Israeli pressure to move their embassies to Jerusalem. The only countries that maintain embassies there are Costa Rica and El Salvador.

The New York Times, 18 June 1999

The arrest of 13 Iranian Jews on charges of spying for the United States and Israel has emerged as a new focal point in the struggle between moderates and conservatives in Tehran.

Diplomats said by telephone from the Iranian capital Thursday that they suspected that the case was being used to try to block better relations between Iran and the United States.

The Iranian Jews, who are from the southern cities of Shiraz and Isfahan, may be executed if convicted, Iranian officials say, but the leader of Iran's Jews is demanding that they receive a fair trial.

The Chicago Tribune, 16 June 1999

Israeli Prime Minister-elect Ehud Barak's coalition-building efforts got a lift Tuesday with the resignation of corruption-tainted Shas Party leader Aryeh Deri.

Deri, convicted in March of taking bribes, announced he was quitting at a meeting of leading Shas activists in Jerusalem, saying the decision would remain in effect pending a ruling on his Supreme Court appeal.

"In order not to be an obstacle, even a symbolic one, to national reconciliation I decided . . . that until the decision on my appeal I will serve neither in title nor de facto as Shas Party leader," his resignation letter said.

Ha'aretz, 16 June 1999

Both Israel and the Palestinian Authority continue to routinely violate human rights, according to Amnesty International's annual report, due to be published today.

Israel arrested 1,200 Palestinians for security offenses last year and issued 270 administrative detention orders, the report said. Of the administrative detainees, 83 were still in jail at the end of the year.

The report also said that more than 1,500 Palestinians in Israeli prisons were sentenced in trials that did not meet international standards of fairness. Among other problems, confessions obtained under torture were accepted as evidence. Israel's security services use torture systematically in interrogations and the Supreme Court has so far failed to intervene, Amnesty said.
