Muslim and Christian leaders celebrate advent of

Ramadan, stress unity against Zionists

 

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (30 December)

Asserting inter-faith unity in the face of Zionist occupation and oppression, Jerusalemite Muslims and Christians last night celebrated the advent of the holy month of Ramadan at Orient House, the unofficial PLO headquarters in East Jerusalem.

At a reception hosted by Faisal Husseini, Christian and Muslim leaders reaffirmed the traditionally good relations between Palestinian Muslims and Christians.

Christians make up around 6% of the Palestinian population in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip.

Rev. Atallah Hana of the Roman Orthodox Patriarchate in Jerusalem congratulated Muslims for the advent of the "holy month of Ramadan."

Attalah said "we are one people facing a common destiny...we are proud that our harmonious coexistence is based on the Uhda al Umariya" the covenant given by the second caliph Omar to Christians on the conquest of the city by Muslim armies in 636 A.D which guaranteed Christian freedom of worship and the sanctity of churches and places of worship.

"For those who would like to fish in troubled waters, I say 'your sly and vindictive designs will not succeed because we are one family," said Attalah alluding to repeated Israeli attempts to implant the seeds of division between Muslims and Christians in Jerusalem and Bethlehem.

The reception, opened with recitation from the Holy Quran, was attended by a number of foreign ambassadors and other dignitaries, including Michael Sabbah, the highest-ranking Roman Catholic clergyman in the Middle East.

Sabbah, a Palestinian from Nazareth, said "we stand here Muslims and Christians to celebrate the advent of the month of Ramadan as we celebrated together a few days ago the birth of Christ...We extend our greeting to our Muslim brothers on the solemn occasion, hoping that God accept their fasting."

He added that "insinuations and innuendoes have been voiced of late concerning the status of Christian-Muslim relations. I'm here to declare that our relations have never been better. We are one big family."

Sheikh Hasan Tahbub, who assumes the religious affairs portfolio in PA administration, said in a brief statement "I warn that al Masjidul Aksa (Aksa Mosque) is a red line and that we will not sit idle if it came under aggression."

Earlier this week, the Israeli authorities uncovered a plot by two ultra religious Jews to throw a pig's head onto the Haram al Sharif compound in the hope that this would spark off bloodshed and violence in the city.

Many ultra-religious Jews and most settlers in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip believe that redemption for the Jewish people can only be realized through "total war against the goyem" or gentiles in which hundreds of thousands, perhaps millions, of people would be killed.

Supreme Islamic Council denounces Jewish racism

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (29 December)

On the eve of the holy month of Ramadan, Israeli troops were put on high alert along the "friction line" between East and West Jerusalem.

The suspects, who confessed to having sought to carry out the blasphemous act, belonged to Gush Emunim, a messianic Jewish movement advocating "total war" with Arabs and Muslims in particular and Gentiles in general.

Sheikh Mohammed Hussein, a council senior member, warned "provocations against us won't hurt Muslims only, Jews would be hurt as well."

Palestinian Leaders: "Muslims! wake up, al Masjidul Aksa is in danger"

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (27 December)

Palestinian leaders representing various political and ideological orientations have again warned that the al Aksa Mosque is facing the danger of destruction at the hands of Jewish extremists in connivance with the Israeli governments.

Netanyahu: I want to see Palestinians at each other's necks

 

Occupied Jerusalem -PT(25 September)

Israeli Prime Minister Benaymin Netanyahu has expressed renewed discontent with the "memorandum of understanding," which contained terms of "security cooperation and coordination" between Israel and Yaser Arafat's Palestinian Authority.

In statements published in leading Israeli dailies on Thursday, Netanyahu said the document was incomplete and needed to be revised.

"We are talking only about a draft copy at the moment. The document has yet to be approved. Our instructions to our representatives were that they should go and listen to the other side and tell them of our demands that they fulfill their commitments. The security document at the moment is still only one of principles and is not a detailed plan," Netanyahu said.

The Israeli premier didn't spell out what he would like to see added to the document.

However, Israeli sources indicated that Netanyahu would like to put in the document clauses obliging the PA to, inter alia, hand over Islamist detainees in PA-run jails to Israel, escalate the policy of administrative detention (incarceration without trial for prolonged periods) ravage the infrastructure of the Islamic movements, exercise tighter scrutiny over the content of Friday sermons, exclude anti-Israeli references from Palestinian media, and close down all Palestinian institutions that are run by individuals or groups deemed anti-Israeli.

Netanyahu said the existing document had many shortcomings and inadequacies, adding that drafts were defective which necessitated that they be resubmitted to the Palestinian Authority.

Netanyahu's statements contradict assertions by key PA officials that the document was signed under the supervision of CIA representatives.

On Wednesday 24 December, Muhammed Dahlan, head of the Preventive Security Force (PSF) in Gaza vowed that the PA "will not agree to change even a single word of the document."

He said the document was acceptable to the PA because it contained the element of reciprocity, a claim the Israelis have strongly denied.

The PA has complained to the United States about the release from an Israeli lockup on Monday 22 December of an Israeli army officer who last year killed an Arab child, Helmi al Shawashe, of the village of Husan near Bethlehem, by smashing his head with his personal fire-arm.

Sa'eb Urekat, the chief PA negotiator, protested during a radio interview with the PA radio station "the Voice of Palestine," that "how could they dare demand that we round up Hamas activists when they quietly release the murderers of our children from jail only a few months or weeks from committing their crimes."

Urekat's lamentation, however, is not likely to be translated into a strong Palestinian stance in the face of Israeli blackmail.

Observers notice that the sole decision-maker in the PA is Yaser Arafat, a man often reputed of caving in to Israeli and American demands, especially those pertaining to repressing the opponents of the Israeli occupation, particularly the Islamists, within the autonomous enclaves.

CIA 'to tell Arafat whom to hold in jail'

The United States will be the ultimate arbiter in decisions over which Islamist militants should be detained or freed as part of moves to put the Middle East peace process back on track according to Israeli news reports yesterday (Friday).

The agreement, worked out by Palestinian, Israeli and US security officials, is aimed at assuaging fears that Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority rounds up extremists after major atrocities and then quietly releases them.

The Israeli prime minister, Binyamin Netanyahu, said that any withdrawal from the West Bank would take place only after a five-month "testing period". His government would proceed only if the Palestinians proved that they were seriously cracking down on militants.

The Yediot Aharonot daily newspaper claimed that an agreement among the parties stipulates that the Palestinians will give the US Central Intelligence Agency's representative in Israel advance notice of the names of detainees they plan to release.

The CIA will then inform Israel of impending releases and Israel will be given an opportunity to voice objections. In the end, the Palestinian Authority can release detainees only with US approval, Yediot said.

A senior Palestinian security official refused to comment on the report. But Ahmed Abdel Rahman, the secretary-general of the Palestinian cabinet, said: "No one has veto power over the Palestinians, not the United States and not Israel."

Mr Netanyahu said that he could not guarantee that his cabinet's deliberations on the scale of any pullback from the West Bank would be completed by the time he expects to meet President Clinton in late January or early February.
