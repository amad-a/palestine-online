Why should we be denied the right to live or die in Jerusalem?

By: Mahmoud al Khatib

Hind's father did not think for a minute that the decision made by the Israeli Ministry of Internal Security to confiscate his Jerusalem residency permit last month would prevent him from burying his daughter in his family's cemetery in the Holy City.

Mustafa Diyab had been shocked twice in two days. First, the Ministry did not allow his wife, Maryam, who was also his cousin but who holds a Jordanian passport, to live with him in Jerusalem. The next day, he went to the same Ministry to get a death certificate for his 12-year-old daughter, Hind. The Israeli officials told him to get the certificate from the Palestinian Authority and denied him the right to bury her in his family's cemetery.

The Palestinian family was forced to bury their daughter in the village of Kufr Aqeb to the north of occupied Jerusalem. The village, in which the Diyab family were living, was outside Jerusalem borders, according to the Israeli Jerusalem Municipality.

The Israeli arbitrary measures, which have denied more than 50,000 Jerusalem Palestinians the right to maintain their residency permits in the Holy City, have become a serious threat to thousands of Jerusalem families who were forced for one reason or another to live outside the official borders of "Greater Jerusalem" set by the Israeli municipal authorities.

In May last year, a 3-month-old Palestinian baby died after being denied the right of obtaining medical treatment at the Israeli Hadassa Hospital in occupied Jerusalem. Baby Hanin needed an urgent heart operation and her doctors recommended Hadassa Hospital for the surgery. But the Israeli national insurance establishment decided that Hanin had no right to medical insurance although her mother was covered.

They alleged that the mother, a Jerusalem resident, lost the right to medical insurance because she had lost the right to reside in Jerusalem after marrying a Palestinian holding a Jordanian passport. Medical insurance is given only to Israelis and Jerusalem Palestinians who carry Israeli residency permits, they said. However, the Israeli law gives Hanin this right since her mother had an Israeli residency permit.

The Israeli officials alleged that the mother failed to prove that she was a legal Jerusalem resident, in spite of the fact that Hanin's mother was living in the heart of the old city of Jerusalem.

An official at the Jerusalem-based Centre for Palestinian Civil and Social Rights warned of the Israeli arbitrary measures against the civil rights of Jerusalem Palestinians. Azmi Abul Saud said that hundreds of Palestinian children similar to Hanin could be denied the right to life and to obtain medical treatment as well as their right to be buried in their city. "This is happening, although the Israeli national insurance is committed to cover all residents with health insurance," he added.

An Israeli regulation was issued at the beginning of 1995 stating that in order for Palestinians to obtain health insurance, they should be living within the Jerusalem borders set by the Israeli Municipality of the Holy City. These borders were approved by the Israeli government earlier this year.

Abul Saud revealed that the new Israeli measures led the Israeli Hadassa Hospital to expel Palestinian patients who suffer from chronic and serious diseases. The patients allegedly lost their right of residency in Jerusalem because they changed their place of residence within Jerusalem.

Citing examples of the tragedies of the Jerusalemite patients, Abul Saud said that the Israeli hospital expelled Mustafa Karaki, who was being treated for lung cancer, just because his house was outside the municipal borders of Jerusalem. Sultan Shabana, who was undergoing kidney dialysis, was also told not to come back for the same reason, the Palestinian official said.

The Palestinian Centre is planning to commemorate Hanin who was denied the right to life by preventing her from getting medical treatment. The Centre will organize a humanitarian demonstration to expose Israeli ethnic cleansing against Palestinian residents of Jerusalem. Representatives of international medical and humanitarian organizations as well as the Red Cross will participate in the demonstration to protest against Israeli arbitrary measures which affect more than 70,000 Jerusalemite residents who face expulsion from the Holy City.

The Israeli authorities offer to grant Israeli citizenship to the Jerusalemite residents as the only solution to sustain the right of residency in Jerusalem.

The Abu-Marzook Affair and What it Means for American Justice

By: Stanley L. Cohen

PT: By the time this month's paper has been issued, Dr. Mousa Abu Marzook should be released and deported to Jordan, an Arab country willing to receive the Hamas leader by permission of its Monarch, King Hussein. Even though the article, which was written by one of Abu Marzook's qualified and well-known defence lawyers, sheds disturbing light on the American so-called justice system, we still opted to print it for the facts and realities contained therein.

After more than twenty-two months of imprisonment in the United States for crimes alleged by the State of Israel, Dr. Mousa Mohammed Abu Marzook now stands vindicated of all charges, following Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's announcement to abandon, "for security purposes," their attempts to extradite him. Indeed, it has been Dr. Abu Marzook's steadfast position since his arrest at New York's Kennedy Airport in July, 1995, that Israel never wanted him to stand trial there, that they never had a plausible case against him. We now know, after almost two years of Justice Department duplicity and a grotesque legal pantomime in the federal courts of this country, that the United States itself at best has colluded with Israel at every level to deprive Dr. Abu Marzook of his freedom, strip him of his dignity and reputation, and abuse his rightful position as a leader of the Palestinian people. Worse yet, it has permitted pressure from Israeli and American Jewish sources to manipulate and destroy the process due him, a lawful U.S. resident.

For more than fifteen years, Hamas has struggled on behalf of its people and has earned their respect, from its educational and social programs to its thorough revival of Islamic identity among the residents of Gaza and the West Bank. Yet their militant and unflagging commitment to freeing their land of Israeli oppression has made them a force with which Israel would rather not carry on a dialogue.

From the beginning, Dr. Abu Marzook has met Israel's criminal charges openly and sincerely, vigorously denying any and all guilt while opposing with all the resources he could muster the United States Justice Department's attempts to extradite him. Finally tiring of endless bad faith on the part of the U.S. and Israeli prosecutions, as well as the clear, pro-Israeli bias on the part of the Federal judge who initially decided his fate, he has withdrawn his legal opposition to extradition, in effect saying, "You want me to stand trial--I will go willingly and fight these charges."

Having called Israel's bluff, Dr. Abu Marzook has forced them at last to reveal their craven agenda for the ruse it has always has been.

Who could believe at the time of Dr. Abu Marzook's arrest that Israel truly intended to carry out their extradition of him? Surely they knew then, as they are forced to admit now, that his presence in an Israeli courtroom, facing baseless charges which the Israeli government knew it could never substantiate, would hardly further the "peace process." Surely they knew then, as they solemnly confess now, that such a trumped-up action against a legitimate spokesman for Palestinian national aspirations, and a man welcomed as a leader by Muslim nations throughout the region, could only enflame the hatred of Hamas' considerable base of support in Gaza and the West Bank, erupting with terrible consequences for all involved--Israeli and Palestinian alike. So it remains now to ask, if these realities have been obvious to any casual observer, much less to professional political strategists, then how is it Dr. Abu Marzook has been incarcerated here by the United States government, separated from his wife and children, deprived of his freedom and dignity for these long months--all for the sake of a foreign power whose insincerity in this matter has never been in question?

The answer, unfortunately, is unpleasant for any American who has held this nation's constitutional stability and standards of fairness in high esteem. The vision of sanctity and inviolability that, in the best of all worlds, guides our independent judiciary has long been--at least in theory--one of this country's greatest constitutional bulwarks against tyrrany. Yet in the Abu Marzook affair, a clear example of the corruption of our federal judiciary by the political will of the executive branch stands now, unrepudiated, as an indictment of the system. Simply put, the action against Dr. Abu Marzook was conceived when the Israeli Cabinet hardly a branch of our judiciary, or even Israel's--resolved that he should be arrested and detained. The U.S. government did not detain Dr. Abu Marzook because an American judge had found probable cause that he had committed a crime--rather, it detained him because non-judicial officials at the State Department asked that he be detained, and the government bent the rules to detain him as if this constituted a legal basis.

To review the legal charade fostered by the federal courts, we need only look at their halting record of proceedings against Dr. Abu Marzook. Initially, the United States detained Dr. Abu Marzook at Kennedy Airport without a warrant, and indeed without any charges whatsoever. Then, the government purported to hold him-still without specific charges-for exclusion, although as a permanent resident alien summary exclusion procedures were legally inapplicable. Their reason? Because the government wanted to minimize, if possible, any due process of law for Dr. Abu Marzook, and it had no charges that it could legitimately assert. Indeed, to this day the only charges asserted by the government to justify any exclusion proceeding are predicated on Israel's allegations, which they now drop. And these very allegations were based entirely upon a "confession" obtained under torture, which was later recanted by the tortured man. Since when has American justice allowed the product of torture to stand as evidence in its federal courts?

Then, the government brought an extradition proceeding to extradite Dr. Abu Marzook to Israel, except that Israel had not actually assembled evidence that he was guilty of extraditable crimes when the government brought the proceeding, forcing the United States government to ask for more time during which Dr. Abu Marzook was detained, still without a proper predicate for holding him or the slightest demonstration that he is a danger to public safety. Next, the government--less than two weeks after it had hastily brought its improper exclusion proceedings --asked the Court for permission to abate these proceedings while it brought the extradition proceeding, based on a statute that had been declared unconstitutional on the very grounds that it ceded judicial independence to the State Department. Now, with Dr. Abu Marzook's extradition unopposed yet ironically undesired by Israel itself, the exclusion proceeding will begin again, and delaying it would mean that the government could seek to hold Dr. Abu Marzook without legal basis for years--the very situation Dr. Abu Marzook's defense team predicted would occur when its request for simultaneous exclusion and extradition proceedings was denied at the beginning of this case.

Israel's "special relationship" with the United States, forged over years of shared military and strategic interests in the Mid-East, is hardly a state secret. American citizens are free to support, oppose or ignore this fact of United States foreign policy as they wish--it is fundamentally a reality created by successive administrations of the Executive Branch of government, fostered by the Legislative Branch through approval of funds, laws and oversight, and implemented by the State Department, which is the President's prerogative. Yet the Judicial Branch of our government is meant to be free of any of these considerations when weighing the freedom and life of a man in its hands.

conclude, after a careful examination of the Abu Marzook affair--which remains unresolved as of this writing, as Dr. Abu Marzook sits in the Metropolitan Correctional Center still--that Israel's legendary power lobby in Washington is much greater than any have dared say before. While its influence over State Department Mid-East policy has been born of the symbiosis of U.S. and Israeli policy objectives in the region, such relationships have long been the prerogative of the executive branch, for better or worse. But now the chilly hand of international and domestic political lobbying has reached out to shape our nominally impartial federal judiciary-using tortured confessions, subterfuge, and racist, anti-Islamic hysteria to further Israel's agenda in the Occupied Territories. That our federal courts and judges have been party to such craven cynicism is only a part of the injustice here.

For beyond these considerations, there is Dr. Abu Marzook himself, who has been imprisoned and humiliated by the U.S. Justice Department for these past twenty-two months. His wife and children, residents of Virginia, have been forced to travel long hours for their weekly meetings with Mousa, putting great hardship on the family. The children must bear the strain of seeing their father in a New York City jail cell, far from home, while Dr. Abu Marzook's wife has had to carry the household responsibilities alone. His aged parents in the Occupied Territories have no contact with their son; indeed, as Dr. Abu Marzook's father declines in health, there is the very real possibility that he may never see his father again. In addition, Dr. Abu Marzook's religious life has been disrupted: isolated from the Islamic community, unable to participate in weekly Friday jum'aa prayers, and deprived of contact with his imam or a personal religious advisor, he has had to practice his faith as best he can, in the noisy, sordid environment of a maximum security prison wing.

Yet, for Dr. Abu Marzook's part, he accepts what has befallen him with the stoic reserve of a man who has dedicated his life to the national struggle of his people. Tens of thousands of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza have suffered jailing, torture and death at the hands of Israel's security forces in the Occupied Territories --any historic human struggle for independence is not without its continuing human costs. Indeed, Mousa's own family's expulsion from the Gaza farm of his boyhood in the great displacement of Palestinians that followed Israeli statehood in 1948 has framed his life in terms of nothing but this struggle to restore his people to their rightful heritage. In the context of the life he has given to this fight, jail and persecution are mere setbacks along the road to freedom.

March Uprisingóa Preparation for a Jerusalem Battle

By Maha Abdel Hadi

Nablus- Before the Israeli Minister of Internal Security Avigdor Kahalani entered the office of his boss on 26 February to make the final decision on building a Jewish settlement on Jabal Abu Ghneim in occupied East Jerusalem, he rushed to tell reporters that "the struggle for Jerusalem has started." He added haughtily that by making that decision, he would establish the fact that Jerusalem was the capital of the Jewish people.

Kahalani might be right by saying that the battle for Jerusalem had started, but he was wrong when he and his government thought that the issue would end with the decision to build the settlement. The battle for Jerusalem had been opened on all fronts and no Israeli government, Likud or Labour, could retreat.

When the Israeli bulldozers started working on Jabal Abu Ghneim settlement (Har Homa), a new Palestinian intifada (uprising) erupted. This renewed last September's al Aqsa intifada following the Israeli decision to open a tunnel under al Aqsa Mosque. The new intifada is no doubt endangering the rightist government of Benjamin Netanyahu.

All now know that the coming explosion will be all-consuming. Its starting point is well-defined but no one will be able to predict how and when it will end. The Tel Aviv government has prepared its army for a comprehensive confrontation with the Palestinian people and has sent additional troops to the borders of the Palestinian territories to suppress any protests against the construction of the new settlement in occupied East Jerusalem.

On the other hand, the Palestinian Authority is practically avoiding intervening in the intifada in an attempt to make the revolution appear as a "naturalî popular reaction to Israeli aggression. The PA security officials issue statements every day stressing that they are well aware of their 'security duties' within the framework of the Oslo Accords signed with the Israelis. However, the Israeli government is still saying it is not satisfied with the PA's commitments to these Accords, especially those points pertaining to security.

Palestinians in the occupied territories are frustrated and disappointed. They have lost any hope of restoring their legitimate rights. They thought that the Oslo Accords would relieve them from Israeli occupation. On the contrary, they discovered that their territories are still being occupied and could be confiscated at any time by occupation troops. This was what first sparked off the Palestinian uprising. Even PA officials and members of the Palestinian Legislative Council are humiliated by Israeli soldiers at the numerous checkpoints.

The Israeli decision regarding the opening of a tunnel in the holy city of Jerusalem last September was the second spark which set off the Palestinian people in rebellion against the vices and atrocities of the Israelis. Palestinian mass demonstrations and confrontations with the Israeli soldiers erupted, killing about 70 Palestinians and injuring more than 1,500 others in less than five days. Nineteen Israeli soldiers were killed and dozens injured in the confrontations, making last September's intifada the bloodiest.

Abu Ghneim settlement was the third spark which angered the Palestinians who are well aware of the Israeli plans to Judaize occupied East Jerusalem. Eight Palestinians have so far been killed and more than 1,000 injured in the present intifada which erupted in Hebron, Ramallah, Bethlehem and a number of neighbouring villages.

The most violent confrontations between Palestinians and occupation soldiers and/or Jewish settlers were reported in Hebron. The Hebronites penetrated the buffer zones between the so-called H1 and H2 areas and stoned Israeli soldiers and settlers. The Israelis used live ammunition and rubber bullets, which are fatal if shot from a short distance, as well as dangerous kinds of tear gas in order to disperse the demonstrators.

It seems that Netanyahu has not learned from the mistakes of his predecessor Peres. In a show of power, Peres ordered the assassination of Engineer Yahya Ayyash. The Palestinian reaction was violent resulting in several suicide bombings which dashed Peres' hopes to win the elections.

Netanyahu blindly follows Peres' policies but in a somewhat different fashion. Peres had succeeded in obtaining help from the Palestinian Authority to spy on the military activities of Hamas and other opposition factions. But Netanyahu is not content to suppress only Hamas; he wants the PA to suppress any Palestinian opposing his stupid policies. In other words, he wants to suppress all the Palestinian people. In order to force the PA to take stricter measures against opposition factions, the Israeli government has suspended talks with the PLO on the final status of Jerusalem and Jewish settlements but will continue talks on security issues only.

Israel accused Arafat of giving a 'green light' to Hamas to resume its suicide bombings during his meeting with Hamas leaders in Gaza after his visit to Washington. But the Israelis should know better. Arafat had tried to stop the bombings but failed, despite his efforts and the efforts of his security officers.

The Likud and Labour parties agree on the settlement plans adopted by the Israeli government.The Labour member of the Knesset, Yori Ore, has said: ëWhen the bulldozers come up to Har Homa (Abu Ghneim), there are no opposition groups in Israel. It is Jews against Palestinians. I hope the Palestinians will understand that and calm down the situation. Today we have one objective; to proceed in the construction of Har Homa (!)"

The Israeli parties may not be aware of the indications and consequences of the new intifada, which includes the Abu Ghneim intifada and the military escalations such as the Tel Aviv and the Baqoura (in Jordan) operations. Israelis will have made a mistake if they believe that they can contain the impact of their decisions concerning Jerusalem. There will be no security or stability unless they withdraw from Arab East Jerusalem.

More Human Rights Abuses in Palestine

By: Robert W. McGee*

Human rights abuses continued as usual in Palestine last month. Palestinian property continued to be confiscated or blown up. Torture of prisoners continued. And the United States unconditional support for Israel was unwavering.

Most of these human rights abuses either go unreported in the American press, or if they are reported, are done so in some sanitized fashion so as to not inflame the hearts, minds and consciences of the many Americans who would be truly outraged if they knew what their tax dollars were going to support.

Recent Israeli army activity has been especially outrageous. The Israeli army has been collectively punishing and brutalizing the village of Surif by various acts of violence. It has imposed and lifted several curfews, which caused severe shortages of basic foodstuffs. Israeli soldiers routinely take shots at Surif residents who venture out of their homes to get food. More than 200 have been injured. At least one person, Khalid Mustafa Arar, died of a heart attack while relatives tried to get him to the hospital during the curfew. His family was sprayed with rubber bullets and tear gas when they tried to bury him. Had Palestinians attacked Israeli mourners, it would have been the first story on the evening news in America.

Palestinian administrative detainees who have been imprisoned without being charged or tried with any crime continue to be abused and unjustifiably imprisoned. The commander of the Megiddo jail in Israel has been told by top military commanders to open fire on prisoners if they protest against their continued illegal detention. Their leaders are to be fired upon with live bullets, rubber bullets or tear gas and are to have their prison sentences extended. Detainees who move or fail to sit properly during the prison count are also to be fired upon. Even non-violent protests such as silent vigils and sit-ins are to be classified as insurrections.

Almost half of the 300 Palestinians who were illegally arrested (in their own country by occupying forces) have been held for more than a year. One-fourth of them have been held for two or more years. These policies violate both international law and common human decency. Their continued detention also violates the Oslo II accords, which the leaders of the occupying Israeli government signed in 1995.

Israelis destroyed the home of the Rashid family on the outskirts of Hebron, thus causing 45 people to become homeless. Family members were not allowed to remove furniture, clothing or other articles. To add to the cruelty, Israeli bulldozers pushed the debris from the demolition into a nearby cave where the family kept livestock to prevent the family from using the cave for shelter. The home was destroyed because it was allegedly built without a permit. But why should anyone need a permit to build on their own property?

Human rights abuses continue unabated, year after year, as the Zionist occupiers pursue genocidal policies not unlike those of the Nazis during the early years of the Nazi regime. Yet the Clinton administration, like administrations before his, continues to veto United Nations resolutions to condemn illegal confiscation of property by the Israelis and their infamous brutalities committed against the Palestinians.

* Prof. Robert W. McGee is the President of The Dumont Institute for Public Policy Research, Dumont, NJ-USA- http://www.dumontinst.com

Palestine Times Interviews Dr. Hassan Turabi

Democracy came through revolution and so did Islam

There are no extremists among Muslims but Westerners think themselves the world's centre

By: Ghassan Abu Hussein

Dr. Hassan al Turabi, now the speaker of the Sudanese parliament, is the most prominent Islamic thinker and strategist. He has written several books on Islam in both Arabic and English. Dr. al Turabi is fluent in both English and French. However, this interview was conducted in Arabic and then translated into English.

Q. About 25 years ago, you called for the renewal of Islamic thought. After eight years in government, what is the contribution the Sudanese Islamic movement has made in this respect?

A. Religion is being refreshed among the people. The Islamic movement is no longer dominated by a small number of Islamic scholars. Ijtihad (independent judgment of theological questions) is now being made by the whole society in Sudan; it is no longer exclusive to the scholars. All people contribute according to their acquisition and understanding of the situation. The scholars know, of course, more than the common people, but they interact with them. Our whole life is based on the religion, and the transactions have become a part of our prayers. Religion has become the common culture for the society.

Q. What are the main future challenges facing the Islamic movements?

A. Some of the Islamic movements became bound by their attainments, even if they were few. Some of the movements are prisoners of the past. A Muslim looks forward to the everlasting abode and plans his life for that eternal life. He may exert all his efforts in this life without waiting for the fruits of his achievements, since his reward will be in that eternal life similar to the martyr. The members of the Islamic movements, in general, do not look to the future nor do they plan for it.

The Islamic movement in Sudan began planning for higher goals in the early 1980s, goals that seemed to be difficult to reach yet they were realized in few years. We plan for this century and for the coming century. Our country is rich with oil and natural resources as well as human resources. The world is now gathering in regional blocs. We do not want to be besieged or to be accused of spreading terrorism or fundamentalism. They (the enemies of Sudan) plan, but we also plan.

The awakening of Islam has affected the life of the whole Nation, which enables us to mobilize the Islamic potential in order to achieve planned objectives. This is the main challenge. The leaders of the Islamic movements are the most active and educated among Muslims. They are also the best to know the real Islam. But what they introduce to the Islamic Nation is not sufficient. The world, during the cold war, was divided into two camps: capitalism and socialism. But now it has become without ideology after the collapse of socialism. Parties were without strategies or programmes; and religion in the West became non-existent. Our underdeveloped world which follows the West and is fascinated by its glamour has become lost. Once the people are lost, any leader will be able to guide them. This is a fact of human history. We know the challenges before us; Islam itself has been targeted since the end of the cold war and the collapse of the Soviet Union. But we should not worry. We want to tell the Muslims in the world that they have a very wide range of opportunities: "Wind blows against them from this side, but water pours on them from the other side."

Q. A number of Islamic movements came into power through democracy like Turkey and others through military coups like in Sudan. Can the Islamic movements use force to rule if they fail to do so through the polls?

A. The intellectual, dialectic, and Jihad (military) potentials should be combined in order to make cultural transformations. This is a reality because the history of the world is like that. Even democracy came about through revolutions. The economic changes came through economic revolutions, and so too the revolutions of religion.

The Qurían is clear. Allah orders us to invite people to Islam with wisdom and beautiful preaching; and to argue with them in ways that are best and most gracious. But we should be ready and cautious in order not to be deceived. We should be ready for Jihad. Each country has its own estimation and status. Force is only used against aggression. The Sudanese opposition factions which claim to teach democracy to people are the ones who are now killing democracy in Sudan.

Even in Turkey, I do not know what is going to happen in the future. There are forces there which intervene in the most trifle of issues like the people's style of dress. In Afghanistan, they did very well in Jihad and had more than 1 million martyrs, but they were not ready to govern. They proved Falsehood false and crushed it, but they were not ready to justify the Truth. Nature does not know a vacuum. If a falsehood is removed and you do not come or do not know how to establish truth or maintain it, another falsehood will come as you see now. Our brothers in Algeria (the Islamic Salvation Front) were involved in the democratic process without having a methodology or an approach. I think if the Westerners had left them reach to power, they would have proven to be a failure because they did not know how to run an Islamic economy. They only say "Islam is the solution", but they do not know how to apply it.

Q. What is the difference between piousness and extremism?

A. We call it 'pigheadedness' but not extremism. Extremism is a Western term; they use it because they think themselves the centre of the world and our world is the Middle or East. In fact, we are the centre of the world; we are the centre geographically and religiously. They think that anyone who goes in a different direction from them is an extremist. The more you abide by your religion, the more extremist you become in their eyes. But the pigheadedness is another issue. We should not be unduly strict.

If the Westerners liked you and you were resisting an enemy, you would be labeled 'resistant', and if they liked you more, you would be labeled a 'revolutionist'. But if they disliked or hated you, you would be considered a 'terrorist'. We should not give in to their terminology or criteria. We have our own criteria and terminology which we should introduce to others through the best of means. We should be fair, pious and disciplined while we continue to perform our religious duties. We should be justly balancedópious but not rash or pigheaded.

When we fight our enemies, we do not kill except those who fight back at us. We do not kill their civilians or children even if we are provoked by their atrocities. We do not fight our enemies by their law of war but by our own law. The Qurían is very clear in these matters. We should be well-balanced.

Q. Some people call for a dialogue with the West and others support clashing with them...

A. We should take the initiative and open a dialogue with them. We should invite them, warn them, and give them glad tidings, especially because we know their religion but they do not know ours; we know their language and they do not know ours.

Regarding clashing, the initiative should be from the other side. We are not allowed to be aggressive. The best conquer is not the conquering of cities or countries, but the conquering of the hearts of people by the light of Islam. Jihad is one of the elements of dialogue. We should be ready for any aggression from the other party. Perhaps if the other party knew that we are not able to fight him, he would not accept to start the dialogue and would even crack down on us.

49th anniversary of the Deir Yassin massacre

'Time does not heal all wounds,

There are those that remain painfully open'

By : Mare H.Ellis

The writer is an American theologian, critical of Israeli policies against the Palestinians, who has written several books on the subject. The article below is an address he delivered at Birzeit University last month commemorating the 49th anniversary of the Deir Yassin massacre.

Forty-nine years after the massacre of Deir Yassin, Palestinians are still not free. Palestinian land is still occupied and the Palestinian diaspora grows in numbers and longevity. Many Palestinians experience the "autonomy" of Oslo as a pretence for a further diminution of Palestinian life and possibility.

It is not the first time that peace in our century has been implemented with such a heavy hand and with the pretence of innocence. Nor is it the first time that a suffering people has empowered itself at the expense of others and celebrated that empowerment as a divine promise. And one can certainly argue from the extensive catalogue of suffering in the 20th century that there are other peoples who have suffered more extensively and intensively than the Palestinians have.

One thinks here of the millions killed in the Soviet Union, China, Cambodia and, more recently of the hundreds of thousands slaughtered in Rwanda. And of course the millions of Jews slaughtered in the heart of Europe. In fact, it is the mass slaughter of Jews that fueled the creation of the state of Israel and the ideology that justifies its actions, including the displacement of the Palestinian People.

Perhaps it is paradoxical that the slaughter of Jews in Europe led to the slaughter of Palestinians by Jews at Deir Yassin. Or perhaps it is something more logical than paradoxical, in that the creation of a Jewish state, like the creation of any state, depends on a certain amount of violence. To create a Jewish state in an Arab country and region was impossible without violence, and the massacre at Deir Yassin stands out only in the extent of violence and the fact that the violence escalated with the surrender of the villagers. This violence as recorded by eyewitnesses, was savage beyond the needs of warfare, and this with the understanding that war is horrible in and of itself.

From the perspective of Palestinian history, the savagery at Deir Yassin was felt throughout Palestine and is only an extreme example of what Palestinians experienced at the hands of Jewish soldiers. One thinks here of the forced marches of thousands of civilians from Lydda and Ramle, ordered as we know by Yitzhak Rabin with the permission of David Ben-Gurion.

In some sense, almost the entire experience of Palestinians at the hands of Jews and the Jewish state can be found in these two events: the military and its excesses, conquering of land and villages, displacement of civilians on a mass scale, the disruption and, in many case, the ending of a rooted ad cultured life in the land of Palestine. In short, and perhaps this is as significant as the larger questions pondered by academics and politicians, Jews have disrupted and ended the ordinary lives of the Palestinian people.

This is the horror of Deir Yassin reflected in the brutality of the avenging Jewish soldiers and magnified for generations after. Battles, even massacres, occur throughout history and though they can never be condoned, let alone celebrated as Menachem Begin did with Deir Yassin, the possibility of burial, mourning and, ultimately healing can Only be accomplished over time with the generations that come after. These generations must be able to see the dead within a larger framework and destiny to address the wounds that exist long after the dead are buried.

Memory is contextual and needs a foundation in narrative as well as geography. Can the dead be mourned, can they finally reach peace for themselves and their descendants when the same history keeps unfolding and the narrative and geography are limited by the same people who committed the massacres? This is what Elie Wiesel, a survivor of Auschwitz, alluded to on the 30th anniversary of the liberation of Auschwitz, when he wrote: "Time does not heal all wounds. There are those that remain painfully open." Is there a way to heal the wounds that time does not? Perhaps not for the victims and the survivors. But the question facing us is whether the larger history of a people can be healed after these events of displacement and murder. For these events rend the fabric of a people's history in ways that are as horrific to the actual victims and can outlive those who were present at the event itself. The formative event of destruction becomes a collective wound through which a history of a people is felt and articulated.

The memory of Deir Yassin as part of the fabric of Palestinian history awaits its final mission and destination, for one day it may fuel a resistance to the rulers who subvert that memory of suffering with power and political piety.

At the same time, it may provide a directed and humble path towards a future beyond power and piety where the dead and those who come after return in narrative and geography to nurture the ordinary life of a people.

The question of how Deir Yassin will function in Palestinian memory and life is both independent of and, to some extent, dependent on how this memory functions in Jewish life, for the wound in a people's history is most often two-sided: those who are wounded and those who wound. Though the pain of Deir Yassin is remembered by Palestinians, who among Jews remember this tragedy? If Palestinians remember Deir Yassin in its individuality and communal meaning, who among Jews remembers it in either dimension? At least in mainstream Jewish writing one searches in vain for references to Deir Yassin and if mentioned at all, it is in a context that buries the tragedy in the struggle for the creation of the state of Israel, a goal, at least to these writers, that far outweighs the tragedy which befell a village and a people. Of course, the other events which make up the larger tapestry of the displacement of the Palestinian people are similarly treated, so that the individual horrors never add up to the corporate horror that befell Palestine. By neglecting the suffering of the Palestinians, such histories diminish Jewish culpability in that suffering and in a curious way disjoin what such suffering creates: a shared history. How else could those responsible for dislocation and death- Menachem Begin, Yitzhak Shamir, Yitzhak Rabin and David Ben Gurion- emerge as heroes and ultimately prime ministers?

Whatever the reasons and justifications, however the importance of Palestinian life is minimised, the reality is there for all to see: millions of Palestinians who are refugees in their own land and outside of it and Israelis who continue to occupy, displace, torture and murder long after the creation of the state and even after the Oslo agreements, which are ostensibly an attempt to end this history.

Yet even here the question remains: Do the Oslo agreements attempt to recognise the joint history of Jew and Palestinian and therefore the culpability of Jews in the displacement of the Palestinians, or do they attempt to bury that history? Do the people of memory, the Jews, seek to establish a political separation so that the Palestinian memory of Deir Yassin will forever be separated from the Jewish people and state as a dangerous memory which calls into question the Jewish claims of innocence and redemption?

..(If) history is only seen as a continuum, a "flow of uninterrupted succession,"' then the powerful are victorious and the defeated are consigned to oblivion, until, of course, they rise up and defeat the powerful and assume their place. If history is without interruption, then morality, ethics, religious faith, is simply an exercise without possibility, a piety without substance.

It is the "gap in time" we can make judgments,reconsider our history, and resolve to take another road. Is despair, the desire for the death of "all of us," simply another way of saying that we have let the continuum of history overwhelm our ability to change direction? The gap in time is found in reflection, often in the silence where the individual is separated from the world, and sometimes in the midst of a communal discussion or action, even in an act of commemoration. Yet there is always a choice involved, as Yad VaShem, in eyesight of Deir Yassin, plainly demonstrates. For even memory can be mobilised to continue the cycle of destruction and death as it too often has been in relation to the Holocaust. But then the victims of destruction, even after they have emerged victorious, remain unhealed.

The history of atrocity suggests that at some point the combatants become weary of battle and, as the corpses pile higher and higher, more and more people seek another way of living with one another, a way of justice and compassion.

At different moments the "gap in time" seems more propitious, as if the continuum has been broken and the stand against past and future has made its point. Those moments are sometimes found in celebration and at other times in mourning and commemoration. When those moments are seized and acted upon, it is like a deep cry of history has been heard.

On the 49th anniversary of Deir Yassin, one of those moments is upon us, awaiting our decision.

Al-Dawayima Before 1948

The village was spread along the top of a wide rocky ridge on the western side of the Hebron Mountains. It overlooked Wadi Qubayba to the north and commanded a view of higher mountains to the east. Although the entire area generally sloped down toward the south coastal plain, this was not obvious from the village because hills to the west of it blocked the view of the plain. A secondary road connected al-Dawayima to the village of Idna, to the northeast. Yet another secondary road led from al-Dawayima to al-Qubayba, to the northwest, and this in turn was linked to the al-Faluja-Bayt Jibrin highway.

The site of al-Dawayima has been tentatively identified with the Old Testament village of Bozqat or Bosqat. In the late nineteenth century, al-Dawayima stood on a high stony ridge that had olive groves beneath it. On a higher ridge to the west stood a shrine that was topped by a white dome. [SWP (1881) III:258]

The people of al-Dawayima were Muslims. They maintained several religious shrines, chief among them the shrine of a Shaykh 'Ali. This shrine had a large courtyard, a number of rooms, and one large hall for prayers, and was surrounded by fig and carob trees and cactuses. It attracted visitors from neighboring villages. [Hudayb 1985:541 A mosque was located in the village center; it was maintained by the followers of al-tariqa al-khalwatiyya, a Sufi mystic order founded by Shaykh 'Umar al-Khalwati, who died in 1397. [Glasse 1989:221 l The villagers expanded and renovated the mosque in the 1930s, and added a tall minaret. [Hudayb 1985:54]

The village had a star-shaped layout, which permitted construction to expand in all directions, particularly northeast and northwest along the above-mentioned roads. Shops were scattered throughout the various neighborhoods of the village center. Houses were made of stone and mud, separated by narrow streets and alleys. The older houses were clustered closely together. Each set of houses shared a hawsh, a large courtyard that provided space for women to do their domestic chores, for children to play, and for families to gather in the evening and on special occasions. As the village expanded people began to build new houses outside of the village core. These new houses were larger and built of whitewashed stone; some of them had thick, stone walls and were called jidaris (from the Arabic word for wall, jidar). Typically, each of these houses stood apart from the others and did not form part of a hawsh. However, the floor plan of the new houses was the same as that of the old. Each house had two levels: the upper level was occupied by the family members and the lower level by their animals. The houses of the well-to-do villagers had their own courtyards and large guest rooms, in addition to animal stables. [Hudayb 1985:51-52]

Al-Dawayima had an elementary school that was opened in the village in 1937. [Hudayb 1985:65] The villagers obtained part of their drinking water from wells that collected rainwater. In the 1940s they drilled three new wells. During this period they also built numerous cisterns for collecting rainwater. [Hudayb 1985:57]

Rainfed agriculture was an important basis of al-Dawayima's economy. The chief crops included a variety of grains, especially wheat, barley, and maize; vegetables and fruits, especially figs and grapes, were also cultivated. In 1944/ 45 the villagers allotted a total of 21,191 dunums of their land to cereals; 1,206 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards. The rugged terrain around the village was covered with trees, such as oaks and suwayd (Rhamnus Palestina).

Equally important to the village economy was animal husbandry. There were twenty-seven clans, or hamulas, in the village [Hudayb 1985:32], and every hamula owned an average of 200 to 300 head of goats or sheep, and a few camels and cows. [Hudayb 1985:591 The villagers pastured their animals on the large blocs of uncultivated land nearby.

In addition to agriculture and animal husbandry, the villagers engaged in the spinning and weaving of tents and sacks for storing grain. They also tanned hides and wove baskets for the market. [Hudayb 1985:60] Al-Dawayima had about thirty to fifty small shops, ten butchers, and two grain mills. The economy of the village became more dynamic in 1944 with the introduction of a weekly Friday market. The market brought in residents from other villages and towns, including Bayt Jibrin, Hebron, and Gaza, who traded in produce, clothes, pastry, and other goods. The market was held on the village's threshing floor and was called the suq al-barrayn ("the market of the two terrains") because it was believed to combine the trade of the mountains and the plains. [Hudayb 1985:61]

Al-Dawayima was built on an archaeological site. In addition, a large number of khirbas lay in the vicinity. This suggests intense occupation of the area in the past.

Occupation and Depopulation

Al-Dawayima was the site of one of the larger, little-known massacres of the war. Carried out on 29 October 1948, during the Israeli army's Operation Yo'av (see Barbara, Gaza District), the massacre precipitated a massive flight of people from the area, according to Israeli historian Benny Morris. He quotes a participant in the attack on the village as saying that about 80 to 100 people, including women and children, were killed by "the first wave of conquerors." The following are excerpts of a description published in the Israeli daily 'Al ha-Mishmar. The children they killed by breaking their heads with sticks. There was not a house without dead.... One commander ordered a sapper to put two old women in a certain house ... and to blow up the house with them. The sapper refused.... The commander then ordered his men to put in the old women and the evil deed was done. One soldier boasted that he had raped a woman and then shot her...."

Another description of the massacre was given by the former mukhtar of the village, interviewed in 1984 by the Israeli daily Hadashot. The mukhtar recalled: "The people fled, and everyone they saw in the houses, they shot and killed. They also killed people in the streets. They came and blew up my house, in the presence of eye-witnesses." The mukhtar added: "The moment that the tanks came and opened fire, I left the village immediately. At about half-past ten, two tanks passed the Darawish Mosque. About 75 old people were there, who had come early for Friday prayers. They gathered in the mosque to pray. They were all killed." The mukhtar also said that about thirty-five families were hiding in caves outside al-Dawayima, including some from the previously occupied village of al-. When the Israeli forces discovered them, "They told them to come out and get into line and start to walk. And as they started to walk, they were shot by machine guns from two sides.... We sent people there that night, who collected the bodies, put them into a cistern, and buried them."

The mukhtar was taken back to the site of his village in 1984 for the first time since the massacre. He showed an Israeli journalist where his home had been, as well as the cistern where the bodies had been buried. A few days later, the journalist returned with four laborers. They dug for a short while and discovered several human bones, including three skulls-one of them a child's. They did not continue digging, but reburied the bones.

Morris writes that a number of investigations were launched into the massacre, but does not mention what they found. They were generally ineffectual and ended with the disciplining of some soldiers and the issuing of a set of rules on treatment of Arab civilians by the Israeli army. The unit responsible for the massacre was the Eighty-Ninth Battalion, which was part of the Eighth Brigade, commanded by General Yitzchaq Sadeh, the founder of the Palmach. Operation commander Yigal Allon told Sadeh to look into the "rumours," but his response is not given. [M:222-23; Al-Fajr (Jerusalem), English-language edition, 719184, pp. 8-9]

In December 1948, during a general discussion of atrocities by an Israeli ministerial committee, the issue of al-Dawayima was raised again. Morris writes that Agriculture Minister Aharon Zisling was probably reacting to a letter he received about this massacre, when he said: "This is something that determines the character of the nation.... Jews too have committed Nazi acts." Although he complained that the investigation was not proceeding as it should, he agreed with other ministers that Israel should admit nothing outwardly, in order to preserve its image. [M:233-34]

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

The settlement of Amatzya (142104), founded in 1955, was built on the ruins of al-Dawayima.

The Village Today

The site has been fenced in. A cowshed, a chicken coop, and granaries have been built at its center (which has been levelled). The southern side of the site contains stone terraces and the remnants of a house. The eastern side is occupied by the residential area of the moshav (see photos). Cactuses, together with many carob and olive trees, grow on the site's slopes.

The Dissident Jewish Voice

By : Abraham Weizfeld

Oft-times the Jewish political culture is taken to be just the Zionist political establishment. This is precisely what the Zionist political parties wish to promote in order to justify their hegemony over the Jewish people.

Despite the virtual dictatorship of the Zionist ideology over the Jewish people, a majority of Jewish people still do not live in the State of Israel and do not wish to. The Jewish emigrants from Russia, for example, prefer not to immigrate to Israel. However, the only political voice heard representing the Jewish community internationally is the voice of Zionism in spite of the non-Zionist character of the majority.

The reasons for this anomaly are that the Zionists seem to offer the only solution for the existential crises of the Jewish people, especially after the genocidal massacre of the European Jewish communities by the Nazi chauvinists of various countries. The Allies did not offer much to those who remained. Instead of conceding those Jewish areas that had initially been liberated by Jewish partisans as a Jewish territory, the Allies chose instead to support the occupation of Palestine by the Zionists thus leading to the further depletion of European Jewry. The Stalinist Marxist regime in the Soviet Union certainly would not have ceded any authority over what used to be called the ìPale of Settlementî in the western-most Ukraine. The pre-war alternative to the Zionist parties, called the General Jewish Workers' Union (the Bund), was eliminated by both the Nazis and the Stalinists and nothing remained to oppose the Zionist chauvinists.

Currently the Peace Now opposition in Israel seems to be the major opposition to Zionism among the Jewish people but despite its occasional militant statements and demonstrations, it functions as the extra-parliamentary support for the Labour Alignment which has been in the forefront of Israeli expansionism. Nonetheless there has arisen various Jewish committees which have expressed their opposition to Zionism as an ideology since the 1967 war of expansion. Now major academic figures are speaking out in the U.S. Jewish milieu. In Canada, a number of committees have worked over the decades to offer a Jewish alternative to Zionism inside the Jewish community and the Jewish People's Liberation Organization (JPLO) represents their continuation as well as the renewal of the old Bundist and Jewish partisan struggle against Nazism and Fascism. The struggle against the racialist Nazi movement still continues against formations such as the National Front as well as the old war criminals hiding in various countries. Because the Zionists have adopted the mentality of European Fascism and its worship of the exclusivist Nation-State, the JPLO struggles against the Zionist parties as well.

The programme to oppose Zionism looks forward to the time that pluralist societies will take the place of the Nation-State in order to nurture a multi-national environment for the various peoples living together to establish reciprocal relations. The following are the major points in the JPLO programme:

1. We call for a complete withdrawal of all the Israeli military and intelligence services from the Palestinian territories occupied by Israel during the 1967 war in accordance with U.N. resolutions. Withdrawal from the occupied Lebanese and Syrian territories is also a necessary pre-condition for peaceful relations between the Middle East countries.

2. We call for the creation of a fully independent Palestinian country; a Civil Society with all the powers of a State in accordance with U.N. resolutions throughout the territories with full U.N. membership rights, including East Jerusalem and serving as a democratic and pluralist territory for all people of Palestinian origin, serving to reunite families continuing to live in refugee camps throughout the Middle East region. Full voting rights for all Palestinians who were forced to flee Palestine during the 1948 and 1967 wars or during the years of Israeli occupation, in addition to holders of Israeli citizenship, are necessary for the Palestine National Council.

3. The interim provisions of the peace process should lead to the formation of a pluralist and bi-national Civil Society in the land of Palestine, with a united Jerusalem. The return of Palestinians to their historic origins shall be a right according to the civil constitution formed by a Constitutional Assembly of all nations inhabiting Palestine. Recuperation or compensation for familial properties of pre-1948 Palestine shall be established by treaty.

4. We insist upon the establishment of a nuclear-free zone throughout the region so that no country possesses or prepares nuclear, biological or chemical weapons of mass destruction. Transport of material used in the production of such devices shall be forbidden by all countries.

5. We furthermore call for an end to sanctions against Iraq, Iran and Libya, except for military arms sales restrictions that are equally applied to all other key countries in the region.

6. The JPLO seeks to liberate the individual civil and collective rights of the Jewish people in the context of the various societies where we reside. Our collective rights include provisions for national-cultural autonomy in particular and auto-determination in general. In particular, it must be recognized that anti-Jewish anti-Semitism cannot be tolerated and refugee rights must be respected irrespective of the existence of the Israeli State.

Algeria, to where?

By: Sheikh Rashed Ghannoushi*

The Algerian crisis reached its peak over the past few months. Its cards were mixed and the unprecedented increase in victims made it a prominent subject in both international news and political arenas despite the attempts by the Algerian government to shield what is going on in its country.

The many victims as a direct result of the numerous massacres obliged the political forces which supported the coup against democracy and Islam in Algeria to speak up about this disaster which should be urgently solved despite the different views among the foreign powers. France spoke up for the first time about the necessity to put an end to the Algerian crisis by allowing the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS) to participate in the upcoming parliamentary elections, a call that annoyed the government and the supporters of the military solution inside and outside Algeria. The Americans, however, considered it a local crisis and indicated that they would not interfere unless the Algerian government called upon them to do so.

This difference in views between France and the United States of America could be a part of the increasing polarization between the two powers in the international arena and in Africa and the Middle East in particular. It might also apply to the increasing influence of the Zionist lobby in the US Administration which is totally biased against Islam and the Islamic movements. Meanwhile, the French policy during President Chirac's era proved to be a tendency towards the adoption of independent attitudes which are often opposite to that of American policies. The French attitude could extend and even meet with the attitudes of the Islamic movements arising in the region and in North Africa in particular. Indications for this assumption are clear in the French attitude towards Sudan, Iran and those countries subject to the brute and unjust US sanctions such as those imposed against Libya and Iraq.

The war in Algeria has become entangled in local and international dimensions and will probably continue because certain army generals want to control the power structure. Despite the internal disputes over power and interests, which could explain the assassinations of President Bu Dhayaf and more recently Ben Hamouda (head of the Labourersí Union), such disputes are still being kept under control because of the cross interests of those involved.

Meanwhile, the combination of the escalation of the economic crisis, the aspects of rebellion in the Algerian culture, and the military experience the Islamic militias attained during the past few years, have all contributed to a balance between the official and the popular violence which has thrust the country into different prospects. Surely, President Zirwal's promise to end the war in favour of the state and to exterminate what he called ìthe terroristsî as well as to close forever the FIS file is not one of those prospects. The events which followed his promise made it unlikely to happen. Other probabilities are many, and all agree on the return of the FIS to the political arena sooner or later. It will come back either as a partner in the government or as a totally governing party. The war will be settled either through peaceful negotiations and power-sharing between the army and the opposition headed by the FIS or through a popular uprising that crushes the army generals. The army could collapse and some army groups might join the opposition and refuse to destroy the country just to appease a few generals. What else is in the interest of Algeria, the army and even President Zirwal himself in being so devout to the military solution and in going ahead on a dead-end road except to achieve some trifle personal interests?

It is evident that the security, political and economic situations in Algeria are ripe for an overall uprising. The situations are being developed in favour of the opposition and not the ruling party. Security is becoming more lax day by day, and the political status of those in power is increasingly isolated locally and externally. President Zirwal has consumed all the benefits he gained in the presidential elections, and the people are no longer optimistic that he could lead the country to a general reconciliation to stop the blood-shed. Instead, he left his generals to sink in the blood bath until he no longer wore a political cover.

Meanwhile, FIS popularity has been enhanced, and its relations with the democratic opposition strengthened. All indications point to the fact that events are being rapidly developed in favour of the opposition led by the FIS, which adopts a moderate approach and a political programme which is a good grounding for national reconciliation.

Mina Fire Overshadows Hajj

Mr. Khalid Amayreh, Palestine Times' correspondent in the West Bank, returned home from Mecca after performing Hajj (pilgrimage). He wrote this article upon his return to the Occupied Territories.

The annual Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca this year, which reached its climax on 15 April with the traditional "Wakfa" or ìstop-overî at Mount Arafat, was marred by a huge blaze that swept through the pilgrim tent city of Mina, destroying more than 70,000 tents, killing 343 pilgrims and injuring another 15,000 people, mostly from the Indian subcontinent.

The blaze, fanned by strong easterly winds, lasted for more than four hours before Saudi fire-fighters finally succeeded in getting it under control. Hot desert temperatures, reaching 42 degrees centigrade, combined with extremely congested roads in the area, seriously hindered efforts to quickly put out the fire. Moreover, the readily flammable substance of which the tents were made obviously accelerated the spread of the blaze.

It is still unclear as to what exactly sparked the colossal fire, but Saudi officials seemed to be sure that a faulty cooking gas canister caused the inferno. Initially the Saudi media, tightly controlled by the government, was laconic about the incident, repeatedly promising listeners and viewers to give more details later. However, as the full scope of the tragedy was becoming more apparent, Interior Minister and President of the Supreme Hajj Committee, Prince Naif Ibn Abdul Aziz announced the "unfortunate disaster," which he rightly said could have resulted in a much greater catastrophe.

Identifying the victims proved an extremely difficult task as many bodies were charred beyond recognition. Saudi Hajj officials, nevertheless, made strenuous efforts to identify the nationality of the victims in cooperation with various national pilgrim missions. On 18 April, it was announced that among the victims were at least 150 Indians, 60 Pakistanis, in addition to scores from other, mostly East-Asian, countries.

An Indian official in Jeddah pointed a finger of blame at the Saudi authorities, saying that virtually all the victims could have been saved had the Saudis taken proper action in time. The Indian consul said that the Saudis had erected a fence around the encampment where the fire took place, which he said prevented the victims from fleeing the site. According to survivors, many of the victims were trampled on by fleeing pilgrims anxious to escape the fire.

The Hajj drew to a final close with Tawaf al Ifada (the final circumambulation around the Kaaba at the Grand Mosque) following the stop-over at Arafat. Saudi authorities declared this year's Hajj clean as no epidemics or contagious diseases were reported among the estimated 2 million pilgrims. Non-Arab Muslims featured more prominently during the Hajj this year, with Arab pilgrims not exceeding 20 per cent of the total number of pilgrims. As many as 170,000 Indonesians made it to Mecca this year, with Pakistan placing second, India third, followed by Iran, Turkey and Egypt respectively. More black Africans reportedly performed Hajj this year than in previous years, which might reflect growing Islamic awareness among the black continent's rising Muslim population. Moreover, King Fahd reportedly invited 1,000 pilgrims from Chechnya to perform Hajj and a similar number from Bosnia. More pilgrims also came from China, especially from its troubled western, predominantly Muslim, region of Xingiang or Eastern Turkestan.

Putting the tragic Mina blaze aside, the Hajj season went generally smoothly, with the Saudi authorities offering nearly excellent services to the pilgrims. State-of-the-art services and accommodations ranged from air-conditioned marble pavement to computerized billboards featuring prayer times and were everywhere at and around the two spacious Grand Mosques in Mecca and Medina. Cold water was available round-the-clock through ubiquitous fountains at the two sanctuaries. Furthermore, there was no shortage of food throughout the pilgrimage term, although a slight rise in prices was noted due to intensified demand.

The Golden Fledgling

(Part 2)

By: E. Yaghi

Bursting through the door, the leader snarled, "Where is the boy? Tell us at once! We know he came in here. We have witnesses. If you don't tell us, weíll kill you!"

Panic-stricken by now, the old woman denied any knowledge of a boy entering her home. "Search the house!" hissed the stocky leader. She cringed in fear as the police rummaged through and broke her few worldly possessions.

"Please," begged Sarah, "Please leave me alone. I know nothing of any boy!"

"Liars," yelled the leader. "You are all liars!" As he shouted, one of his men forced open the cellar door. The boy crouched in horror at his foreseeable discovery.

At first the policemen did not see the boy and then as his eyes strained in the unaccustomed dark, he saw Haitham trying to make himself invisible in a corner.

"Here he is!" the policeman shouted as he ripped the boy out of the cellar with one forcible swing of his arm. "This is one of the brats that was throwing stones!"

The five policemen all wrenched the boy from one another and began mercilessly beating him with their thick clubs. The young boy screamed with pain and horror as his blood gushed out from the relentless blows to his head and body.

Sarah shrieked in anger, entreating the world and the village to stop the inhumane beating of the boy. "In God's mercy, " she cried, "leave the boy alone! You are armed with weapons and clubs. You call yourselves police yet you all chase one small boy. Tyrants!"

As she spoke, blood oozed through Haitham's silky hair and over his clothes. While she was still pleading, one of the policemen struck her in the head with his club. She sank down praying for God's mercy, for the safety of her sons and Haitham, and one final prayer for herself. She died helplessly murdered in a pool of her own blood.

Unheeding the death of the old woman, the police grabbed the boy, dragged him outside and threw him in their van. Inside the vehicle, other young prisoners, also guilty of stone-throwing, wiped Haitham's face, trying to stop the flow of blood that still gushed out of his head wounds. He felt a blinding pain and helpless, hopeless fear. He remembered old Sarah dying for him and felt responsible for her death. His own demise seemed imminent.

He was taken with the other captives to an underground dungeon. Unearthly dank odors were everywhere. He could hear the screams of prisoners being tortured. He felt meek and numb. The guards threw him into a small cell with about 50 other boys and men.

"Let's make an example out of this one," a vicious policeman told his friend. "At midnight we'll come and take the boy out and hang him. He will be a good lesson for all stone-throwers. Freedom, equality, bah! All of them should be killed so we can be rid of them and take this country for ourselves!"

Hungry, cold and paralyzed with fear, Haitham huddled against an older boy whose face was swollen from beating and his right arm was broken. "Hey, kid," he offered, "youíll be all right. Youíre a hero! Good for you and damn the Occupation! The world is deaf to our torment and torture. If the mountains, hills and rivers of our country could talk as witnesses to our agony, they would scream in mortification for all our suffering! Long live freedom, democracy and our country! Our women and children fight a courageous yet helpless fight. Quiet, my boy, you are here with us!" But Haitham wondered who his mate was trying to convince to be brave.

At midnight, two policemen came and forced Haitham out of his cell. The other prisoners demanded that the police leave the boy alone and let him go home to his family, but the police only clubbed the resisting cell-mates while the other raised his gun on them.

The world lay sleeping as one small, frightened boy was forced into the police car and taken outside the village. As it approached an ancient olive tree, the car silently crept to a halt. The police grabbed a thick rough rope from the back seat of the vehicle.

"Please, what are you going to do to me? Please let me go home!" pleaded Haitham. By now, his blood had matted his hair and thickened to a crust. He saw the bulky, snake-like rope with the same policeman who had heartlessly struck down the old woman.

"Here, boy, we are going to make you an example for all stone-throwers and revolutionists. May you all perish and be damned," growled one of the murderers.

Reaching deep into his small body, the young 'Golden Eagle' drew up the strength of a man and cried out, "No, may you be damned for injustice, for murder, for not letting a boy be just a boy. Our only weapons are stones. If you kill me, I shall die a martyr, but you shall go to Hell! My blood will be on your hands and you won't be able to wash it off! Long live Palestine! Down with Occupation!"

With this last sentence, the murderers tied a noose around the neck of the deserted innocent boy, a boy who in any other country would have been playing with his friends, not fighting for liberty. The two policemen, contradicting their own uniforms, roughly strung the fledgling up in the tree. He kicked and yelled, fighting the rope that choked the life out of him until his neck snapped.

The evil deed done, the two criminals slunk to their car and returned to their own homes and children, not once thinking of the young eagle left to die alone while the world slept its dreary lazy sleep.

In the morning, Haitham's small dead body was carried to his home. His little brothers cried in dismay at his death. His mother gave one long, piercing scream and then fainted. The neighbours, some with their own husbands and sons in prison or dead, came to comfort her. Only one of many small lives was snuffed out as if it were only a candle flame. Another life joined the list of the unending and seemingly hopeless cause of liberty and freedom. Meanwhile, around the world, most people woke to a new day to enjoy a cup of tea or coffee, and to pursue a leisurely, carefree or busy morning, completely unaware of the suffering and pain of those in a less fortunate state of being than themselves.
