PA: One dove in the hand is better than ten on the branch

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khalid Amayreh

As had been widely expected, and after saying for two months that he would not accept Israeli maps detailing the second phase of redeployment as stipulated in the revised Wye Memorandum signed at Sharm el-Sheikh in the autumn of 1999, Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat decided on 5 January 2000 to accept the Israeli plan “as it is.”

On 5 January, Palestinian negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat, acting on instructions from Arafat, told his Israeli counterpart during an “interim-phase meeting” in West Jerusalem that the PA was simply accepting the maps.

With downcast spirit, Ureikat told reporters that the Palestinians “made no gains” since the Israeli government refused to accommodate any of the Palestinian demands concerning the location of the territories slated to be transferred to the PA’s full or partial administration.

Israeli officials had argued all along that Israel alone has the right to decide which land is to be transferred to the Palestinians, irrespective of PA objections.

However, unlike in past redeployments when Israel preferred to hand-over Palestinian population centres to the PA, this time, arid, sparsely-populated territory was offered for area (B) where the PA is entitled to have only civilian responsibilities.

PA officials were taken off-guard by the Israeli logic, prompting one official to remark, “Are we supposed to look after the welfare of snakes and scorpions in these areas?”

But, eventually, on 7 and 8 January, and after the United States made clear to Arafat that it would not exert pressure on Israel to modify the redeployment plan, the second phase of redeployment did take place in accordance with the Israeli maps.

Thus, 2% of the West Bank (minus Jerusalem, since Israel does not recognize that East Jerusalem is part of the West Bank) was transferred from area (B) to area (A), and 3% from area (C) to area (B).

The areas transferred to the PA administration included villages and hamlets in the northern and central parts of the West Bank, as well as two former Jordanian army camps, abandoned by the Israeli army several years ago.

The third and final stage of redeployment (according to Wye) is supposed to take place on 20 January; Israel is supposed to hand over up to 6.1% of the West Bank to the PA, including some fairly large towns such as Dura, 10 miles south-west of Hebron, which will become area (A).

Seeking to extenuate their embarrassment of having given in to Israeli intransigence on the second-redeployment maps, Ureikat claimed that Israel promised that it would take Palestinian “views” into consideration when implementing the aforementioned third redeployment.

However, Eran was quick to deny this saying, “We only promised to listen to them,” suggesting that Israel will unilaterally decide the areas that will be handed over to the Palestinians in the third redeployment as well.

When the third redeployment is carried out, Israel and the PA will still have to tackle the more important issue of the third grand phase of redeployment, stipulated in the Oslo Accords.

According to the Declaration of Principles, by the end of the “interim phase” (as opposed to the final-status talks which began while the interim phase was far from ending) the Israeli army is suppose to vacate the entirety of the West Bank save the settlements, military camps and East Jerusalem.

However, the successive Israeli governments, including the current government of Ehud Barak, voiced a different interpretation of the stipulations to that effect in the Oslo Accords.

Former Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu said on many occasions that Israel would not “give up” more than a lone 1% of the West Bank in the context of the third phase of redeployment.

And his successor Barak, though more tight-lipped on the issue, indicated that he would not cede more than his predecessor was willing to and that he saw no separation between the interim and the final-status issues.

Indeed, Barak made strenuous efforts, with some notable successes, to merge the interim phase with the final status talks. His strategy to that effect is illustrated by the fact that Odid Eran is Head of the Israeli negotiating teams for both the final status talks and the interim phase talks with the Palestinians.

For their part, the Palestinians sought to bring about an absolute separation between the “two tracks,” by appointing PA information minister Yasser Abed Rabbou to head its final status talks team while Sa’eb Ureikat remained chief negotiator for the interim phase issues.

The PA’s acceptance of Israel’s virtual dictates on the redeployment maps suggests that Arafat is now adopting the old adage, “A pigeon in the hand is better than ten on the branch.”

According to observers here, Arafat’s retreat stemmed from his conviction that the final status talks will go nowhere, and that the PA will be in a better position with up to 40% of the West Bank in case of declaring Palestinian Statehood.

Hence, the prevailing thinking in the PA circles is that “we should get all we can get now before the ultimate crises in the final status talks paralyses the entire peace process with Israel.”

In light of the above, and if the deadlock in the final status continues, it would be reasonable to expect Yasser Arafat to declare a Palestinian State sooner rather than later, either unilaterally or in tacit coordination with Israel and the United States. Indeed, Arafat cannot embark on a grave step as such without some coordination with the Jewish State, which is most directly involved in this.

And, predictably, Israel’s consent to the declaration of Palestinian Statehood would not be without a price.

That price is 40%-45% of the West Bank (as, indeed, Odid Eran made it clear to Yasser Abed Rabbou during their meeting in Ramallah in the last week of December), including most of the settlements; Gush Etzion, extending from the southern suburbs of Jerusalem to the northern outskirts of Hebron; and a 4-kilometre security zone along the former armistice line between the West Bank and Israel proper.

It is, of course, unexpected that Arafat will accept the Israeli fait accompli, since it would fly in the face of United Nations resolutions 242 and 338 and would be utterly unacceptable to the Palestinians, as well as to Fatah, the political backbone of his regime.

However, this should not mean that the ageing and increasingly frail Palestinian leader would not reach at least a tacit “understanding” with Israel whereby the new situation would be treated as “status quo” recognized by the two sides but necessarily accepted as “permanent” by either or both of them.

In this case, the declaration of a small, truncated and deformed Palestinian State on parts of the West Bank would be the result of the failure of the peace process (especially if the final status talks remained deadlocked as expected), rather than a culmination of its success.

In other words, it would be a prescription for the resumption of the conflict between Israel and the Palestinians and probably also for regional instability as well.

Human rights violations resulting
from the Oslo process

New York - The following text, prepared by the New York-based Center for Economic and Social Rights (CESR), was circulated during mid-January as a two-part brief by the Center for Policy Analysis on Palestine.

I. Palestinian Authority Responsibility
While not formally recognized as a State under the Oslo process, the Palestinian Authority (PA) in fact exercises State-like powers through effective control over area (A), now 12% of the West Bank. (Under the Oslo agreements, Israel presently retains full control over 60% of the West Bank and exercises overriding security jurisdiction over an additional 28%.) Moreover, the PA has a sovereign legal identity as a delegated authority of the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO), which enjoys diplomatic relations with over 125 countries. Under the PLO’s 1988 Declaration of Independence, “The State of Palestine declares its commitment to . . . the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.” The PA has also made frequent public declarations to abide by human rights principles.

Under the Oslo process, the PA was assigned the unenviable task of protecting Israeli security (defined as the personal safety of every Israeli) without sufficient political and economic control to pursue real self-determination or development for the Palestinian population. Even if Israel fully implements the Sharm el-Sheikh Memorandum of September 1999, the PA would control only 18.1% of the West Bank. The PA’s inability to fulfil the most basic political and economic demands of the people it claims to represent is one of the major reasons for its widespread violations of human rights.

II. PA Human Rights Abuses
Abuses by the PA, especially violations of civil and political rights, have been well documented. In the face of public dissatisfaction and protest over declining living standards, the PA has clamped down on any public expression of dissent to its policies under Oslo. The recent campaign of repression against 20 prominent Palestinians (9 of whom are members of the elected Palestinian Legislative Council) who signed a petition protesting corruption and political weakness in the PA is only the most recent and best-publicized example. The PA’s ubiquitous security apparatus relies on indiscriminate sweeps and arrests, arbitrary detention, military courts and torture. These illegal measures have not only been demanded by Israel in the name of fighting “terrorism”, but also condoned by the same donor governments that fund PA training programmes on the rule of law and good governance.

These violations of civil and political rights stem in part from popular opposition to PA economic policies and practices. According to a 1997 audit conducted by the Legislative Council, PA officials have mismanaged a staggering 40% of foreign aid and revenues. Widespread public corruption at the highest levels has been a major factor causing economic decline, reducing Palestinians’ access to health care, education and other basic services. Moreover, taking advantage of the closure’s restricted access to external markets, officials close to PA President Yasser Arafat have established monopolies on most basic goods, leading to sharp price increases. This combination of corruption and monopolies violates the economic, social and cultural rights (ESCR) of Palestinians.

III. Violations by International Donors
All international actors—not just States—have duties under human rights laws regardless of whether or not they have ratified specific treaties. The Universal Declaration mandates that “every individual and every organ of society shall . . . promote respect for these rights and freedoms.” States and international actors have been found liable for indirect or third-party support of human rights violations by others. For example, the International Court of Justice found the U.S. responsible for abuses committed by Nicaraguan contras backed by the U.S.

Under Oslo, international donors have legal obligations. The fact that European and U.S. funders are bankrolling the Oslo process makes them responsible for human rights violations committed with these funds. International donors provide a large share of the PA’s budget yet only earmark 1% of funds for the judiciary and legislature combined. Most of these funds go to political organs controlled directly by Arafat, and end up supporting repression. Indeed, the U.S. Agency for International Development funds have disproportionately financed PA police and security forces. The astonishing imbalance in foreign funds given to the PA may be the single largest factor in undermining the rule of law and shaping the increasingly lawless security apparatus in PA-controlled areas.

It is also disturbing that international donors have not demanded an end to the Israeli closure, despite the fact that their aid has been diverted away from real development and toward emergency measures, such as temporary job creation, in effect subsidizing the closure. Instead, donors have proposed a model for Palestinian economic development that assumes the continuation of closure. These plans call for more export processing zones such as Karni at the Eretz military checkpoint on the border of Gaza. Similar to the maquiladoras at the U.S.-Mexico border, these areas allow Israeli and foreign capital to exploit cheap Palestinian labour while maintaining a strict closure confining Palestinians within their enclaves.

IV. Structural Flaws in the Oslo Framework
The recent increase in human rights abuses by Israel, the PA and international donors stems from structural flaws in the Oslo process itself. The most significant flaw is the abandonment of established international law in favour of bilateral negotiations between a strong party and a much weaker one. As a result, the Oslo agreements overwhelmingly reflect Israeli concerns about its political recognition and its security while either dismissing central Palestinian concerns or relegating them to undefined “final status” negotiations. Israel has also exercised its unfettered power to unilaterally dictate the terms of Oslo’s implementation, effectively crippling the Palestinian economy through closure and seizing more Palestinian land and resources without facing public pressure for violating human rights and international law. For its part, the PA has responded to its political and economic weakness under Oslo by building a formidable security apparatus to repress popular dissent while instituting a form of “governance” marked by cronyism and corruption.

The final chapter of the Oslo process is imminent, with final status negotiations due to conclude by the end of this year. The general shape of the final agreement is easy to predict: a politically and economically nonviable Palestinian “State” in isolated enclaves comprising less than 50% of Gaza and the West Bank, subject to Israeli control, dependent on international aid and brutally policed by Palestinian security forces.

Expected to bring a measure of prosperity and security to a population that had suffered 27 years of military occupation, the Oslo process instead has caused an increase in economic deprivation and human rights violations. The impact can be seen in higher unemployment rates, lower household incomes, restricted access to such basic services as health care and education, and severe limitations on freedom of speech and movement.

In essence, Oslo’s structural failure to link peace with human rights has created a situation in which Palestinians now face double repression from Israel and their own nascent government.

After Oslo
The dominant public discourse insists that Oslo is the only viable framework for resolving the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Opposition to Oslo is equated with opposition to peace, without recognition that any “peace process” built on a foundation of human rights violations and economic exploitation is not sustainable except through increasing levels of repression. A just peace in the region based on human rights and international law offers the best hope for challenging the ongoing economic and political disenfranchisement of the Palestinian people.

Sheikh Yassin: America sees the
Middle East with only one eye

Occupied Jerusalem - Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin told an American college student delegation he met with on 16 January that the United States sees the Middle East with only one eye and that the current “peace process” would bring neither justice for the Palestinians nor security for Israel.

The delegation, headed by Yehuda Looker, Vice President of the George Mason University, had met with many Israeli and Palestinian leaders in order to be aware of the various views of the main players in the conflict.

Yassin told the students that the Palestinian people were eager for peace and justice.

“A peace that is void of justice will not be accepted by the people and therefore will not last long,” Yassin told the American delegation.

Yassin pointed out that the resolution of the Palestinian question did not lie in declaring Palestinian Statehood, but rather in enabling some four million Palestinian refugees to return to their historical homeland.

“Without this, there simply can be no enduring peace.”

Yassin said declaring Palestinian Statehood under current circumstances would not do the Palestinians much good.

“What would we make of a State without land and freedom?” he asked.

He said Israel and the United States would be glad if the Palestinian cause would be effectively liquidated under the rubric of a deformed Palestinian State on 2% or 3% of Palestine.

Yassin argued that the refugees were still dispersed throughout the four corners of the globe, while settlements were being erected on grabbed Arab land in the West Bank and Gaza.

The Palestinian Islamist leader lambasted American policy in the Middle East, saying “justice was conspicuously absent from that policy.”

Yassin said the deportation of four Hamas leaders from Jordan to Qatar in November was “a blunt violation of Jordanian and international laws.”

“Where in the world does a respectable country deport its citizens?” he asked.

Yassin said Hamas was the victim of vilification and demonization in the United States owing to the fact that Zionist Jews control much of the media in the West.

He said, “America sees us with one eye—an eye that sees legitimate resistance against an evil and sinister occupation as terrorism.”

He argued that Hamas was not only a military group, but a human, social, political and cultural movement, adding that “we have the right to fight the usurpers of our rights and the grabbers of our homeland.”

“Even if my own brother takes over my home, I’ll fight him,” he said.

Yassin asked the delegation why Israel is not labelled a terrorist State despite the fact that it has committed a long list of massacres against innocent civilians, including Deir Yassin, Sabra and Shatila, Qana, the Ibrahimi Mosque and Bahr al-Bakr.

Israeli media overplay Goshe’s statements

Occupied Jerusalem - Israeli media sought to overplay statements attributed to Hamas spokesman Ibrahim Goshe in which he reportedly criticized the Muslim Brothers in Jordan for not adopting Hamas’ strategy for the liberation of Palestine.

The Israeli radio and television spoke of “a serious rift” between Hamas and the Muslim Brotherhood.

The Israeli State-run radio quoted experts as saying that “Goshe’s statements were reflecting the predicament of Hamas abroad.”

Moreover, the Zionist media suggested that the deportation by the Jordanian government of four Hamas leaders two months ago contributed to strengthening the “local leadership.”

Israel, Hamas’ arch enemy, gloated over the deportation of the four Islamist leaders.

The Israeli Mossad sought unsuccessfully nearly two years ago to assassinate in Amman Khalid Misha’al, the head of Hamas politburo.

Jordan was believed to have carried out the deportation under pressure from the United States, Israel and the Palestinian Authority.

A Jordanian newspaper, Al-Arab al-Yawm, on 23 January quoted Goshe as saying that the Muslim Brotherhood played a hesitant role in the crisis between Hamas and the Jordanian government.

Meanwhile, Hamas founder and spiritual leader Sheikh Ahmed Yassin last month moved to defuse the recent tension between his Palestinian Islamic resistance movement and the Muslim Brothers of Jordan which was caused by Mr. Ghoshe’s statements.

Goshe had accused the Muslim Brothers' leadership of abandoning the Palestinian cause and of not doing enough to prevent the Jordanian government from deporting Hamas officials from Amman.

Yassin spoke via telephone with Jamil Abu Baker, the official spokesperson of the Muslim Brothers in Amman.

According to Abu Baker, Yassin emphasized the umbilical relationship between Hamas and the Muslim Brothers.

Hamas generally follows the same ideological line of the Muslim Brothers, which calls for the reinstitution of the Islamic political authority throughout the Muslim world.

Palestinian council member
accuses Arafat of gangsterism

Ramallah- A Palestinian Legislative council member accused Palestinian Authority Chairman Yaser Arafat of “gangster-like behavior” and “of personalizing Palestinian politics and polity.”

Azmi Shua’ebi castigated what he called the “state of lawlessness throughout the Palestinian society” which he said “was being fostered by Arafat’s supporters who couldn’t do what they do without a green light from him.”

Shua’ebi pointed out that while the PA claims that there is only one authority, pro-Arafat militias and thugs often scour the streets of the West Bank, intimidating civilians by firing into the air.

Last month, scores of masked Fatah activists rampaged through the streets of Ramallah, firing into the air from their automatic weapons in full view of PA police.

PA officials refused to comment on the incident, saying, “it is being dealt with at the highest level.”

Last December, Fatah thugs shot Palestinian Legislative Council member Mu’awiya al Masri three times in the leg after he had signed a petition exposing widespread graft and corruption in the PA regime.

Another co-signer, councilor and former minister Abdul Jawad Saleh, was also assaulted and badly hurt by General Intelligence thugs while visiting interned fellow petitioners in a Jericho lockup.

Human rights groups accuses Israel
of war crimes against Lebanese

Occupied Jerusalem- The Israeli human rights group, B’Tselem, accused Israel committing serious human rights violations that constitute war crimes in the Israeli occupied “security zone” in southern Lebanon.

The group issued a 67-page report, illustrating the various forms of human rights violations by the Israeli occupation army.

It pointed out that the occupation army “flagrantly and continuously violates fundamental human rights of Lebanese civilians “either directly through its own armed forces or indirectly through its south Lebanese army (SLA).”

The report cited several forms of human rights violations, including the extra-judicial detention and torture of Lebanese by the SLA of at the militia’s notorious Khayam Jail, the abduction of Lebanese and their imprisonment without trail in Israel where they are held as “bargaining chips” for information on the fate of missing Israeli soldiers, the expulsion of residents from the zone by the SLA and its practice of forcible conscription, including minors.

The report also pointed out to the indiscriminate firing in violation of the rules of war and the use by the Israeli army of weapons such as phosphorous shells that fire thousands of darts in circumstances that have killed and wounded civilians.

The report said these violations whether committed directly or indirectly constitute war crimes under international law.

Israel disappointed at lack of
information on missing soldier

Occupied Jerusalem- The Israeli government has expressed disappointment at Hizbullah refusal to respond to the release by the Israeli occupation authorities of 15 Lebanese hostages last December, Israeli sources said.

Israel was hoping that the release of the Lebanese hostages would lead to some insight about the whereabouts of missing Israeli air navigator Ron Arad.

Israel still holds 16 Lebanese hostages without charge or trial, some for more than ten years.

There had been unofficial reports that the release of the five hostages was part of a three way deal involving Israel, Lebanon and Germany, whereby Israel would receive information about Arad, Iran would ameliorate the plight of 13 Jews on trial for treason, a German citizen sentenced to death in Iran would be freed and the five Lebanese hostages would go home.

Hizbullah official Naim Kasem has said that his organization would seek to get information about Arad to obtain the release of the remaining 16 hostages, including Mustafa Dirani and Sheikh Abdul Karim Obid.

Palestinian Authority to build
a large nightclub in Jericho

Occupied Jerusalem - The Palestinian Authority is reportedly planning to open a large nightclub in the West Bank of Jericho.

Palestinian sources in Jericho said the nightclub would include a bar, a large dance hall and "recreational facilities".

The sources said the building housing the nightclub was being constructed not far away from the "Oasis Casino", considered one of the largest in the Middle East.

A spokesman for the Jericho municipal council said he was not aware of any plans to that effect.

"They carry out such sensitive projects without getting us involved…perhaps it has to do with Palestinian national security," said the secretary, apparently jokingly.

Sheikh Harb Jaber, a local Islamic leader, lambasted the PA for "spreading decadence in our society."

"Instead of building factories and finding jobs for the unemployed, they are opening dens of adultery and promiscuity…this is not what the Palestinian people had longed for."

Arafat reticent about Ha'aretz' report on refugees

Occupied Jerusalem - Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat refused to elaborate on an Israeli newspaper report suggesting that he had indicated to U.S. President Bill Clinton that he was willing to waive the Palestinian refugees' right of return.

Palestinian sources said Arafat refused to answer several questions on the subject before he left Gaza for Davus, Switzerland, to take part in the International Economic Forum.

Several Palestinian Authority officials have denied the report.

However, those officials may only be speaking for themselves since they are not part of the decision-making process in the autocratic regime of Arafat.

Meanwhile, many Palestinian civic leaders have begun a campaign to alert Palestinians at home and in the Diaspora against a possible sell-out of the refugees' rights by Yasser Arafat.

Palestinian sources indicated that the coming weeks and months were likely to witness high-profile activities to that effect.

PA official accuses Iran of adopting
inimical policies against PA

Occupied Jerusalem - A Palestinian Authority official accused Iran of adopting policies hostile to the Palestinian leadership.

Al-Tayeb Abdulrahim, Secretary-General of Arafat' office, also claimed that Iran financed armed operations against Israel for the purpose of destabilizing the PA and derailing the peace process.

"Iran is the only country in the region that has an interest in undermining the peace process and the Palestinian Authority," the Egyptian weekly, al-Ahram al-Arabi, quoted Abdulrahim as saying.

The PA official said, though, that many Iranian officials were supportive of Arafat and the peace process.

Hamas denied Abdulrahim's allegations.

"He has been making this nonsense for a long time," said Hamas Gaza official, Ismael Abu-Shanab.

Hamas, he said, is an independent Palestinian movement and its convictions stem from its own ideology.

Mufti of Palestine: “Aqsa Mosque
is none of Israel’s business”

Occupied Jerusalem - The Mufti of Palestine and Jerusalem, Sheikh Ikrema Sabri, warned the Zionist regime against “tampering with the issue of al-Aqsa Mosque,” saying, “this issue is an exclusive Muslim affair and Israel has no right to have any say in it.”

Sabri described Islamic holy places in Jerusalem as “absolute red lines.”

“Israeli extremists are advised to keep off this issue because al-Aqsa Mosque is the ultimate red line for Muslims here and everywhere,” the Palestinian daily al-Ayyam quoted Sabri as saying.

Sabri’s remarks came after Israeli extremists petitioned the Israeli Supreme Court to stop renovation work on al-Aqsa Mosque carried out by Wakf authorities.

Sabri said the Holy Shrine of al-Aqsa Mosque was and would continue to be outside the confines of the jurisdiction of Israeli courts, adding that the “issue transcends legal and political calculations.”

The Wakf authorities last December reopened a gate to the Islamic shrine to be used in cases of an emergency, particularly during the month of Ramadan when hundreds of thousands of Muslims come to the Mosque for prayers.

Some Israeli circles would like to see the Aqsa Mosque erased in order to expedite the building of a Jewish temple on its site.

Report: Israel still detains 14 children since 22 December

Occupied Jerusalem - The Israeli army is reportedly still interning 14 Palestinian children aged 12-16 whom it rounded up on 22 December.

According to the World Organization Against Torture (OMCT), large numbers of Israeli troops raided the houses of the children in Beituanian and the Jalazon refugee camps between 26 December and 1 January, and forcefully demanded that the children be handed over to them. The one exception was a 14-year-old who was arrested on 22 December.

According to the children’s lawyer, Khalid Quzmar, they were taken for interrogation at Beit Eal detention centre, near Ramallah, immediately following their arrest, without allowing the children to sleep.

During interrogation, it was reported that two of the children were beaten and the others were threatened with physical abuse, and confessions were extracted from the arrested children.

Since the time of the arrests, four of the minors (ages between 12 and 13) have been released, two of whom were forced to pay $450 each.
