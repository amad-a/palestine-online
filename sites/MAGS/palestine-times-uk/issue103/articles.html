Arafat’s looming reign of terror

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khalid Amayreh

The Palestinian Authority released on 19 December six of the eight political activists arrested for three weeks for co-signing what has come to be known as “Petition-20”, which accused Yasser Arafat’s regime of widespread graft and corruption and of betraying the trust of the Palestinian people.

Most of the released are either prominent members of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP), namely Ahmed Qatamesh, or generally associated with the Palestinian Left. Hence, their release which was ordered by Arafat may have been linked to the Palestinian leader’s efforts to co-opt the PFLP to the Oslo process.

The two activists remaining in jail are Ahmed Dudin of Dura, a veteran Fatah activist, and Abdul Sattar Qasem, Professor of Political Science at the Najah National University in Nablus.

The two have adamantly refused to recant the Petition or, indeed, ask for forgiveness from Arafat. Instead, they retorted to a virulent campaign against them carried out by the PA media, arguing that they were merely reflecting the sentiments of the Palestinian masses and had nothing to regret or apologize for.

Dudin, who had spent many months of administrative detention in the Israeli desert detention centre at Ketseot in the Negev, was even more defiant. He told the Qatari satellite television al-Jazira on 10 December that it was shameful that the PA resorted to punishing the petitioners instead of punishing the “the lords of corruption” who “were corroding our country from within.”

He described PA accusations that the petitioners were acting on behalf of “outside powers”—a clear allusion to Syria—as “brash lies”, saying “it seems that one can no longer criticize morbid phenomena in society without being accused of being an agent for a foreign power.”

Likewise, Qasem, an outspoken critic of Arafat and author of a book entitled, Oslo: the Road to Disaster (banned by the PA), refused to plead for mercy from Arafat, saying he preferred to stay in jail rather than “betray his conscience.”

Qasem, himself a former supporter of the PLO, was the target of an assassination attempt by Fatah activists in 1994, ostensibly after he made scathing criticisms of the Oslo process and Arafat’s leadership.

The imprisoned signatories of Petition-20 were earlier threatened with prosecution before the so-called State Security Court (SSC), founded by Arafat in 1995 at Yitzhak Rabin’s insistence mainly to try and punish Palestinian critics of the Oslo process.

However, it seemed that Arafat opted not to go that far, probably after he had received recommendations from his advisors and probably from the Americans as well, that a draconian measure such as trying the eight critics before the SSC would ultimately further tarnish the image of the PA and its ruinous justice system. It remains to be said that the incarceration of the eight activists was ordered by the President with little, if any, intervention from the judicial system.

Indeed, more than one PA official referred to the imprisoned activists as “mutakalin ala themat al Ra’is” or “ prisoners detained by orders of the President.”

Decision to release political prisoners has ulterior motive

Arafat’s decision to release the six co-signers of the petition, along with the possible release of Dudin and Qasem probably before the end of Ramadan, does not mean, however, that the PA President is becoming less intolerant of political dissent, however peaceful that may be.

In fact, the opposite is true.

According to the Palestinian Human Rights monitoring Group (PHRMG), press censorship became tougher than usual and PA tolerance of freedom of speech was growing thinner than ever.

This took the form of refusing to allow newspapers to publish views defending the petitioners, even as “letters to the editor,” or replies to PA writers and columnists parroting the regime’s official line. Likewise, the PA-run radio and television continued to heap calumnies on “the advocates of division and tumult,” accusing them of “playing into the hands of the enemies of our people and of confusing the people at this crucial juncture of our struggle.”

Furthermore, on many occasions PA police confiscated tapes from cameramen and violently suppressed pro-freedom demonstrations in Nablus, Ramallah and Bethlehem.

Nonetheless, the most disturbing after-effect of Petition-20 was the recurrence of physical attacks on figures deemed disloyal to Arafat or too outspoken against his autocracy.

On 16 December, members of the General Intelligence (GI) badly beat Abdul Jawad Saleh, one of the signatories to the Petition, as he sought to visit the imprisoned petitioners in a Jericho jail.

Saleh, 66, reportedly was dragged off and beaten in utter disregard for his parliamentary immunity. He suffered bruises and cuts on the face and a resulting nosebleed, which required a brief hospitalization.

The incident, condemned by the powerless councillors, but utterly ignored by Arafat and the PA-run media, was widely viewed as a warning by Arafat to the “rowdy lawmakers” against going too far in opposing the President.

The next day, a human rights activist at Beir Zeit University, Hanan Maseou, sustained moderate injuries after she was assaulted by “unknown elements.”

Maseou, who is also in charge of a human rights programme at the University, reportedly found a written note pasted on one of the stones pelted at here, warning her against continuing to raise human rights violations against the PA enclaves.

Earlier this month, on 2 December, Moawiya al-Masri, one of the PLC members who signed Petition-20, was shot in the legs by several masked men armed with pistols and daggers.

In the three incidents, the perpetrators were not arrested, raising suggestions that somebody from the PA security agencies was responsible.

It is difficult to say for sure if the three incidents, ominous as they are, represent an advance harbinger for a looming reign of terror, or just reflecting nervousness on the part of the increasingly beleaguered Palestinian leader. The coming weeks and months will only tell. In any case, however, Arafat cannot be given the benefit of the doubt. Last week, Palestinian sources identified with Fatah intimated to (PT) that Arafat stunned senior cadres of the movement, whom he met in Ramallah recently, when he said that the Palestinian people “understood only the language of the stick.” Arafat reportedly uttered these words when Marwan Kanafani (PLC member and Head of Fatah regional committees in the West Bank) asked him to release from jail the signatories of Petition-20.

PLO council meeting with PA postponed

Meanwhile, the Palestinian Authority decided on 18 December to postpone until mid-January a crucial PLO Central Council meeting, which will take place on 25 December.

The meeting was scheduled to discuss the declaration of Palestinian Statehood in light of the current impasse on the Palestinian-Israeli track.

A PA communiqu� said the postponement was necessary in light of the “murky political situation” surrounding the prospects of negotiations with Israel on both the interim phase and final status talk levels.

The decision to postpone the meeting, which PA officials had said it would be decisive on the issue of Statehood, was probably taken because Arafat did not want to sustain another “Statehood-declaration fiasco,” particularly given the fact that an increasing number of PLO officials, who are utterly disenchanted by Israeli procrastination and intransigence, seem to be set on declaring Statehood now.

Palestinian Democracy “Flunks” a Consequential Test

By Ramzy Baroud

Every democratic experience has its moments of triumph and failure. Although we should be wary not to judge an entire experience based on one or two occurrences, we must not overlook those experiences that could serve as a tool to determine the success or failure of any democracy around the world. The recent Palestinian quarrel following the distribution of an anti-corruption leaflet is considered to be one of the Palestinian democracy’s greatest challenges since the signing of Oslo in 1993.

The importance of the leaflet incident is drawn from the fact that Arafat himself was the target of the signers’ criticism. Although PA president Yasser Arafat’s publicity throughout Palestine can hardly be denied, a legitimate democracy ought to open the stage for criticism, regardless of the position held by the scrutinized figure and regardless of the level of his popularity. Unfortunately, the PA’s swift reaction against those who chose to sign the leaflet failed to provide such legitimacy to the Palestinian democracy. Even though it is hasty to conclude that Palestinian democracy has failed, it is crucial to acknowledge its failure in meeting a vital provision in any truthful democracy, that being freedom of speech.

“The manifesto was incitement that went beyond the right of free speech”, Nabil Amr, a senior Arafat advisor, told the press while the issue was still developing. Mr. Amr justified this declaration stating that “the timing is sensitive”. Official Palestinian views are themselves alarming and ought to be reconsidered. First, the statement assumes that there is a boundary that constrains and limits freedom of speech. If such a boundary exists, who should be the one to determine its limits? From the hasty arrests that were made of those who signed the leaflet, the PA took it upon itself to ultimately determine what “exceeds” and what stays within the limits of freedom of speech.

Secondly, justifying any crackdown on freedom of speech, democracy and those who promote these rights by simply stating that “the timing is sensitive” should be rejected, as every period in the Palestinian struggle has been both sensitive and consequential. To accept Mr. Amr’s argument is simply to accept the disablement of the Palestinian democratic struggle for many years to come. Truthfully, there is no proof that freedom of speech has ever hindered any nation in its struggle to achieve liberation and obtain sovereignty.

My argument should not be understood as an approval of the content within the leaflet signed by 11 Palestinian intellectuals and 9 lawmakers. The argument is, however, that real democracy neither places anyone above the law nor eliminates those who dare to speak out, even if we assume the contortion of their logic. Democracy is about honesty, openness and self-determination. If every Palestinian feels that being honest will jeopardize his freedom, safety and perhaps livelihood, few are those who may risk speaking out in the future.

The statements made within the infamous leaflet were an expression of opinion. Their stand neither contained an act of treason nor a threat to Palestinian national unity. Those who signed it were well-known and respected Palestinian academics and activists. The fact that the document was presented by a considerable number of local leaders within Palestinian society indicates that their views must have been shared by some if not many Palestinians. The matter, therefore, is alarming and must be followed with deep examination. If corruption truly exists within the PA, the silencing of those who defied that corruption shall hinder the facts from surfacing. On the other hand, if no corruption is in fact taking place, the reaction of the PA against those who made such claims has weakened the PA’s stand and has strengthened the position asserted within the leaflet.

Instead of arresting and defaming those who made the allegations, the PA should have worked hand in hand with its opposition to find the truth, regardless of whom the truth pleases or whom it disturbs. Otherwise, the PA should have simply defended its policy and conduct and left the matter for the Palestinian people to decide for themselves. The feelings of endearment, which many Palestinians hold for PA President Yasser Arafat, must not violate the right of other fellow Palestinians to see Arafat within a different scope. Such a question contains, in fact, the true essence and meaning of democracy. If that fundamental quality in the democratic experience is diminished, what remains is a mere imitation of democracy, with no validity and with little efficiency.

Though the latest Palestinian dispute has proved that the current state of democracy in Palestine is in an unsatisfactory condition, the finding itself was truly valuable, for Palestinians are now more aware of their weaknesses than ever before. With that realization in mind, a tough task faces Palestinians in the coming years—the task of shaping their democratic experience to satisfy the needs of all, not only the needs of those who govern.

A Lawmaker, Not a Lawbreaker

By E. Yaghi

My name is Abdel Jawad and I am a 69-year-old Palestinian lawmaker living in the West Bank. On Thursday, 16 December, I was released from the hospital after treatment I received for being severely beaten by Palestinian police. The reason for my brutal beating was because I signed a manifesto holding Palestinian President Yasser Arafat guilty of government corruption.

My beating took place after I joined a peaceful protest outside a jail in the West Bank town of Jericho, where three intellectuals who also signed the same manifesto were being held without trial or charges.

Plainclothes policemen asked me to enter the prison compound and locked me inside a room where they severely beat me. Six or seven so-called “security” people started beating me, then they threw water on me and tossed me in the street. I collapsed and was taken by fellow demonstrators to the hospital. My body bears proof of the severity of my beating. I have bandages on my nose and right leg, am covered with bruises and also have lash marks on my arms and legs.

I am not a criminal; I am not a renegade. I am a lawmaker who was separated from my family for the greater part of my life. My children were raised alone by their mother and grew up without me. For a long time, the only contact I had with my wife and children was by phone because I was deported from my home by the Israelis to a neighbouring Arab country for speaking out against all the oppression and tyranny that my people suffered under the invaders’ occupation.

I could almost understand my deportation and that I was considered a threat (intellectual at that) by those people who had swept into Palestine and had taken my land away from me. But I cannot understand at all how members of the Palestine Authority could arrest me, beat me and throw me in the street for speaking out against the corruption of Yasser Arafat and some of his gang. They do not represent freedom and justice. They are merely a puppet regime acting as agents for their Israeli counterparts.

There have been corruption charges leveled against the Palestine Authority in the past, but allegations focused mainly on Cabinet ministers and never on Mr. President himself.

But my President, noble and kind as he is, lips trembling when he speaks and all, responded swiftly, arresting 11 of the signatories and threatening to lift the immunity from the rest of us unless we lawmakers retract our statements. Of course, we refused to do this. It is a matter of conscience and doing what is right. We stand for justice and legality. We cannot condone corruption, especially from the head of our dwindling disappearing country which now suffers under a double occupation.

Earlier this month, signatory Mwawya al-Masri, also a lawmaker, was shot in the leg by three hooded thugs outside his Nablus home. Al-Masri later confided to me that plainclothes Palestinian police shot him as punishment for signing the manifesto.

I leave it up to you, the readers, to decide the charges of corruption leveled against the single man who at one time was a symbol of what all Palestinians fought for and believed in. As far as I am concerned, he is a traitor to the very things that he once symbolized. I rest my case.

“War on children” is both an invention and legacy of the twentieth century. During the last decade, more than 1.5 million children have been killed in wars. Palestinian children have not been exempted from this scourge. Three percent of them under the age of 15 are disabled. Over 22,000 were injured by live ammunition during the 1987-1993 Intifadah. Although they now constitute over 50 percent of their own population, the suffering of Palestinian children under the age of 18 evoked no special mention at the celebrations marking the tenth anniversary of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child in November1999. Hence, to commemorate the thirty-fifth anniversary of the Palestinian Revolution, Dr. Daud Abdullah* dedicates:

A special tribute to the forgotten children

When the monthly magazine Our Palestine first began to circulate throughout the Arab world in late 1959, few readers took it seriously. Addressed principally to ‘The Children of the Catastrophe’, its stated purpose was to summon the Palestinian nation back to life. That life was, however, not an end in itself. It was desired only insofar as it enabled the Palestinian people to regain their land, freedom and dignity. New Year’s Day 1965 witnessed one of the most significant undertakings on this long road to national redemption.

Military Communiqu� No.1, which was slipped into the offices of several Beirut newspapers, announced the launch of armed operations inside the occupied territories and, with them, the Palestinian Revolution. Although it did not overturn an old regime in the classic manner as the Cuban Revolution, the Palestinian Revolution has, unquestionably, inscribed permanent changes upon the political map of the Middle East. It established Palestine as the key to a resolution of the Arab-Israeli conflict. And, while securing for them the freedom to organize and engage in political activity, it also restored Palestinian national pride and self-respect. Apathy, resignation and fear gave way to self-confidence and a firm resolution not to be refugees or strangers in the countries of others. One 18-year-old schoolboy spoke for the entire nation when he said, “The Revolution gave me the answer to who I am.”

That being the case, there still remain other crucial questions that cry out for answers. When will the long exile of the Palestinian people come to an end? When will The Children of the Catastrophe enjoy, without let or hindrance, the rights embodied in the 32 articles of the UN Convention? Furthermore, will the French for that matter ever set up a commission to facilitate the compensation of Palestinian children orphaned during the Nakba [Catastrophe] as their Justice Minister did last November for Jewish children orphaned during the holocaust?!

Despite their enormous and persistent losses, Palestinian children have yet given much to humanity. The images of the young stone-throwers being fired upon by Israeli soldiers in 1988-1989 must have, in retrospect, hastened the proclamation of the Convention on the Rights of the Child. Born for the most part after 1967, they have known nothing but foreign military occupation, sieges and curfews in the West Bank and Gaza Strip (WBGS). The result has been low educational achievements, a high dropout rate from schools, economic exploitation and an imposed dependence upon Israel.

Today’s victims

While they are being condemned to lives of misery and poverty in other people’s countries, Israeli settlers continue to grow rich with the land and property of the evicted Palestinians. At a time when schools are forever in short supply in the occupied territories, recreational facilities are virtually non-existent. In Bethlehem where the Blessed Child was born 2,000 years ago, there is not a single playground today. This, of course, is a crying shame and betrayal of civilized norms. It is disgraceful because better can be done and it is a betrayal because it constitutes a denial of rights affirmed in the November 1959 UN Declaration of the Rights of the Child. They are the rights to social security including adequate nutrition, housing, recreation, medical services, and protection from neglect, cruelty and exploitation.

The first and most striking impression one gets upon arriving in the Gaza Strip today is that it is a reservoir overflowing with children. Half of the one million Palestinians there are below the age of 15. Many of them suffer from anxiety, depression, post-traumatic stress disorder, stuttering, bed-wetting, insomnia, aggression and diminished concentration. The worst affected children are those who witnessed their fathers and elders being brutalized or whose homes were demolished. Despite its vaunted credentials to democracy, human rights and peace, the Barak government ordered the demolition of 70 Palestinian homes two days after signing the Sharm el-Sheikh Memorandum in October 1999. Thus, Palestinian children must still pay the heavy price so that Israel can preserve its record of being the only country in the world that systematically demolishes the homes of a section of its population.

The policy of house demolitions and land confiscation has naturally taken a heavy toll on Palestinian family life. Extended families are forced to live together in overcrowded conditions. It is, therefore, quite common to find 30 people residing in 3 bedroom houses. The Guidance and Training Centre for the Child and the Family, which provides counseling for residents of the West Bank and Jerusalem, confirmed that 80% of its clients are victims of political oppression experienced both before and during the Oslo peace process.

Tomorrow’s people

That the future of every nation lies in the hands of its children is a common truth that cannot be overstated. People of every race and creed invest invaluable time and resources in their young, with the hope that their future will not only be different but actually better than their past and present. While the challenges facing Palestinian children are by no means insurmountable, they are certainly formidable. Instead of being inspired by pleasurable childhood memories, they are slowed down and impeded at every turn by the permanent psychological injuries they sustained during years of confrontation with the occupier. Against this background, the British Member of Parliament, Mr. Richard Burden, told a House of Commons debate (30/11/99) on the Palestinian right to return that “half the refugees are children, who are growing up with an uncertain future.”

As long as the clouds of political uncertainty continue to hang over the WBGS, the rights of Palestinian children would remain constantly under threat. Yet, in the present circumstances, there is no definite answer as to who is responsible for protecting them. In theory, Article 22 of the Basic Law of Palestine states that: “Motherhood, childhood, the family, the young and the youth have the right to protection and to the availability of proper opportunities for the development of talents.” In reality, though, the Palestinian Authority (PA) has only limited control over parts of the WBGS. It does not have the full control over its borders that enables it to import medicine or transfer patients abroad for treatment if need be. Since only States Parties can ratify international conventions, the PA was only able to provide a verbal ratification of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child. Therefore, until these matters of sovereignty, borders and legislation are resolved, the rights of Palestinian children in the territories will remain dead letters on paper.

In the meanwhile, there is much that the PA can do by itself to improve the well-being of children and provide opportunities for the development of their talents. One obvious area is the reorganization of national priorities. Having resigned itself to a peaceful coexistence with the Jewish State, the PA cannot now by any stretch of the imagination justify the allocation of 36% of its budget to security—five times more than is currently being spent on health, social services and education combined. Surely the Children of the Catastrophe deserve better.

In hindsight, Camp Palestinians often give three causes for the Revolution: the conquest of Palestine and the establishment of a racially exclusive State in it; the dispersal of the Arab Palestinians from their homes; and the enduring tradition of struggle to regain independence on their land. A school principal in Burj el-Barajneh Camp in Lebanon recalled, “When the Revolution came, those who reacted to it most were students in the intermediate classes; they joined the Revolution, and supported it.”1 As the schools became training camps, so too did the teacher-student relationship change. Within a very short period, the former was called upon to interpret political events, organize and provide direction for children who sought deliverance from sinister attempts to bury their national existence.

The politicization of The Children of the Catastrophe was neither limited to a particular time nor locality. From the camps of the diaspora in the 1970s, this consciousness was transferred to the occupied territories in the 1980s. Wherever they were, The Children of the Catastrophe acquired a rare ability to see through attempts to deceive and weaken them. In the WBGS, they realized that the beating of their fathers and uncles was not simply to cause physical pain. They learned from bitter experience that the real aim of the Israeli occupier is always to undermine the authority and position of fathers in their families by humiliating them in front of their children, thereby weakening the social fabric and cohesiveness of Palestinian society. Although the policy did prove effective in some cases, it has, on the whole, failed to work entirely to the advantage of Israel.

As it stands, the Jewish State has failed to change the widespread perception of itself as an occupying power. Its growing campaign within the United Nations to become a part of the West European regional group and recent attendance at the Istanbul Summit of the Organization for Security and Cooperation in Europe have reinforced Israel’s image as a Western surrogate in the Arab world. When added to the incendiary elements of political repression, economic deprivation and social exclusion, the question is not if, but rather when will the next upheaval take place.

Having long shattered the barriers of fear, The Children of the Catastrophe are, without doubt, Palestine’s greatest asset. Tested and pushed to the limits many times in the past, they have proven themselves stronger in some ways than their heavily armed adversaries. Surely, “It takes much more courage for a child armed with stones to confront gun-toting troops than it does for those troops to confront children.”2 Forgotten by many and remembered by few, they still symbolize the life and destiny of the Palestinian nation.

* The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and editor of its Return Review.

PA’s Mafia

By Shaaban Abdel Rahman

What is dangerous in the scandals of corruption within the Palestinian Authority is not the disappearance of hundreds of millions of dollars into the pockets of the PA officials, but it is that we have discovered that this is an organization which dedicates itself to sucking the blood of the Palestinian People and to denying them the simplest of human rights.

This organization we are referring to is speaking on behalf of the Palestinian People, negotiating on their behalf, and it is supposed, in the near future, to sign the so-called final peace settlement of the Palestinian Cause with the Zionist regime. Have those who failed in the “honesty” examination regarding some million of dollars a right to be entrusted with resolving the most dangerous issue for the Arabs and Muslims?!

The other face of the picture exposes the reality behind enabling the PA—a trust given by all Arab parties concerned with the peace process—to talk and sign on behalf of the Palestinian People as their sole legitimate representative, even though it has proven itself to be dishonest and without conscience. Can the United States, which sponsors the peace process, and Israel find an easier entity to relinquish the rights of the Palestinian People than the PA at present?

This time, the people who exposed the corruption and thefts within the self-rule Authority were not from the Palestinian Opposition but from the PA itself, and some of them are senior officials and very influential inside the Authority. This in itself proves that the PA is deteriorating from within. However, further proof of the PA’s deterioration was clearly exhibited when Chairman Arafat got fed up with those who distributed a leaflet (also known as Petition-20) exposing the PA’s corruption and consequently ordered the arrest of some of them, put some others under house arrest and threatened to strip the members of the Legislative Council of their immunity.

What we know is that serious and honest liberation movements should always do their best to clean their ranks and to accept the self-criticism in order to obtain credibility among their supporters and people. They should also use all means to be close to their people in order to be strong against the enemy and strong round the table of negotiations. But this is not the case with the Palestinian Authority.

How can such an authority deal with the Zionist enemy and negotiate with it while it is accused by its people of theft and embezzlement of more than $1 billion from foreign contributions to the Palestinian People? It seems that the PA has not left any of this money to its people!

It is surprising that Arafat himself was accused by a member of the Legislative Council and former minister, namely Abdel Jawad Saleh, of withdrawing $200 million from the PA’s budget and depositing it into his secret accounts. He is also accused by Jawid Ghusain, the former chief of the PLO’s National Fund, of depositing millions of PLO dollars into his secret accounts in Israeli banks!

Isn’t this authority giving the Zionists a rare and historic chance to do whatever they want regarding the Palestinian Question?

Can we also understand why all the evil powers are now allies in the bid to get rid of Hamas?

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

By Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 4

Jewish Fundamentalism Within Jewish Society

The issue of normalcy most divides the Israeli right from the left. The left longs for normalcy and wants Jews to be a nation like all other nations. The entire Israeli right, on the other hand, is united in its resentment of the idea of normalcy and its belief, along the lines of the Jewish religion5 that Jews are exceptional - different from other people and nations. Reverence for the national past allegedly solidifies this uniqueness. Religious Jews believe that God made the Jews unique3 many of the secular right believe that Jews are doomed to be unique by their past and have no free choice in this matter.

Another, but somewhat less important3 reason for the affinity between the secular right and religious Jews is that the latter are capable of providing "convincing" arguments for perpetual Jewish rule over the land of Israel and for the denial of certain basic rights to the Palestinians. These arguments are not only put in terms of national security but more importantly in terms of the God-given right to these territories. The secular Likud scholars and politicians are often far too alienated from the Jewish past and Jewish values to talk competently, or indeed even to understand properly, such matters. Only the religious can provide an in-depth rationale for Likud's policies, which are grounded not in short-term strategic considerations but rather in the long history of the special relationship between God and his chosen people.

Although far more intense among members of Israel B, these same sentiments can be discerned among members of Israel A. This fact provides the explanation for the political concessions made to the religions parties. (Foreign observers have too often incorrectly attributed these concessions merely to the size and/or the lobbying power of the religious parties.) These sentiments have also affected Jewish historiography and education. Since the late 1950s, and especially after the 1967 war, Israeli Jewish historians, scholars in allied fields and popularizers, although generally less dishonest in their writings than most of their diaspora colleagues, have too often unduly beautified and romanticized past Jewish societies and have carefully avoided normal criticism. This type of apologia constituted a new trend. From the late nineteenth century until the mid-twentieth century, early Zionists and others in modem Jewish movements were severely critical of many aspects of their own religious cultural tradition and tried to change, in many cases even to destroy, parts of that tradition. Since the late 1980s, some younger Israeli historians, perhaps prompted by a growing polarization of Israeli Jewish society, have written and published some critical works that have shaken to some extent the still current apologetic trend.

The comparison of the world outlook and fears of the secular right with those of the Haredim requires more explanation. Standard Haredic perceptions of the world can only be understood as relics of pre-modern times. Menachem Friedman, a Westernized observant Jew, a highly regarded authority on the Haredim in both mandatory Palestine and the state of Israel and a professor at the religious Bar-Ilan University, provided an excellent description of these Haredic perceptions in a Davar article published on November 4, 1988. Friedman wrote this article to explain the electoral fiasco that developed from the unsuccessful attempt of some candidates on the religious list of 1988 to advocate some moderation regarding the treatment of Palestinians. Friedman explained:

The Haredi world is Judeocentric. The essence of Haredi thought is the notion of an abyss separating the Jews from the Gentiles. This is why any coalition between Labor and Haredi doves is impossible. There actually is no such thing as a Haredi dove. People who speak about the Haredi world usually do not know how to read its signs. They do not understand that world nor its prominent personalities. The distance between Haredi doves and hawks is not great. Haredi doves and hawks share a common point of departure. Both see the relationship between non-Jews and Jews as they had seen them before Israel was established. They assume that non-Jews and Jews are poles apart. Non-Jews want to kill and destroy the Jews; the rightful differences between Jews should only be about how they should react to the ever-present non-Jewish desire. Currently, these are two alternative Haredi reactions to that common assumption. Rabbi Shach [the spiritual leader of one of the two Haredi factions] says that since the non-Jews hate us we need to keep quiet and refrain from provoking them by not reminding them of our existence. The Lubovitcher Rebbe says that we should be strong. [The Lubovitcher Rebbe, Rabbi Menachem Schneerson, died in 1992.] Those are two alternative answers, both arising from the common concept that a gap separates Jews from non-Jews. Rabbi Shach is not a dove in the same sense as Shulamit Aloni [a former Meretz Party leader] is a dove. Aloni is a dove, because she believes in a humanism that emphasizes the fundamental equality of all human beings and nations and the capability of different human beings and nations to communicate. Rabbi Shach believes that communicating with non-Jews is not possible and that they may only be able to forget that Jews exist. The Lubovitcher Rebbe states that we should be strong in order to defend ourselves against the non-Jews who always want to destroy us. [The difference between the two leaders] can be illustrated by their respective attitudes toward the peace [treaty] with Egypt. They both say that there is no peace and there can never be one, because the Egyptians want to exterminate us. Rabbi Shach, however, adds that we should try to minimize [Jewish casualties] by keeping quiet. The Lubovitcher Rebbe says that, because the peace does not exist in any case, we should refuse to make any concessions. The Haredi dove does not believe in any kind of peace, and, therefore, all the talk about a narrow coalition, headed by Labor [and including Haredim] is completely baseless. Subsequent political developments in Israel, including the election of Netanyahu in May 1996, have confirmed the truth of Professor Friedman's analysis. From another Haredi perspective Rabbi Ovadia Yoseph, the spiritual authority of the Shas Party, corroborated this article. In raising his 16-year-old son Talal, Ahmad wants to keep alive the family dream of returning to Palestine. “I want my son to know about my culture. I want him to know about the village where his grandfather grew up. I want to pass this on to him.”

Ahmad waits and hopes for a breakthrough in the peace process, looking for signs that the unimaginable could become reality.

In a speech given to the United Nations General Assembly in September, Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat stressed that the right of the four million Palestinian refugees to return to their homeland needed to be addressed in final status talks with Israel. The two sides hope to reach an agreement by next September, when Arafat aspires to attend the UN Millennium Summit as president of an independent Palestine.

Following a 1 November meeting between Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak and Arafat in Oslo, Norway—birthplace of the current peace process—a fresh round of talks opened up recently in the West Bank town of Ramallah. The two sides hope to agree to a timetable for negotiations over the next ten months concerning the future of Palestinian refugees, Jewish settlements and Jerusalem.

In the fall of 1948, Ahmad’s parents, older siblings and dozens of other relatives were told at gunpoint to leave their homes and were driven north to the Lebanese border by Israeli soldiers. Emptying 450 such Palestinian villages was part of the Israeli reaction to the attack on Jewish settlements by the armies of Egypt, Transjordan, Syria, Lebanon and Iraq earlier in the year when Israel announced its independence. The young nation was putting into motion UN resolution 181, which called for the partition of Palestine into two States: one Arab and one Jewish. That 1947 resolution also stated, however, that Meirun village was to lie within the new Arab State of Palestine, a State which Israel never allowed to form.

Growing up in exile meant many years of poverty, uncertainty and fear for the Kawashes. Very little of the family fortune, tied to the land and its fertility, made it out of Palestine. As Ahmad grew older, what he had thought of as “a normal life” began to seem strange as he noticed discrepancies between his family’s sparse surroundings in the Mia Mia refugee camp and the more comfortable homes of native Lebanese living in the nearby town of Saidon. He grew up in hand-me-down clothes, without toys, and for many years lived in a house made of clay.

After the Six Day War in 1967, the camp came under more frequent air attacks from Israel. PLO recruitment and guerilla activity, which many in Ahmad’s family were involved in, increased. “They bombed my friend’s house. They bombed my neighbour’s house. You always felt fear, never knowing when an F-16 was going to appear in the sky and drop bombs on the camp.”

Eventually, as family members found employment in the West, the Gulf and elsewhere and sent remittances home, the family’s living conditions improved. The increased stability allowed the Kawashes new opportunities, and Ahmad embarked on an American education in 1978 as Lebanon’s implosive 15-year civil war was heating up.

At the University of Mississippi—where he studied civil engineering before transferring to Northeastern University in Boston—Ahmad developed a passion for cooking in an attempt to bring the missed tastes of home to fellow Arab student friends. “We were sick of eating hamburgers and sponge bread,” he says. After finding a can of dough which he managed to turn into bread which “wasn’t 100 percent, but closer to home,” Ahmad soon developed a reputation for good cooking among his friends.

Like most of his friends, Ahmad was determined to return to Lebanon after graduating. Israel’s invasion of Lebanon in 1982, when Ahmad earned his engineering degree, helped him decide that staying in the United States and earning money and “proper identification documents” was the best option for the time being. Without a green card, Ahmad opted to find work in the food service industry, where personal records are not as stringently checked. Since then, he has owned, leased, or worked in various restaurants and delis, most recently serving the fare of his homeland in Moody’s Falafel Palace and the Middle East Caf�, both in Central Square, Cambridge.

“When I cook, its like I’m in a different world. It releases a lot of stress. I enjoy serving people, and when they tell me they like my cooking. I want to have my own diner—it’s good money. The food business lets you interact with people, that’s what I really like,” he says. Ahmad also hopes to start a cooking show on the Arab Broadcasting Corporation, with an emphasis on his “healthier” recipes for often-fatty Middle Eastern dishes.

As American living became more routine and comfortable, Ahmad’s desire to return home lessened, and he obtained his U.S. citizenship in 1992. He continues to visit the Middle East regularly though. “You could even say that getting an American passport was more important than the degree,” he says. He describes the document issued to him in Lebanon that allows him to travel, though labels him a Palestinian refugee, as “a curse. Wherever you go, the assumption is you are a terrorist.”

When Ahmad learned that the landmark peace agreement signed by President Arafat and Israeli Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin on the White House lawn in 1993 would not directly address Galilee, he says he felt betrayed. “It was those of us from Galilee, from northern Palestine, my uncles and cousins, who were killed, who were involved in the revolution from the outset,” he says. “It didn’t even mention us. They were talking about the West Bank and Gaza. We gave a lot, but so far we have received nothing.”

As he talks on this rainy evening, a thermos of day-old Turkish coffee sits, aging, on Ahmad’s low coffee table, which sits atop the dark, intricate patterns of an Oriental rug. He says that letting the coffee “ferment” for a few days lets its flavor fully develop.

Time, Ahmad says, is the most crucial factor in the tortuous road to peace in his homeland and the rest of the Middle East. “I was the most radical person until two years ago. I used to say, ‘It’s either us or them.’ But you have to be realistic. I know there will never be a perfect solution, either for us or for them. We have to have some sort of compromise.”

Ahmad pours two cups of the coffee into two classically small cups and warns not to drink it too quickly. “You have to let it settle,” he says.

Family pictures, PLO posters of soldiers and revolutionary slogans, and artwork featuring Islamic holy sites in Mecca and Jerusalem cover much of the wall space in his modest, dimly lit Cambridge apartment. Prominently centred on one wall is a large childhood photograph of Talal, his only child.

Ahmad won custody of his son after the break-up of his short-lived marriage to an American woman he met while studying at Northeastern.

“In the Middle East, we are very attached to the children we bring into this world,” he says. Talal is named after Ahmad’s eldest brother—a regular visitor to the apartment—who is in turn named after his grandfather. “I named my son Talal in honour of my eldest brother, who didn’t have any sons,” he explains.

After two decades of living in America and his entire life in exile, Ahmad keeps faithful to his dream of going to Meirun, and building a life there. He hopes to build a villa on his ancestral homeland and hire hands to cultivate the land, or perhaps open a guesthouse. “If not me, then my children or grandchildren. I have not been there, but I have a spiritual connection to the land,” he says.

“Time is the most important factor. Somehow, I am optimistic.”

The Islamic Movement Inside The Green Line:

The Contributions Of Shaykh Ra’ed Salah

Dr. Ibrahim M. Abu Rabi’

There were many casualties in 1948, and only one tragedy. One of the direct casualties of the Palestinian tragedy, which lies at the heart of the Arab-Israeli conflict, has been the oft-forgotten Palestinian community inside the Green Line (the Israeli Arabs). Almost overnight, it became a minority in its ancestral homeland. Since 1948, this community has struggled to recapture its lost identity and define a new sense of meaning for its existence vis-�-vis an aggressive Zionist project that successfully built the strongest military State in the Middle East, mobilized millions of world Jewry either to immigrate or to support financially and morally the newly created Israeli State, and gained a distinctive international and military presence.

The Palestinians use the term nakba or disaster to refer to the 1948 defeat. On the other hand, the Israelis, and with them many Jewish and Christian theologians in the West, hail their victory in 1948 as a divine intervention on behalf of an exiled nation returning to its ancestral land after more than 2,000 years of exile and alienation. The Israeli victory in 1948 cannot be understood apart from the pain and suffering of the Palestinian people. Israel did not build its institutions and infrastructure in a vacuum, but in the shadow of a tragic Palestinian memory, and on the shoulders of the few remaining Palestinians who were spared physical exile.

The most subtle form of exile is cultural and national loss and confusion. Different factors tend to exasperate this exile, most notably the flight of the indigenous intelligentsia and the noise of an approaching foreign culture with indefinite hegemonic possibilities. After 1948, the remaining Palestinian community underwent a traumatic experience and suffered from the pain of separation and solitude. It has taken it several decades to come to terms with this separation; yet, the wounds are still fresh and the challenges are so many.

One of the major challenges facing the Palestinian community in Israel (it stands a little bit over a million people in 1999) has been to preserve its homogeneous cultural and national identity, especially as Israel has followed a formal policy of dividing the Arabs into Muslims, Christian, Druze and Bedouin. This division, which does not follow any consistent rules, further subdivided the Palestinian community into competing tribes, clans and families, which even the so-called Israeli Left uses when referring to the Palestinian community inside the Green Line. The divide-and-rule policy was also enhanced by a more conspicuous policy of confiscating Arab land (never a Jewish land), and forcing some members of the community to serve in the Israeli army without any consideration for their religious or national feelings as Arabs. The “Law of Return” promulgated by the Knesset in 1951 gives every Jew in the world the right to “return” to Israel, while the Palestinians cannot enjoy this right.

The period between 1948 and 1967 was particularly bleak for the Palestinian community inside the Green Line. It was totally cut off and insulated from the rest of Palestinian society and the Arab and Muslim world, and its deep wounds and silent anguish were known only to their family members in exile. In this period, the Palestinian community faced military rule, land confiscation, and a major social and economic upheaval. The nascent Israeli State, with a flux of immigrants and Western money, was in the process of building its infrastructure for which a large labour force was needed. Its economy was flourishing thanks to the efforts of the international Zionist movement and the financial and military support of the Western world. Overnight, the Israeli economy found it feasible to convert the Arabs from farmers into labourers working for the huge Israeli markets. This was made easy by the fact that large areas were confiscated from the Palestinian community.

In the absence of the traditional Palestinian intelligentsia, and under the impact of the secularized Israeli State, the Israeli Communist Party began to play a major role in the political and cultural life of the Palestinian community. From the beginning, and under pressure from Moscow, the Communist Party recognized Israel and hoped that Israel would become a pro-Soviet country in the Middle East.

Although Israel did not turn out to be a pro-Soviet State in the Middle East, the Israeli Communist Party exerted much effort trying to build political support in the heart of the Palestinian community. The success of the Party was owing to several political and social factors and to the efforts of the so-called resistance poets, such as Mahmoud Darwish, Tawfiq Zayyad, Salem Jubran, and the literary critic Emile Habibe, who shaped the cultural policies of the Israeli Communist Party. With Soviet and East European help, the Communist Party sponsored the university education of a good number of Palestinian students, some of whom returned to assume leadership roles in different sectors of Palestinian society. All this indicates that between 1948 and 1967 a process was under way to create a new kind of intelligentsia, secular by nature, to represent the Palestinian community.

But things began to change dramatically after 1967. The Arab defeat in the war and the subsequent Israeli occupation of Arab land—the Golan Heights, the West Bank, the Gaza Strip and the Sinai—have had major repercussions on the whole area. Aside from its deep political and military consequences, the 1967 war reunited, ironically enough, the lost Palestinian community inside the Green Line with its brothers and sisters on the West Bank and in Gaza. “Reunification under occupation” is a phenomenal paradox with deep implications. In the long run, it helped to diminish the power of the Israeli Communist Party and give rise to nationalist and Islamic elements within the Palestinian community.

The rise of the Islamic movement inside the Green Line, a phenomenon unheard of before 1967, represented the most serious challenge to the hegemony of the Israeli Communist Party over the Arab masses in Israel, and the most rigorous response to the cultural and political policies of the Israeli State. Some considered the rise of the Islamic movement on the political and cultural scene to be a fading phenomenon. Its rise after 1967, however, can be ascribed to the following main reasons: first and foremost is the “reunification” of the Palestinians under the Israeli occupation and the resumption of cultural and social activities between the Palestinians of the Green Line and those of the occupied West Bank and Gaza. The second reason relates to the rise of Islamic activism in both Gaza and the West Bank and its impact on the Palestinians inside. One inevitable result of the Israeli occupation was that many Palestinians from inside the Green Line began to seek religious education in different West Bank universities and Shari’ah colleges. This situation gave birth to a new, young and religiously committed leadership which capitalized on the religious feelings of the masses and offered a new interpretation of Islam to suit the particular needs of the Palestinians in Israel. The net result of all of this was the gradual weakening of the hold of the Communist Party over the Palestinians.

What is interesting, however, is that most of the leadership of the new Islamic movement come from a rural background; they are from the Triangle area, an area that was handed over by King Abdullah of Jordan to Israel in 1949. Bereft of any sort of Palestinian leadership inside, the Palestinian community worked hard to create its own leadership, which took at least three decades after 1948. The most difficult task confronting the Islamic movement has been how to bring back confidence to a wounded nation suffering from exile and alienation in its homeland.

To embody its vision of a dynamic Islamic presence inside the borders of 1948, the Islamic movement began to build its own mosques and Islamic centres in the main Arab villages and towns. From there, the movement began to draw large masses into its religious and political activities. As a result, the Communist Party gradually lost its ground of support among the Arab, especially Muslim, masses in Israel. Two major events after 1967 underscore the emergence of the Islamic movement as a major mass organization: the Land Day of 1976 and the Palestinian intifadah, which began in Gaza and the West Bank in 1987.

The rise of the Islamic movement as a credible religious voice representing the downtrodden and the dispossessed was not without its difficulties and problems. At the present, there are two Islamic movements representing two different Islamic discourses or approaches to deal with the problems of the Arab community in Israel. The first is represented by Sheikh ‘Abdalla Nimr Darwish of Kufr Qasim, and the second by Sheikh Ra’ed Salah, the mayor of the city of Umm al-Fahem, both in the Triangle. Sheikh Darwish’s Islamic philosophy is somewhat different from Sheikh Ra’ed Salah’s. Darwish prefers to run in the Knesset elections as a way to deal with the problems of the Palestinian community, while Sheikh Salah looks at the whole in a much wider sense. It is important, he notes in one of his interviews, to rebuild fresh connections between the Palestinian community and the larger Arab and Muslim realities of the world. From this angle, it is important to build alliances with the Islamic movement in different parts of the Muslim world, in Egypt, Turkey, Jordan and India. The main bone of contention between the two movements is not simply running in the Israeli elections, but the whole question of Islamic identity and the best ways to preserve and enhance it in a totally secularized and antagonistic context.

Max Weber speaks of charisma as the main reason behind the rise and progress of social movements throughout human history. Ra’ed Salah, a charismatic, humble and quiet personality, represents the aspirations of the Palestinian generation that was born after 1948. The Islamic movement led by him and his deputy, Sheikh Kamal al-Khatib of the village of Kufr Kanna, north of Nazareth, has emerged in recent years as the most outspoken movement with deep grass-roots organization and following.

In an interview I conducted with Sheikh Ra’ed Salah in Nazareth in February 1999, he gave a number of reasons for the rise of Islamic activism in Israel in the early 1970s. For many years after the foundation of Israel, argues Sheikh Salah, the Muslim youth, under pressure from Israeli society, lived in a state of jahiliya (ignorance), with alcoholism and drug use on the rise. The whole community experienced a loss of identity, and it took a while for a new leadership to emerge and offer a programme to diminish the real reasons behind this loss. What lies at the heart of this Islamic programme of action is the Islamic notion of authenticity or asalah, which expresses the need for a cultural link with the Islamic civilization and the larger Arab and Muslim world.

The Islamic movement has set up two major committees: the first is the committee for the defense of the Aqsa mosque and the Islamic sacred places, and the second is a charitable committee to raise money for the needy and the refugees. In the current Kosovo crisis, the Islamic movement led by Sheikh Ra’ed Salah sent a medical team to Albania and collected over $1 million on behalf of the Albanian refugees from Kosovo. Aware of the importance of journalism, the Islamic movement sponsors a weekly newspaper, Sawt al-Haq wa’l Hurriyya, which deals with local news, analysis and fiqh lessons.

Based on its recent activities, which attract people by the thousands, the movement has become very popular since it carries the pain and concerns of the Palestinian masses inside the Green Line. It speaks the language of a suffering community. In the mind of the movement, the Palestinians inside the Green Line are part of the larger Arab and Muslim world, and the official division adopted by the State of Israel since 1948 has intended to take advantage of a weakened Palestinian body.

Constructing the infrastructure of the Islamic movement is only half the job. The other half is enhancing the Islamic identity of the movement and the Palestinians in a country, i.e. Israel, that has fought relentlessly to establish its foundations in derogation of Palestinian and Arab rights. Furthermore, the Islamic movement, as an anti-Zionist movement, struggles deeply against the confiscation of land inside the Green Line and the suffocation of the Palestinians in the occupied land. No wonder, then, that the official Israeli media has considered the movement as a reactionary, confrontational and fundamentalist movement seeking to dismantle the State of Israel.

The Islamic movement in Israel has shouldered its responsibilities with rare courage and deep vision. Its leadership is young and dynamic, and it represents a very young population, most of whom were born after 1948. The challenges, however, are great. It struggles to leave its mark on the Arab population in a State that technically is not theirs. The right wing often proclaims that Israel is a land for the Jews, and it is high time that the Arab guests leave. The Islamic movement is under the watchful eyes of the Israeli authorities, and for good reason. It does not buy into the Zionist philosophy of the State.

The Islamic movement was born in difficult times, and its survival depends on the creative solutions it finds to the difficult problems facing the growing Palestinian minority in Israeli society. The State of Israel is determined to continue the divide-and-rule and confiscation policies until it completes the Judaization of Eretz Yisrael. Tough challenges create tough movements. The Islamic movement inside the Green Line can survive, however, only with the thorough support of the masses inside as well as outside the Green Line.
