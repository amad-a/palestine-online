Palestinians unimpressed by Barak’s victory

From Khalid Amayreh in Hebron (West Bank)

Caution, ambivalence and a certain degree of apathy have characterized Palestinian reactions to the election of Isaeli Labor Party leader Ehud Barak as the Jewish State’s Prime Minister.

Indeed, while the Palestinian Authority (PA) expressed relief at Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanayhu’s political downfall, very few ordinary Palestinians lauded Ehud Barak’s triumph, suggesting that Palestinians do not see any real difference between Netanyahu and Barak on fundamental issues pertaining to the Palestinian-Israeli strife.

PA Chairman Yasser Arafat said in a terse statement after it became clear that Barak was poised to become Prime Minister that he hoped the new Labor-led government would recommit Israel to the cause of peace.

On 18 May, Arafat called Barak to convey his congratulations to him on his electoral victory.

The PA Chairman, however, did not appear outspoken in his praise for the new change in Israel, which suggests that he did not think the downfall of Netanyahu and the triumph of Barak was going to trigger a genuine breakthrough in the peace process.

However, Barak’s recalcitrant statements during his election victory speech at dawn on 18 May, in which he made it clear that he would not offer the Palestinians any fundamental concessions on the West Bank, apparently dampened PA expectations of the new Israeli premier.

Barak’s hard-line speech also ostensibly made PA officials modify and somewhat qualify their erstwhile disproportionate praise for the new Israeli leader.

(Barak said he would observe four red lines concerning the peace process with the Palestinians, including that of Jerusalem remaining under Israeli sovereignty, no return to the 1967 borders, no dismantling of Jewish settlements, and not allowing any “foreign” armies west of the River Jordan.)

Freih Abu Maddin, the PA minister of justice, reacted to Barak’s “set of Nos”, describing them as “dangerous”, “coming too soon”, and “auguring very bad for the future.”

“One would understand such statements during the election campaign, but repeating them now is dangerous and unacceptable.”

Abu Maddin said it was clear that the declared stance of Barak was a “non-starter” for the resumption of the peace process, adding that “no Palestinian could ever come to terms with such conditions”, which he said, “were a mere carbon copy of Netanyahu’s policies and positions.”

Other PA officials, who probably could not resist or hide their relief at the political disappearance of Netanyahu, initially cordially welcomed the new “political revolution” in Israel.

Yasser Abed Rabbu, the PA minister of Culture and Information, opined that the defeat of Netanyahu was a defeat for “all morbid rightist ideas” and “a clear proof that the Israeli people are opting for peace.”

“The fact that Netanyahu is gone is in itself a reason for optimism,” said Abed Rabbu.

Abed Rabbu made his somewhat euphoric remarks before Barak’s election victory speech at Rabin Square in Tel Aviv on 18 May.

Legislative Council speaker Ahmed Qrei also spoke along the same lines, arguing that Barak would set the peace process back on track.

Qrei, perhaps out of ignorance of Barak and the Labor Party, dismissed Barak’s statements as “post-election rhetoric that ought not to be taken seriously.”

However, the overall tone of the Palestinians’ official reactions suggested that the PA was more relieved by Netanyahu’s downfall than heartened by Barak’s victory.

This was apparent in the tone of PA official Sa’eb Ereikat, who called Barak’s avowed red lines “unacceptable” since they are themselves “Palestinian red lines”.

Unlike other PA leaders, who seemed to be getting out of their skins to laud the former Israeli general, Ereikat opined that “we must wait to see Barak’s actions, not just his words.”

“Barak must demonstrate that he is committed to the peace process,” said Ereikat.

“This means that before going to the final status talks, he will have to carry out the remaining phase of the Wye River memorandum, release Palestinian prisoners, open the safe crossing between the West Bank and Gaza Strip, and open the seaport in Gaza…and above all, order an immediate halt to the settlement expansion, confiscation of land and demolition of houses.”

It is unlikely that Barak, who had voiced serious reservations about the original Oslo Accords, will hasten to carry out this long list of agreements just to prove to the PA that he is serious about peace.

The former general may well argue that even Rabin and Peres did not go that far, and that there was no reason that he should go any farther.

Islamist reactions
For its part, the Islamist opposition viewed Barak’s “recalcitrant statements” as a vindication of the Islamists’ long-standing position that Barak and Netanyahu were merely two sides of the same coin as far as Palestinian rights were concerned.

“It is true that Barak, when compared to Netanyahu, may look the lesser of the two evils to many Palestinians, but it would be na�ve and dangerous to give him the benefit of the doubt since all Zionist faces are hostile to our rights,” said Sheikh Yasin in a telephone interview from his home in Gaza.

He indicated though that he was worried that Barak might succeed in cajoling some Arab countries, especially in the Gulf region, to resume the normalization process with Israel, virtually at no price, as Shimon Peres had done, rather successfully.

Hence, he urged the Palestinian leadership as well as Arab regimes “not to be deceived again by a new illusion called Barak.”

Barak and Netanyahu, he said, were like tweedledum and tweedledee, and Barak’s positions were not really different from those of Netanyahu.

A communiqu� issued by the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) described Barak as a new professional terrorist. “The victory of the terrorist Ehud Barak has never and will never change anything about the nature of the Zionist project which is based on expansion, hegemony, and oppression. This was asserted by Barak more than once during his election campaign which marketed a platform similar to the Likud’s one without any political differences. This platform does not change the hostile and aggressive nature of the Zionist society against our people and nation,” Hamas said.

Ordinary Palestinians generally welcomed Netanyahu’s defeat but greeted Barak’s victory with caution and a great deal of skepticism.

This circumspect posture stems mainly from the realization on the part of most Palestinians that it was the Labor Party that first adopted the policy of implanting Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

“We should not go into rhapsody welcoming Barak, whose hands are stained with our blood and who still denies us the right to freedom and self-determination,” said a Bethlehem cab driver, who added with a whiff of cynicism that “if our leadership thinks he is the long-awaited lark of peace, then we may as well wait for more years to witness his wonderful works.”

The Israeli Elections: Lessons and Options

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

Apart from a few enthusiasts, most observers in the Arab world of the recent Israeli elections are not optimistic. Senior Palestinian Authority (PA) officials, including Nabil Sha’at and Hanan Ashrawi, were clearly cautious in their assessment of the prospects of peace under Barak. It is widely accepted that the change the recent Israeli election has brought and promises to bring to the Knesset and to Israeli political life in general is not the outcome of a shift in Israeli public opinion in favour of a more lenient negotiating position vis-�-vis the PA. Such a change is more likely to be the outcome of a referendum over Benyamin Netanyahu’s character, attitude and domestic policy. If any external issues were of significance to the Israeli electorate, these might have included the Israeli occupation of south Lebanon and the deteriorating relations between Israel and the United States of America.

This is of course not to say that Israeli Prime Minister elect Barak will not seek to resume the extremely stalled negotiations with the Palestinians. He will definitely seek to do so, as this is one of his electoral promises. But he will not be under any public pressure, compared to that generated by the growing passion against keeping troops in South Lebanon, to offer any significant concessions to the Palestinians. At least on all major issues, such as Jerusalem, the settlements, the borders and the refugees, Barak maintains an almost identical position as his predecessor. His position is almost identical because while maintaining things ‘mostly’ as they are, Barak may be more capable than Netanyahu of making marginal face-saving gestures at the PA. These may include additional, very limited re-deployment in exchange for more security guarantees, the removal of some insignificant settlement posts, such as those established recently by settlers from nearby officially recognized and government-sponsored settlements, and permitting Orient House to continue functioning in the eastern part of the Holy City.

The recent Israeli elections had, however, been very enlightening. On the one hand they shed light on the unique domestic situation of this society of immigrant nations. This particular election has exposed more than any previous one the growing rifts along ethnic and ideological divides. Apart from the Arab minority, which is indeed a sizeable and potentially influential minority, Israelis can roughly be said to belong to three major Zionist camps: the secularist left, the religious right and the utilitarian Russian.

The first camp, which in this election was represented by Barak’s “One Israel”, is the one that identifies most with the original Zionist ideology. This camp is an extension of the European, socialist and secularist pioneers who established the Jewish State in Palestine more than 50 years ago. Religion for the affiliates of this camp is less than secondary, perhaps no more than a tool used when some form of metaphysical justification of oppression is needed. A mix of Jewish nationalism, socialism and secularism is what constitutes the backbone of this camp.

The second camp comprises the religious Jews, both Sephardis and Ashkenazis, for whom classic Jewish doctrines, interpreted to serve the ambitions of modern Zionism, take precedence over all other considerations. The leaders of this camp go as far as claiming to speak in the name of the Lord, and would therefore have no hesitation to object to the ‘norms’ of Israel’s democracy if and when they are found to clash with the wishes of the Divine. When Deri, leader of the Sephardic ultra-Orthodox Shas party, was indicted by an Israeli court for corruption, a Shas grand Rabbi proclaimed him innocent in the eyes of God despite having been found guilty by a court of law.

The third camp, which comprises what has become known as the Russians, is a club to which belong those who came from the former Soviet Union. Most of these immigrants believe that Israel is not the promised land but a transit station to the real promised land in America. These Israelis have no ideological ties with the Jewish State to which they were forced to migrate after the United States shut its doors in their faces in the early 1990s. Relying on their electoral power, the Russians seek to serve the interests of their community by forging alliances with whoever promises to deliver and is capable of doing so. In the 1996 election, their votes carried Netanyahu to the premiership; in the most recent one, they favoured Barak. Their eyes are set on the Interior Ministry, which had been the prerogative of their foes in Shas.

In the midst of all of this, the 1948 Palestinians, commonly referred to as the Israeli Arabs, have emerged as a political power to reckon with. With ten seats in the Knesset, the Arabs may be invited to join a coalition government. They may not find it opportune yet to do so this time, but it is definitely a significant step along the path of empowerment. They may even be found to be an important player at some future stage when their votes in the Knesset may help bring down a government in crisis. The exercise undertaken by Dr. Azmi Bishara is no less significant. He, or another Arab candidate, may in a future election be capable of forcing a second round of elections, the aversion of which would necessitate conceding some demands to his camp. This is, of course, in case the election system does not change. But even if it does, there are clear signs of awareness on the part of the Arabs in Israel of the political power they possess and the potential influential role they may be able to play.

What can the Palestinian Authority learn from the Israeli elections? Very much indeed. For one, Barak’s determination, which seems to enjoy almost unanimous support, to withdraw Israeli troops from South Lebanon is an example of how an external matter may become a daily preoccupation for the majority of Israelis. The cost of maintaining control over a narrow strip of land, supposed to be a necessary measure to guarantee the security of Israeli northern towns, has haunted Israelis and changed their perceptions. They would rather now give up the security zone, and even enter into negotiations with Syria, which in turn may lead to giving up the Golan Heights or part of them. The Israelis simply cannot afford to keep losing young men no matter how small their proportion compared with the Lebanese losses. Thanks to the resistance by Hizbullah and other Lebanese resistance groups, Lebanon will eventually liberate its land and Syria may eventually regain control of its territory that has been under Israeli occupation since 1967.

This is the lesson the PA should learn. In other more explicit language, the Palestinian Authority should relax its security measures so as to permit Palestinians who wish to avenge the confiscation of their land, the demolition of their houses or the killing of their sons and daughters by Israeli troops or settlers to do so. Israel’s security should be linked to Palestinian security. If Israel is capable of, and gets away with, terrorizing the Palestinians, no Palestinian reaction to such terrorism should be hampered or foiled. Just as the Lebanese government sides with its people in the South and blesses their resistance until the land is liberated, similarly the Palestinian Authority has no honourable option other than siding with its people by forcing the Israelis to refrain from all provocations. This is the only way settlement building and expansion will cease, and this is the only way Israelis will think many times over before they consider demolishing a house or a water reservoir or destroying an olive grove or vineyard.

* Director of the London-based Liberty for Human Rights in Muslim World

We Need A United Front Immediately!

Dr. Musil Shihadeh*

It was quite a disappointment for those few who entertained the thought that the election of Ehud Barak could bring peace to the Palestinians. His rejection of dismantling the settlements and strengthening his hold on Jerusalem should convince the skeptics that peace will never come through any Zionist leadership, but only through our combined front to frustrate the Zionist dreams. There is no “Third Way” or fourth way, for that matter, but only one way, namely our united progressive front for the liberation of Palestine.

I believe we urgently need to solicit the combined efforts of the Palestinian masses to form a very wide front that can absorb all Palestinians who believe in the liberation of Palestine. Such a front should have no political ideology or religious character, but a military and political struggle for the liberation of Palestine. If we do differ in political ideology or religious affiliations, and we do indeed, we should postpone such differences until after liberation. We should be smart enough to realize that different fractionalization of our efforts can only pour into the Zionist advantage.

We have been subjected to small doses of hope through various meetings and conferences that adopted such a strategy, but so far we have failed to realize such intentions into a practical and progressive movement. It might sound ironic, but I call for some kind of a new conference that should be held somewhere in the Western world, so as to avoid becoming a movement that follows the policy of any Arab regime, as total independence in our decisions should become a very essential lesson that we should learn from the past. Arafat and his supporters have unfortunately become the defenders of Israel’s security, while Israel is exploiting such weakness to the maximum so as to Judaize the entire West Bank and keep it as a collection of ghettoes under Israel’s hegemony.

With the most optimistic expectations, the future of the so-called Palestinians State, in accordance with Oslo and the Wye river agreement, can only allow the Palestinians to live in not more than 10% of Palestine when our people used to own more than 90% in retrospect. Even then, this 10% is dissected into small discontinuous ghettoes while inter-ghetto movement is subject to Israeli checkpoints. Our refugees will remain living in their sub-human conditions and under the mercy of the Arab regimes as there is no hope under the present agreement for any repatriation or compensation. Therefore the “wait and see” policy is nothing more than expended useful time that should be used immediately to unify our efforts. I beseech our opposition to start global organizations for our Palestinian people in order to start the struggle.

Our people have proven time after time that they are capable of meeting any challenge; it was only the defeatism of our leaders that has led us to the miserable status quo. Are there any of our people listening to my call? If so, let’s start the ball rolling.

*The writer is a free-lance American of Palestinian origin whose writings and ideas have frequented the American media.

Why Kosovo must not become ‘a land without a people’:

Lessons from the Palestinian Nakba

Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Few Palestinian journalists have savoured the international political limelight in the way that Nasser el-Din Nashashibi has. For more than five decades he covered from very close quarters the persecution of his people. Hosted and toasted by kings, presidents, prime ministers and revolutionaries, he acquired throughout his lengthy career an astonishing wealth of information and experiences. One of these, he recalls, took place on 25 June 1982.

On that day, columns of Israeli tanks began to move on the besieged Palestinian strongholds in West Beirut. This was after weeks of intense aerial bombings that failed to break their resistance. The political and strategic aim of the attack was apparently to destroy the PLO (Palestinian Liberation Organization) and eliminate it as a principal player in the Middle East conflict. It was at this point, according to Nashashibi, the Saudi monarch, Fahd ibn Abdul Aziz, telephoned the White House and demanded to speak to President Reagan. His words to the American leader were clear and to the point: “Am I required to go to Mecca and declare to the people from the Holy Mosque that American aircraft, missiles and bombs are the weapons used by Israel to invade Lebanon and kill thousands of women and children?”1 As the story goes, Reagan did not only promise to contact Menachem Begin but, moreover, put an immediate end to the killings.

In any case, neither the threat by the Saudi king nor the intervention of the American president was enough to prevent the massacre of thousands more during the long, hot summer of 1982. Since then, Israel has continued to illegally occupy Lebanese territory. At the same time, it has increased its willful and indiscriminate attacks against Lebanese civilian and resistance “targets” as well as Palestinian refugee camps. Despite the incalculable human and material costs, the occupation of Lebanon, like that of Palestine, has deprived the world’s paramount ethnic State of peace and security. Given its historic origins in the belly of European colonialism, the Zionist project and its genocidal consequences must, therefore, serve as a grim warning why Serbian chauvinism must not be allowed to create another Palestine in the heartland of Europe.

Oppression breeds resistance
To begin with, the Palestinian encounter with Zionism underscores an undeniable truth about tyranny: no matter how brutal or criminal its methods, political terror and repression can never silence the will of a people determined to preserve their dignity, honour and freedom. Thus, after 100 years of military, political, economic and social onslaught, the Zionist enterprise has yet to devise a formula powerful enough to extinguish the flame of Palestinian resistance.

This outcome will almost certainly repeat itself in Kosovo if a comprehensive solution is not found to the conflict there. Surely contemporary history has not known a more despicable form of terror than the forceful “transfer” or ethnic cleansing of a people from their land. Their uprooting, separation and eviction from the places which symbolize their identity and national existence all bear the hallmark of a kind of terrorism that cannot be equaled by any violence or torture. Any attempt, therefore, to force the Kosovars to accept less than their full political rights will meet with the same response that was witnessed in the case of the Palestinians. As the latter did not melt into their places of refuge or become appeased with the “humanitarian” assistance of the United Nations, so too the Kosovars will continue to reject every partial settlement, even if initially superimposed by threats and force.

When questioned about the correctness of protecting the Muslim Albanians, Western leaders have always been careful to point out, and perhaps rightly so, that the issue is not simply a matter of religion but, moreover, one of undue human suffering. This combined sense of guilt and responsibility is, altogether, well understood because since 1987 no effective measures were taken to prevent Milosevic from redrawing the demographic map of the Balkans. Clearly, the signs did begin to appear on the wall when he revoked Kosovo’s self-ruling status in 1987 and outlawed the Albanian language as a medium of teaching at Pristina University—a decree that resulted in the closure of the university from that time until today. Thus, the present searches, beatings and seizures of Albanian identity papers and money at Serbian check-points are actually the latter stages of a deliberate policy that began long ago.

With regard to the Palestinians, the systematic and prolonged denial of their national rights has, similarly, undermined all efforts at securing peace in their region. For more than 50 years, the free and democratic world has watched passively at their brutal expulsion and dispersal to the distant corners of the globe. The destruction of their homes and agricultural infrastructure has gone hand-in-hand with the building of settlements for foreign immigrants. In these settlements, Jews from Russia, Argentina—everywhere—are instantly provided with electric-wired housing and irrigated land while the Palestinians in the neighbouring camps cannot even find water to drink. With more than 2,000 Palestinian homes still listed for demolition, the Director of the Israeli Committee Against House Demolitions, Jeff Halper, acknowledged that the word “cleansing” has now become an integral part of official Israeli terminology.2 Hence, in order to show how committed they are to peace, the Israelis have since Oslo destroyed 300,000 Palestinian olive and fruit trees! Significantly, the distress caused by these policies, especially in the area between Ramallah and Nablus—the nerve centre of the West Bank—has not produced an exodus of Palestinians as the Israelis hoped. Quite to the contrary, it has stiffened their resolve and turned the territories into a veritable time-bomb waiting to explode.

Racism is no substitute for democracy
As the attention of the world community remains firmly focused on events in the Balkans, Israel’s extremist authorities have intensified their own campaign to complete the ethnic cleansing of Jerusalem—a city which both the United Nations and the European Union regard as illegally occupied. There, the arbitrary confiscation of Palestinian identification papers, destruction of their homes and refusal to grant them building or repair licenses are all akin to the Fascist policies pursued by the Belgrade regime in Kosovo. Still, few lessons have been learned from recent history.

When the crude passions of nationalism erupted in Europe in the late nineteenth century, Western leaders took no significant initiatives to reassure or integrate their Jewish populations into their societies as equals. They sought instead to exploit the weakness and decline of the Ottoman Empire by encouraging the Jews to “return” to the “Promised Land” (Palestine) under the pretext that they were “God’s chosen people”. The partition of Palestine, eviction of half its population, and the creation of a Jewish State on its soil has left the region in a state of permanent turmoil.

Belgrade’s current attempt to readjust the balance of forces in the Balkans along ethnic lines will, inevitably, have similar disastrous consequences for Europe. As in the case of Palestine, many misleading voices are calling for an ethnic partition of Kosovo. Like the Zionist colonial-settlers who could not see beyond the occupation of Palestinian land, Serbian extremists are also clamouring for a partition that would leave them with the mineral deposits of northern Kosovo and its western areas along with its historic churches. In that case, they would naturally find it much easier to resettle the Serbs from Croatia’s Krajina region, who were displaced by the fighting during the early 1990s. History has nevertheless confirmed that the ethnic cleansing of Palestine did not bring security to the Zionists. Hence, their stockpiles of unconventional weapons and refusal to sign the Non-Proliferation Treaty banning the manufacture of nuclear weapons. While Belgrade may find it difficult to reactivate and develop its nuclear programme, it may nevertheless find it easier to acquire an arsenal of unconventional weapons for its killing-machine.

An end to human suffering
In the build-up to the present crisis, it appears that Slobodan Milosevic had committed a fatal error of judgment. He misread the political and military resolve of NATO’s leaders to stop him in his tracks. Nothing of the sort was enforced in Palestine to protect human lives and uphold international legitimacy. In fact, Israeli intransigence and deviance was always encouraged by the conviction that America will not confront them, no matter how inhuman or illegal their policies toward the Palestinians might be. A rare example of disapproval, however, occurred in 1956 after Israel occupied the Sinai and Gaza Strip during the trilateral attack on Egypt.

When President Eisenhower ordered the Israelis to withdraw unconditionally from the occupied territories, Prime Minister Ben Gurion told the Knesset (on 7 November 1956) that the historical Sinai was an indivisible part of Israel. His was a response that would have fitted perfectly with the belligerent rhetoric which the Israelis broadcast today concerning the West Bank and Gaza Strip. Indeed, he ordered a partial withdrawal from Sinai and kept the remainder of his forces in Gaza and Sharm al-Shaykh. Eisenhower was, however, having none of it; hence when he issued his ultimatum on American national television, the Israelis packed up and withdrew all their forces within the first two weeks of March 1957! Is there not a lesson here for the authors of Oslo, Cairo, Taba and Wye?

The Albanian population of Kosovo, like the Palestinian Arabs, will never accept a settlement of their problem that denies their national rights and aspirations. On the other extreme, Milosevic, like his Israeli admirers, will continue to resist the return of the Albanian population to Kosovo. Dispossessed of their heritage, robbed of their dignity and scattered across Europe, they now face a dark future of unemployment, poverty and disease in their overcrowded refugee camps.

For all its worth, none of the exiled Albanians will be deluded by Israel’s offer to grant them refuge in occupied Palestine. Having tasted the bitter fruits of ethnic cleansing and forced expulsion, they know all too well that these offers are mere cosmetic gestures designed to cover-up one of the greatest evils of the twentieth century—the dispossession and expulsion of the Palestinians. If Israel is sincere in its desire to end human suffering in Europe it must first allow the displaced Palestinians to exercise their legitimate right of return. As their struggle had often united and divided the Arabs in the past, so too the Kosovo problem has every potential of uniting or dividing the Europeans for many years to come. If the painful consequences of division are to be avoided, Kosovo must not become “a land without a people”.

*The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and editor of its Return Review.

Islamists and the Zionist World Movement

Dr. Ahmad Yousef*

(1-2)

Foreword
It is anathema to speak now about the possibilities of coexistence between Islam and Zionism. In other words, it is premature to talk about the convergence of opposites and the conversion of swords into ploughshares when forces of revulsion and compulsion turn into forces for proclivity and affinity. Historically, Islam has accommodated Judaism as a religion and Jews as a people. But Zionism as a racial movement has dwelled on denying the “others” their rights and lands. Zionism has made it very hard to tolerate and accept a Jewish presence that springs from racism and exclusivism.

The colonialist face of contemporary Judaism gained its features in the womb of Zionist racism. This has broadened an already unbridgeable cleavage, making it more difficult to entertain the idea of coexistence or even dialogue. This will probably remain the scenario as long as the Zionist movement remains the locomotive of the current religious and political expressions of the State of Israel. It may be unthinkable and unimaginable today to expect an encounter between both sides to discuss a coexistence between Islam on one side and Judeo-Zionism on the other side. The fact of the matter is Israel continues to antagonize the Arab and the Islamic worlds. Israel goes from one strategy of aggression to the next. It issues countless policies of discrimination and repression against the Palestinians without any qualms. Jewish settlers cannot seem to satiate their appetite for more Palestinian land. Jewish politicians in Israel and the West arrogantly state that Jerusalem is the Hebrew State’s eternal and indivisible capital.

International parties, well known for their open channels with the Zionist movement, have come up with interfaith forums to try to facilitate a rapprochement with the Islamists. The object is to bring down the resistance barriers in the Arab and Islamic world. Zionist contingents within Western political and religious institutions put these types of activities into high gear. But in the end, none of this seems to have produced anything tangible. In a short time, the participants begin to discover the underlying causes and objectives of such forums, which is the acceptance of Israel and the normalization of relations with it.

True, peoples and nations are living a relative calm that followed the collapse of the bi-polar world and the hostilities of the cold war. But the thought of an Islamic-Zionist life of peace, the thought itself in such circumstances, is asphyxiating. The idea itself violates established facts and trespasses red lines. Such an idea is enough to classify anyone on any side as an enemy and to discredit anything else he or she may say. This idea is paralysis, period.

Due to the sensitivity of this subject, the whole issue has to be defined and clarified within the reason and need concerning such an issue for discussion.

Targeting Islamic Activism
There are more than a few reasons for taking aim at Islamic activism. We may sum them up in four points.

1. To give a bad image to armed resistance that defies Israeli occupation

The Islamic movement in general, but the Palestinian and Lebanese sectors in particular, have come under malicious assault by the media. Pulling the strings of this media campaign are well-established Zionist and Jewish figures in the West. The Zionist-inspired crusade aims at defaming the scene of those who are legitimately struggling and resisting Israeli occupation. This is done by trying to incriminate the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and Hizbullah as they target Israeli military and para-military objectives. Media mouthpieces of the Israeli persuasion pick up on the injuries or deaths of some civilians in the crossfire as an excuse to fuel its drive. Then the picture is developed in the lexicon laboratory of these media outlets to make the Islamic resistance movement appear as if it is part and parcel of international terrorism! The script reads something like this: These operations of armed resistance are “extremist” acts working to destroy the peace process and to foster instability in the Middle East. Embedded in this campaign are those voices that, from time to time, call on the international and local authorities to consider all sorts of armed resistance as a “crime.” This comes at a time when, in the post cold-war era, people are enjoying their right to speak out against oppression and press on with their demands for rights through international institutions and organizations. Now, people who had support for resisting occupation during the cold-war years should make-do with considering their causes obsolete and should settle in and accept occupation and tyranny. Any such “resistance movements” should consider from now on any activity to be a form of violence or terrorism that has no sponsor to protect it or offer it cover, and hence has no legitimacy. This “logic” also extends to include any and all States that dare to offer backing or lend support to such resistance movements.

2. Circumventing the Islamic plan

Zionist forces with all their means and methods are keen on ejecting Islam out of this conflict. They say that the issue to begin with is political and not religious. That is why we find these campaign managers doing whatever they can to see to it that Islamic revolutionary thought is plucked out of its institutions. They are trying hard to domesticate the Islamic movement. They work at all levels to cut off Palestine from its religious dimension. Then they turn around and try to condition the public mind to accept the Israeli settler presence in Palestine with all the supremacy arrogance that goes with it. It is at this stage that we begin to realize how the current war against the Islamic movement in most of the Arab and Islamic countries falls into place. This war takes on many forms: ranging from the persecution of the Islamic movement’s leaders through a witch-hunt for Islamists in universities, labour unions, missionary institutions, and humanitarian organizations. The goal is to marginalize the gains and progress made throughout the years and to block future success which is capable of bringing in exponential growth for the general Islamic trend in the form of upper political mobility. Egypt, Tunisia, Algeria, Turkey and Malaysia are case examples of this. Israel sees the Islamic movement as its mortal threat. For this reason it wants the Islamic factor out of this engagement equation. To this end, Israel is trying to pull in international powers—especially the U.S.—to its side, or if it can, Israel would like to have the U.S. doing the fighting for it. The past five years seem to indicate that Israel has been successful in dragging the U.S. into the fray. Israel has set up the U.S. as a party in its conflict with the Islamic movement. The massive efforts and the wide-ranging potentials deployed by the U.S. to down-size the Islamic resistance phenomenon has been partially successful. Washington’s carrot-and-stick policy with Arab and Islamic—even European—countries has been relatively fruitful in garnering support for its schemes that seek to bolt down the spirit that resists the Zionist project. Desecrating the Sabbath to Save Life

According to the Talmudic Encyclopedia: 'He who has carnal knowledge of the wife of a Gentile is not liable to the death penalty, for it is written: "thy fellow's wife" rather than the alien's wife; and even the precept that a man "shall cleave unto his wife" which is addressed to the Gentiles does not apply to a Jew. just there is no matrimony for a heathen; and although a married Gentile woman is forbidden to the Gentiles, in any case a Jew is exempted.'

But the main punishment is inflicted on the Gentile woman; she must be executed, even if she was raped by the Jew: 'If a Jew has coitus with a Gentile woman, whether she be a child of three or an adult, whether married or unmarried, and even if he is a minor aged only nine years and one day- because he had willful coitus with her, she must be killed, as is the case with a beast, because through her a Jew got into trouble. The Jew, however, must be flogged, and if he is a Kohen (member of the priestly tribe) he must receive double the number of lashes, because he has committed a double offence: a Kohen must not have intercourse with a prostitute, and all Gentile women are presumed to be prostitutes.

Money and Property
1 Gifts. The Talmud bluntly forbids giving a gift to a Gentile. However, classical rabbinical authorities bent this rule because it is customary among businessmen to give gifts to business contacts. It was therefore laid down that a Jew may give a gift to a Gentile acquaintance, since this is regarded not as a true gift but as a sort of investment, for which some return is expected. Gifts to 'unfamiliar Gentiles' remain forbidden. A broadly similar rule applies to almsgiving. Giving alms to a Jewish beggar is an important religious duty. Alms to Gentile beggars are merely permitted for the sake of peace. However there are numerous rabbinical warnings against allowing the Gentile poor to become 'accustomed' to receiving alms from Jews, so that it should be possible to withhold such alms without arousing undue hostility.

2 Taking of interest. Anti-Gentile discrimination in this matter has become largely theoretical, in view of the dispensation which in effect allows interest to be exacted even from a Jewish borrower. However, it is still the case that granting an interest-free loan to a Jew is recommended as an act of charity, but from a Gentile borrower it is mandatory to exact interest. In fact, many - though not all- rabbinical authorities, including Maimonides, consider it mandatory to exact as much usury as possible on a loan to a Gentile.

3 Lost property. If a Jew finds property whose probable owner is Jewish, the finder is strictly enjoined to make a positive effort to return his find by advertising it publicly. In contrast, the Talmud and all the early rabbinical authorities not only allow a Jewish finder to appropriate an article lost by a Gentile, but actually forbid him or her to return it. In more recent times, when laws were passed in most countries making it mandatory to return lost articles, the rabbinical authorities instructed Jews to do what these laws say, as an act of civil obedience to the state - but not as a religious duty, that is without making a positive effort to discover the owner if it is not probable that he is Jewish.

4 Deception in business. It is a grave sin to practice any kind of deception whatsoever against a Jew. Against a Gentile it is only forbidden to practice direct deception. Indirect deception is allowed, unless it is likely to cause hostility towards Jews or insult to the Jewish religion. The paradigmatic example is mistaken calculation of the price during purchase. If a Jew makes a mistake unfavourable to himself, it is one's religious duty to correct him. If a Gentile is spotted making such a mistake, one need not let him know about it, but say 'I rely on your calculation', so as to forestall his hostility in case he subsequently discovers his own mistake.

5 Fraud. It is forbidden to defraud a Jew by selling or buying at an unreasonable price. However, 'Fraud does not apply to Gentiles, or it is written: "Do not defraud each man his brother"; but a Gentile who defrauds a Jew should be compelled to make good the fraud, but should not be punished more severely than a Jew [in a similar case].'

6 Theft and robbery. Stealing (without violence) is absolutely forbidden - as the Shulhan 'Arukh so nicely puts it: 'even from a Gentile'. Robbery (with violence) is strictly forbidden if the victim is Jewish. However, robbery of a Gentile by a Jew is not forbidden outright but only under certain circumstances such as 'when the Gentiles are not under our rule', but is permitted 'when they are under our rule'. Rabbinical authorities differ among themselves as to the precise details of the circumstances under which a Jew may rob a Gentile, but the whole debate is concerned only with the relative power of Jews and Gentiles rather than with universal considerations of justice and humanity. This may explain why so very few rabbis have protested against the robbery of Palestinian property in Israel: it was backed by overwhelming Jewish power.

Intra-Civilizational Debate Within Islam:
Secularism Versus Islamism-The Case Of Algeria

Prof. John P. Entelis*

Introduction

The debate about conflict within the Islamic world between sectarianism and secularism has been framed in civilizational and cultural terms. Yet, in my view, the Algerian experience clearly demonstrates that the real source of conflict lies in questions of political economy and the way in which power, privilege, and prestige are monopolized and manipulated by the few at the expense of the many. To be sure, ever since the Iranian revolution, the political economy dimension has been subordinated to civilizational, cultural, and religious concerns. Yet, by their very nature, the latter are infinitely more difficult to resolve or even to correctly conceptualize than are problems of political economy

Added to the conceptual problem lies the question of political context within which debates about such issues can take place. In democratic and democratizing societies, free and open debate is permitted, which allows a full airing of differences and perspectives. In Algeria and throughout much of the Arab Middle East and North Africa, however, little opportunity exists for a free and fair exchange of ideas about civilizational or political questions. One result is that virtually all of the exchanges taking place on these issues are occurring outside the region by observers and not participants. Even in the limited instances where contested debates take place, they operate in highly constrained and controlled political environments. It is no surprise, therefore, that those most affected by the outcome of intra-civilizational debates are the ones least permitted to articulate their views. Thus, to argue, that questions of democracy and elections must be addressed after the civilizational question has been resolved amounts to putting the cart before the horse. The reality in the Arab-Islamic world as elsewhere is that issues of secularism and sectarianism can be approached, debated, resolved, and synthesized only in a politically democratic framework if they are to endure and be truly representative and comprehensive. Thus, democracy first, debate about cultural policy later.

Another preliminary point concerns the question of the international dimension of the so-called civilizational question. By this I mean the dominant role played by external actors in attempting to influence the debate. The very framing of the issue in terms of secularism-religion betrays a Western bias and preference for certain cultural outcomes and a denial of competing interpretations that could constitute valid alternative paradigms for non-Western societies and polities.

The Algerian Case

What people often describe as a conflict between "Muslim fundamentalists" and "secularists" in Algeria is really at heart a conflict of political economy and power, a struggle over who gets, what, when, and how.

A false dichotomy has defined Algeria in the period since the outbreak of nation-wide riots in October 1988, which, in their aftermath, saw the rise of a populist Islamist movement bent on challenging the long-entrenched power of a military-led authoritarian political order. Indeed, authoritarianism's deep-rootedness is at the heart of Algeria's current conflict, not Algeria's cultural, civilizational or religious identity. Even before French colonialism in 1830, Algerian society was characterized by a multiplicity of regional, cultural, and ethnic orientations. Yet, none of these sociological cleavages or differences overrode the dominating influence of Islam as religion, culture, and identity. To this day and contrary to certain Western opinion, Algeria remains a unified country integrated within a powerful sense of Algerian-ness which has Islam at its core. It is also a cautious and conservative society notwithstanding its revolutionary pretensions. To be sure, society has evolved into a complex network of class differences and bureaucratic types which make Algeria difficult to characterize in any simple way. Yet, out of this mass modernization has emerged over the years a relatively small stratum of privileged elite mostly of French persuasion, who have come to monopolize state power and society's resources, including its access to education, travel, and the media. As such, this unrepresentative elite, supported by the army officer class of which it is an integral part, has sought to portray the current conflict in Algeria as one between "secularists" defending modernist interests fighting against “obscurantist” Islamists. Nothing could be further from the truth. The reality of course, is much more complex, yet one fact stands out the way in which Algeria's political economy has been completely "high-jacked" by the Francophone elite, who now seek to explain the inevitable catastrophe such a highjacking has created in terms of a cultural or civilizational clash.

Islam And Governance

To illustrate the above point that the Algerian crisis is the result of a mismanaged political economy including a systematic degradation of Islamic culture, let us retrace the role of the Islamic Salvation Front or the FIS in Algeria’s experience with democracy, however brief.

In its more moderate and accommodationist form, political Islam as represented by the FIS was essentially an expression of popular discontent with existing political economy arrangements, including cultural policy. In its essence, the FIS was and remains as "secular" and as religious as any other party contesting political power, save that it was the most popular by a very wide margin. This appeal was first demonstrated in the June, 1990, local and regional elections, which the FIS overwhelmingly won, gaining the votes of an incredible cross-section of Algerian society, including its youth, middle class, and Westernized women, among others. Once permitted to govern, the FIS proved unexceptional in its performance. In some instances, the Islamist party was inefficient and inept while in others it was responsible and effective.

In the national parliamentary elections of December, 1991, the FIS again demonstrated its broad appeal among the Algerian people. Yet, this appeal had less to do with FIS’s ideology, which remained purposively vague and ambiguous especially on issues of economic policy, but more with its practical performance on the ground where it could be evaluated based not on what it promised but on what it had accomplished. Obviously, for a great many Algerians, FIS deserved continued support based on its public policy performance. In other words, it was not a question of Islamism versus secularism that animated the Algerian electorate but rather such issues as political honesty, integrity and accountability economic performance, cultural coherence, and overall standards of governance. In this regard, both "secularists" and "sectarians" supported the FIS.

Those who most suffered from such policies were the privileged elite who could not envision a new social order in which democracy, justice, economic development, and cultural integrity would now be available to all Algerians! It was no surprise, therefore, that the praetorian guard protecting this class put an immediate and abrupt halt to the democratization process, justifying its actions in the name of "saving" democracy, secularism, and modernity. No one was fooled, however. Yet unlike the situations elsewhere in which the anti-democratic actions of military juntas would arouse the wrath and disdain of the world's leading democracies, there was virtually no criticism from overseas regarding the Algerian coup d'etat. Clearly the country's geostrategic importance, the role of Algerian oil and gas in the world economy, and the exaggerated fears of an "Islamic threat” made silence rather than outrage the policy of choice for the leading Western powers, including France and the United States.

Since the coup, the country has descended into a vicious bloodbath pitting extremists from whatever camp against one another. While the military and some of its misguided allies in civil society have sought to transform this conflict into an intra-civilizational debate in which the so- called secularists in the military are doing everything in their power to project an image of noble defenders of the country's modernity, development, democracy, and secularism, they have effectively failed in this endeavor, losing even the support of one of the most "fundamentalist secularists," to borrow John Esposito's term, in the process, namely, Saad Sadi, the leader of the chauvinistic Berberist party, the RCD - Rally for Democracy and Culture.

The behavior of the military and groups like the RCD in the face of a popular will for democratic transformation reveals the stark reality of the current situation, for these groups are neither democratic nor secularist but opportunists seeking to maintain their privileged positions of power and influence against the will of the majority of ordinary Algerians. In no way can these so-called secularists be defined as possessing a rationalistic, scientific, modernistic, and democratic view of social and political life. Indeed, guided by a strong sense of cultural and religious identity, the "lslamists" are much more "secular" than their enemies for they view the material world in a clear, rational, and scientific way, being able to clearly distinguish the world of the spirit from its earthly counterpart.

Conclusion

All this would remain academic given the overwhelming support most Algerians give to political Islam as the appropriate instrument for achieving a democratic peace, were it not for the decisive role of external actors whose multiple interventions in the areas of military and intelligence support, economic aid, business investment, and diplomatic advocacy have made it possible for the army to stay in power regardless of the death tolls, human rights abuses, and damage to state and society. This damage to Algeria and its peoples cannot long endure. One possible exit from this tragedy could begin with the elimination of this false dichotomy artificially imposed on Algeria and other Arab and Muslim countries, namely, that what is really at stake is a "clash of intra-civilization," when in fact it is a struggle, in its very essence, between democracy and dictatorship.

Seeking The Wind, Reaping A Storm

By Erik Paul

If you are a person who has lived under an oppressive form of government, at first, America will seem like a political utopia. At least in the U.S. the American government as well as its president can be criticized without fear of reprisal.

You can stand up on any street corner, or attend any political function and vehemently denounce whatever government practice or policy you wish and you will feel pretty sure that in those wee dark hours of the night, no secret police will arrest you.

However, America is not an ideal place to raise children. There is so much going on. Children are exposed to moral and spiritual corruption in all aspects of their lives and there are very few TV programmes that are really suitable for your kids. Many shows that label themselves as viewable for all members of the family are often spiced with sensual innuendoes and a lack of family values.

And if they are not exposed to promiscuity in the American media, then your children will often be exposed to violence. But quite sadly, many times, they will be exposed to both. Too often for the young minds, saturated with an overabundance of sex, evil and brutality, it is difficult to distinguish between what is real, what is not and what role morality and social values have to play in the concrete world. Many American families are secular. That is, they raise their children without any form of religion or belief in God. Some parents do not even believe in God at all. Many children are now being raised in one-parent homes. Those Muslims who do face the tremendously difficult task of raising their children as pious and faithful followers of Islam in America have a great responsibility and possibly a more difficult job than they would otherwise have.

But the latest school shooting incidents in Colorado and now in Alabama have posed a multitude of questions. One very confusing thought to many Americans is that the youths who were guilty of turning their guns on their fellow students came from well-to-do two-parent families, living in comfortable neighbourhoods. Where did all the anger that these young people accumulate stem from? They were not impoverished. They lacked nothing in material possessions nor the basic comforts and necessities of life. Their parents were well educated and the children themselves never had to work for a living or worry about where their next meal was coming from.

What then has gone so drastically wrong that young people without a care in the world would even consider harming their classmates or fellow students? Is it that their parents never made any effort to raise them as moral and responsible citizens? Did their parents make no effort to instill in them a sense of human decency and the ability to choose between right and wrong? Were the video and computer games and TV shows that their children were playing and watching monitored? Were these youths exposed to such an excessive amount of violent games and films that it made them feel that taking another’s life was as simple as tying their shoes?

On 20 May, tragedy struck again when a 15-year-old sophomore wielding a .22-calibre rifle began shooting at his schoolmates in Conyers, Georgia. Only one month prior to this attack, two high school boys shot and killed 13 and injured 23 others in their “trench-coat” Mafia attack. In Springfield, Oregon, 4 were killed and more than 20 hurt on 21 May 1998. In Fayetteville, Tennessee, 1 was killed on 19 May 1998. In Jonesboro, Arkansas, 5 were killed and 10 wounded on 24 March 1998. On 1 December 1997, in West Paducah, Kentucky, 3 were killed and 5 injured, and in Pearl, Missouri on 1 October 1997, 3 were killed and 7 wounded.

In Conyers, Georgia, when 15-year-old Thomas Solomon, Jr. began firing at his schoolmates, the chatter and laughter in the school’s common area turned into screams of horror, chaos and a stampede of fear-struck students. Some students took cover under benches and behind partitions, others raced outside the one-storey building, while at least one teacher took students into a closet and recited Bible verses.

But was the sudden urgency to return to religion too late? If prevention is better than cure, then is there no hope to halt the disease? If President Clinton preaches against violence and then without mercy leads NATO bombings on helpless civilians in Yugoslavia and continues his war of genocide against the Iraqis, how does he then expect his own citizens not to wage a war on themselves?

The best way to cleanse the American psyche is by doing a lot of soul-searching and to bring the fear of God back into American society. Stricter rules must also be passed that will keep lethal weapons out of the reach of children. The Americans sought the wind, but because of their loose morals and lack of religion, have reaped the storm.

So, on the one hand, there is an abundance of political freedom, but on the other, there is also an abundance of moral and spiritual corruption. Once the American media begins to produce more wholesome products and computer and video games that promote learning through clean and useful ways of having fun, and once the American government stops killing the innocents all over the world, then perhaps school shootings will become only a part of a sinister past instead of a new way of life.
