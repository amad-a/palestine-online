Comment

Barak: a new illusion

It is quite nice that some Palestinian, Arab and Muslim leaders have refrained from lauding the Zionist Prime Minister-elect Ehud Barak. They have many good reasons for doing so.

To begin with, Barak, like the rest of the Zionist warlords, is an accomplished murderer and a career killer. He himself, in the course of his election campaign, excessively boasted of and about his murderous record whether in Lebanon or in the West Bank. (Obviously, he wouldn’t have done that publicly had he not been convinced of the “magical effect” this would have on a society that romanticizes murder and bloodshed.)

Indeed, it seems that the bitter experience of the past three years with the outgoing Zionist premier Benyamin Netanyahu, whom some Arab statesmen initially gave the benefit of the doubt, embittered them and made them a little wiser this time, following the old adage “once bitten, twice shy.”

Even Palestinian Authority leader Yasser Arafat, whose tongue often functions much more swiftly than his mind, was relatively circumspect in his reactions to Barak’s election victory.

Arafat, who has been markedly less outspoken about this Israeli election than he was about the 1996 election, said he wouldn’t hasten to pass judgement on Barak.

For his part, Barak himself vindicated in earnest Arab fears and doubts about him. For soon after he was declared the winner on 17 May, he made it brashly clear that while he and Netanyahu differed only on some domestic Zionist issues, they were almost completely united in their opposition to Palestinian rights, including the right to freedom, liberation and self-determination.

Barak outlined four “red lines” he said he would not cross under any circumstances.

These included: no returning East Jerusalem to the Palestinians; no withdrawal to the 1967 borders; no dismantling Jewish settlements; and no Arab army west of the river Jordan.

Barak said what he said consciously and deliberately; so he meant what he was saying, and he said it as precisely and concisely and, yes, as provocatively as possible.

Unfortunately, some Arab leaders, who often act in total disregard of their peoples’ interests, were not deterred by Barak’s recalcitrant statements and extremely hawkish posture.

Hence, they did not find in his outspoken extremism a sufficient reason for reconsidering their political calculations. This is sad and lamentable for two reasons:

1. Barak has been brazenly clear about his political stance. He is adamantly opposed to the restoration of the very minimum of Palestinian rights, e.g. a complete Israeli pull-out from the territories occupied in the 1967 war and the establishment of an independent Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital.

However, the euphoria, which some gullible Arabs and Palestinians voice on hearing such deceptive talk, soon gives way to depression or reasonable judgement when they are made to understand that what Barak has in mind is only a small and innocuous autonomous entity on 45%-55% of the West Bank and 65% of the Gaza Strip, without Jerusalem, of course, and with virtually all Jewish settlements remaining under Zionist sovereignty.

2. It is sufficiently clear that the Palestinian demographic and political scene will not be able to withstand four more years of political deception and ineptitude, of illusion, mirage and unfulfilled promises.

The West Bank, the very land where Arafat dreams to establish his independent Palestinian State, is already congested with Jewish settlements, so much so that one has no choice but to wonder if Arafat is really serious about his threats and vows to that effect.

What can the Palestinians do and how should they act in the face of these gloomy prospects? Should they, as some advocates of capitulation argue, accept whatever is given to them now on the ground that today’s camel would be tomorrow’s lamb and after tomorrow’s hen?

The Palestinians must seek to rid themselves of the fettering and humiliating shackles of Oslo, which gave the Zionists all the assets and the Palestinian people all the liabilities, so much so that Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza Strip are left with virtually no water to drink.

But in order for the Palestinians to get the calamity of Oslo behind them, they, or more correctly the Palestinian Authority, must first resign to the bitter reality that Oslo has been a great blunder, even a gigantic fiasco.

This supposedly can be done through the organization of a free and democratic election in the occupied territories to let the people decide.

Only this can deliver the autocratic Palestinian leadership from its enduring vulnerability to Zionist and American blackmail, and therefore, enable the Palestinian masses to freely decide their fate.
