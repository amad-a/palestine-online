Press Cuttings

Ha'aretz, Friday, 31 March 2000

Nasser Malham complained of pain. An x-ray revealed that he was suffering from an internal hemorrhage in the left testicle, and emergency surgery showed that the hemorrhage had been caused by a beating. Thus, Dr.Mahmoud Jabarin, a surgeon, summed up Malham's medical condition at the end of February.

Nasser Malham and his brother-in-law Ayman Malham said that Border Policeman beat them brutally with a flashlight and the butt of a pistol. The two complained through B'Tselem to the division for the investigation of police officers at the State Prosecutor's Office and to the military prosecutor.

According to B'Tselem, there has been a significant rise recently in the number of complaints submitted by Palestinians against police, and border police in particular. The complaints claim to have been beaten at checkpoints, particularly the one at the post near Jenin. The organization says that it has received a large number of complaints during the months of February and March.

The Los Angeles Times, 30 March 2000

In a new and dramatic break with the traditional understanding of biblical morality, rabbis of the nation's largest Jewish movement Wednesday declared that their members are free to officiate at same-sex unions.

A relationship between two Jewish men or two Jewish women is "worthy of affirmation through appropriate Jewish ritual," the resolution by the Central Conference of American Rabbis--the rabbinical arm of Reform Judaism--declared. The vote marked the first in a series of wrenching debates expected this year among both Christian and Jewish denominations over homosexuality.

Before Wednesday's vote, the Unitarian church and the United Church of Christ--both traditionally liberal Christian denominations--were the only American religious bodies to formally approve having clergy officiate at same-gender unions.

The Washington Post, 29 March 29 2000

JERUSALEM- Wrapping up a seven-month fraud investigation, Israeli police recommended today that former Prime Minister Binyamin Netanyahu and his wife, Sara, be indicted on criminal charges.

The case stems from allegations that the Netanyahus illegally kept 700 state gifts received while he was in office that are said to be worth $100,000. Among the gifts were silverware, menorahs, carpets, paintings and, according to Israeli TV, a golden letter opener from Vice President Gore.

The inquiry has become emblematic of a series of corruption inquiries involving the top echelons of Israeli government and business, including President Ezer Weizman, the governing Labor Party and the publisher of a leading newspaper.

The New York Times, 29 March 2000

WASHINGTON- President Clinton, commenting publicly for the first time on the failure of his talks with the Syrian leader, Hafez al-Assad, said today that it is up to Mr. Assad to make the next move.

"The ball's in his court now, and I'm going to look forward to hearing from him," Mr. Clinton said at the White House before meeting with the Egyptian president, Hosni Mubarak.

"I went to Switzerland to meet President Assad to clarify to him what I thought the options were and to hear from him what his needs are," Mr. Clinton added, "and I asked him to come back to me with what he thought ought to be done."

Senior administration officials continued to express disappointment today at the outcome of the meeting with Mr. Assad, during which he stuck to his long-held view that Israel should return land seized from Syria during the 1967 Arab-Israeli war.

The Washington Post, 28 March 28 2000

Since taking office in January 1997, Secretary of State Madeleine K. Albright has barnstormed the country from Alaska to Maine, delivering 89 speeches in 41 cities. "The thing I just love watching are teenage girls," said Barbara Larkin, the assistant secretary of state for legislative affairs, who sometimes accompanies her boss. "They treat her like she's a rock star."

Three years ago, Albright was at the top of her game, a blunt-spoken former Georgetown University professor with a reputation for wearing outsize brooches and staring down dictators. Today her influence is much diminished, eroded by rival agencies and the White House and undercut by widespread criticism of her outspoken, sometimes schoolmarmish style.

Within the department, Albright is regarded by many career diplomats as an insecure and indifferent leader who is quick to anger and obsessed by her public image.

The Financial Times, 27 Mar 2000

Ehud Barak's fragile coalition took another battering on Monday after the attorney-general said he was ordering a police investigation into Rabbi Ovadia Yosef, the spiritual leader of Shas.

The announcement by Elyakim Rubinstein exacerbated the ethnic differences and secular-religious divide that are fast becoming the hallmarks of the Barak government.

It was immediately condemned by Shas,the kingmaker in the coalition, which said it would decide if it would remain in the government. In either case, it robs Mr. Barak of any chance of stability over the coming weeks as he tries to salvage the Syria and Palestinian peace talks.

Shlomo Benizri, Shas health minister, said the justice ministry's decision was "racist because we are Sephardic and ultra Orthodox".
