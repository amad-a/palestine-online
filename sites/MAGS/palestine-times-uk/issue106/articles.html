Taiba’s bloody Thursday

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

On 2 March, four Islamist guerrillas, belonging to the military wing of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and who were apparently on their way to carry out attacks inside 1948-Palestine, were martyred in an unqualified confrontation with hundreds of Zionist troops.

A fifth member of the Islamist guerrilla cell was captured in still-ambiguous circumstances, while the sixth member fled to the West Bank town of Tulkarm, where he, too, was eventually captured by Palestinian Authority police.

The bloody episode began Wednesday night, 1 March, when Israel’s chief domestic intelligence agency, the Shin Bet, received information that a number of Hamas guerrillas rented an apartment in the Israeli-Palestinian town of Taiba, north-east of Tel Aviv.

Immediately, the peaceable town was put under curfew (reminiscent of the Israeli military rule to which the Arab minority was subjected in the 1950s and early 1960s), and hundreds of Israeli soldiers and snipers were deployed around the building inside which the guerrillas barricaded themselves.

Israeli sources claimed one guerrilla fired (or sought to fire) a pistol at the overwhelming Israeli force, and that a bag packed with explosives carried by a second guerrilla exploded during the “exchange of fire(!!)” killing them both and slightly wounding an Israeli policeman in the foot.

A few hours later, a robot with mounted video camera and a remote-operated rifle was sent in to the ground-floor flat in search of any remaining guerrillas, but the search was in vain.

Then came the grisliest part of the operation when a huge army bulldozer moved to demolish the building, crushing whoever was inside. One remaining guerrilla, whose hands protruded from amidst the rubble, was “finished off”—using the words of an Israeli army radio correspondent.

The fourth guerrilla was apparently buried alive, and later was shot several times in the head to verify that he was dead.

Israeli security and political officials, while congratulating themselves on the grand achievement at Taiba, admitted, nonetheless, that the “liquidation of the terrorist cell” was no guarantee that further attempts or successful attacks by Palestinian Islamic guerrillas would not take place.

“In our estimation, people connected to the same squad have the capability of carrying out a terrorist attack,” said the Israeli police inspector, General Yehuda Wilk.

He added that, “I believe Hamas will continue to try to carry out attacks at one level or another. There are people who are prepared to carry out suicide attacks, and there is a capability to manufacture explosive devices, and the combination of the two constitutes the potential for a terrorist attack.”

Underscoring these fears, Israel, on 5 March, placed its security forces on high alert to forestall possible attacks by Hamas, and roadblocks were set up on main routes and on entrances to main towns.

The alert remained in effect, despite the fact that the cell’s sixth member was arrested by PA security forces in Tukarm and the fact that a bomb factory was discovered in the town on 5 May that allegedly was used to provide the six would-be suicidal bombers with the explosives needed for their mission.

The state of alert was ended only after the Eid ul-Adha holiday when the two remaining members of the cell were arrested by the PA Preventive Security Force reportedly based on information provided by Israel.

Meanwhile, Israeli officials, particularly the heads of Shin Bet, made no secret of their deep satisfaction with the “closer-than-usual coordination” with the PA security and intelligence apparatus.

Indeed, even PA security officials themselves brazenly admitted that security cooperation between the two sides during and after the Taiba episode was creating a good “political” atmosphere between the two sides. The Israeli paper, the Jerusalem Post, on 6 March quoted an unidentified high-ranking security official as saying that, “We have made it very clear to our security people that they must continue to cooperate with Israel on the security level, whatever the political situation.”

Indeed, in addition to arresting the sixth member of the guerrilla cell and to discovering the bomb factory in Tulkarm, the PA also arrested two guards of Hamas founder Sheikh Ahmed Yassin on suspicion that they were connected with the cell.

Obviously, this was music to Israeli government officials’ ears, including Foreign Minister David Levy, who only a few days earlier vowed to incinerate Lebanon and kill Lebanese children if Hizbullah attacked northern Israeli settlements.

Seeking to pay back the gratitude for their earnest security cooperation, Levy suggested that Israel adopt a series of “confidence-building measures” to encourage the Palestinians.

These, he said, would include freeing some Palestinian prisoners as a gesture of goodwill on the eve of the Eid ul-Adha holiday and displaying more generosity in “economic matters.”

Military operations planned against soldiers, not civilians

According to the Israeli Hebrew newspaper, Ma’ariv (5 March), the six-man Gazan cell was planning to carry out simultaneous suicide-bombing operations against Israeli soldiers, not civilians, as was earlier suggested by some Israeli officials.

The paper said the guerrillas were planning to carry out spectacular operations similar to the 1995-Bit Lid suicidal bombing in which over 20 Israeli soldiers were killed.

If true, the information, which was apparently obtained from the captured guerrilla, gives credence to the Hamas decision last year to refrain from attacking Israeli civilians, particularly suicidal bomb attacks in Israeli urban centres.

Resumption of Hamas armed resistance

The latest events seem to indicate that Hamas has decided to resume its armed resistance against Israel, and the movement’s fiasco at Taiba is unlikely to have a long-lasting effect on the implementation of that decision.

Indeed, the rampant collective Palestinian frustration throughout the self-rule enclaves, which is engulfing Islamists and non-Islamists alike, and the growing despair concerning the prospects of the peace process, given continued Israeli equivocation and procrastination, are fostering an incendiary atmosphere which makes the renewal of armed attacks (including suicidal bombings) against Israel not only expected but also welcomed by the majority of Palestinians.

Interview with Abdul Khaleq al-Natshe, Hamas' spokesman in Hebron

Palestinian Authority is our oppressor, but our conflict is with Israel

Abdul Khaleq al-Natshe was born in 1954 into a prominent Hebronite family. After he finished high school in Hebron, he went to Saudi Arabia where he earned a Bachelor of Arts degree from the Islamic University at Medina. For the last 10 years, Natshe has been an on-again/off-again prisoner in both Israeli and Palestinian Authority jails. Last week, Natshe spoke to the Palestine Times. Here are excerpts of what he said.

Question: Your activism within the Islamic movement has caused you lots of difficulties. Do you regret having selected the Islamic line?

Answer: You are right. This is not an easy path, and walking on it invites difficulties and hardships. In 1984, I was arrested by the Israeli occupation authorities, along with a number of my colleagues who graduated from the Islamic University at Medina. The interrogation then centred on our Islamic activities in Saudi Arabia . And since then, I've been barred from leaving the country.

Q. Were you arrested again?

A. Yes. I was given an extra-judicial imprisonment for six months in 1988, and four more months in 1989; then in 1990, I was imprisoned for an entire year on charges of belonging to Hamas. In 1991, I was among the 400 Palestinian Islamists deported to Marj al-Zuhur in South Lebanon, and upon my return, I was arrested for two weeks. In 1996, I was imprisoned again, this time for two years, and the charge was my participation in the dialogue talks between Hamas and the PA. All in all, I could say I have entered all Israeli jails.

Q. And how do you look back at all of this?

A. I am quite satisfied, because all our sacrifices were for the sake of our faith and in defence of our rights and land.

Q. But between you and yourself, don't you feel that you suffered too much?

A. No, I know well the risks and hazards inherent on this road.

Q. You are referred to as the official spokesman of the Islamic Movement in Hebron; aren't you concerned about the possible risks as a result of this?

A. I am aware that the Zionists follow my movement, but this is our way, and we will continue what we are doing because this is the right way.

Q. Do you feel secure in area (A)?

A. More or less, yes.

Q. We hear about Hamas and the Muslim Brothers…are they the same?

A. The name is not very important. We are Muslim activists intent on the reinstitution of the political authority of Islam. The Brothers created the Islamic movement in Egypt. They were the main body. And then Hamas appeared, an indigenous Palestinian plant, but in the final analysis, we are the Muslim Brothers' strong arm.

Q. Is the Muslim Brotherhood an asset or a liability for you?

A. We are quite proud to be associated with them.

Q. Does Hamas have a say in the Shura Council of the Worldwide Muslim Brothers?

A. These are administrative arrangements, otherwise there is administrative independence in each area. But ultimately we are inextricably entwined because our goal is the same.

Q. Is their coordination between Hamas and other Muslim movements?

A. There is a modicum of coordination, but not at the decision-making level.

Q. Doesn't this vindicate the PA charges that Hamas acts at the beck and call of foreign powers?

A. These are false allegations. The PLO itself has contacts with the outside. We have to remember that an important part of our people are in the Diaspora.

Q. Is Hamas Palestinian first, or Islamic first?

A. According to Hamas's charter, Hamas is a Palestinian liberation movement based on Islam.

Q. How could a Palestinian citizen join Hamas?

A. He can find the way, I am sure about that.

Q. Some say Hamas has stopped recruiting members and mainly depends on popular support?

A. Hamas is an Islamic movement with its own peculiar organizational apparatus; anything without organization is doomed to failure.

Q. Can a Japanese Muslim join Hamas?

A. Hamas welcomes any Muslim wishing to work for the Palestinian Cause. We would also welcome a Christian if he accepted the Charter.

Q. Many think that the Islamists' entry into the struggle was late, is this true?

A. This is nonsense. In fact the Islamic movement entered the struggle before the nationalists. We all know the heroic struggle of the Ikhwan in Palestine in 1948 and how the bankrupt regimes responded to that heroism by arresting the Mujahideen. Indeed, the assassination of Imam Hasan al-Banna was a reprisal against his declaration of Jihad against the Zionists and the British.

Q. Why did you decide to join the intifadah?

A. We are those who triggered the intifadah, and if you carefully examine the pre-intifadah period, you will find that there were Islamist fighting cells: one headed by Sheikh Ahmed Yassin. We felt we had to intervene and do something because the Palestinian situation was deteriorating rapidly.

Q. But don't you feel that the intifadah eventually led to this Oslo situation?

A. Maybe.

Q. Do you regret the intifadah?

A. We can never regret our jihad against the occupiers of our homeland.

Q. Who are the leaders of the Islamic movement in Palestine?

A. They are prominent Muslim politicians, but I don't know who they are, I don't know them since this is a secret matter.

Q. How are the leaders chosen in Hamas?

A. Through the institutions of the movement.

Q. Is there a real separation between Hamas' political and military wings?

A. Yes, there is a very real separation.

Q. From time to time, we hear about Hamas of the inside and Hamas of the outside; is there such a thing?

A. This is largely a concocted matter. Hamas is one with members inside and outside the homeland, just like the PLO was before the establishment of the PA.

Q. Does Hamas have a presence in Lebanon?

A. Yes.

Q. How do you control the movements' financial operations?

A. There are internal bylaws and regulations, and there are certain punishments for violators , such as dismissal and freezing of membership.

Q. Do you support killing Palestinians who act as informers for Israel?

A. Each case is examined on its merits. There can be no generalization here.

Q. Was Talal Sider dismissed from Hamas?

A. You have to make a distinction between dismissing somebody from Hamas and banishing him from the ranks of Islam. Sider is still a Muslim brother.

Q. How do you view him and others like him?

A. They are members of the PA, that is enough.

Q. Can they return to Hamas?

A. I don't think they will want to, but in case they opted to, the institutions of the movement would decide.

Q. How would you describe your relations with other Islamic movements, such as Hizbul Tahrir (the Islamic Liberation Party)?

A. We deeply respect other Islamic movements, since we believe our way is one and our enemy is one and our goal is one.

Q. And your relations with the Authority?

A. The Authority is our oppressor, but again we don't like to accentuate this too much, since our conflict is with Israel. Hamas is the ultimate safety valve that prevents the Palestinian society from sliding into a civil war.

Q. How many Hamas' activists are currently imprisoned in PA custody?

A. About 150.

Q. And in Israel?

A. 60% of all Palestinian prisoners in Israel are affiliated with Hamas.

Q. Is Hamas popular now?

A. Just look at the universities. We control most of the student councils there.

Q. Will you take part in a general election if held?

A. We will cross that bridge when we arrive there.

Since Apologies Are In Order

By Erik Paul

One can only admire the pilgrimage of ailing Pope John Paul II to the Holy Land. That this person undertook such a difficult and demanding journey is incredible. One cannot help but admire, also, the good and noble deeds of a man who has been committed to peace and love.

In an historic visit to Israel’s Yad Vashem Memorial, the Pope publicly mourned the allegedly millions of Jews killed during the Holocaust. In a solemn voice, he stated, “As Bishop of Rome and Successor of the Apostle Peter, I assure the Jewish people that the Catholic Church...is deeply saddened by the hatred, the acts of persecution and the displays of anti-Semitism directed against the Jews by Christians at any time and in my place.”

Jews were not the only victims of Hitler’s Nazi regime. And that millions of Jews were killed during the Holocaust is also debatable. Many European Jews fled from fear of persecution and later returned to their homes after the Second World War was over. The Pope is not responsible for what Hitler did just as the Palestinian people are not responsible either and they should not have to be the new victims of the “Israeli Holocaust” being waged on them.

It is not just, civilized or rational that a people who had nothing to do with the persecution of Jews in Europe during centuries of discrimination have to pay the price by losing their homes, their land, their money, their occupations, and even, at times, their physical safety or lives. How many Palestinians live in the Diaspora now? How many Palestinians have been slaughtered for no reason other than the fact that they are the indigenous inhabitants of their own land? What right, morally, religiously or legally do Jews from all over the world have to come and take a whole country away from its rightful owners? When Iraq occupied Kuwait not so long ago, there were more than 30 nations involved in bombing the Iraqi civilians to pieces! Where is this same sense of warped justice when it comes to the Palestinians? How can any group of people just go and occupy the country of another? Palestine has been Palestinian for over 2,000 years. For it suddenly to not exist does not make sense. And when one speaks of Palestinians, it includes Christian, Jewish and Muslim Arabs. It must be remembered that these people lived in relative peace before the advent of the European Jews who were looking for not only a scapegoat in the Palestinian people, but also looking to make them victims of their own expediency.

How many Palestinian children have grown up as orphans because their fathers died as martyrs from the atrocities committed by Israeli soldiers and civilians? Is it fair for humanity to cry tears for the persecution committed against one group of people and then to turn a blind eye to the slaughter and attempts at genocide of another? At the memorial where the Pope attended and presented his apology, there were six Jewish survivors of the Holocaust...each one supposedly representing the six million killed in the genocide. These Jews and others around them cried for what happened to Jews during Hitler’s regime. But who will cry for the Palestinians so ruthlessly and brutally killed by the Israeli regime? How many memorials will be erected for those Palestinians who have been butchered for no other reason except that they were Palestinian? Who will cry for the Palestinian children who have been crippled by Israeli bullets and who will cry for Palestinian children who are incarcerated when they should be out playing and just enjoying the luxury of being children? Who will cry for all those Palestinian children who were so heartlessly massacred? Who will cry for the mothers who have lost their children or who cannot sleep at night because their sons are in prison? Who can even comprehend how countless Palestinians have been physically and emotionally tortured by the Israeli Shin Bet (and who put Hitler’s SS to shame), the same group who are devoted to breaking the spirit and the will of the Palestinians?

If anyone should apologize, it should not be Pope John Paul II for crimes he is not guilty of, but it should rather be the Israelis for all the dark and inhuman deeds that they have perpetrated against the Palestinians. And even then, an apology can only be the beginning of a path to justice and peace. As long as Palestinians continue to suffer at the hands of the Israelis and as long as Jews confiscate more and more Palestinian land, there will never be a true, just or comprehensive peace in the Middle East. Peace must be built on justice and decency. If there is no justice and if the Israelis are not decent, whatever peace they speak of with their forked tongues hangs on a very shaky foundation and will soon crumble into disaster.

Damned Are Those Who Win a War
and Lose to a Verse of Poetry

By Ramzy Baroud*

Books found at exhibitions are often categorized in a specific order, usually based on topic, theme or even price and bargain. Palestinian book exhibits are also categorized, yet differently, according to a unique priority and order. Just like almost every aspect of Palestinian life, their ongoing struggle against Israel, their quest for freedom and painful memories are the dominant messages conveyed by most of their published books. A component of Israeli propaganda aimed at covering the tracks of the many crimes committed against Palestinians was to silence their voices, sensor their books, shut down their newspapers, and even assassinate their intellectuals. It should come as a no surprise that Israeli Education Minister Yossi Sarid’s idea to use several pieces of prose and poetry written by Palestinian poet Mahmoud Darwish in the Israeli school curriculum has threatened to topple the Israeli government.

At first it appeared bizarre, absurd and even comical when Israeli television channels showed angry Israeli parliament members resorting to bantering and name-calling, and even urging to vote down the government over a few lines of poetry written by Darwish. The story, however, is neither comical nor amusing. The Israeli outrage and panic is, without a doubt, a sign of the deficiency in their Zionist ideology, of the fragility of their moral stand and, first and foremost, of the fear of the truth, which they have hardly sought to identify.

In my first visit to the Palestinian Writers Union in Gaza in the early 1990s, I was stunned to see that most of their posted books were written from inside prison walls. In fact, the phenomenon is so old and common that Palestinian literature is known as “the literature of prisons.”

Assuming that we are able to understand why Israel would imprison a freedom fighter or a child caught with a rock in his hand, why do so many Palestinian poets, writers and journalists spend many years of their lives in prison, without trial and without any clear reason, except that of ‘threatening the security of the State of Israel’? What does the Palestinian pen have that could threaten the existence of a State which obtains enough nuclear capabilities to burn half of this world? The truth…maybe?

Throughout the years, since its establishment on the ruins of Palestinian towns and villages, Israel aimed at destroying the Palestinian account of the story. But its mission was no easy task, since most Palestinians were banished to every corner of the world seeking refuge and hoping for return.

It is as if the Israeli scheme meant to eliminate those who are most likely to eloquently and skilfully uncover and diminish the Israeli myth. Those who possess a great threat, of course, are the intellectuals who possess the power to awaken the conscience and the minds of the people to Israel’s fabrications.

Between July of 1972 and July of 1973, over a dozen Palestinian intellectuals were murdered by exploding letters or car bombs in many world capitals. The implementation of this tactic was hardly designed to destroy the intellect of an entire nation. Israel meant to send another message, beside the exploding ones. It was a warning signal to those who would try to challenge Israel’s perspective on the legacy. The warning simply advised that anyone who would try to contest Israel’s reading of history would collide with the same destiny as those Palestinian intellectuals.

Unlike what many believe, Israel’s argument and justification of historical events is shabby, inconsistent, and too irrational even for unenlightened philosophy. In a balanced world, the Israeli narration can hardly stand the simple yet convincing logic of Palestinian intellect. And because Israeli leaders have always been aware of such a fact, they worked, and are still working ceaselessly to forbid the Palestinian word from reaching those who were never given the chance to hear it.

Strangely enough, the past 52 years, which served to nurture and strengthen the philosophy of Zionism—the mother of current Israeli ideology—have failed to provide even a small “security zone” that would grant minimum protection from the power of reason.

The recent political crisis in Israel created by Sarid’s decision to use some of Darwish's poetry was not exactly political nor was it inspired by a personal disliking of the Palestinian poetic style. At any rate, it was understandable. In a court where the criminal was being set free for lack of evidence, it would be absurd if his lawyer allowed the testimony of a witness that might reverse the verdict and find him guilty.

With little means, Palestinians have courageously fought and many continue to fight. While the Intifada challenged the legitimacy of the occupation with its troops of children possessing only weapons of stones and small Palestinian flags, it defeated that suppression. Now Israeli ideology faces the weapons of Palestinian prose and literature, and again, it will eventually be defeated. How amazing and pathetic for a country to withstand many wars and revolutions…only to crumble under a few verses of poetry.

*Ramzy Baroud is a Seattle-based Palestinian. For more about Ramzy’s writings, visit The Palestine Chronicle at www.palestinechronicle.com

I Cannot Visit My Father’s Grave

By Samia Abdennour

Samia Abdennour, a Palestinian refugee, recalls the diaspora of her family 50 years ago, its impact on her as a child and, later, as a mother.

One bright morning in June of 1947, my mother woke up and exclaimed, “Children, I have been giving the matter a lot of thought. We are going to Egypt.” My father had died five years before, leaving us well taken care of financially, though not rich. My eldest sister and brother had graduated from high school and wanted to enrol at university, and the options we had were either Cairo or Beirut. My mother’s choice was agreeable to us all. We fully intended to return back (to Palestine) after completing our studies, and left the keys of our home with an uncle to look after the house. Early in August, we bade our adieus to family and friends and boarded the train that ran from Haifa directly to Cairo, to the land of glory and learning.

Upon arriving at Bab El-Hadid, our eager faces turned to bewilderment. It was past midnight, yet the station was buzzing with life. Crowds were bustling here and there, porters were shouting and pushing, peddlers were singing their wares and the neon lights cast a greenish glow that made people look grotesque. My aunt and uncle came to meet us and drove us by hantour to their home in Choubra. It was a memorable night.

Within less than a week, we found and rented a beautiful villa in Heliopolis. My eldest sister, Aida, and brother, Farouk, were accepted by the Faculties of Art and Pharmacy respectively. Amal, Souhail and myself were enrolled in school and all our misgivings were allayed. Many relatives, whom we children did not know, came to visit and through them we met and befriended many families with children of our own ages. Also, before long, we became friends with all our neighbours. Life was smiling at us.

Most of the cities and towns in Palestine were small, and practically everybody in town knew everybody else. We grew up in the friendly atmosphere of a large family. Moreover, both a maternal and a paternal aunt had died in childbirth, and my mother did not hesitate for a second to accept both infants to be raised with her brood. For many years, we were seven boisterous children living under the same roof.

As soon as we settled in our new premises, the entire family began an intensive correspondence with our friends in Haifa, Nablus and Jerusalem. The postman came to know us by name, and nearly every day he would smilingly call to deliver one or more letters. Even the Post Office officials at headquarters got so used to the sudden influx of letters bearing our name that they directed a letter to my sister which was simply addressed: Miss Aida Abdennour, Heliopolis, Egypt.

Though we settled down quickly to a happy, normal life, we were apprehensive for our loved ones left behind in these disturbing times. We did not really appreciate the horrors they were living through, until the unexpected arrival of our grandmother, aunt and cousin Chibly. They described to us the nightmare they had suffered while still living in Haifa and related why they had fled to the safety of our home in Egypt.

A group of well-armed Zionists raided the area where my grandmother and her two married children lived with their families. The whole family ran to the shelter of their basement. Some Arab residents, poorly armed, retaliated and incurred very heavy losses. Following this barbaric attack, the Zionists broke into the three-storey building firing left and right, smashing whatever was in sight—furniture, windows, crockery, doors—leaving behind only rubble.

We were horrified to hear of such a barbaric monstrosity and welcomed them with open arms, relieved that they were safe. But we worried over the rest of my uncle’s and aunt’s families. Eventually we heard from them a couple of months later, from Lebanon and Syria. Unlike my grandmother, they had stayed behind trying to salvage some of their belongings. But when the tension grew too strong to bear, they made their escape on foot, in a small rowing boat and on the back of a donkey.

It was hard to believe that this was the country and the people we had left just a few months ago. Palestinians—Muslims, Christians and Jews—had faced no problems living together. They had lived amicably, sharing traditions, interests and experiences. All our cities—Jerusalem, Haifa, Jaffa, Nazareth—held very dear heirlooms to all three religions. The arrival of the Zionists changed this peaceful coexistence. How could these emigrants profane this sacred land? What right did these monsters have to come from the four corners of the world and usurp our homes?

Haifa, where I was born and spent my early childhood, holds many cherished memories. It is a jewel of a city. It extends from the top of Mount Carmel, sloping gracefully to the sea, combining both mountain and sea air. Its scenery is lovely. None of the houses rose higher than three storeys, allowing everyone to enjoy the gorgeous panorama. Traffic was very well organized, we queued at bus stations and I remember the decorous manner with which the police officers directed the flow of the cars. There were public gardens, playgrounds, tennis courts and beaches. All were kept clean and functioning. Our school, Notre Dame de Nazareth, was built on the mountain slope, surrounded by huge trees which we used to climb mischievously to hide from the nuns and their punishments.

My father owned a plot of land on the slope of Mount Carmel, planted mainly with olive trees, with a few scattered citrus and carob trees. There was also a two-storey house and small shack for the keeper. We did not actually live there, but rented it to a Jewish family. Before coming to Egypt, we lived for a few years in Nablus owing to my father’s work. However, whenever we were in Haifa, we visited our tenants and played on the swing my father had installed on the lower porch.

The climax, of course, was May 1948. The Zionists took over our beautiful country and in Cairo we suddenly found ourselves destitute. The regular monetary transfers that we had arranged from Haifa came abruptly to a stop. The problem of feeding nine people—six growing children with very healthy appetites—was no simple matter.

Believing this to be a temporary episode, we started by selling mother’s jewelry. One by one her jewels were sold but still no silver lining appeared on the horizon. We then set about, each in his or her own way, to find a solution. My mother, who normally sewed our dresses, became a dressmaker, sewing first the uniforms for our school, then accepting clients at home. Aida left her faculty and taught in two different schools, also doing secretarial work in the evenings. Whenever his time permitted, Farouk worked in a pharmacy during the day and paid home visits in the evenings to give injections. My aunt taught kindergarten and my grandmother took over the task of housekeeping and cooking. The nuns at our school very kindly and discreetly allowed my sister and I to finish our studies without paying tuition fees. During the summer holidays, we all worked. The twins, Souhail and Amal, painted wooden toys in a toy factory, while I helped a newly established shop with its advertising campaign. I was given an old Remington typewriter (with the letter Q missing), a few cartons filled with envelopes, the Who’s Who in Egypt and the Middle East book, and was told to type out the names and addresses found therein. I was paid the exorbitant amount of half a piastre for every four typed envelopes. Quite a score—one Egyptian pound for every 800 typed envelopes!

My mother firmly believed in higher education, and she also believed in priorities. Men came foremost in this respect . She held the opinion that they are the breadwinners and must be “armed with all the necessary equipment to ensure a happy and comfortable life for their families,” whereas women depend on their husbands for their livelihood. Therefore, her daughters, falling under the second category, worked, while the boys went to university. We did not feel we were making a sacrifice, nor were we envious of our brothers and cousin, but took our mother’s argument for granted and did as required.

Eventually, the male members of our family graduated from university and each found a job in his field. Our financial situation eased considerably, and Amal regained her ambition to become a painter. She went to Les Beaux Arts to further her studies, got involved with a political group and landed in jail for 28 months. Upon her release, she accepted a job with the FAO in Libya and from there travelled and settled in Paris to pursue her artistic ambitions.

We all led a normal life, except for occasional jolts. One such incident concerned my brother Souhail and his residence visa. Palestinians, like all foreigners, were required to obtain an annual residence visa. One of the clauses in the application necessitated a written letter from his employer. The time for the renewal of the visa coincided with a time when Souhail was not on good terms with his superior. The latter seized the opportunity and wrote a letter to the effect that Souhail was not in the least indispensable. This resulted in the Passport Authorities issuing an order for Souhail’s deportation within a few days. It was a catastrophe! We argued our Statelessness and pleaded with the authorities, but to no avail. As a last resort, Souhail sent a telegram to President Nasser outlining his case, whereupon the order was cancelled and the visa immediately granted.

Shortly afterwards, Souhail went to Lebanon in search of work. He applied and obtained Lebanese nationality, based on his argument that we were part of a Lebanese family bearing the same surname. He then accepted a job in Doha, Qatar, which he left after three months to join cousin Chibly in Kuwait. Returning to Lebanon, he got married and settled with his wife and two children, only to be ousted once again during the Lebanese civil war. He now lives in Jordan.

Farouk was quite content living in Cairo, until the emigration bug hit his family—wife and two sons. To comply with their nagging, he applied and obtained his emigration papers to Canada, but assured everybody he was coming back. He accompanied the family to Montreal, but with the exception of his younger son, Nadir, both his wife and elder son disliked life in Montreal and returned to the safety of Egypt and its people. While helping Nadir settle in his new surroundings, he discovered that he had very advanced lung cancer. He returned to Cairo for one week, straightened his finances, allotting all his material possessions to his family, bade us good-bye and returned to the hospital in Canada where he passed away.

Aida is married to an Egyptian and lives in Paris where, before retirement, she held an important position at UNESCO. Our mother passed away ten years ago and is much missed by her family and the Palestinian organizations in Cairo with which she worked. She had helped organize and supervise workshops where young girls did knitting and needlework and also acted as interpreter whenever foreign delegations visited their organizations.

Now, nearly 51 years have passed since our arrival in Egypt and I feel exceedingly happy and very lucky in many ways. My only regret is that our family is so dispersed. I have uncles and aunts living in Lebanon, England, Switzerland, Honduras, Canada, the U.S., Paris, Jordan, Syria and have lost contact with most of them. I was pleasantly surprised to discover, two years ago, that the wife of the Ambassador of Honduras was my cousin, whom I had last seen in August 1947. I also feel sad that my children hardly know their uncles, aunts and cousins, which is a far cry from the happy, clannish way we were brought up.

On war crimes, confessions and justice

By Dr. Daud Abdullah*

“I bow my head in humility before those who were murdered, those who have no graves at which I could ask their forgiveness.” These were the words of German President, Johannes Rau, as he addressed the Israeli Knesset on 17 February 2000. Somehow, they did not sound like the words of a wily old politician seeking a convenient appeasement. While they may have met certain demands, they also opened a vault of skeletons, long buried but not forgotten.

What of the other victims? For how much longer will the world continue to cover up and ignore the crimes committed by the Nazi victims against the Palestinian victims? One of the most appalling aspects of the Palestinian catastrophe is, undoubtedly, the denial of their humanity and history. Every attempt on their part to tell their narrative has been met with distortion, denial and outright scorn. For those who insist on refuting the Palestinian version, we refer them to one of Britain’s finest historians of the twentieth century, Arnold J. Toynbee.

Writing the forward to John & Hadawi’s Palestine Diary (1970), he declared, “The story is a tragedy, and the essence of this tragedy is that about 1,500,000 Palestinian Arabs (estimated today at 5,000,000) have now become refugees as a result of the intervention of foreign powers in their country’s affairs…The tragedy in Palestine is not just a local one; it is a tragedy for the World, because it is an injustice that is a menace to the World’s peace. Britain’s guilt is not diminished by the humiliating fact that she is now impotent to redress the wrong that she has done. On the face of it, the Jewish State and its allies prefer that it be judged not by its deeds but by its intent. And since its motive is to “form a part of a wall of defence for Europe in Asia, an outpost of civilization against barbarism”, it has become quite easy for Westerners to forget the massacres, the war crimes and crimes against humanity that were committed in April 1948. It is even more understandable why they perceived the murder of women and children at Deir Yassin as “self-defence”, “accidental” or “collateral damage”. The first victim of the 1973 operations was Dr. Basil al-Kubaisi—gunned down on a Paris street on 6 April. Writing in their study of Israel’s Secret Wars, Benny Morris and Ian Black declared, “The next blow against the PLO was far more important” (p. 469). It came on 16 April 1988. The victim was Khalil al-Wazir, murdered in his Tunis home by “Israeli commandos”, according to the authors. Had the operation been the reverse, the perpetrators would certainly have been “Palestinian terrorists.” Significantly, Mr. Morris is widely acclaimed today as a pioneer of Israel’s “New Historians.”

It started on 11 April 1996 with the bombing of the southern suburbs of Beirut, the Bekaa Valley and Baalbek. Although it was not carried out by “commandos” as Benny Morris would prefer to call them, it was the handiwork of the even more curiously named “Israeli Defense Forces.” When it ended on 27 April, 165 persons were killed in Lebanon, 401 wounded, 23,500 shells were fired and 600 air raids were conducted during the 16 days. By all accounts, the most revolting act of the entire operation was the massacre of 109 civilians sheltering in the United Nations compound at Qana on 18 April.

It preceded a general election in Israel that was dominated by the issue of security and peace. For all its ferocity, though, Operation Grapes of Wrath failed to secure the re-election of incumbent Prime Minister Peres. His nemesis, Benyamin Netanyahu, skilfully portrayed him as a man who had no answers to the growing audacity of Hamas at home and Hizbullah abroad. A former member of Israel’s special forces and editor of a book entitled, Terrorism: How the West Can Win (1986), Netanyahu appeared eminently qualified for the job—not that he was any different from the man he succeeded.

Mr. Netanyahu’s mission was plain and simple: to ensure that Israel’s self-defence prevailed over Palestinian self-determination. Toward this end, the support of the West was more important than any agreement with the Palestinians. This mind-set was well-illustrated by the title of his book. For even though he served as his country’s Ambassador to the United Nations, Netanyahu gave no consideration to the fact that since 1973 the organization had proclaimed that international terrorism also consists of repressive acts by colonial, racist or alien regimes against people struggling for liberation. Resilient as the olive tree

The tragedy of their suffering is not just a matter of a few refugee camps here and there. It is, moreover, the agony of an entire people at whose expense a foreign State was established on their land. While the Jewish State has recognized the territorial nature of the conflict, it has, ever since the 1940s, rejected the extra-territorial dimension: that there is a Palestinian diaspora. One that is legally and morally entitled to the right of return to its homes, wherever that may have been in Palestine before their expulsion.

Palestinians in the diaspora, such as the eminent academics and professionals who, last February, reaffirmed this right of return in an international declaration, have already been condemned as “enemies of peace.” To become friends of peace, they must support the type of political settlement described by Israel’s Minister of Public Security, Shlomo Ben-Ami, during his recent visit to London, in which the conflict would be watered down to a local “border dispute” between the Jewish State and the Palestinian entity in the occupied territories. As if the Palestinian nation had sacrificed thousands of martyrs only to settle for their permanent exile in foreign lands.

All the declarations and press releases concerning the current Middle East peace process suggest that Israel’s aim is to win Arab support while blocking their support to the Palestinians. Any progress in this direction, clearly, spells more disaster for the Palestinian Cause. For just as Arab division led to the loss of Palestine, likewise their disunity will prevent its restoration. Whatever the outcome, this would not break the will of the people who, like the native olive tree of Palestine, cannot be totally expunged from the land.

Surely any process which views the Palestinians more as pawns in a political jigsaw and less than human beings will not succeed. Likewise, any peace process that is not based on international law will only compound the problem. It would not stop the cycle of human suffering. And so, even before the law is enforced, Israel must take another giant step. Like President Rau did on behalf of his people, it must genuinely acknowledge its culpability in crimes against the Palestinian people and against humanity. Upon this premise, let the law be implemented to protect the weak.

Oslo And The Demise Of The Two State Solution

Dr. Naseer Aruri*

(1-2)

There is a new reality, unwittingly produced by the Oslo accords, a reality that had escaped the minds of many who watched the “historic” signing either in person or on the screen from around the globe four and a half years ago. The requirements for a just and durable peace are far different today from what they used to be. These accords have dealt a crippling blow to the foundations of the global consensus that defined the prerequisites for a just and durable peace during the 1970s and 80s. That peace was predicated on the right of the Palestinian people to establish their own independent state alongside Israel. That peace was to occur after Israel completed its withdrawal from occupied territories in accordance with U.N. Security Council resolution 242, and after the Palestinians recognized Israel's existence and sovereignty in the largest part of their own national patrimony.
Six years after the "historic handshake," it has become clear that the pursuit of a negotiated settlement based on two states has probably run its course. That project has been dealt a severe blow by a colossal imbalance of power, by a steady and growing Israelization of American Middle East policy, by a vigorous drive of settler colonization, by Arab disarray and failure to respond to the Israeli challenge, and by the exigencies of the post-Cold-War era. The Oslo process has demonstrated that the so-called partners not only are far apart conceptually but are hopelessly divided over interpretations and over what the end results of the process should be. We have seen one agreement after another; from Oslo I to Oslo II, from Cairo I to Cairo II, from Early Empowerment to the disempowerment of the Hebron agreement, to the Wye River agreement, and now to the Sharm al-Shaykh agreement, in which Arafat seems to have accepted, by implication, that U.N. resolutions may not have constituted the basis for a final settlement. During the past year, we have witnessed how the lone superpower had to employ vigorous diplomatic resources to persuade Netanyahu, and then Barak, to meet partial commitments under the Hebron and Wye Agreements. Clearly, these are agreements not meant for implementation. They are drafted in such a way as to enable Israel to conquer territory and to displace and dispossess under no-war conditions. Thousands of dunams of land have been confiscated and thousands of Palestinians have been dispossessed since the Oslo signing, while the built-in impasse continues unabated. It has proven to be most efficacious for Israel, which supplies the drafts and invests in deliberate ambiguity.

Oslo was a unique agreement that lacked a framework defining the rules of negotiations and the ends of the negotiating process. There is neither an overarching principle, a vision, nor a road map. The Palestinians were invited to discover whether they have rights, but not to claim their internationally-guaranteed rights. They came on the implicit assumption that Israel's military occupation does not even exist, and, therefore, Israel's claim to the disputed (but not occupied) territories was at least as good as theirs.

The Israeli side, however, knows exactly what it wants; the Palestinian side dreams about what it wants. The Israeli side continues to adhere to the letter - their own letter - of the agreement; the Palestinian side continues to invoke the spirit. The letter says that the enterprise is a mere agreement to reach agreement that Palestinian sovereignty with jurisdiction over land and resources and at the points of entry and exit is utterly out of the question; that Jerusalem is an Israeli city; and that the Palestinians' struggle for emancipation- from the status of refugees and that of an occupied people - will be dependent on Israeli good will, when and if final status negotiations are held, and after the Palestine Authority complies with ever expanding requirements, which totally negate all prospects for Palestinian emancipation. The letter of Oslo renders the goal of Palestinian statehood impractical and obsolete; and yet the Palestinian Oslo dream continues to hang on the spirit, which is nothing more than a thin thread of hope devoid of any substance.

Paradoxically, the Oslo process has led to an inevitable conclusion, which its own architects had neither envisaged, contemplated, nor pursued, namely, the future struggle is toward integration not separation, toward a pluralistic existence, not exclusion, toward parity, mutuality common humanity, and a common destiny. This is the new and important reality that the Oslo process

has generated. Ironically, this reality might be laying the foundations for a joint Palestinian-Israeli struggle, which would emanate from a realization that the lives of Palestinians and Israelis are inextricably intertwined. There is a common interest in the economy, employment, water distribution, ecology, human rights, and foreign relations. Readiness to translate this commonality into a structural framework that would enable both people to derive equal benefits is not in the offing at the present, nor is it likely to be in the near future.

Even if the Oslo process were to lead miraculously to some kind of a breakthrough, the maximum gain for the Palestinians would be a fractured collection of "bantustans" or non-contiguous enclaves on about 35-40 percent of the West Bank and on about 65 percent of Gaza. Moreover, under most optimal conditions, with social engineering similar to what was supplied and designed by Yossi Bellin and Mahmoud Abbas (Abu-Mazin), something called Palestine may emerge, but will be only nominally independent. As is well-known, independence had already been ruled out by the Labor-Likud agreement of January 22,1997, entitled "National Agreement Regarding the Negotiations on the Permanent Settlement with The Palestinians." That document excluded Palestinian sovereignty, removal of the settlements, negotiating Jerusalem, repatriating the refugees, and dismantling the occupation, thereby upholding the status-quo but with a great deal of cosmetic surgery. The difference between the surgeons of Likud and those of Labor is that the latter are better prepared to disguise the structural flaws and to exaggerate the beauty.

And to those who argue that Labor's classical Zionist doctrine, which espouses separation, would grant the Palestinians their separate political existence, I say that the doctrine of separation has already been adapted to Likud's notion of "population mixture." This "mixture" idea was enunciated by Begin and Shamir. Inherent in the autonomy scheme, it now translates into cantonization. Rabin's peace, which Arafat often describes as a peace of the brave, has converged with Netanyahu's process on bantustanization for 1/3 of the Palestinian people, leaving the other 2/3 living in permanent exile or as second class citizens in Israel. This enterprise has been discussed rather candidly by Likud supporter, William Safire of the New York Times (September 10, 1997), who wrote that the map of a workable final deal stares us all in the face: A Palestinian flag in a majority of the West Bank land and 98 percent of its people, with road tunnels and over-passes making the new state's large enclaves contiguous and independent of Israel.

The key words in Safire's statement are, of course, enclaves, tunnels, and overpasses, which negate contiguity and independence. Since Oslo II, the Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza have begun to realize that they are residents of enclaves "separated" from each other and from Israel, but indeed part of a "greater Israel." They are separated from the settlements, from Jerusalem, from each other's cities and even villages, and from the Palestinian diaspora. This fragmentation is now becoming social, economic, physical, and of course regional, despite Oslo's provision for a single-unit Palestinian entity.

In view of all this, if something called a state were to emerge, it would be economically strangled by Israel, financially dominated by the World Bank and the IMF, constrained by the requirements of globalization, militarily under Israel's mercy and always anxious to please Washington and the international donors. Hence it will have to be intolerant and repressive toward dissent, which has already been re-classified as "terrorism.” It will be pressed to seek a confederal relationship with Jordan and some kind of association with Israel, in which a Middle East version of NAFTA with maquiladoras would serve as a symbol of development. Moreover, the price of that fictitious statehood would include yet a new deferral of two key issues in the so-called final status negotiations: Jerusalem and the refugees. This proposal was made by Ehud Barak on September 1, 1999.

Quite clearly this is not what the Palestinian people have struggled for. Nor is their struggle for an independent state within the Oslo framework likely to succeed, for all the reasons already cited. This brings us to the new reality emerging out of Oslo's inherent flaws. This is a new discourse about a broader social-economic struggle for equal rights, equal citizenship, and equal legitimacy within a single Israeli-Palestinian entity. It is seen by a growing number of people on both sides as a viable alternative to perpetual conflict. On the Palestinian side for example, Edward Said advanced the idea in an interview in the Christian Science Monitor (May 27, 1997):

“The whole idea of trying to produce two states is at an end. The Oslo peace process is really in tatters. The lives of Israelis and Palestinians are hopelessly intertwined. There is no way to separate them. You can have fantasy and denial, or put people in ghettos. But in reality there is a common history. So we have to find a way to live together. It may take 50 years. But ... the Israeli experience will gradually turn back towards the world they really live in, the Islamic Arab world. And that can only come through Palestinians.”

Other Palestinian intellectuals, including Nadim Rouhana and Adel Samara, have been calling for a single state. Samara writes:

“The only just and feasible form of a bi-national state is the socialist one. It will have to be a state that will dissolve the Zionist regime, ... cancel the Law of Return, stop importing new settlers, and guarantee the Palestinian right of return and equality of land and resources (News From Within, April, 1997).

On the Israeli side, endorsement of hi-nationalism comes from an unusually divergent group, including liberal politicians, secular leftists, and right-wing rabbis. For example, Meron Benvinisti writes:

“The reality in Eretz Israel is a bi-national one. The reality inside the green line is also bi-national. ...The model closest to my heart is Belgium. Two people, the Flemings and the Walloons; two regional governments, and one central government. The direction I would prefer is cantonization, the division of Eretz Israel West of the Jordan River into Jewish and Arab cantons; I want it to be clear that I include the Galillee and the Triangle in this proposal.”

Haim Baram, a secular leftist, who is known to Western audiences through his regular columns in Middle East International, has also adopted the idea of bi-nationalism in order to avert an apartheid regime. On the religious right, we learn from an article by Yair Sheleg (News From Within, March, 1997) that Rabbi Menachem Fruman supports the idea of hi-nationalism on the grounds that it would guarantee the "wholeness of the land of Israel" as well as the continuation of the settlements in the West Bank:

“ I prefer loyalty to the land over loyalty to the state. I see the whole Israel movement as a post-Zionist movement, which represents an advance for Zionism over what it is today.”

Fruman expresses no apologies, however, for advocating two legal standards in the single state - one for Jews and one for Arabs. Apartheid has its roots in the hierarchy of classes during the Middle Ages. He adds: “If you want, you can write down that I want to take us back to the ‘darkness’ of the Middle Ages.”

Obviously it is difficult to propose a blue print for bi-nationalism or to even debate at this juncture the democratic secular state versus the bi-national state; suffice it to say that the two people have been connected and are therefore being challenged to explore the basis for a common existence, the proper modalities, the redress of grievances, and paths to a common vision.

Islamic Thought and the Issue of Globalization*

Zaki al-Milad

(3-3)

In the view of George Tarabishi, the concept of globalization has not been established yet, either in the English language in which the term was invented, or in the various world languages, including Arabic, where scholars have difficulty in translating the concept. Without having consensus on the meaning of globalization, the derivation of the word itself suggests contradictory aspects of nationalism. Globalization is a historical phenomenon of the end of the 20th century and the beginning of the 21st century in the same way that nationalism in economics, politics, and culture was a phenomenon of the end of the 19th and the beginning of the 20th century.14

Jaber Asfour contends that, "The meaning of the term 'globalization' does not diverge from the meaning of compartmentalization, which means imposing a framework. The original derivation of both terms goes to the form fa'ala, which connotes imposition. The connotation of force or imposition inherent in the derivation of globalization, denoting compartmentalization or patriotism in one framework or a bigger system, extends to the whole globe, and it does not differ from the realities of modern serfdom."15

Arabic writers have tried to present a description and analysis of a phenomenon produced by another entity. The picture of globalization is not yet complete in the Arab world, which lives its effects. Thus it is difficult to describe this phenomenon, whose very nature is complex and interconnected. This explains the differences in opinion in these writings, which have not gone beyond their own structural weaknesses, due to the excessive dependence on issues and concepts produced by more advanced nations that are increasing not decreasing in conceptual distance. There is a large and deep historical and temporal and civilizational division between our world and the world of the advanced nations. The phenomena that reach us from there take our time and use up our attention, even though they have no real presence in our world. The way we concern ourselves with globalization gives the impression that it is we who have conceived of it, even though the reality is otherwise.

Arabic writers do not comprehend what they want from globalization, and do not identify its dimensions and traits in a quantitative and qualitative manner, nor do they agree on its nature and philosophy and movement and temporal framework. The methodological and structural imbalance that occurred with previous concepts will repeat itself. Before we complete our view of globalization, and understand its dimensions and variations in our present reality, and know what we want from it, and know how to respond to it, we will be faced with a different and critical view from the West stemming from post-globalization or the end of globalization, in the same way that previous terms were addressed, such as modernity and post-modernity, industrial society and post-industrial society, philosophy and the end of philosophy, and history and the end of history. We will be caught up in the discussion of post-globalization or the end of globalization if it originates in the West, since it is the source of legitimacy for these terms, as far as the misguided writings are concerned, until a new term comes to us from the West, and we will end up repeating the cycle.

Islamic Thought and Globalization

There is still a long way to go before the Islamic writers begin to address modern issues and phenomena. There is a delay in addressing these concerns in terms of description, analysis, and critique. This exposes the lack of understanding in this literature. Globalization has been an area of concern and has engendered the widest array of discussions and arguments and critiques among world centers. Nevertheless, it still has not captured the concern of the Islamic writers in the Arab world, especially concerning the issues and phenomena that have captured the world's attention. An exception is the issue of cloning. This has attracted the attention of Islamic writers, who have weighed in with Islamic thought in this area and have garnered the attention of others. The early concern with this issue has arisen due to the highly sensitive legal and religious dimensions of cloning.

The absence of Islamic thought in dealing with contemporary issues and affairs of international importance has left a clear mark on the presence and effectiveness of this thought on the world stage. This has occurred at a time when Islam, as a religion and ideology and civilization, has attracted the widest world attention. This makes the absence of Islamic thought all the more puzzling and explains why we cannot provide a review of how Islamic literature has analyzed and critiqued globalization.

The West has presented and commented on globalization, which has scared the world, and which has received a great deal of criticism and resistance. This does not mean that globalization is the only crushing issue in the world that is imposed on nations without choice or desire. Islam contains active capabilities, moral, legal, and civilizational that are very important for expressing an alternative globalization, fundamentally different in its content, form, philosophy, and goals from Western globalization. Islam came for the world and for all people, without racism, class division, or discrimination, and it is one of the first civilizational ideologies and religions to be global.

Al Jabiri tried to differentiate between globalization, coming from the West, and universalism, coming from Islamic civilization. He states, "Globalization is one thing and universalism is another. Universalism opens up to the world, and to other cultures, and preserves ideological differences. Globalization is a negation of the other, and creates a cultural breach rather than an ideological debate."16

Islamic concepts of globalization give rise to the need for a strategy that Dr. Robert Crane, the Muslim founder of The Center for Civilizational Renewal, two decades ago called "peaceful engagement." He distinguishes this from the hostility of civilizational clash and from the indifference or passivity of peaceful coexistence.

One of the most relevant verses portraying the Islamic view in this regard is God's statement: "O people we have created you from a male and a female, and we have made you peoples and tribes so that you may know one another; verily the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most pious; verily Allah is most knowledgable and most informed."17

This verse establishes a foundation for relations among peoples and civilizations on the basis of knowing one another, and not on the basis of conflict or confrontation or negation or banishment. A theory has been put forth based on an analysis of this verse known as the theory of inter-civilizational knowing. This can be a third way in need of exploration to counter what is presented in the West as the clash of civilizations and the dialogue of civilizations. If asked a question by someone while working, he told the questioner: "Work on this irrigation canal for me while I ponder your question." The last important rabbi who fully supported such behavior was Maimonides, who died in 1204. Maimonides' ruling in his Learning Torah Laws (chapter 3, verse 10) is often quoted by secular, Jewish Israelis:

Anyone supposing that he will engage in Torah [talmudic study] and not engage in labor, thus taking his livelihood from charity should be considered a person who: has extinguished the light of religion, put Torah to shame, caused evil to himself and lost his chance to enter paradise, since it is forbidden to make profit form the sayings of Torah in this world. Many Israeli secular Jews use this statement of Maimonides to document. their contention that all rabbis especially rabbis in Israel, are robbers.

He, let his memory be blessed [Maimonides], brought the example of Hillel, who was wood- cutter while a talmudic student. This is not proof. The Economics of Zionist Peace*

Introduction

Economics as the Extension of War through Non-violent Means

In bygone days, the abundance of economic resources and a large vibrant economy were crucial prerequisites for the financing of military effort in times of war and peace. An economy served thus as a strategic reserve and helped project a nation's military power and political will onto other nations through the sheer formidability of its readiness for war.

Every economy gained in this context a security dimension. However, after the Second World War and the massive introduction of nuclear and other unconventional weapons, new interactions emerged in the synergy of the economy and the military. Nuclear dtente meant that direct military conflict between superpowers became a less viable option. Thus, economic means in international conflicts ceased to play second fiddle to military means and leaped to the forefront of the stage to become the very instrument with which engagements and wars between nations are fought.

The defeat of the Soviet Union in the Cold War aptly illustrates this point. The historical record indicates that it is not true that the Soviet Union failed totally in the economic sphere. But ultimately, it was in that sphere that the Soviet Union finally found its defeat. In fact, the Soviet system succeeded in completing the industrial revolution launched by Peter the Great and in providing for the basic needs of the Soviet peoples through the mass-production of homogeneous goods for food, clothing and housing. It succeeded furthermore in building superb technological capabilities in the field of military industry. However, where the Soviet Union failed crucially was in the economic competition in the civilian sector. This was most evident in the failure of central planning to mass-produce good-quality, non-homogeneous or durable consumer goods (such as TVs, VCRs, PCs, automobiles, etc.). A weak civilian sector meant a lower standard of living and provided fewer resources for military purposes or economic growth. The fact that the Soviets preferred to divert scarce resources to the defense industry despite this highlights the primacy of the economic factor. Subsequently, the failure to keep up with the West economically was aggravated by the severe depletion of scarce economic resources in an arms race that was accelerated by the West in the 1980s, specifically with the aim of bankrupting the Soviet economy. The Achilles heel of the Soviet Union therefore was not in its military, but rather in its economy.

Where the Soviet Union failed then was at peace, not at war. It would have been very costly for the West to rely upon military means to try to defeat a Soviet Union armed to the teeth with nuclear and conventional weapons, especially after the West's partial or total failures in Korea, Vietnam and other regional conflicts. Economics was therefore used as a tool for the perpetuation of war through non-violent means. The Soviet Union fell without a shot being fired by the West. And ever since, a new era in international relations has emerged where economics, rather than being a force to bind nations as neo-liberal theory claims, became the war zone itself. Military means are still occasionally used as a supplement to reinforce America's economic and political dominance in international relations, but only when the risk is low and the stakes are high. Economics as a tool of domination in international affairs became thus a crucial aspect of globalization under the auspices of the New World Order.

The Economics of Zionist Peace

Why the Soviet Union failed to keep pace with the West in the economic sphere, especially in the 1970s and1980s, is beyond the scope of this article. Understanding the contemporary economic dimension of international conflicts is crucial, however, to understanding the economics of Zionist peace, as propounded by Shimon Peres in his book, The New Middle East, for example. In that book, Peres delineates “a new vision for the Middle East” where security (for “Israel”) rests upon economic rather than conventional military or political considerations. Economic and political hegemony through bilateral and multilateral agreements, rather than strategic depth in the geographical sense, assumes the geopolitical importance previously accorded to the direct occupation of physical space. Buffer zones such as South Lebanon and the Golan Heights, according to Zionists who embrace this view, need to become outdated.

At the end of this article, we will try to show that the characteristics of “Israel's” economic and technological strength will generate “Israeli” economic hegemony in the region EVEN IF “Israel” managed by some miracle to rid itself of its racist, expansionist and militaristic essence. However, “Israel” is not about to rid itself of that intrinsic essence as Peres demonstrates in his aforementioned book. In fact, these Zionist characteristics are undergoing a metamorphosis in which they assume an inherently more sinister, yet seemingly more benign form. The conventional appearance of Zionist aggression is about to be dropped in exchange for a post-Zionist economic and political hegemony that is not any less dangerous. Here, once again, economics is viewed as a battlefield, as the perpetuation of war through cheaper and currently more effective means. In the meantime, the value of the economic prize of normal and peaceful exchange relationships with “Israel” were summarized by a group of authors in a study in Hebrew, namely “Israel 2020: A Comprehensive Plan for the New Millenium, 1996.” According to that report, there are five reasons why the so-called peace treaties with Arab regimes are shoring up the Zionist entity economically in the short run. These are:

1. Lightening the security burden on the “Israeli” economy

Since the mid-1980s, the share of military expenditure to GNP (gross national product) decreased steadily. In 1967, the percentage of military expenditures to GNP was 6%. During the war of attrition with Egypt in the late 1960s that percentage rose to 10% then 14%. After the October war in 1973, it rose to 15% and then 18%. This percentage began to decline again after the Camp David Treaty with Egypt, and the subsequent invasion of Lebanon in 1982, to eventually reach 8% in 1995 after the signing of Oslo and Wadi Arabah. There was a qualitative benefit to these treaties as well, manifesting itself in the transfer of highly skilled workers, engineers and scientists from the army to the civilian sector. The lightening of the military burden was not affected by the Egyptian military build-up in peacetime either, since the primary source of Egyptian weaponry has been the friendly United States, according to the “Israeli” report. The same report moreover adds that after the collapse of the Soviet Union, Syria lost its main source of arms supplies. Combined with a weak economy, Syria stopped trying to pretend that it was remotely close to achieving a “strategic balance” with “Israel”. (Of course, keep in mind that the last two conclusions with respect to having Syria and Egypt under control should be taken with more than a grain of salt since the inculcation of defeatism in the Arab psyche has been a long-standing Zionist objective - FAV.)

2. Expanding “Israeli” exports to previously closed markets

Indeed, after the signing of peace treaties, “Israeli” exports (and diplomatic relations) expanded to previously closed markets. In traditional export markets in North America, Western Europe, and Southeast Asia, “Israeli” exporters gained renewed confidence and a more enthusiastic reception. For the same reasons, tourism to “Israel” flourished, with the exception of the years of bus attacks carried out by Hamas.

After the Madrid Conference, and after the United States government agreed to grant “Israel” $10 billion in loan guarantees, international credit markets opened up enthusiastically to the “Israeli” government. foreign investment into “Israel”

The Long-Term Effects

Normal economic exchange between the Arab States and any type of “Israel”, even a seemingly non-racist and non-expansionist one, will have to be analyzed in terms of three dimensions: (1) the movement of goods and services; (2) the movement of labour; and (3) the movement of capital.

Looking at those dimensions one by one will inevitably reflect the uneven terms of exchange that will necessarily arise between an advanced and technology-driven “Israeli” economy, and the underdeveloped Arab economies, whether oil-producing or not.

1. The movement of goods and services

Eliminating tariff and non-tariff barriers before trade with “Israel” will bring about a division of labour between “Israel” and its Arab neighbours that will reflect the comparative advantages enjoyed by the “Israeli” economy, especially in the area of high technology. The advantage of geographic proximity will lower transportation costs, thus enabling “Israeli” products, made in “Israel” or by “Israeli”-owned firms, to replace local products and other foreign imports. In the case of light industry and agricultural goods, trade with adjacent Arab neighbours will lower the cost of importation for “Israel” thus improving its balance-of-trade deficit, before leaping forth to control these economic sectors financially. The volume and value of trade with “Israel” will increase perhaps to the point of creating dependency on “Israel” and will strengthen its economy to the point of dwarfing neighbouring Arab States economically and politically. Thus the New Middle East is achieved!

This process does not need to take place directly. As Arab States join trade organizations in which “Israel” is already a member, like the World Trade Organization or the Agreement of European Partnership, impediments to trade between Arab States and “Israel” are effectively removed without the political fuss that accompanies direct agreements between “Israel” and Arab States. This is how we should view the linkage between globalization and the Zionification of the Arab World.

On the other hand, Arabs could reap the benefits of specialization, mass-production and large markets by simply establishing an Arab common market, or by joining other economic associations that do not include “Israel”. In fact, it is “Israel” that needs the Arabs much more than the other way around.

Countries are better off exporting the goods and services that currently unemployed workers can supply, rather than exporting the unemployed workers themselves to the metropolis to increase the value added of the products that “Israel” will just turn around to sell back to them.

3. The movement of capital

Levelling all obstacles before the inflow of foreign and “Israeli” capital to the Arab countries is touted as an ambitious goal by those who think that this will turn their Arab States into another Singapore or Hong Kong! To begin with, the size of the “Israeli” economy, and hence the “Israeli” market, is larger than that of Egypt, Syria, Lebanon and Jordan combined. Furthermore, the size of a market is not measured by the size of the population per se, but rather by the size of the residents' average income. Henceforth, “Israeli” per capita income is 12 to 20 times greater than that of the Arab States, with the exception of a few small Emirates. The disparity in technology, advanced industry and infrastructure between “Israel” and the Arab States was not bridged by the very slow growth rates in the 1980s and the 1990s. Cheap labour in many Arab States, on the other hand, is a necessary but insufficient condition to attract direct foreign investment in light industry, for example. But other factors, such as the size of a market, or the availability of a good infra-structure and a well-developed institutional setting (such as a court system, State bureaucracy, and relevant laws) that is congenial to business, might be just as important as cheap labour. Therefore, provided with easy access to Arab markets from “Israel”, direct foreign investment is more likely to locate in “Israel” to feed peripheral Arab demand rather than the other way around.

This factor is exacerbated by the fact that skilled and highly skilled labour is more plentiful in “Israel” and at a lower pay scale than in the West. Indeed, direct foreign investment in Arab natural resources, such as oil or phosphate, might be attracted to some Arab States. But those investments roaming for environments rich in specialized or high-productivity inputs or for the local ability to produce high-quality, intermediate goods that increase the value added to final output would rather head for “Israel” than for the Arab States.

Furthermore, there is another aspect in the problem of attracting foreign capital that many Arab and third world States don't seem to give enough attention. The investment decisions of transnational corporations are centralized decisions that are made on the basis of strategic considerations that have led to the generation of a new division of labour on an international scale. Third world and Arab States have been competing to attract foreign capital by liberalizing their economies and removing all restrictions on free (foreign) enterprise, among other things. But the fact that these measures to liberalize the economy have been adopted EQUALLY by most countries in the world has made investment in more developed countries more attractive on the average. Consequently, in spite of all these liberalization efforts, developed countries continue to receive the lion's share of direct foreign investment worldwide, and developing countries are still marginalized. Subsequently, the experiences of Singapore and Hong Kong, and the Asian tigers in general are not necessarily replicable. For these economic success stories were partially the result of Cold War considerations which led to the heavy transfer of capital from Japan and the West into Southeast Asia to make the masses of China, Indo-China and the Korean Peninsula marvel at the greatness of capitalism. They were also—especially in second-generation success stories like China, Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand and South Korea—the result of internal factors such as careful government planning and coordination, efficient systems to build human capital through education, and a set of social and cultural values which emphasized saving and hard work.

Another aspect of the competition to attract foreign investment in the 1990s is that it has not led to the inflow of direct foreign investment to developing countries, but to heavy movements by short-term capital seeking to turn quick profits from speculation, stocks, bonds, derivatives and foreign exchange. Short-term capital inflows are not associated with beefing up the productive capacity of nations since they do not lead to an increase in the quantity or quality of factories, farms, freeways or finished products that a nation possesses. In fact, short-term movements of capital can cause a lot of instability in the national economy as happened in Southeast Asia in 1997, and in Russia and Brazil in 1999.

Opting for regional blocs in and amongst the developing countries, such as an Arab Common Market, an African Common Market, a Latin American Common Market, and so forth, might be easier and cheaper than restructuring the economies of the developing countries in a European or an international framework dominated by the West and transnational corporations. In fact, even the attraction of direct foreign investments will be more likely within these regional blocs as they will have a larger size and benefit from economies of scale.
