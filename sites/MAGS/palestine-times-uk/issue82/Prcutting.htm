The Guardian, March 12, 1998

The Israeli army deployed troop reinforcements and armoured cars yesterday in an attempt to contain rioting which spread across the West Bank after three Palestinian workers were shot dead at a military checkpoint..."One of the workers, Ikab Sayed Ahmed, opened the door and was shouting in Hebrew, 'We are workers,' but the shooting carried on." Later soldiers pulled the 37 year-old out of the minibus and laid him on the road beside the bodies of his friends.

 

The International Herald Tribune, March 12, 1998

The [Israeli] Supreme Court has again ventured down a slippery slope in the area of human rights. Through the court's repeated backing, administrative detention has become a routine phenomenon, and as a result hundreds of Palestinians are being held in prolonged captivity without trial, under pretext that they pose a threat to local security. Now the Supreme Court has put its stamp of approval on a new disgrace. In a ruling handed down four months ago and partially made public last week, it has authorized the continued detention of Lebanese citizens who have been imprisoned for many years, simply because they can be ''bargaining chips'' in an effort to locate and release [Israeli] soldiers who are captive or missing in action.

 

The Los Angeles Times, March 11, 1998

Did he blunder and give comfort to Israel's enemies or was he candidly expressing what he and many Israelis in their hearts believe? Either way, Labor Party leader Ehud Barak's remark last week that if he had been born a Palestinian, he might have joined one of the groups fighting Israel has erupted into a major political flap for the former army chief who wants Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu's job. Right-wing politicians are exploiting the comment, saying Barak has provided some legitimacy for groups like Hamas that have wreaked havoc in Israel... But some Israelis have come forth to say that Barak was only stating an obvious truth, that if Israelis were in the Palestinians' shoes, their actions might be similar.

 

The Wall Street Journal Europe, March 11, 1998

After several years of squandering aid money on a corrupt and unaccountable Palestinian Authority, the European Union has been complaining recently about not being accorded a constructive role in the Mideast peace process. Now that Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has offered to withdraw troops from Southern Lebanon if the conditions are right, the EU has that chance. The question is whether members will seize it, or continue trying to make themselves an "honest broker" in the sexier Palestinian conflict.

 

The Boston Globe, March 22, 1998

The reaction to last week's Vatican statement on the Holocaust was decidedly mixed. Jewish leaders tended to be polite but did not disguise their disappointment. On the other hand, Roman Catholic commentators tended to be positive. One is tempted to say self-congratulatory. Rabbi David Rosen, director of the Israel office of the Anti-Defamation League and a liaison with the Vatican, acknowledged the statement's import but called it ''disappointing in certain respects.'' He pointed out - correctly, in my judgment - that earlier statements by the Catholic bishops of France and Germany went further in accepting responsibility for the moral climate that allowed Nazism to flourish in Christian (and largely Catholic) Europe.

 

The Chicago Tribune, March 25, 1998

After months of stalemate and under pressure from the U.S., Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has sent President Clinton a compromise offer on an Israeli withdrawal from the West Bank. While the withdrawal Netanyahu proposes is smaller than the one the United States is suggesting, it would cede more land than Israel has been willing to give up in the past. And the proposal would offer the Palestinian leader, Yasser Arafat, more adjacent land; Palestinians have complained Israel was offering them isolated patches.

 

The Washington Post, March 24, 1998

The fierce reaction by the Netanyahu government against even offering the (American) plan is said to have taken the Americans by surprise. Netanyahu twice called President Clinton directly last week, he has dispatched other officials to Washington, and he has encouraged the heads of Jewish-American groups to try to intervene. So far, the Clinton administration is reported to have refused the entreaties... The details of the American plan have been widely reported, and were thought by American officials to be only a modest advancement of the Israeli position. The U.S. plan would call for Israeli withdrawal in stages from a further 13.1 percent of the West Bank, and Israel has hinted that it might agree to 9 percent.
