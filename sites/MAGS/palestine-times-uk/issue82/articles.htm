Israelis Slap Britain on the Face

By Azzam Tamimi*

The British Foreign Secretary Robin Cook, who visited Palestine in March as a representative of the European Union, is now a hero in the eyes of the Palestinians, the Arabs and the Muslims. It seems that he may not have had much leverage to push the peace process forward or to persuade the Israelis to halt their illegal settlement programme in Jerusalem. However, he may have, as the Israeli ambassador in London stated in a BBC television interview, provoked the Israelis-or, more accurately, provided them with the pretext (not that they really need one)--to defy, as blatantly as they are capable of, the International Community by confiscating more Palestinian land to expand or build additional settlements. He may have provoked the Zionist lobby in Britain, and he may have to be made to pay for his heroic stance as Miller did before him. But he has certainly succeeded in standing up to the Israelis in a manner rarely seen among Western politicians. And in doing so, he has spoken on behalf of millions of Britons, who constitute a large silent majority, fed up with the Zionist arrogance and blackmail (to use the words of a young British journalist who intimated that many more British intellectuals and journalists support Palestinian rights now than ever before). Indeed, the insulting treatment of Mr. Cook by the Israelis has outraged many British commentators. Some dared to speak openly while some expressed dismay and anger only privately. A British politician is reported to have intimated in a small gathering with Arab businessmen that the Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu behaved like a recalcitrant, thankless child. It was the British who promised to give the Jews Palestine as their national home; it was the British who prepared the ground for the creation of the State of Israel; and it is the British who have been supporting the right of the State of Israel to exist peacefully and securely in the Middle East. Now, all the British get is a slap on the face and a kick in the butt.

Mr. Cook's visit to Palestine could not have come at a better time for the Palestinians. The Israelis had been making one provocation after the other, and the beleaguered Palestinians wished for someone like Mr. Cook to come and see for himself and to listen to complaints about the continuing Israeli disregard for the basic rights of the Palestinians. Days before Cook departed for Palestine, a Commission of Inquiry set up by the Israeli Government to investigate the attempted assassination in Amman in September 1997 of Khaled Misha'al, a Hamas official, showed very clearly the arrogance of the Israelis. The Commission concentrated on the failure of the attack rather than its illegality. Its report has been described by Amnesty International as 'shocking in its contempt for a rule of law in which justice and human rights are respected.' Israel, which has since its creation blackmailed the Western world in the name of fighting terrorism, received Cook with a series of scandals exposed by the discovery of a number of failed illegal espionage operations in Europe, specifically in Switzerland and Britain. It also received Cook with its decision to release the Israeli soldiers who killed in cold blood three Palestinian labourers at a checkpoint on their way back home from work. Cook was also received with a statement of defiance by Ariel Sharon, Israeli Infrastructure Minister and former Israeli Defence Minister responsible for the massacre of thousands of civilian Palestinians in Lebanon. Sharon told Israeli television that Israel would still want to assassinate Khaled Misha'al and would once more try to execute him.

In the meantime, the Palestinian Authority (PA) is being pushed to the limit. No progress is taking place in the peace process, and the economic situation in the territories has only been getting worse. By now, not a single Palestinian inside Gaza and the West Bank doubts the real nature of the Oslo peace settlement and the real function of the PA. The image of the PA as a guard employed by Israel to contain the Palestinians and provide protection for Jewish settlers and Israeli troops has, more than any time since the signing of the Oslo agreement in 1993, become annoying and truly humiliating for the PA leadership. It is believed that recent angry statements by senior PA officials, especially in the wake of the murder of the three Palestinian labourers, have been purposely sanctioned by the leadership in order to salvage its rapidly deteriorating reputation (not that it has not already been fatally damaged). At a press conference in Hebron, Al Rajoub, the notorious PA security chief, threatened that in response to the Israeli soldiers' attack on the Palestinians, the Palestinians would not, from then on, guarantee the safety of any Jewish settler to enter the zone under direct PA security control. Some Palestinian media even reported the statement in a much stronger claim, attributing to Al Rajoub (a relative of whom was one of the three labourers killed) the warning that "from now on, any Jewish settler to enter our area will not leave it alive." This was too much for the Israelis, who strongly protested and asked him to apologize. However, the more serious development in this regard has been the statement made to Egyptian journalists by the PA-appointed Mufti of Jerusalem, Sheikh 'Ikrema Sabri. He defended suicide bombers and considered the killing of Israeli women and children legitimate in view of the continuing Israeli attacks on Palestinian civilians. Sheikh 'Ikrema even refused to condemn Palestinians who oppose peace with Israel and condoned the attitude of Friday Prayer imams who criticize the peace process and call for a resumption of armed struggle against Israel.

It may turn out that these statements do not reflect a real change in PA policy or a wavering of its declared commitment to the peace process. In fact, they may have been designed primarily to improve the image of the PA by playing to the tune of the Palestinian opposition, whose position has been vindicated in the light of the Israeli government's repeated slaps on the face of Yasser Arafat and his PA comrades. However, it is possible that these PA-sanctioned declarations may have been in anticipation of the resumption of military attacks on Israeli targets to avenge the killing of the three Palestinian workers. After all, it has been quite some time, thanks primarily to the role of the PA in monitoring and aborting potential attacks against the Israelis, since the last major operation was executed.

It is not uncommon now to hear frustrated Palestinians shout (in demonstrations and funerals) and whisper (behind closed doors and into the ears of senior PA officials) that their being humiliated at the hands of the Israelis is the fault of the PA, whose main concern is to protect the Israelis rather than the Palestinians. It is time, many Palestinians believe, that the Israelis should be made to pay.

The new PA rhetoric is indeed a source of anxiety for the Israelis, who see in it a sign of rapprochement between the PA and its critics within the Palestinian community, and possibly a sign of covert coordination and cooperation between some factions of the PA and Israel's arch-enemy, the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas). In the aftermath of the killing of the three Palestinian workers, Hamas issued a statement vowing to retaliate.

The Israelis should be worried that any act of retaliation by Hamas will be designed to punish the Israelis, not only for the murder of the three Palestinians, but also for their failed assassination attempt on the life of Misha'al and for Sharon's insistence that it would be repeated.

 

* Director of the London-based Liberty for the Muslim World

West Bank seethes in anger following Tarqumya carnage

Hebron- From Khalid Amayreh

The seemingly deliberate murder of three Palestinian labourers by Israeli soldiers at the Tarqumya junction on 10 March ignited a "mini intifadah" throughout the occupied territories. Sporadic but violent clashes between stone-hurling Palestinian protesters and heavily armed Israeli soldiers erupted in Hebron, Dura, Bethlehem, Ramallah and other localities in the West Bank.

Ten days after the Tarqumya bloodbath, violent clashes were still taking place in Hebron and other localities, especially after the death of 11-year-old Samer Karameh early on Tuesday, 17 March. He succumbed to his critical injury sustained the previous week when Israeli soldiers opened fire at a group of Palestinian youngsters protesting the Tarqumya carnage.

According to sources at the Al Ahli hospital in Hebron, Karameh had been "clinically dead" ever since his admission to hospital on Wednesday, 11 March. Hospital Administrator Dr. Yousef al Sharawi told "Palestine Times" that a rubber-coated metal bullet (inaccurately referred to as just "rubber bullets") pierced the child's brain causing a massive and fatal hemorrhage. A few days later, the child was pronounced dead.

In Hebron, most of the unrest occurred at and around the Bab El Zawiya neighbourhood and nearby Shallala Street, the so-called "friction line" separating the PA-administered area, known as H-1, and the Israeli-controlled Old Town, known as H-2, where the Ibrahimi Mosque in located. Generally speaking, the protests assumed a hit-and-run fashion whereby Palestinian protesters, mostly youngsters and students, hurled stones toward (rather than at or even on) fairly distant and fully armed Israeli soldiers, who responded with the often-lethal rubber-coated metal bullets, eventually killing the child Karameh. As of Tuesday, 17 March, over 50 other Palestinians had been injured.

The unrest took an ugly turn on Friday night, 13 March, when Israeli settlers celebrating a Jewish holiday entered the PA-administered area, attacking Palestinians and vandalizing property. However, when Palestinian cameramen sought to videotape the frantic rampage, Israeli soldiers stationed on neighbouring rooftops opened fire on the journalists, injuring eight of them. Of the eight, journalist Nael Shuyokhi was seriously injured.

The ostensibly deliberate shooting at the Palestinian cameramen was apparently meant to intimidate Palestinian and other journalists and cameramen and deter them from "crossing the red lines," e.g. filming the settlers while re-enacting their own crystal nacht, this time in Hebron.

Protesting the shooting, more than 25 Palestinian journalists and cameramen organized a demonstration in Hebron during which they carried their cameras on stretchers to dramatize the real perils facing them while covering events in the occupied territories. One sign carried by the journalists read, "Don't kill the messenger," and another said, "I'm a journalist; don't kill me." The protesting journalists then held a press conference at Hebron City Hall where they called on their colleagues around the world to identify with their plight and to try to exert pressure on the Israeli government to stop attacks on journalists. According to Naim Tubasi, head of the Palestinian Journalist Union, 54 Palestinian journalists have been moderately-to-seriously injured by Israeli gunfire since September 1996.

Elsewhere in the West Bank, thousands of mostly Islamist demonstrators set ablaze American and Israeli flags in Nablus to protest the Tarqumya carnage. The protesters marched toward an Israeli military position in Nablus, shouting "Gestapo, Gestapo" at Israeli soldiers. The demonstrators were eventually repulsed by PA police using tear-gas to disperse them.

In Dura, 13 miles southwest of Hebron, the three murdered labourers were laid to rest on Wednesday, 11 March, in a solemn procession unprecedented in the Town's history. An eyewitness to the carnage, who is also a survivor of it, related that Israeli soldiers continued to open fire at the workers even as they were screaming "we are workers, we are workers." Hamdan Rajoub, who narrowly escaped the soldiers' bullets, stressed that the car posed no threats whatsoever to the lives of the soldiers, adding that they "killed in cold blood for the sake of killing."

Indeed, the murder of the three apolitical and poor labourers was a stunning surprise in Dura. Sheikh Nayef Rajoub, imam of the Grand Mosque in Dura, eulogized the three workers before their burial and ridiculed the Israeli account of the incident. He asked, "What would make those poor workers who were returning to their families provoke the heavily armed and trigger-happy Israeli soldiers."

Moreover, Rajoub pointed out that the massacre of the three workers had no effect on the Israeli collective conscience and that the opposite was true, since Israeli political circles, as well as the press, criticized the brief questioning of the three soldiers who committed the crime.

"This lays out naked the evil mentality of our enemy to such an extent that one is forced to wonder how can we possibly make peace with a society as such," Rajoub said.

Initially, the Israeli army said the shots were fired from the Palestinian car prompting the soldiers to return fire. Then, a few hours later, an Israeli army spokesman said the car was trying to run over one of the soldiers, a charge which nearly a hundred eyewitnesses said, "It is concocted and has no iota of truth."

 

Adding insult to injury

The Tarqumya atrocity and its aftermath enraged an already exasperated Palestinian public opinion. First, the Palestinians were enraged by the cold-blooded murder of three day labourers, who never posed any danger to anybody, as their main preoccupation was to earn bread for their children. Second, a further outrage resulted from the exoneration and subsequent release of the three Jewish killers only after spending 13 hours in a token incarceration. Third, and most gruesome of all, the Palestinian got a rather phantasmagoric collective image of the Israeli precipitin of Palestinians and non-Jews. Indeed, there was an outcry in Israel following the incident. However, the affronting thing is that the outcry was not over the murder of the three Palestinian labourers, but rather over the "unjust incarceration of the three soldiers." In other words, the crime from the collective Israeli viewpoint was not the butchering of the three Palestinians, but the incarceration for 13 hours of the three butchers. In fact, an Israeli government minister, Transportation Minister Joshua Yahlom, was audacious enough to demand that the military officer who ordered the incarceration of the three soldiers be dismissed from service.

The release of the three soldiers and the ensuing outcry over their brief detention left a predictably bitter impression on Palestinians, who evidently wrongly thought the Israeli society was making a transition from the psychology of war to the psychology of peace.

Arafat's entrapment!

By: Musil J. Shihadeh*

Arafat is a sitting duck in the middle of the crossfire between the Israeli Likud government that humiliates him by dictating to him the unacceptable terms of total surrender and the Palestinian masses that cannot acquiesce to the same terms. On one hand, he has made a commitment to the world to abandon armed struggle in exchange for achieving an honourable peace through negotiations, while on the other hand he is getting nothing from the Israeli government except the crumbs thrown at him from the Israeli table of "peace". In the light of statements made by Israeli officials on the future of the West Bank, the future of the so-called Palestinian "State" is going down the drain into oblivion.

As time goes on, the clandestine Israeli plans for the West Bank unravel a grotesque and gloomy picture of what the future Palestinian State would look like. I cannot, with all honesty, call them "cantons", since the inhabitants of such subdivisions would still enjoy freedom of motion and equal rights and protection under a higher authority. The Israeli new lines of entrapment would chain the Palestinian "cantons", and the higher authority would be in Israeli hands. I think it would be more appropriate to call the future Palestinian State a collection of subdivided ghettoes, where the oppressed masses would have to be satisfied to accept sub-human conditions of survival. Geographically, this Lilliputian "State" would be dissected into many parts in order to accommodate the passage of super-highways connecting the Jewish settlements, over 165 of them (and still increasing), with Israel proper.

In retrospect, the pre-Oslo status quo is far more superior to the planned future; at least then, the hope for a viable independent State was still an achievable goal. In essence, the peace negotiations, which basically brought to the forefront the demands sought by Israel since its creation, are nothing more than a stratagem to continue its expansionist policies, but in the name of "peace". The obvious results have been the radicalization of the Palestinian masses in the direction of extremism, which would signal a new bloody beginning of violence and bloodshed that will claim many victims on both sides.

From this perspective Israeli leadership is myopic in its outlook for the future, since injustice and oppression will always breed new violence irrespective of the strength of the oppressor and by no means can avoid such a bleak future except to redress the justified grievances of the oppressed. One does not need to consult a political scholar or an educated historian to arrive at this sad conclusion as a sincere study of colonialism and tyranny versus the oppressed throughout history should confirm this inevitable conclusion. It should behoove the Israeli leadership to keep this in mind before they sink deep into a new misadventure on the Palestinian front. It should also behoove Mr. Arafat to seek other options before his leadership is held responsible for participating with Israel in the futile attempt to kill the modest dreams of the Palestinians.

Infidelity, religion, and gun-boat diplomacy :- crisis in the region

By Dr. Daud A. Abdullah*

When Hillary Clinton appeared on American television last month to dispel claims of her husband's sexual impropriety she accused a right-wing cabal in Washington of attempting to destroy the President. The First Lady, a lawyer by profession, was not afraid to name names. For after five years in the byzantine circles of Capitol Hill she had come to know of most of the protagonists in this festering scandal. Hence, without giving much credence to the likes of Monica Lewinsky, Gennifer Flowers, and Paula Jones, Mrs. Clinton pointed the finger of accusation straight at the very Rev. Jerry Falwell. She had, apparently, no doubt that he was the mastermind of the disturbing revelations which shook the foundations of the American political establishment and sent shock waves as far away as the Middle East.

So who is this man Jerry Falwell; and why should he want to bring a premature end to the Clinton presidency? Is it because the latter has failed to live up to the puritan ideals of Protestant America? Of course not. The issue at stake here is not one of marital infidelity or even perjury, as sections of the American media would have us believe. A careful assessment of the facts indicates that the bone of contention between the President and Rev. Falwell is one of politics and support for Zionist-Israel and its religio-nationalistic policies in the Middle East.

 

The religious right

Two days before news of the Lewinsky affair hit the news waves of the world, Israel's prime minister Benyamin Netanyaho had met privately with Jerry Falwell and other evangelical leaders in Washington. The purpose of the meeting according to Falwell was to mobilize the evangelical churches against American attempts to force Israel to give up more Palestinian territory in the West Bank and Gaza. In a press statement issued on 20 January Falwell declared; "There are about 200,000 evangelical pastors in America, and we are asking them all through e-mail, faxes, letters, telephone, to go to their pulpits and use their influence in support of the state of Israel and the prime minister." In retrospect, statements of this kind are not entirely surprising since Falwell himself was awarded the highest Zionist honor: The Jabotinsky Prize for service rendered to Israel, plus $100 million from Israel and $140 million from the Swaggert donation.

In reality, Zionist courtship of western Christendom did not begin with either Netanyaho or Falwell. Bearing in mind that the entire settler project in Palestine was founded on western non-Jewish Zionist support. Indeed, the notion that the Second Coming of Christ would not be fulfilled until the Jews had returned to Palestine had gained currency in English literature as early as the 1640s. It continued to attract followers in every historical period after the Reformation and culminated in the 20th. century American fundamentalism which claims that the state of Israel represents the fulfillment of prophecy in modern history. It was against this background that the Time (21 June 1976) recorded former President Carter's declaration to a gathering at the Synagogue of Elizabeth in New Jersey; "I honor the same God as you. We (Baptists) study the same Bible as you." And on account of this, "The survival of Israel does not come down to politics. It is a moral duty."

 

Israel's use of banned weapons

With regard to the West Bank and Gaza Strip, the central issue in the current crisis, the Israelis have since 1967 been very critical of American Christian leaders for not supporting their expansionist adventure in the territories. Writing in the New York Times (June 27, 1967) Rabbi Pesach Levowitz, President of the Rabbinical Council of America, "Expressed deep disappointment" with what he called the failure of "major segments of the world and American Christian community to raise their voices in defence of Israel..." Later on that same year, the UN Security Council unanimously passed resolution 242; "Emphasizing the inadmissibility of the acquisition of territory by war." Yet, the fact that the resolution only demanded the "withdrawal of Israeli armed forces from territories," and not "all' or "the" territories gave Israel a technical loophole to maintain its occupation which has continued until today.

By law, the same principles used by the UN to determine the inadmissibility of the acquisition of territory by war in 1967, should naturally also be applied to the territories which Israel captured in 1948. As a consequence, Israel must be made to surrender the territory which it seized in 1948 and which the General Assembly actually declared should be part of the Palestinian state. Any formula which fails to make these provisions will certainly reinforce the widespread belief throughout the Arab world that Israel is above international law. This conviction has, as a matter fact, become much stronger in the light of the current mobilization of American and British forces in the Arab Gulf.

Although the professed objective of the immanent attack upon Iraq is to deny it the capacity to produce chemical and biological weapons, observers are absolutely appalled by the fact that Israel is allowed to stockpile and use unconventional weapons against its neighbors. The International Commission which met in Nicosia to investigate Israeli crimes in Lebanon in 1982 confirmed that the Zionist state did extensively use phosphorous and napalm bombs during its invasion of that country. Canadian and Greek doctors testifying before the Commission recalled that many civilian casualties were so disfigured by burns from napalm and phosphorous bombs that the holes in their bodies made them resemble filters.2 Israel's continued and indiscriminate use of these banned weapons has, evidently, escalated in recent years. Despite their persistent official denials, a former Israeli commander in southern Lebanon, Ephraim Sneh, admitted in 1996 that his country used tank-fired shells filled with flachettes in Lebanon.3 A flachette shell is an antipersonnel weapon that contains ten to fourteen thousand 1.5 inch steel darts which when released from a canister, spread out in an arc that can reach a maximum width of about ninety-four yards. It is therefore significant to recall here that while Israel receives the technical assistance and other benefits of being a member of the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), it continues to refuse to sign the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), and accordingly subject itself to the same inspection and monitoring regime that 180 other countries are subjected to by the IAEA.

Furthermore, during the 1973 Arab-Israeli war, Israel attempted to shoot down the U.S. reconnaissance aircraft SR-71 when it flew over the secret nuclear sight at Dimona while transporting arms to Israel. The Chairman of the U.S. Joint Chiefs of Staff at the time, Admiral Thomas L. Moorer, reported that after correctly identifying the aircraft on their radar, the Israelis ordered its downing in order to shield their nuclear facilities. The plane only escaped because it was flying too high for the Israeli fighters to reach.

On the basis of this evidence, it is abundantly clear that Israel's past record and current conduct is no less macabre than Iraq's. For it is prepared to attack not just its enemies but even its friends to maintain its nuclear capacity. At any rate, since both the United States and Israel have themselves refused to sign existing international conventions banning the use of biological weapons, the underlying motives for attacking Iraq have become entirely suspect and reprehensible. There are, indeed, tangible evidence which suggest that the operation to dismember and cripple Iraq was conceived in Jerusalem long before the invasion of Kuwait. In February 1982 the World Zionist Organization disclosed in its review, Kivounim (Orientations), that; "The disintegration of Syria and Iraq into regions, based on ethnic or religious criteria, must be, in the long term, a primary goal for Israel; the first step is the destruction of the military power of these states." Thus, "Oil-rich, and victim of internal strife, Iraq is in the Israeli firing line. Its dissolution would be, for us, more important than Syria's, because it is Iraq, in the short term, that represents the more serious threat for Israel."4 That the economic and military strangulation of Iraq should take place at a time when the Zionist lobby in Washington has taken full control of American foreign and defence policies is therefore not at all surprising.

 

What is to be done

What is however deeply disturbing is that in the post Cold War era, European policy toward the Middle East has remained essentially unchanged. By maintaining their traditional loyalty and "special relationship" with the United States, the Europeans have inadvertently delayed the modernization of their role in the region. For whereas the evolution of the modern state should actually reflect a corresponding development in its foreign policy, this has not been demonstrated in European involvement in the Middle East. Quite to the contrary, it has persisted as the appendage of an American approach which mainly relies upon military force in an age when political, economic and cultural discourse have become extremely imperative.

In certain ways though, the present crisis in the region has brought to the fore the underlying differences between the major European powers and their inability to deal with the United States as a unified body. French opposition to the use of force in Iraq is a good example of this. Albeit that it is really through their collective power that the European Union would ultimately enhance the influence of its individual members. Having upheld the traditions of democracy and self-determination which secured the independence of Australia, New Zealand and the United States of America itself, the Europeans and Britain in particular would restore much of their lost credibility if they were to show similar concern for the 3 million Palestinians who have lived under Israeli military occupation for the past thirty years. Not to say the least of their estimated 4 million kith and kin who have since 1948 been consigned to a stateless existence throughout the world. Thus, in the same way that the European Union has resisted U.S. pressure to impose sanctions on European companies trading with Iran, so too they can and ought to resist American attempts to solve the complex political problems of the Middle east through military means.

Several lessons have already been learnt from the current crisis. That in the last decade of the 20th.century, national sovereignty has become more the legal basis by which the economic and political freedom of all peoples can be safeguarded. Similarly, that sovereignty itself is indivisible. For either a state has the right to act independently or it does not. Hence, whether it be Iraq, Palestine or Kuwait, every state must have the ultimate power to determine its destiny and decide its own affairs if it is to become truly sovereign. Finally, that real security comes more from the values and "ethical principles" which give a country its particular character. Therefore, the security which Israel and the United States seek to achieve in the Middle East through the force of arms can actually be attained more easily through other means. They entail respect for the ideals of freedom and guarantee of the rights of all peoples in the region, including the Palestinians to self-determination. Surely, national security is neither exclusively, nor primarily, a military function. It comes more importantly from the fair and just application of international law to all peoples and without fear or favor. As Abraham Lincoln the American patriarch had warned long ago; those who in the name of national security, "destroy the spirit that prizes liberty as the heritage of all men in all lands everywhere" have effectively "planted the seeds of despotism around your doors."

 

* The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre in London and editor of the Return Review

How the Day was Won?

By Muneer Shafiq*

It was inevitable for the agreement between the Iraqi government and Kofi Annan, the Secretary-General of the United Nations, to include a clause binding Iraq to the implementation of Security Council resolutions against it. Also inevitable was opening up for inspection all sites, including Presidential Palaces.

After all, that was what was requested of Iraq by almost all Arab and Islamic States, and by the superpowers leaning Iraq-wise in the crisis such as Russia, China and France.

Neither was Iraq opposed in principle to inspection even as it prevented UNSCOM's team into the Palaces! Rather, it was clear from the start that inspection was negotiable if Kofi Annan and the majority of the Security Council would go along with proposed Iraqi changes to inspection teams and with the setting of some kind of conception as to ending the state of siege once the inspections were through.

Inspections then would be conducted within a definite time frame, even under any maximum limit.

That's why the U.S. government's battle revolved, diplomatically speaking, around depriving Iraq from that element.

Hence, the essence of the American position has been to dominate inspection teams and to totally forfeit decision-making, especially with regards to whence inspections are to be declared finished. With the ill intention of leaving the sanctions indefinitely in place, this malicious policy has thus far driven UNSCOM's inspection efforts for the past seven years.

Therefore, (1) participating in inspection teams by other members of the Security Council and giving them a supervising and chaperoning role, (2) adjusting the structure of those teams, and 3) the implicit promising thereof to lift the sanctions when the work of the born-again teams is over, represents a political defeat all in all for U.S. foreign policy.

The U.S. Administration might try to sweep that defeat under the rug by arguing that its demand of unrestricted access to all sites has been achieved. Yet nothing can be more indicative of the truth of that defeat more than U.S. Administration insistence that it reserves the right to reject the Iraq-UN deal and to strike Iraq unilaterally, even before Secretary-General Annan has had the opportunity to take the agreement to the Security Council; doing otherwise would be tantamount to conceding diplomatic defeat before Arab and Islamic States, and before Russia, China and France.

True, the scale of the defeat was relatively small! But with far-reaching and meaningful implications for the international balance-of-power, nevertheless.

But if the U.S. Administration didn't accept its diplomatic defeat, it would then make a more grievous mistake by entering a war alone without counting even on British support or with British reservations.

Striking Iraq or entering a war in the midst of such international isolation, and in the shadow of the confirmed deal between the UN and Iraq would, however, make political defeat more certain and more macabre than simply backing off by agreeing to the UN-brokered deal.

That's why Iraq "did good" by reaching that agreement with Kofi Annan...

 

*Palestinian writer and thinker and former director of the PLO Planning Department.

A visit to Palestine

By Alison Browning

For most of my life I have wanted to visit the Holy Land of Palestine. I grew up in a very religious home, and the land of Palestine was part of my daily life as each day we had to read the bible and pray. Palestine was close to my heart although I am neither Jew nor Arab. My religious upbringing urged me to have a great interest in the cause of the Palestinian people and the Middle East in general.

It all began in Sunday school when I was maybe five or six. We learned about how God wanted the Israelites to have the land of Canaan, and they were told by God to go and totally wipe out the people of the Canaanite city of Jericho. Even then I thought it was outrageous, killing in the name of God. This attitude has existed in the minds of Zionists and fundamentalist Christians since then with regard to the descendants of those ancient people.

I started to write to a political prisoner in an Israeli jail eight years ago when I was a student. She was soon one of many political prisoners with whom I corresponded but she was always the most special of all my pen friends.

At last my husband and I had the money to go to Palestine and visit Intissar (that is my friend's name) and her family in East Jerusalem. Intissar has a large family that includes a journalist, an academic and a judge in Ramallah. They are a politically active family. After eight years I was finally going to meet the family in person.

Upon my arrival at Tel Aviv airport, I was amazed at the total absence of anything relating to the Palestinian people. No Arabic signs and no evidence at all of Arabs; they seemed non-existent. Intissar told me that her family had suffered harassment from airport officials as they had arrived to meet us.

As we drove to Jerusalem, I noticed how modern and western Israeli areas were in comparison with Palestinian areas, and this was not even Gaza, Palestine's Soweto. The Palestinians were completely under-class, second-rate citizens, even the middle-class Palestinians. They were like the blacks in South Africa under Apartheid and the Indians during the Raj. Of course I knew this, I had read enough, but now I was actually witnessing it for real. It was not fair.

Besides visiting the holy places, I spent time with Intissar's family and learned of their difficulties and the Israeli bureaucracy, especially regarding the building of homes; Israelis can build where they like, Palestinians get their houses knocked down if they go against the Israeli red tape.

One day, in order to enter a Jewish park near Jericho so that Sara, one of the children, could play on the park equipment, Intissar's husband Mohammed and cousin Waleed had to pretend they were Israelis. Arabs are forbidden into the area. They are seen as terrorists. We attended the engagement party of one of Intissar's sisters. It was a happy occasion, attended by half the village of Silwan. They were making up for the many celebrations that had been spoiled by the curfew imposed during the Intifadah. Intissar had been unable to have a proper engagement party because of the curfews and even her wedding day was spoiled by them.

Students from some Arab and Muslim countries face repressive measures

By Ghada Khouri*

Students from Arab countries viewed with suspicion by the U.S. State Department may be prevented from pursuing a higher education in scientific fields in the United States. During a recent Senate hearing, Sen. "I have grave reservations regarding the practice of issuing visas to terrorist-supporting countries and the inability of the INS to track those who come into the country either using a student visa or using fraudulent documents," Feinstein told the Senate Subcommittee on Technology, Terrorism and Government Information on 24 February during a hearing on "Foreign Terrorists in America: Five Years After the World Trade Center Bombing."

Efforts to curb the admission of Arab students into American colleges and universities are influenced by a report issued last September by the Washington Institute for Near East Policy (WINEP), a conservative think tank. The report, entitled "Open Admissions: U.S. Policy Toward Students from Terrorism-Supporting Countries in the Middle East," advocates restrictions on students from Iraq, Iran, Libya, Sudan and Syria.

The report, written by Hillary Mann, recommends that the U.S. government take steps to:

* Require in-depth background checks on students coming from States designated as sponsors of terrorism by the U.S. Department of State and deny entry to those students wishing to study subjects that could contribute to their countries' nuclear, biological or chemical weapons programmes.

* Prohibit governments designated as sponsors of terrorism from financing their students' higher education in the United States.

* Expand an INS pilot programme designed to monitor foreign students in the United States.

* Coordinate efforts with other countries that attract students from States designated as sponsors of terrorism to impede their access to weapons-related technology.

An existing law, Title 8 of the U.S. Code, already gives the U.S. government the legal authority to deny visas to prospective students who may be involved in terrorist activities or who may seek to illegally acquire nonconventional weapons technology.

However, Mann cites government data indicating that nearly 10,000 student visas were issued to students from "terrorist" States between 1991 and 1996. She adds that only "a very small number" of visa applications were denied based on concerns about terrorism or technology transfer and that a "significant percentage" of those initially denied subsequently obtained a visa on appeal. She implies that the mere fact that denials were low indicates a loophole in the law. Her argument ignores the possibility that the small number of denials actually reflects the fact that the overwhelming majority of Arab students pursuing scientific studies are driven by ethical purposes.

"The report attempts to show that because large numbers of students from five particular Middle Eastern countries are studying science and engineering, they should be monitored as potential terrorists," said Gary Althen, President of the National Association of Foreign Student Advisers. "The fact is that most foreign students in the United States, especially those from Third World countries, study science and engineering. These areas of expertise are what developing countries need the most to raise standards of living and to enter the highly competitive global economy."

Despite these criticisms, the WINEP report is gaining interest in Congress. One of its most alarming recommendations is the expansion of an existing INS monitoring system of foreign student activities in the United States. The programme was adopted in response to a memorandum issued after the 1993 World Trade Center bombing by FBI Director Louis Freeh, in which he outlined loopholes in the U.S. visa system. The INS pilot programme, which was implemented in June 1997, makes it mandatory for colleges and universities to provide information on foreign students, including their address, academic status and any disciplinary action taken against them by the institution.

Critics say the debate on restricting foreign student access to U.S. universities is shaped by anti-Arab sentiments and broad generalizations about the implications of allowing students from some Arab countries to pursue their education in American institutions. However, one day before the hearing, Emerson's name was added to the list of witnesses.

They cited a 1995 Executive Order that primarily prohibits financial and business transactions with Iran, but makes no mention of students.

Critics warn that such measures will inevitably lead to an erosion of civil liberties. In a country that prides itself on the free flow of information, preventing students from some countries from pursuing scientific studies essentially pronounces them guilty by association.

This is their story:

Um Jamal recounts the 1948 "disaster", as she calls it. Her parents fled Hirbiyeh, near Majdal (Ashqelon area) and she was born five weeks later on the way south. When she was only two her parents died and her eldest brother, then only 13, and her six year-old sister looked after the other children. "I don't remember my parents; I don't even know what they looked like. We have no photos of them."

The family spent the first six years in tents in the Tuffah neighbourhood of old Gaza and then moved to Jabalia after UNRWA started building a camp there. In 1984 Um Jamal, her husband and children moved to Beach camp to be near her sister. In 1997, Um Jamal and Abu Jamal, their sons, unmarried daughters, daughters-in-law and grandchildren all lived in the shelter - 18 people in all.

Twenty-seven year-old Jamal, the oldest son and father of Bustan and Mohammad, also lives in the shelter with his wife and third child. Jamal suffers from poor eyesight and broken teeth as a result of four years in Israeli prisons in the late 1980s. He is a prolific writer and has recorded in a diary all his thoughts since those days. In prison he also embroidered, "There is no home like the homeland, my son" and framed it with two of his prison photos.

On the day he was released, during the intifada in 1990, a curfew had been imposed. As he tried to find his way home he was shot in the neck for breaking the curfew and had to wait 14 hours before being taken to hospital. UNRWA later sent him to St John's Ophthalmic Hospital in East Jerusalem for eye treatment.

Despite four years of prison and four years of unemployment afterwards, Jamal still had the energy and ambition to start his own small business. In the beginning, he displayed his goods on the ground in the camp market. Then in 1996, with a small business loan of $1,000 from UNRWA's micro-enterprise programme in Gaza, he was able to rent a small shop in the market where he mainly sells children's clothes, cosmetics and accessories. Business was going well, especially on Fridays when the market is usually packed. Jamal paid off the first loan and then a second one of $2,000. In 1997 he took a third loan of $4,000.

Then Jamal's business, like many others, began to suffer under the impact of Israeli-imposed closures on Gaza and tougher restrictions on Palestinian labourers entering Israel for work. Products he had ordered and paid for in advance from the West Bank could not be delivered into Gaza and prices began to rise. Soon he could not make the monthly repayments.

It is Um Jamal's determination that keeps the family going. What does she think of the younger generation? "There was greater poverty when I was a child, but my life was still easier than Jamal's generation. They have had the hardest time. They experienced the intifada and prison. Also, they are educated. They know their rights and they have a sense of themselves. They won't compromise."

Talal, one of Jamal's younger brothers, wants to get married but there is no room in the overcrowded shelter. Another brother, Kamal, also lives there with his wife and five children. There is also the youngest brother, Noor, who is still at school, and three sisters, 20 year-old Samah, 16 year-old Sabreen and nine year-old Leina. Those members of the family who work are trying to raise enough money to build another room in the shelter, which will cost between $3,500 and $7,000.

The cement block shelter, built in the 1960s as temporary housing, is old and needs constant repair. Rainwater comes in through holes in the kitchen roof. Water from the bathroom seeps through to the adjoining kitchen floor, so this too needs to be repaired. Abu Jamal's main concern, though, is the lack of space for a growing family.

Another major worry is the rising cost of living. Food has become more expensive. Fifty kilos of flour costs $20 and it goes too quickly with so many mouths to feed. It is hard to keep up the electricity and water payments.

Um Jamal works with 13 other women and girls in a sewing group she administers. They are part of the Creative Women group whose members number about 60 in different camps in the Gaza Strip. She is skilled in traditional Palestinian embroidery and teaches younger women. With a small loan of $280 from the Save the Children Federation in Gaza, she bought thread and fabrics. Now she produces traditional dresses, tablecloths, scarves, cushion covers and other items that the group sells.

Economic problems, social and family problems, overcrowding. Everyone is becoming more and more stressed. Jamal borrowed his parents' savings to pay the monthly repayment on the UNRWA small business loan.

Now there is no money to build another room or partition. "This is

breaking us," Um Jamal says. Jamal's father tells him that he and his family have two months to leave the shelter to make room for the younger brother, who wants to get married. In the meantime, Jamal and his family stay in their room but the door into the common area is locked and a new door into the alley is opened up. To reach the shared kitchen and bathroom now means a trek down the narrow alleyway.

Frustrated and locked in his own thoughts, Jamal starts to write. His hopes for the future are for a shelter for his family and stable employment. "But our problem, more than at any other time, is political not economic," he writes. "I need to breathe first and then eat. The political situation is ignoring the refugees. If we don't fight for ourselves, then who will?" The electricity goes off. Again. It could be off for hours. He continues to write by the light of a paraffin lamp...

A Vibrant Palestine: Israel's Key to Longevity

By Maurice D. Harris*

2-2

 

Conclusions

In the short time, Israel will continue to have the political and military upper-hand in the region. Its army has the most troops and the highest level of training. The air force is probably the most advanced and well-trained in the world. It has a successful nuclear programme and possesses the capability one or more enemy population centres in a matter of minutes. It takes in over a billion dollars a year in military aid from the United States. And, its military intelligence team has penetrated every enemy nation and could probably provide advance warning of any planned attack. Finally, Israel has the psychological military advantage of ultimately having nothing to lose if it looked like it was really going to be destroyed. This makes potential attackers afraid of what Israel might be willing to do if pushed to the brink. All this is a potent combination-- so potent that it is hard for us to imagine any major change in this reality.

Israeli right-wingers believe that this short-term reality is one that can be sustained into the long-term future. They are resigned to the idea that the Arab majority will never accept their existence, and therefore the only option for long-term survival is to maintain military superiority ad infinitum. This political assessment leads them to think that the Israeli left's call for an end to the occupation and a Palestinian state is based entirely on moral belief.

In fact, many on the right will even concede that the left's vision is the moral one in the debate. But they will tell you that this moral position is incompatible with the political and military requirements for survival.

As a supporter of the Israeli left, I must admit that I am primarily drawn to the left-wing vision because of its morality. My strong belief is that a state based on Jewish identity should also be based on some fundamental Jewish principles: chief of which is that it is unholy to oppress another people. But I am not writing this essay to argue the morality of the left's vision. I am writing to point out that Israel will not be able to survive another fifty years if it does not move to the left.

The bitter pill for Jews is accepting that Israel will ultimately have to depend on its neighbours' willingness to allow it to exist. When one looks at the long term factors discussed above (our previous issue), it becomes clear that it is Israel, not the Palestinians or other Arab nations, who actually has the least sustainable political and military position in the Middle East.

Now, the idea of being politically dependent on anybody, much less the Arabs, is something most Israelis can't stand. Molded by the devastation of the Holocaust and nineteen centuries of exile, persecution and catastrophe, Israel's core identity is founded upon the idea that Jews cannot count on surviving as long as they have to depend on the acceptance or good-will of any other nation. The existence of Israel represents, to most Jews, finally being done with that precarious role in history. What will be difficult for most Israelis, and Jewish supporters, to accept is that, while it may be true that Jews are at risk without an independent state, they are also at risk even with a state.

In order to safeguard their long term survival, Israel and the Jewish people need to start by coming to term with the fact that there is no such thing as a total security for small peoples in this world. Nothing can purchase that kind of invulnerability: not a long-term strategy of maintaining military superiority, not even atomic bombs and the willingness to use them.

After accepting that they will eventually have to depend on their neighbours to exist, the next thing Israelis need to do is pursue a political path that will prevent the dependency from being a one-way street. They need to create long-term relationships with their neighbours that will also make their neighbors dependent upon them for their own success and well-being. If Israel can manage to form long-term, sturdy, interdependent economic, cultural, medical, industrial, infrastructural, and possibly military ties with its Arab neighbours, then Israel's ongoing existence will be in its neighbours' interests.

This kind of security vision is what Shimon Peres has promoted for years, and it is usually scoffed at as idealistic and highly risky. But this vision is a blueprint of the necessary political conditions for Israel to be a viable state over the next fifty years.

What is so distressing about the Netanyahu government's course of action is that it is squandering the best opportunity Israel has ever had to start constructing this kind of regional interdependence. The end of the Cold War thrust all of Israel's neighbouring states into the position of having to search for new ways to re-formulate their own separate political stabilities. A climate emerged in which second and third-world heads of state all over the world, including the Middle East, had to look for new stable relationships with other powers. This sudden period of political fluidity presented many Arab leaders with the opportunity to bolt from the Arab League's previous long-term refusal of any acceptance of Israel. Israel needed to make a move first, however, in order to make such a shift possible for the Arabs.

Shimon Peres was able to convince Yitzhak Rabin that Israel's long-term security lay in creating a new Middle East-- one that was extremely interdependence. Since Israel was in the fortunate position of being able to offer lots of economic and technological goodies to Arab countries, it stood a chance to interest Arab leaders in beginning to form interdependent ties. That is why Rabin rescued the career of his mortal enemy, Yasser Arafat, from the brink of total collapse by extending his hand to him.

He saw that it was necessary. He also saw that other steps would be necessary on Israel's part-- letting go of the West Bank and Gaza, letting the Palestinian's establish a state, and even making compromises on the issues of Jerusalem and Palestinian refugees.

Rabin, whose entire military and political career was based upon safeguarding Israel's security, came to accept his intra-party rival, Peres's, vision of interdependence slowly. But once he saw the realpolitik truth of it-- that this was Israel's only real chance of a permanent place in the region-- he abandoned outdated security strategies and pursued better ones.

And he received, in return, many early indications that the strategy was working. Arab leaders began making secret, and then public, overtures to Israel. The Arab boycott was eased, then dropped, allowing billions of dollars to enter Israel's economy. Jordan offered a cease-fire, and then made peace. Rabin made unprecedented official state visit to the King of Morocco and the Sultan of Oman. Even Saudi officials shook hands in public with Rabin and Peres on the White House lawn.

Israel was also able to begin the work of integrating its economy and infrastructure with its neighbours-- the next phase of the interdependence survival strategy. Israeli-Jordanian and Israeli-Palestinian teams began trade talks, water and electrical system talks, agricultural talks, and military joint-exercises. Regional tourism opened.

It is all this hard work that Netanyahu and his government are undoing. In the name of their vision of long-term security based on uncompromising nationalist land claims and the fallacy of "permanent military superiority", they are ironically destroying what might be Israel's only chance of real longevity.

The scariest part is that, as the new post-Cold War order of political relationships and structures solidifies into the next major global political era, Israel may be cementing its place in that order as an isolated military power with no chance at normal relations with its own region. Without a major market to entice the American corporate oligarchy, without significant natural resources, and without a Cold War, Israel may position itself into being an expensive and annoying burden to the U.S., costing America a lot of money to keep it viable, and causing American business to lose out to other wealthy industrial nations in the next century's battle for huge third-world markets in Africa and the Middle East. (Enter America's far right wing politicians at this point, railing against Jewish interests ruining life for middle-America.) Couple that with an American Jewish population that is shrinking due to assimilation, and that is losing its ability to identify with an Israel that is allowing the ultra-Orthodox to dominate, and you see how probable it is that there will not be enough pro-Israel political support in the U.S. to outweigh the liability Israel will become to the corporate, ruling interests.

This is the path Bibi & Co. are charting for Israel. If they succeed, don't count on there being an Israel for your grandchildren to visit. Instead, prepare to explain to them how Israel came apart at the seams around the year 2030. You night say to them that a brave, visionary man named Yitzhak Rabin tried to protect his country's future, but was assassinated by one of his own people. Tell them that shortly after he died, Israeli support for his vision grew to record levels.

But then, you will say, fundamentalist Muslims in Gaza and the West Bank, encouraged by backers in Iran, saw what was happening. Seeing that their wish for a strict Islamic state in all of 'Israel' and Palestine would never materialize of Israel became integrated into the region, they deliberately set out to get Israelis to abandon their new, integrationist strategy. They needed Israel to return to its previous uncompromising strategy of being the neighbourhood bully, so they could win back the support of the entire Arab world. They needed Jews the world over to get stuck in the short-sighted trap of their own psychology of fear and mistrust.

You can finish the story by telling the grand kids that a couple of bus bombings later, Benyamin Netanyahu rose to power, and in a couple years' time demolished his own country's best chance at long-term stability more thoroughly and effectively than Hamas ever could have on its own. As they look up at you, you can close with, "And that's how come we don't have an Israel anymore, honey."

History will record this, if we let it. But, Jewish tradition teaches us that we are not helpless to the momentum of history. Indeed, our 3,500 year survival represents what could be the longest human odyssey against historical odds. Now is the time for all of us who care about Israel's future, and about the future of a just peace, to see that these are intertwined. Now is the time for the pro-Israel peace-camp to get busy.

 

* Maurice Harris is the Director of the Holocaust Oral History Project in San Mateo, California and a leader in the activist group, a Jewish Voice for Peace.

Jewish History

By Israel Shahak*

 

Part 3

Prejudice and Prevarication

The first difficulty in writing about this subject is that the term 'Jew' has been used during the last 150 years with two rather different meanings. To understand this, let us imagine ourselves in the year 1780. Then the universally accepted meaning of the term 'Jew' basically coincided with what the Jews themselves understood as constituting their own identity. This identity was primarily religious, but the precepts of religion governed the details of daily behaviour in all aspects of life, both social and private, among the Jews themselves as well as in their relation to non-Jews. It was then literally true that a Jew could not even drink a glass of water in the home of a non-Jew. And the same basic laws of behaviour towards non-Jews were equally valid from Yemen to New York. Whatever the term by which the Jews of 1780 may he described - and I do not wish to enter into a metaphysical dispute about terms like, 'nation' and 'people' - it is clear that all Jewish communities at that time were separate from the non-Jewish societies in the midst of which they were living.

However, all this was changed by two parallel processes beginning in Holland and England, continuing in revolutionary France and in countries which followed the example of the French Revolution and then in the modern monarchies of the 19th century: the Jews gained a significant level of individual rights (in some cases full legal equality), and the legal power of the Jewish community over its members was destroyed. It should be noted that both developments were simultaneous, and that the latter is even more important, albeit less widely known, than the former.

Since the time of the late Roman Empire, Jewish communities had considerable legal powers over their members. Not only powers which arise through voluntary mobilisation of social pressure (for example refusal to have any dealing whatsoever with an excommunicated Jew or even to bury his body), but a power of naked coercion: to flog, to imprison, to expel - all this could be inflicted quite legally on an individual Jew by the rabbinical courts for all kinds of offences. In many countries- Spain and Poland are notable examples - even capital punishment could be and was inflicted, sometimes using particularly cruel methods such as flogging to death. All this was not only permitted but positively encouraged by the state authorities in both Christian and Muslim countries, who besides their general interest in preserving 'law and order' had in some cases a more direct financial interest as well. For example, in Spanish archives dating from the 13th and 14th centuries there are records of many detailed orders issued by those most devout Catholic Kings of Castile and Aragon, instructing their no less devout officials to co-operate with the rabbis in enforcing observance of the Sabbath by the Jews. Why? Because whenever a Jew was fined by a rabbinical court for violating the Sabbath, the rabbis had to hand nine tenths of the fine over to the king - a very profitable and effective arrangement. Similarly, one can quote from the responsa written shortly before 1832 by the famous Rabbi Moshe Sofer of Pressburg (now Bratislava), in what was then the autonomous Hungarian Kingdom in the Austrian Empire, and addressed to Vienna in Austria proper, where the Jews had already been granted some considerable individual rights. He laments the fact that since the Jewish congregation in Vienna lost its powers to punish offenders, the Jews there have become lax in matters of religious observance, and adds: 'Here in Pressburg when I am told that a Jewish shopkeeper dared to open his shop during the Lesser Holidays, I immediately send a policeman to imprison him.'

This was the most important social fact of Jewish existence before the advent of the modern state: observance of the religious laws of Judaism, as well as their inculcation through education, were enforced on Jews by physical coercion, from which one could only escape by conversion to the religion of the majority, amounting in the circumstances to a total social break and for that reason very impracticable, except during a religious crisis.

However, once the modern state had come into existence, the Jewish community lost its powers to punish or intimidate the individual Jew. The bonds of one of the most closed of 'closed societies', one of the most totalitarian societies in the whole history of mankind were snapped. This act of liberation came mostly from outside; although there were some Jews who helped it from within, these were at first very few. This form of liberation had very grave consequences for the future. Just as in the case of Germany (according to the masterly analysis of A.J. P. Taylor) it was easy to ally the cause of reaction with patriotism, because in actual fact individual rights and equality before the law were brought into Germany by the armies of the French Revolution and of Napoleon, and one could brand liberty as 'un-German', exactly so it turned out to be very easy among the Jews, particularly in Israel, to amount a very effective attack against all the notions and ideals of humanism and the rule of law (not to say democracy) as something 'un-Jewish' or 'anti-Jewish' - as indeed they are, in a historical sense - and as principles which may be used in the 'Jewish interest', but which have no validity against the 'Jewish interest', for example when Arabs invoke these same principles. This has also led - again just as in Germany and other nations of Mitteleuropa - to a deceitful, sentimental and ultra-romantic Jewish historiography, from which all inconvenient facts have been expunged.

So one will not find in Hannah Arendt's voluminous writings, whether on totalitarianism or on Jews, or on both, the smallest hint as to what Jewish society in Germany was really like in the 18th century: burning of books, persecution of writers, disputes about the magic powers of amulets, bans on the most elementary 'non-Jewish' education such as the teaching of correct German or indeed German written in the Latin alphabet. Nor can one find in the numerous English-language 'Jewish histories' the elementary facts about the attitude of Jewish mysticism (so fashionable at present in certain quarters) to non-Jews: that they are considered to be, literally, limbs of Satan, and that the few non-satanic individuals among them (that is, those who convert to Judaism) are in reality 'Jewish souls' who got lost when Satan violated the Holy Lady (Shekhinah or Matronit, one of the female components of the Godhead, sister and wife of the younger male God according to the cabbala) in her heavenly abode. The great authorities, such as Gershom Scholem, have lent their authority to a system of deceptions in all the 'sensitive' areas, the more popular ones being the most dishonest and misleading.

But the social consequence of this process of liberalisation was that, for the first time since about AD 200, a Jew could be free to do what he liked, within the bounds of his country's civil law, without having to pay for this freedom by converting to another religion. The freedom to learn and read books in modern languages, the freedom to read and write books in Hebrew not approved by the rabbis (as any Hebrew or Yiddish book previously had to be), the freedom to eat non-kosher food, the freedom to ignore the numerous absurd taboos regulating sexual life, even the freedom to think - for 'forbidden thoughts' are among the most serious sins - all these were granted to the Jews of Europe (and subsequently of other countries) by modern or even absolutist European regimes, although the latter were at the same time antisemitic and oppressive. Nicholas I of Russia was a notorious antisemite and issued many laws against the Jews of his state. But he also strengthened the forces of 'law and order' in Russia - not only the secret police but also the regular police and the gendarmerie - with the consequence that it became difficult to murder Jews on the order of their rabbis, whereas in pre-1795 Poland it had been quite easy. 'Official' Jewish history condemns him on both counts. For example, in the late 1830s a 'Holy Rabbi' (Tzadik) in a small Jewish town in the Ukraine ordered the murder of a heretic by throwing him into the boiling water of the town baths, and contemporary Jewish sources note with astonishment and horror that bribery was 'no longer effective' and that not only the actual perpetrators but also the Holy Man were severely punished. The Metternich regime of pre-1848 Austria was notoriously reactionary and quite unfriendly to Jews, but it did not allow people, even liberal Jewish rabbis, to be poisoned. During 1848 when the regime's power was temporarily weakened, the first thing the leaders of the Jewish community in the Galician city of Lemberg (now Lvov) did with their newly regained freedom was to poison the liberal rabbi of the city, whom the tiny non-Orthodox Jewish group in the city had imported from Germany One of his greatest heresies, by the way, was the advocacy and actual performance of the Bar Mitzvah ceremony, which had recently been invented.

 

Liberation from Outside

In the last 150 years, the term 'Jew' has therefore acquired a dual meaning, to the great confusion of some well-meaning people, particularly in the English-speaking countries, who imagine that the Jews they meet socially are 'representative' of Jews 'in general'. In the countries of east Europe as well as in the Arab world, the Jews were liberated from the tyranny of their own religion and of their own communities by outside forces, too late and in circumstances too unfavourable for genuine internalised social change. In most cases, and particularly in Israel, the old concept of society, the same ideology - especially as directed towards non-Jews - and the same utterly false conception of history have been preserved. This applies even to some of those Jews who joined 'progressive' or leftist movements. An examination of radical, socialist and communist parties can provide many examples of disguised Jewish chauvinists and racists, who joined these parties merely for reasons of 'Jewish interest' and are, in Israel, in favour of 'anti-Gentile' discrimination. One need only check how many Jewish 'socialists' have managed to write about the kibbutz without taking the trouble to mention that it is a racist institution from which non-Jewish citizens of Israel are rigorously excluded, to see that the phenomenon we are alluding to is by no means uncommon.

It is important to note that all the supposedly 'Jewish characteristics'- by which I mean the traits which vulgar so-called intellectuals in the West attribute to 'the Jews'- are modern characteristics, quite unknown during most of Jewish history, and appeared only when the totalitarian Jewish community began to lose its power. Take, for example, the famous Jewish sense of humour. Not only is humour very rare in Hebrew literature before the 19th century but humour and jokes are strictly forbidden by the Jewish religion- except, significantly, jokes against other religions. The critical sense, which is supposedly so characteristic of Jews, was totally absent, and nothing was so forbidden, feared and therefore persecuted as the most modest innovation or the most innocent criticism.

It was a world sunk in the most abject superstition, fanaticism and ignorance, a world in which the preface to the first work on geography in Hebrew (published in 1803 in Russia) could complain that very many great rabbis were denying the existence of the American continent and saying that it is 'impossible'. Between that world and what is often taken in the West to 'charactercise' Jews there is nothing in common except the mistaken name.

However, a great many present-day Jews are nostalgic for that world, their lost paradise, the comfortable closed society from which they were not so much liberated as expelled. A large part of the zionist movement always wanted to restore it -and this part has gained the upper hand. Many of the motives behind Israeli politics, which so bewilder the poor confused western 'friends of Israel', are perfectly explicable sense which this word has had for the last two hundred years: a forced and in many respects innovative, and therefore illusory, return to the closed society of the Jewish past.

Sounds of Silence

By E. Yaghi

Najah Taffal was born deaf. This means that she has never heard the sound of falling rain, the magic ripple of her children's laughter or the agonies of their cries, the songs of early morning birds or any other sounds that we may find either annoying or precious to our ears and which we tend to take for granted. She was born twenty-eight years ago in Amman and when she was old enough, attended the Salt School for the Deaf until the sixth grade. She then left school and started to learn sewing but also quit because she didn't like it. When she was fourteen and a half years old, she got married to a deaf man and had three children. While marriage for a young deaf girl could have seemed like the end of all hope for any kind of decent future and may well have fated her to do menial jobs, Najah's marriage apparently gave birth to the emergence of her true spirit of resolve and determination to not only better her life but the lives of as many deaf people as she can.

When I first met Najah the clean modern Specialized Audiology Centre in Jordan, I sensed that she was a bright young woman and I saw excitement and spirit flash in her dark eyes as she tried to converse with me in her native tongue-sign language. To my distress, Manal Hamzeh, the director of the centre, left Najah and me alone for a few minutes to see how well we could communicate together and to give me a chance to almost feel how it is to be deaf. As soon as Manal went out of the room, I perceived that I was the one who was deaf for there was no effective way for me to adequately express myself or understand her. Before this isolation I had noticed how well she and Manal could converse with each other in sign language and how I enviously watched them as if I were hearing a new and strange language for the first time, a complete foreigner, for Manal and Najah were quite fluent in the language. However, Najah and I sat down at a round white table and tried to "talk" to each other. We ended up by laughing at our efforts while we tried to draw silly pictures and make gestures but we didn't make much progress.

Manal saved me from further embarrassment and from then on served as our mutual interpreter. Through her I learned that Najah studied both Arabic and English typing even though she couldn't hear. She is presently employed at the Specialized Audiology Centre and makes use of her typing skills. Before she came to work with Manal, she was the first deaf woman to work as a government employee in a position she held for two years. She also became interested in establishing a deaf women's club because there weren't any but there were deaf men's clubs. Deaf women had been left out, therefore, the National Speech and Hearing Centre where she worked, encouraged her to start one. In addition, some friends came from the United States and persuaded the centre to each sign language and interpreting. The Centre wisely understood that the best person suited to teach such courses was a deaf one, hence, Najah began her second job as instructor of sign language. She published the first sign language book in Jordan which contained about 300 symbols for sign language.

Parents of deaf children were also prompted to learn sign language. Previously, communication between parents and their deaf children was almost impossible, but courses in sign language enabled these parents to correspond with their children. Najah consequently started teaching courses for the deaf at the University of Jordan through the Friends of Handicapped Students for a period of three months. At present, all deaf clubs give these courses.

Since Najah believes it is her duty to help all deaf people, she went on a trip to the Society For The Handicapped in Gaza. The situation there differs sharply from that in Jordan, for Gaza is a very difficult place to live in due to extreme overcrowding and the conditions of occupation and oppression. It is often the case that people all over the world stigmatize the deaf as handicapped and therefore, lacking intelligence. True, being deaf is indeed a handicap, but then every person has some sort of handicap or other and deafness is an obstacle that can be surmounted by the improvement of other senses which become more acute and further, being deaf has nothing to do with how smart or stupid a person is. In Gaza, Najah taught her course to fifty teachers who work in home intervention programs and she raised their awareness about language and deafness. Besides teaching such courses, she went to some homes of deaf children who were taking classes and realized that the sign language level there was too low. It is imperative that deaf children have continuous contact with each other. They shouldn't be isolated in their homes, for no man is an island, but exposed to role models of deaf adults. Najah recommended this idea to the director of the society. She also aims to gather and develop deaf leaders who are proud of themselves.

She insists that it is vital all mothers of deaf children learn sign language. When the Gaza mothers saw her, they were very surprised and asked, "Are you really deaf? You are so smart and active!"

Najah's goal is not to be on her own as a deaf leader, but to concentrate on procuring other leaders. In Gaza, the Society For The Handicapped approved the sign language program that she will be a big part of. At this time, there are no deaf clubs there, unlike Jordan where there are many now. People in Gaza are very suppressed. Najah hopes by working hard in three to four years the Gazans will have their own clubs. She finds Gaza to be a great challenge inspite of the occupation and the intense effort required of her. She doesn't mind working to improve things. Even without a degree, there is an inborn talent in her to teach her mother tongue which is sign language. She has also written and organized courses for sign language and the Ministry of Social Development is taking her course curriculum as a guide for other clubs to give and use. Such courses are best taught by a deaf person and not a hearing one. This is the first time that a real guide has been employed for these courses.

As mentioned earlier, Najah has three children. Even though their parents are both deaf, the children hear. The eldest son now about 13 interprets for his mother. All the children attend private schools in order to have a good background in both Arabic and English. Najah knows the importance of giving her children the advantage of a good education because she didn't have such an opportunity. Her son wants to attend college and work with the deaf when he grows up but his mother says that he must make h is own decisions. Whatever she has lost in sound and education, she more than makes up for these shortcomings with an automatic mind which keeps innovating things to do for the deaf. It is her dream that the deaf in Jordan will be like those in America with the same opportunities. She is very fortunate to have a patient and wise husband who lets her be what she must and lets her struggle for those goals that make her life have meaning.
