PT interviews former Algerian Prime Minister

Al Ibrahimi: This is my solution for the crisis in Algeria

Crisis is political but government does not want a political solution

On the eve of the parliamentary elections in Algeria, the first since the military seized power following the December 1991 overwhelming victory of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS) in the elections, Yasser Za'atreh conducted an interview with Dr. Abdel Hameed Al Ibrahimi, a former Algerian Prime Minister (1984-1988) to shed more light on the present crisis in Algeria. Dr. Al Ibrahimi is now living in London heading a centre for studies on North African affairs.

Q. The military authority in Algeria is dealing with the local crisis in two ways: security-wise and political-wise. Let's start with the security approach. The government says the "violence" is about to end. Is it true? To what extent are the Islamic groups responsible for the mass killings and bombings? Is the government involved in these incidents? Is the FIS involved ?

A. The crisis in Algeria which was created by the military coup in January 1992 still exists and is becoming worse and more complicated. This is because the present regime is still insisting on using force and suppression to remain in power and to preserve the illegal benefits it gained at the expense of the general interests of the Algerian people.

The crisis in Algeria is basically political. But the regime does not want a true political solution. Instead, it insists on a military solution despite the deterioration of security and economic and social conditions in the country. Moreover, the security policy that has been implemented for the last five years has totally failed.

As for the Islamic armed groups, they are penetrated by the military intelligence service. It is known that most of the mass killings and bombings are made by the government itself whether through special forces or through the local militias (about 200,000 armed men), but the government accuses the Islamists of the violence. All know that the victims of the mass killings are Islamists or ordinary citizens well-known for their support of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS). Bombings always occur in quarters known to be affiliated with the FIS.

As a member of the National Liberation Front, and according to definite information, I am sure that the FIS is absolutely not responsible for such savage and criminal atrocities. On the contrary, the FIS is a victim of these atrocities and has condemned them on several occasions. The military regime has adopted this policy of terrorism to achieve a number of objectives, such as:

- To wreak vengeance on members and supporters of the FIS until they give up their political ambitions;

- To terrify the Algerian people who suffer from the deterioration of the security and socio-economic conditions in the country in order to force them to accept the political and dictatorial policy of the present regime;

- To deform, locally and abroad, the picture of the FIS and the Islamic project in general;

- To mislead international public opinion so that the regime could obtain additional financial, political, and diplomatic support from France and other Western countries. The regime wants to show itself as the defender of the West against fundamentalism in Algeria and as an acclaimed partner in defending the French and Western interests in the region.

I challenge the regime if it dares to set up an independent national committee or to allow international committees such as human rights organizations to investigate and verify the criminal atrocities being carried out in Algeria. Such investigations will definitely reveal the real criminals responsible for these crimes.

Q. In the political approach, President Zirwal believes he gained legitimacy after winning the presidential elections last November. He also believes that by the time parliamentary elections take place, he would close forever the door on the crisis and achieve stability. What is your comment about such assumptions?

A. President Zirwal had promised in his electoral campaign in November 1995 to rectify the situation and find a peaceful solution to the Algerian crisis if elected. But what has he done since his promise? The security situation has become worse and so has the socio-economic situation.

The coming parliamentary elections will not solve the crisis. They could never be honest and free in the presence of military suppression, political siege, misleading media coverage, the exclusion of real powers and parties, as well as the confiscation of public freedoms. The holding of parliamentary elections under the current circumstances will certainly complicate the crisis politically, socially, and economically in the same way as the 1995 presidential elections did.

Q. Regarding the amendment of the Constitution, observers say that President Zirwal granted himself many authorities and emptied the parliament from its powers. How do you assess these amendments and their political impact?

A. The amendment of the Constitution and the laws concerning parties and elections was a strong blow in the face of democracy which has been non-existent for the past five years. These amendments aim to legitimize the regime and marginalize the role of the Algerian people in political life. I consider these amendments an attempt by the regime to monopolize political power and to disdain the Algerian people.

Q. Do you think that the establishment of the National Constitutional Party means that it will be the regime's party as in some Arab countries and that it will add a parliamentary majority to the President's powers?

A. Clearly, this newly established party will be the President's party and it will officially become the major party in the country. Through the coming parliamentary elections, the regime wants to change the political map in Algeria to serve its own interests. But can the regime delete the millions of votes that the FIS collected in the last elections by resorting to such tactics? Can the regime alter the fact that the people who are suffering from suppression and poverty do not trust it? The answer is very clear because the political crisis cannot be resolved unless the reasons which caused the crisis are removed and unless the regime reverts to democracy after putting an end to the bloodshed.

Q. The Algerian President stressed on various occasions that the FIS was finished. What do you say?

A. The FIS is a main party in solving the crisis. Excluding it from the national dialogue and from the elections will lead the country to a total deadlock as the case has been for the past five years. FIS has become more than a party; it is a political and social phenomenon which is supported by millions of Algerians from various social and professional categories in cities and villages.

Q. Some reports talk about conflicts between different factions in the army. What are the political consequences of such conflicts?

A. The conflicts among the senior officers of the military institution are not serious. There is a large gap between the small pro-France leadership that rules the country and the big number of junior peaceful officers who want the Algerian army to withdraw from the political scene and stop suppressing the people. The latter also want the army to stick to the role assigned to it by the Constitution which is to protect the national soil and to defend the country against any possible external enemy.

Q. The French policy of dealing with the Algerian affair is confusing and contradictory. How do you assess this policy?

A. I think the French policy concerning Algeria was always hostile towards Arab Islamic tendencies that appeared in Algeria since its independence. We have information about its (Franceís) role in the arrest of Abbas Madani and Ali Balhaj (FIS leaders) in 1991 and in the January 1992 military coup. Since that time, France has been providing the Algerian regime with military, political, diplomatic and financial support.

Algeria's imports from France, for example, increased from 17 per cent of its total foreign imports in the late 1980s to 40 per cent in 1994. We also noticed that the prices of the products imported from France are high, far in excess of such prices of similar products in the international market. This is because France enjoys a monopoly in the Algerian market. The Algerian Health Ministry, for example, discovered last summer that prices of medicines imported from France (Algeria imports 90 per cent of its medical supplies from France) were 65 per cent higher than their prices in the French market itself (!)

On the other hand, France is playing its Francophone card, which is a an ideology it used to adopt in its old colonies within the context of its struggle against the Arab Islamic civilization, especially in the fighting of Islamic awakening in North Africa.

France has been fighting Islamic and national movements in Algeria for more than five years using "France's party" which dominates the Algerian army and the important ministries.

Q. Observers can see a kind of "courtingî of the Algerian regime by the U.S. Administration. What is the significance of this courting?

A. I do not think that there is a competition between France and the United States over Algeria because the U.S., regrettably, considers Algeria a French domainóa thing that is strongly rejected by the Algerian people.

Q. How do you assess the economic situation in Algeria and what are its political and social impacts on the future of the country?

A. After the 1992 coup, the military authorities alleged that the coming to power of the FIS through parliamentary elections (which were later cancelled) would lead to the deterioration of the political and economic situation in Algeria. The following figures show what has been happening since 1992:

1- More than 100,000 Algerians were killed.

2- The per capita income decreased from $2,500 in 1990 to $1,200 in 1995 (a reduction of 52 per cent in six years).

3- The agricultural production decreased by 25 per cent although the population increased by more than 4 million in the last six years. This led to the increase of food imports which now form 90 per cent of the total national food consumption amounting up to $3 billion a year.

4-The countryís industries are operating (except for petroleum and gas) at only 20 per cent of their actual capacity which means that 80 per cent of the Algerian industries, except for the petroleum industries, are now out of work.

5- Investment has been reduced to a level lower than it was before Independence. Food and industrial consumable imports form 49 per cent of total imports. The military expenditure increased by 45 per cent in 1994 and by 144 per cent in 1995. All of that was at the expense of the investment in production sectors.

6- The unemployment rate rose to more than 30 per cent in 1996. 83 per cent of the now 2.5 million unemployed Algerians are youth between 16 and 29 years of age. The number of unemployed citizens is expected to increase to 3 million after completing privatization of public establishments during this year because more than 400,000 workers will lose their jobs to privatization.

7- For the first time since Independence, the general level of prices increased by 40 per cent in 1994 while the prices of the foodstuffs increased by 80 per cent which increased poverty among people and consumers.

8- The foreign debts increased from $26 billion in 1992 to $35 billion in 1997 reaching a total of $40 billion if we add the military debts. The foreign debt is a heavy and serious burden on Algeria which, if the present political situation continues, will lead to the deterioration of the economic crisis in the future.

In brief, the Algerian people are now suffering from suppression and poverty. They are languishing in a valley of blood and tears. The "French generals" who control the military institution in Algeria are responsible for this corrupt situation and for this destructive policy since 1992.

Q. As a former Prime Minister in Algeria, what would be your perspective on a solution to Algeria's crisis if you were requested to mediate?

A. The present crisis in Algeria can be solved through the conducting of a real and serious national dialogue between the government and popular parties without excluding the Islamic Salvation Front. This dialogue should discuss means to stop bloodshed in the first instance and to revert to true democracy in the second. People should be given the right to elect their rulers in full freedom, dignity and transparency. Algeria is passing through a transitional period in which a number of measures should be made to calm down the situation. These measures are:

1- The release of all political prisoners.

2- Lifting the siege imposed on the people.

3- Cancelling all arbitrary and suppressive laws that have been issued since the military coup in 1992.

4- Allowing the public to enjoy their individual and public freedoms, especially the freedom of expression and using all local media without discrimination.

5- Allowing all parties to practice their political activities and giving them the right to rally and organize public meetings.

This transitional period should continue for a short period of time after which honest, true and free public elections should be held without the exclusion of any political party. The results of these elections should be respected. I believe the Algerian people know who the qualified candidates are. When qualified young leaders are elected and gain their people's trust, economic and social reforms can be made easily and freely.

Israel flies in the face of Erbakan

Occupied Jerusalem- By: Khalid Amayreh

Israeli military officials scoffed anew at Turkish Prime Minister Necmettin Erbakan's efforts to scale-down strategic cooperation between Turkey and Israel.

On Tuesday, 13 May, Israeli Defense Minister Yitzhak Mordechai said military cooperation with Turkey, including joint naval exercises, would go ahead despite Erbakan's objections.

Erbakan had earlier announced that the three-way Turkish-Israeli- American navel manoeuvres slated for this summer were "postponed to coming years."

"The joint cooperation between Israel and Turkey, including the naval exercise, is a common interest of both countries," said Avi Benyahu, media advisor for Defense Minister Yitzhak Mordechai, in reaction to Erbakan's remarks.

Benyahu said Israel did not receive any information or an official announcement to postpone the manoeuvres.

According to Israeli sources, the Israeli government has been urging the anti-Islamic Turkish military to "ignore Erbakan" and "to put an end to his anti-Israeli stance."

Mordechai reportedly places great importance on the joint manoeuvres with Turkey. On Wednesday, 14 May, Mordechai was quoted by the Israeli English daily, the Jerusalem Post, as saying that the navel exercises would serve as a show of stability and strength in the eastern Mediterranean and would help quell potential dangers.

The diplomatic talk, however, conceals a possessive desire on Israel's part to outflank certain Arab and Muslim countries in the region, particularly Syria and Iran, and to make sure that "the military in Turkey maintains its grip on power" in the strategically important Muslim country.

Israeli intelligence circles reportedly recommended recently that Israel continue to pay ample attention to developments in Turkey, particularly to "heightened Islamic awareness among Turks."

According to Israeli press reports, Israeli intelligence suggested that Turkey would face a greater danger from Islamic fundamentalists than that coming from the PKK.

Erbakan's surprise statement came just a week after the Deputy Chief of Staff of the Turkish army, GTen. Cevik Bir, paid a publicized visit to Israel.

On 14 May, Turkish generals vowed to go ahead with the slated manoeuvres with Israel regardless of what Erbakan says.

One general was quoted as saying that "matters of this kind are none of Erbakan's business."

The latest mini-crisis reflected the stark dichotomy between the Islamist Erbakan and the staunchly anti-Islamic Turkish army top brass who view Islam as a threat to their grip on the Turkish people.

A LESSON FROM IRAN

BY: Khalid Mohammed

Occupied Jerusalem- In sharp contrast to the bleak state of affairs in neighbouring Arab countries, where despots stay in power until death, assassination or senility, the sun of democracy rose last Friday in Tehran, Isfahan, Shiraz, Mashhad and Tabris as well as other parts of Iran. As many as 30 million Iranians went to the polls to elect a new president to replace incumbent President Hujattul Islam Ali Akbar Hashmi Rafsanjani whose term of office is due to expire in August. And in ways largely unknown in the Third World, and Iran is a Third World country, the presidential election passed smoothly, quietly and peacefully, without being marred by violence or bloodshedóa genuine manifestation of civility. More important, however, the election was held in a free and fair atmosphere, as testified to by friends and foe alike, and tacitly recognized by the enemies of truth in Tel Aviv and Washington, and some regional capitals, who impatiently wait for the "winds of doom and gloom" to engulf the Islamic Republic.

Indeed, the Iranian regime, contrary to all American and Israeli-disseminated propaganda and misinformation, seems to have succeeded in the problematic test of democracy. The fact that the ìcandidate of the regimeî failed in the face of the "candidate for change" Muhammed Khatimi, without causing any compunction in the executive branch, demonstrates that Iran (regime and people) has moved a long way toward political maturity without sacrificing its Islamic values. The fact that the Iranians have been able to replace an incumbent president by a new one peacefully and, above all, freely, speaks volumes as it stands in stark contrast to what happened in Algeria six years ago and the bloody civil war that has rocked that North African country ever since.

So, hail to Iran, and "Mubarak" to President Khatimi whose mission has just begun; and so many thanks to President Rafsanjani who oversaw this carnival of Islamic democracy. But the most special thanks must go to Hujattul Islam Ali Akbar Natek Nuri, who not only accepted the will of the people with exemplary sportsmanship, but sought to congratulate Khatimi for his victory.

May we learn from Iran. Indeed, we have much to learn
