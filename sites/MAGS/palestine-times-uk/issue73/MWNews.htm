An evaluation attempt

On the occasion of the 9th anniversary of the Sudan revolution

By Randa Awadh

Eight years have passed since the Sudan revolution. Many thought it would only survive a few months. The new government was born into an environment of international change. Soon after the new Sudanís birth, the Soviet Union collapsed leaving only one dominant power in the international arena to control and monopolize the world through what is now called "the new world order". All of a sudden Sudan became a target for all kinds of cleverly devised accusations such as hosting terrorism and violating human rights.

In the opinion of many observers the Sudanese government has survived because of the deep trust and loyalty of the people to their leaders. This compatibility has been achieved through the following:

1. The Islamic attitude adopted by the revolution was a dream come true for the Sudanese people.

2. Many achievements were accomplished in spite of unfavourable and difficult conditions which prevailed.

3. The leaders of the revolution are from the people themselves--an example of fairness and asceticism. This made the people trust in the government and in any programmes, however difficult, it adopted.

The political administration should be a reflection of the nationís wants and needs. Soon after the revolution the western provinces asked the government to apply Islamic law and the government conceded, thus fulfilling the dream of the Sudanese people while gaining their trust and loyalty at the same time; a dream ignored and put aside by other political parties. Of course such a step wrought many difficulties and dangers for the Sudanese people both locally and internationally.

In building the new Sudan a number of points were taken into consideration by the government:

1. Reviving hope and life in the people.

2. Concentrating on programmes which emphasized independence, pride and self-esteem.

3. Defining the problems of the country and setting solutions and reform plans.

These points helped Sudan cut the red tape put in place by the new world order as a result of itís pro-Islamic government. Slogans appeared such as ìwe eat what we plantî and ìwe wear what we makeî. The government also fought international monopoly of sugar and wheat and succeeded, thus achieving self-sufficiency in these vital commodities.

These victories resulted in many problems. Suddenly Sudan was making international news headlines -- first as a terrorist country and second as a violator of human rights. Consequently, the international community isolated it politically and economically. However, neither did the Sudan government nor did the people waver; they stood fast.

Another problem boosted by the international community was the southern rebels led by John Garang. By 1989, owing to the weakness of the Sudanese army and internal strife among its political parties, rebels had seized about 90% of southern Sudan. The new government made its number-one priority to free all cities. This was achieved by 1993 which increased the peopleís trust.

One of the most important positive points, as mentioned by Lebanese politician, writer and prominent party leader Kareem Bakardouni, is the Sudanese leaders themselves. They are honest, fair and ascetic. Their lifestyle is simple and rough; similar to any Sudanese common man. Nobody doubts their political, strategic, intellectual or leadership capabilities. It would be very difficult, if not impossible, for any external force to threaten or terrorize them or the people.

The future is brighter for Sudan for two reasons:

1. The peace agreement signed with the rebels and neighbouring countries in the south. Although both Uganda and Eriteria are still trying to remove, indirectly, the Islamic government in Khartoum, there are rumours of a plan to attack the eastern part of Sudan. However, the Sudanese government is militarily ready to fight off any attack in order to protect its sovereignty over its land.

2. Oil has been discovered and mined. It will be utilized in the future for the benefit of attaining an independent state.

The problem with Egypt remains. Establishing good relations is important because of the strategic role Egypt plays in the future of Sudan. ìHow will Sudan deal with this crisisî will be the title of the next episode on Sudanís battle of survival.

Liberty Denounces Detention of Tunisian

Opposition Leader at Frankfurt Airport

 

London- Liberty for the Muslim World denounced the detention on 19 June, of a Tunisian opposition leader by the German security authorities at Frankfurt Airport.

A report issued by the London-based organization which watches the human rights in the Muslim World said the German security officials arrested on 5 June, the Tunisian journalist and political opposition leader Al-Habib Al-Mukni who was on a routine journalistic visit to Germany. Mr. Al-Mukni is the editor in chief of Al-Insan periodical, which is published in Paris, is a founding and leading member of the Tunisian Ennahda (Renaissance) Party.

"Liberty for the Muslim World fears for the well-being of Mr. Al-Mukni who, as past experience has shown, is likely to have been arrested in response to an extradition request from the authorities in his own country, Tunisia," said the report adding that for the past several years, enormous pressures have been exerted by the Tunisian government on European governments as well as on those of the United States and Canada to urge them to expel or extradite Tunisians suspected of opposing the regime in Tunis.

Mr. Al-Mukni is 45 years old, married and has three children. He has been living in the French capital for several years since his request for asylum was granted by the French government.

"While strongly condemning the detention of Mr. Al-Mukni, Liberty for the Muslim World calls for his immediate release and appeals to the French authorities, whose travel document is appropriately issued by them, to intervene with the German authorities in order to secure his release and safe return to his place of residence. Any attempt by the Tunisian regime to extradite him against his will to Tunisia should be resisted," stressed the report.

Liberty pointed out that scores of Tunisians suspected of opposition to the current regime in Tunisia are subjected to harassment and threats of deportation every year throughout the European continent. "The Tunisian government has benefited from the security cooperation agreement it has signed with various European governments, and has thus been pressuring them to extradite or expel its opponents," it added.

Algeria's Military-Prescribed Democracy is a Mockery- Liberty

Liberty for the Muslim World is of the opinion that the current 'democratic' exercise in Algeria is futile, a report issued by the London-based Islamic human rights organization.

"By holding the parliamentary elections, the Algerian military authorities only seek to consolidate their control and legitimise their hegemony in the name of democracy. At least sixty thousand Algerians have lost their lives for the military junta's project to materialise, namely the project of establishing a system of governance that is above questioning or checking," said Liberty.

"Clearly, the current exercise has been designed, and was only permitted to proceed, in order to give rise to a rubber stamp parliament. The 'elected' council would be expected to endorse the policies dictated by a minority regime born out of a coup against the will of the public as it became clear that the only fair and clean democratic process - not only in the history of Algeria but in the history of the entire Arab region - was bound to transfer power from a corrupt minority that sunk the whole country in heaps of foreign debt to the duly elected representatives of the people."

Liberty's report went on to say that "the most obvious indication of the futility of the exercise is that it is taking place in circumstances where terrorising and killing civilians has not for a moment abated; where several thousands of Algerians suspected of opposing the military regime lavish in crowded detention centres, including the elected representatives of the people in the canceled 1991 elections; where thousands of Algerians have fled persecution and are now forced to live in exile; where heavy restrictions are imposed on political activities; where human rights are violated on massive scale; and where freedom of expression is confiscated. What purpose would such elections serve other than to maintain the status quo, especially as the largest and most popular political party, the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS), continues to be banned and excluded from the process?"

Liberty for the Muslim World sees that the holding of the elections in such an atmosphere only confirms the suspicion that the new era will be none but an extension of the previous one. "It is indeed regrettable that the leaders of participating political parties have failed to see that the generals who intervened in order to ban and destroy FIS will not hesitate to intervene once more if they feel that the interests of the minority they represent are being threatened. Undoubtedly, the interests of the elite which squandered national funds and ruled the majority by terror are incompatible with the interests of the people."

The report concluded by saying that "the sudden birth of a political party that represents the ruling elite is a clear indication that Algeria is heading toward a future dominated by a single party dictatorship of the type that existed when the National Liberation Front (FLN) was the only political party in the country, acting for several decades as a political platform for the military junta."

 

Liberty Concerned Over Fate of

130 (Chinese) Muslim Refugees in Pakistan

Liberty for the Muslim World expressed its deeply concern over the fate of about 130 Uighur Muslim students (normally inhabitants of Zinjiang province which is currently under Chinese rule) who have fled Chinese persecution and sought refuge in Pakistan. Liberty also strongly condemned the Pakistani government's decision on 13 May 1997 to extradite to China, upon the official request of its government, 12 such students who were all immediately executed by the Chinese authorities.

"The Pakistani government is thus held responsible for the death of these students for having agreed to hand them over despite knowing they would be executed," a report issued by the Islamic human rights organization said.

"The Muslims of Zinjiang province have always suffered discrimination and their human rights have been severely violated so much so that the persecution is believed to amount to a slow genocide with the objective of replacing the indigenous Uighur population with a Chinese one. It is estimated that the Chinese population of the province has increased from 2.5% in 1944, when the province was annexed to China, to 30% at the present. Thus, the indigenous population has felt threatened and several political initiatives have been made to demand respect for human rights and the recognition of the Muslim majority's right to self-determination. The freedom movement is particularly popular among the students. Local sources say that sixty thousand students suspected of involvement in political activities are currently imprisoned by the Chinese authorities."

Liberty for the Muslim World feared that the Pakistani authorities "will once again succumb to Chinese demands and hand over the Uighur Muslim refugees, who would certainly meet the same fate their extradited colleagues met before."

Some Pakistani media has recently published the names of the 12 students who were executed upon extradition. They are as follows: 1) Abdullah 2) Abdurrashid 3) Abdussamad 4) Muhammad Ali 5)Muhammad Karim 6) Abulqasim 7) Abdurrahim 8) Muhammad

Yassin 9) Abdussalam 10) Mur Ali 11) Abdullah 12)Muhammad Trawi

Liberty appealed to the Pakistani President, the Pakistani Prime Minister and all concerned Pakistani officials to cease the practice of extraditing political refugees to China. "For by agreeing to extradite the refugees, Pakistan sentences them to death, as if it were the judge while China played the role of the executioner," said Liberty. It called upon all human rights organisations and on local and international Islamic associations to "intervene so as to persuade the Pakistani authorities to reconsider its policy of security cooperation with China, a policy that clearly involves the violation of the most basic human right, the right to life."
