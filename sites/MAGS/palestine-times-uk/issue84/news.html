Hamas calls on Palestinians to escalate Intifadah

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) condemned the massacre perpetrated by the Zionist troops in the West Bank and Gaza against Palestinians who were commemorating the 50th anniversary of the Palestinian Nakba (catastrophe) on 14 May. Nine Palestinians were killed and more than 400 others were injured, most of them in the Gaza Strip, by Israeli soldiers on what was called Black Thursday.
"Soldiers of the Enemy opened fire savagely and cruelly directly at the Palestinian protesters who gathered in each Palestinian city, village and street to reiterate our right to our own land and holy places and to renew the challenge against the Zionist enemy that usurped our land on the 50th anniversary of the establishment of their terrorist State, making our people homeless and exiled," Hamas said.
"Nine martyrs and more than 400 Palestinians were injured proving that the blood bath will continue and that our people's resistance and Jihad will never stop as long as there is occupation," Hamas added.
It went on to say that the savage massacre committed by gangs of the Zionist army happened while the gangs of Jewish settlers were blowing up one of the gates of the Holy Mosque of Al Aqsa under the supervision of the Occupation soldiers. "They reveal their Talmudic hatred and dreams to destroy Al Masjid Al Aqsa and build their alleged temple on it," Hamas pointed out.
The Islamic Movement asserted that the "Zionist crime against our people on the day of Nakba reiterates the hostile and malicious nature of the Zionist enemy against the Palestinians and that the so-called peace will never change this nature."
Hamas also called on the Palestinian Authority "to reunite with its people who completely reject its political conduct that destroys our Cause and to stop running after the fata morgana (mirage) of the peace process which is already dead." It urged the self-rule Authority to take a lesson and consider the People's demands to continue the struggle and Jihad against the Zionist Occupation and "who praise Ezzul Deen Al Qassam Brigades' deeds."
Hamas called on the Palestinian People to support the option of resistance and to escalate their street confrontations everywhere. "Our arrogant Enemy does not understand any language but the resistance and steadfastness." The Islamic Movement urged the Arab and Muslim governments and peoples to condemn the Zionist crime against Al Aqsa Mosque and to support the Jihad of the Palestinian People who defend Al Aqsa and all other holy places in Palestine.
Meanwhile, Hamas condemned, in another communiqué, the 12 May murder of Martyr Khairy Alqam by Jewish terrorists in Occupied Jerusalem.
Martyr Alqam, 51 and father of seven children, was stabbed to death by Jewish settlers while he was going to work in the Holy City.
"This crime cannot be separated from the mentality of the continued Zionist terrorism against our people. It proves the continued struggle against an enemy that does not understand any language except force."
Hamas assailed the Palestinian Authority's efforts to check the Jihad and resistance against Occupation under the pretexts of "protecting futile agreements".
"While an innocent Palestinian man is killed in Jerusalem, the security bodies of the self-rule Authority save no efforts in chasing and harassing the Mujahideen, and in arresting and torturing scores of our People to abort their Jihad," Hamas said.
Hamas warned of a new security arm inside the Palestinian Authority that works to protect the Occupation security against the Palestinian anger. "Despite the stumbling peace process, the PA's police are still chasing and arresting our Mujahideen and are participating in joint security training courses with Zionist security officers in Germany and the United States," Hamas said.

Amnesty: Israel should observe call for immediate halt to torture
By UN Committee against Torture

Amnesty International welcomed last month's conclusions by the United Nations Committee against Torture, which reiterated its call to Israel to cease immediately the use of torture and ill treatment during interrogation. The appeal came as the Committee gave its conclusions on Israel's second periodic report.

The Committee found that Israel, on its own admission, used "hooding, shackling in painful positions, sleep-deprivation and shaking" against detainees. The Committee reiterated that all these methods constituted torture, banned by Article 1 of the Convention against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment (the Convention).

The Committee also expressed concern about the holding of detainees in administrative detention, without charge or trial, for long periods, particularly where the detainees themselves posed no threat to State security. This was an apparent reference to the November 1997 decision of the Israeli Supreme Court endorsing the holding of 10 Lebanese detainees as "bargaining chips". The Committee recommended that Israel review the practice of administrative detention to ensure that it does not amount to cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment.

The Committee also noted Israel's "apparent failure" to implement any of its previous recommendations made in 1994 and 1997, and reaffirmed their recommendation.

The Committee asked the Israeli Government to incorporate the Convention's provisions into Israeli law. Amnesty International called for a review of the draft law on the General Security Service, currently before the Knesset. The bill includes provisions that violate the Convention. For example, the draft law grants GSS employees immunity from criminal liability if they are "acting in good faith and in a reasonable manner". If interrogation rules continue to sanction torture and ill treatment, as they do now, GSS agents will still be able to use such methods with impunity, in violation of Article 4 of the Convention.

Last month, the Israeli Supreme Court held its second session reviewing the legality of official rules that apparently authorize the GSS to use interrogation methods such as violent shaking; sleep deprivation for prolonged periods; shackling in painful positions; hooding; and forcing of detainees to listen to loud noise for prolonged periods.

Amnesty International urged the Supreme Court to take note of the Committee's recommendations and declare illegal all GSS interrogation methods that violate the Convention's prohibition on torture and cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment, methods condemned by the Committee as torture.

Arafat scraps municipal elections for now
Occupied Jerusalem - PT Staff

The decision by Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat to postpone "until further notice" the organization of local elections in the occupied territories has passed quietly, without receiving the attention it deserves. PA officials from Arafat downward have cited a variety of largely unconvincing reasons for the "temporary freeze." Dr. Saeb Ereikat, the chief Palestinian negotiator (who devotes little time, if any, to his official post as Minister of Local Governance), claimed that "unhelpful political conditions" have necessitated the delay--an allusion to the Israeli refusal to carry out the second phase of redeployment in the West Bank.

"We want to make sure that the elections are free from any Israeli interference," said Ereikat during a recent phone-in programme on Sawt Falastin, the official PA radio station.

Arafat, sounding like the great democrat he is not, told foreign journalists a few months ago that "we have other priorities... and this is not the most opportune time for elections."

In reality, the real reason for the effective cancellation of the elections seems to have very little to do with possible Israeli interference as alleged by the PA. Arafat, after all, was elected along with the Legislative Council in the shadow of Israeli tanks and nobody said anything about that. Moreover, the PLO-affiliated mayors, namely the late Fahd al Kawasmeh of Hebron and Karim Khalaf of Ramallah, as well as Basam al Shaka'a of Nablus, were all freely and democratically elected in 1976 when the Israeli occupation was as ubiquitous and insidious as ever.

So, the real reason for the postponement must squarely lie in the PA anxiety that if a free and democratic election were to take place now, Arafat and his entire autonomy scheme would incur a major defeat. This would send a clear and unwanted signal to Arafat of the massive Palestinian disillusionment with the Oslo process and disenchantment with Arafat's autocratic and unsuccessful leadership.

In other words, the elections could turn into "a referendum on the peace process", which if allowed to materialize, would effectively amount to a vote of no confidence in Arafat himself and the entire Oslo process.

This apprehension on the part of the PA was recently voiced by Legislative Council member Rawiya al Shawwa, who said in response when questioned that the postponement of local elections "has in reality nothing to do with the stalled peace process but has everything to do with the PA fear that the opposition would win if elections were to be held at a time when PA popularity is down the drain."

Last year, the Preventive Security Chief in Gaza, Muhammad Dahalan, handed a secret memo to Arafat in which he recommended that local and municipal elections "be put off indefinitely or scrapped altogether in order to forestall a possible victory by Hamas, which would be embarrassing for the Palestinian Authority." (Hamas and the rest of the Palestinian opposition groups expressed willingness to take part in the local elections as opposed to the legislative elections.)

Dahalan has never denied the existence of the memo. Instead, he pointed out that "things of this nature happen all over the world...and it is our duty as a security agency to issue recommendations to the political leadership concerning real or perceived dangers."

Abu Ammar did not throw the "Dahalan document," as it later came to be known, into wastepaper basket. He rather instructed Ereikat to see to it that "municipal committees" made up of mostly "Fatah activists" be set up in virtually every town. The "consolidation process" did not stop there. The PA Chairman also ordered that his loyalists be appointed to key positions in all municipal councils.

In smaller villages and hamlets, Arafat ordered the appointment of "local committees" composed of young and inexperienced former Fatah intifadah activists who, using the words of a former West Bank mayor, don't know a potato from a watermelon.

In some cases, elected mayors were forced to "submit their resignations", letting Arafat's loyalists take their place. In Tulkarm, for example, the elected and long-serving mayor, Helmi Hanun, was forced to quit, only to be replaced by another Fatah committee that is, first and foremost, responsible to Arafat, not to the people. In Nablus, the elected mayor Basam al Shaka'a, who lost his both legs in a Gush Emunim terrorist attack in the late 1970s, was also ostracized and replaced by his pro-Arafat cousin, Ghassan al Shaka'a. The appointment process itself is fraught with all types of administrative and ethical flaws. Favoritism, nepotism, bribery and cronyism are rife features in the bulk of the new councils.

The policy of "appointment," the antithesis of everything the Palestinian people aspired to, has understandably generated a preponderance of anger and helplessness among many Palestinians. As a result, protests took place in Tulkarm demanding that "we choose our mayors." In Ramallah, a mixed Christian-Muslim town, the town's appointed mayor, Dr. Shawki Harb (a prominent cardiologist), resigned from his post.

"They (the PA) told us you will be a care-taker for only six months...we can't accept appointment as the norm rather than the exception," said Harb upon submitting his resignation to Ereikat.

In addition to appointing mayors in major Palestinian towns, Ereikat et. al also embarked on a spate of hiring "nationalist" mukhtars, or clan notables, to replace the more traditional mukhtars. This act revived dormant inter- and intra-clan animosities that were once thought to have disappeared irreversibly in the years of the intifadah.

Palestinian intellectuals, who described it as "reactionary" and "taking us back to the Ottoman era", scathingly criticized the stupid and harmful feat. However, neither the manifest backwardness of the silly business of appointing mukhtars nor the criticism by the media and intellectuals prompted the PA to reconsider its policy. Interestingly, the only PA response was "we have merely been following the desire of the people."

Bloody "Nakba day"
From Khalid Amayreh (Hebron)

Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians took to the streets on 14 May to commemorate the 50th anniversary of the Nakba, the loss of Palestine to the Jews in 1948, the brutal uprooting of most Palestinians from their homeland and their subsequent dispersal to the Four Corners of the world.

The event, organized by the Palestinian Authority, was meant to remind the world of the enormous (and continuing) nightmare inflicted upon the Palestinians as a result of the establishment of Israel.

The "march of the million," as it was called, was also designed to convey a message to "all concerned" that the Palestinian people, far from being obliterated or assimilated, were still awaiting a justice 50 years after the Nakba.

Highlighting this message, Palestinian children at the Dheishe refugee camp near Bethlehem marched through the dusty streets of their camp, holding aloft rusty keys to their former family homes in Palestine to symbolize their enduring plight and unabated determination to return to the homeland of their forefathers.

Moreover, the children carried signs reading the names of their former villages, some of them only a few miles across the "Green Line," the 1948 armistice line between Israeli proper and the West Bank.

In Nablus, where as many as 50,000 marchers took to the streets, masked Fatah activists held automatic rifles rather than keys, firing repeatedly into the air. Their message was clear, namely that they were readily willing to revert to armed struggle as soon as a death certificate was issued to the moribund peace process.

Hamas was also vociferous. Sheikh Hamed al Beitawi, a senior Hamas leader in the Nablus area, led a march of 15,000 Hamas supporters, and fired a pistol into the air to mark "the end of this humiliating peace and the beginning of a new era of resistance."

"The Palestinian Authority should be brave enough to tell the people that Oslo was a failure, so that we could resume the armed struggle side by side," said al Beitawi as Israeli and American flags and Benyamin Netanyahu's effigy were being trampled on and set ablaze.

In Ramallah's Manara Square, PA Chairman Yasser Arafat addressed the marchers in a live radio and television broadcast.

"The right of return is a sacred right that no one has the right to deny us," said Arafat in a rather soft voice, reflecting his deteriorating health.

The PA Chairman reiterated essentially the same words he usually utters in his public speeches, saying "We aren't asking for the moon, but rather for an independent Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital."

However, to suit the occasion, Arafat added that "We are not a guest in the world but right-owners who deserve a homeland like others in this vast world."

Nonetheless, in an unconscious indication reflecting his lack of faith in the Oslo process, Arafat recited a Qur'anic verse three times that prophesizes the ultimate demise of Israel at the hands of Muslims:

And We decreed for the Children of Israel in the Scripture, that indeed you would do mischief on the earth twice and you will become tyrants and extremely arrogant! So, when the promise came for the first of the two, We sent against you slaves of Ours given to terrible warfare. They entered the very innermost parts of your homes. And it was a promise (completely) fulfilled. Then We gave you once again, a return of victory over them. And We helped you with wealth and children and made you more numerous in manpower. (And We said): "If you do good for your own selves, and if you do evil (you do it) against yourselves." Then, when the second promise came to pass, (We permitted your enemies) to make your faces sorrowful and to enter the mosque (of Jerusalem) as they had entered it before, and to destroy with utter destruction all that fell in their hands (Dr. Muhammad Taqi-ud-Din Al-Hilali and Dr. Muhammad Muhsin Khan, Interpretation of the Meanings of The Noble Qur'an in the English Language, Al-Isra, Surah XVII:4-7.

The above Qur'anic verses constitute the essence of Islamist (e.g. Hamas) perception on how the conflict between Jews and Muslims over Palestine will ultimately come to an end, a prophetic view shared by Jewish and Christian scriptures.

Protests turn bloody
The commemoration of Nakba, which the PA said was planned to be peaceful, soon turned into "clashes" between stone-hurling Palestinian youngsters and well-entrenched and ever-ready Israeli soldiers, who did not hesitate to shoot to kill.

The most violent outburst took place in the Gaza Strip along the so-called friction lines around the Eretz checkpoint in the north and the Kush Katif settlement bloc in the south. There, thousands of Palestinian youths, depressed by the claustrophobic and narrow horizons of the small Gaza enclave where they are squeezed hopelessly between Israel and the sea, sought to vent their frustration by throwing stones at the heavily armed and well protected Israeli soldiers. They retaliated with metal-coated rubber bullets and eventually live ammunition.

The result was five Palestinians dead, including a male nurse who showed up to give first-aid to the wounded, and as many as 400 injured--10 of them with serious gun-shot wounds.

In the West Bank, at least one Palestinian was killed at the Qalandia refugee camp, and scores of demonstrators were injured in Hebron at the Bab El Zawiya Square, a few hundred metres from the Jewish settlement enclave in the heart of the town. Similarly violent clashes also took place in Ramallah, resulting in about 100 Palestinian injuries.

PA information minister, Yasser Abed Rabbou described the high Palestinian casualties as a massacre, accusing IDF soldiers of having adopted a "a shoot-to-kill policy." For their part, the Israelis retorted as usual by saying that they opened fire only when their soldiers' lives were endangered.

Eventually, by nightfall, the clashes receded as Palestinian police forcefully removed protesters from "the friction lines", especially after receiving a stern warning from Israeli military officials who reportedly threatened to enter PA-held areas if calm was not restored.

Bloody Thursday, as it is now dubbed by many Palestinians, was an expression of Palestinian disillusionment with what has evidently become a disgraceful peace process that is not worth maintaining.

Moreover, it was also an example of typical Israeli brutality on the very day Palestinians were remembering when their plight began 50 years ago, a metaphor suggesting that what was true then still holds true now.


Jewish terrorists set fire to Al Aqsa gate
By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem (14 May) Around midnight on 13 May, Jewish extremists set fire to Bab El Maghanmeh, one of Al Aqsa Mosque's main entrance gates, destroying the large solid wood door and causing some damage to nearby structures.

Sheikh Mohammed Hussein, Director of Al Masjidul Aqsa, said the Jewish perpetrators tossed a highly flammable substance at the huge gate/door around midnight, setting the door on fire.

Sheikh Hussein said Jewish extremists perpetrated the crime, possibly in connivance with circles within the Israeli government and security forces.

"We are well aware that several Jewish groups have been trying to attack the Al Aqsa Mosque; we also know that the Israeli government is tacitly encouraging the terrorists," said Sheikh Hussein, who helped extinguish the fire.

Moreover, the Sheikh warned that the incident was not auguring well for the future, saying, "I have no doubt that their ultimate goal is the destruction of the Mosque."

In 1969, a Zionist extremist set fire to the Mosque, destroying much of its interior, including the ancient mimbar.

Moreover, Israeli authorities opened a subterranean tunnel beneath the al Aqsa Mosque in 1996, triggering widespread Palestinian protests.

The recent crime of arson took place at a time when hundreds of thousands of Palestinians are marking the Nakba, the usurpation of Palestine by the Zionists.

Netanyahu: "Peace process is dead; not our fault"

Occupied Jerusalem (22 May)- Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu admitted for the first time on 22 May that the Israeli-Palestinian peace process was dead.
Speaking before the diplomatic corps at West Jerusalem's King David Hotel, Netanyahu said, "The peace process has ceased to exist," adding that "Israel was not responsible for the ostensible collapse of the peace process."
The extremist Israeli premier, who in the run-up to the Israeli general elections in 1996 vowed to undo the Oslo process, blamed the Palestinians for "the death of paralysis" of the Oslo process, arguing that resistance operations by Hamas and the Islamic Jihad were directly responsible for corroding the peace process and creating an atmosphere of absolute distrust between the parties to the peace process.
However, Netanyahu ignored the ongoing Israeli campaign of settlement building in the West Bank and adamant refusal to carry out the second phase of redeployment as stipulated in the Oslo agreement.
Earlier, Netanyahu reportedly said he was inclined to hold a summit meeting with Palestinian Chairman Yasser Arafat in order to try to convince him to "lower Palestinian expectations and accept a compromise deal that would guarantee Israeli security while giving Palestinians a dignified autonomy."
According to Netanyahu's communications director, David Bar Elan, Netanyahu raised the idea of a new summit with Arafat during his recent visit to the United States.
Never the less, it seems unlikely that Arafat will agree to a new summit with Netanyahu as the Palestinian leader has become convinced that Netanyahu was after the destruction of the peace process.
On 21 May, Arafat warned again that the entire Middle East would explode in fire if the peace process was allowed to collapse.
Speaking in Gaza following a meeting of the Fatah Revolutionary Council, Arafat said the Palestinians had other choices, which could mean a possible resumption of the Intifadah.
In other developments, an Israeli military court ruled on 22 May that the three Israeli soldiers who murdered three Palestinian labourers near Tarqumya in March were innocent and therefore would not stand trial.
The decision was announced after army prosecutors opined that the soldiers in question behaved properly in accordance with standing orders and committed no wrongdoing.
A representative of the bereaved Palestinian families was not surprised.
Hasan Rajoub, a relative of Adnan Rajoub, one of the three labourers murdered by the Zionist soldiers, said he didn't expect "one iota of justice from the Zionists."
"The judge is my enemy," said Rajoub.

Liberty calls for an end to torture in PA prisons
and for immediate release of political detainees

London-PT:
Liberty for the Muslim World criticized the Palestinian self-rule authority for its record of human rights abuses and continued arbitrary conduct against the Palestinians in the occupied territories.
In a statement it issued on 13 May, Liberty said that since the announcement more than a month and a half ago by the Palestinian Authority (PA) that it was investigating the murder of Muhyiddin Al-Sharif, a Hamas military leader, various Palestinian security bodies have continued to target and detain any person suspected of association with Al-Sharif and the Hamas military wing, or suspected of involvement in Al-Sharif's assassination, whose circumstances remain obscure.
"These detentions have been taking place in what is clearly a total absence of the rule of law, a matter that has been a source of utmost concern to local lawyers and human rights activists," Liberty said, adding that the Palestinian security apparatus seems to have been given free rein to pressure detainees, both physically and psychologically, and to intimidate them so as to confess to having had a role in murdering Al-Sharif.
"The excesses of Palestinian interrogators and the acquiescence of the PA political leadership has prompted the Attorney General to resign his job in protest. After detaining a number of Hamas leaders, who have challenged the PA's claim that Al-Sharif was killed by his own comrades within the Hamas military wing, the security apparatus have arrested a number journalists for allegedly having transgressed the limits set by the Palestinian Authority in covering the story of Al-Sharif's assassination. Abbas Al-Mumani (25 years old), who worked as a photographer for Reuters press agency, continues to be in detention."
Liberty pointed out that Al-Mumani, who managed to escape from detention only to be re-arrested several hours later, has been subjected to brutal persecution at the hands of his Palestinian interrogators who seek to extract from him the source of a video tape, which Reuters released to the world media. It added that the PA was agitated by the appearance in the tape of a Hamas military leader who refuted the PA murder story and accused Israel of being ultimately responsible for the liquidation of his comrade.
In addition to Al-Mumani, a number of Palestinian students from Bir Zeit University, An-Najah University, Bethlehem University and the Islamic University in Gaza have been arrested and are said to be persecuted. The students have been either suspected of association with Hamas or are being punished for protesting against the detention of their colleagues.
"Liberty for the Muslim World is abhorred that the Palestinian Authority resorts to the very same measures the Palestinians under occupation have for many years condemned the Israelis for adopting. It is quite astonishing that Western political circles, especially in the United States, exhibit total indifference toward the PA violation of human rights in spite of the fact that the PA acquires both legitimacy and existence from the Western powers that sponsor the current peace process between the PA and Israel," said the statement.
It strongly denounced the PA violations of Palestinian human rights, including the storming of houses, arbitrary arrests, the use of physical and psychological torture and threats of liquidation or abuse of relatives, especially female family members. "Such internationally forbidden practices seem to have become routine practices in the areas administered by the PA," Liberty asserted.
It called for the immediate release of all detainees, including journalist Abbas Al-Mumani. In order to guarantee respect for human rights and put an end to excesses, it is essential that the rule of law and respect for the role of the judiciary be restored. "Furthermore, it would be conducive to the public interest to prosecute security officers responsible for massive violations of human rights. Liberty urges Reuters, who has been accused by local human rights groups of deserting Al-Mumani after his detention in spite of his services, to raise his case at the highest levels possible and to exert all possible efforts in order to secure his release," said the statement.

Israel celebrates anniversary of Jerusalem usurpation

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (24 May) In a step meant to reaffirm Zionist usurpation of Baytul Makdes (Jerusalem), the Israeli occupation authorities celebrated on 24 May the thirty-first anniversary of its military occupation and subsequent annexation of East Jerusalem, a city of utmost religious importance to Muslims, Christians and Jews.

Celebrations took the form of several rallies, marches and concerts, as well as a military parade in the streets of the Holy City during which speakers vowed to "clean Jerusalem of goyem" (non-Jews).

Moreover, hundreds of Messianic Jews belonging to ultra-orthodox groups sought to forcibly enter the Haram al Sharif plaza in order to reassert "Jewish ambitions of seeing the rebuilding of the Temple of Solomon."

However, Muslim Wakf officials, who warned earlier that any encroachment into the Islamic shrine by the extremists could turn into a bloodbath, repulsed the Jewish settlers.

The extremists are affiliated with such terrorist groups as "Hay've Kayam," "Kahane Hay" and "the Temple Mount Faithfuls," which make the "prayed-for destruction of the Al Aqsa Mosque" the centre of their ideology and theology.

Having failed to enter the Haram al Sharif compound, the rowdy settlers then headed towards the Orient House, the unofficial headquarters of the PLO in East Jerusalem.

There the settlers shouted racist slogans against Muslims and Arabs, including "death to the Arabs," and "Arabs out of Jerusalem."

The largely nervous festivities continued till after nightfall as thousands of soldiers representing the various branches of the Israeli army paraded through the occupied Arab town.

Marking the occasion, Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu reiterated his government's intransigent stance on the West Bank.

"The River Jordan will always be Israel's eastern border," said Netanyahu before a large rally at Jabal al Mukabber, renamed by the Zionists as Mount Herzel.

Netanyahu also repeated earlier statements that the Oslo peace process had died two years ago, even before he became Prime Minister in May 1996.

Israel's ostentatious celebrations of the conquest of Jerusalem drew sharp and angry reactions from the Palestinian Authority.

PA Chairman Yasser Arafat called the celebrations "provocative", arguing that "Jerusalem was an occupied city just like the rest of the West Bank."

Another Palestinian official, Ahmed Abdul Rahman, threatened to "withdraw our recognition of Israel" and "resume armed struggle."

Meanwhile, around 13 Palestinian labourers were injured by Israeli gunfire on 24 May near an Israeli roadblock at the northern edge of the autonomous enclave of Bethlehem.

Palestinian sources said the labourers were prevented from proceeding to Jerusalem by Israeli soldiers prompting them to hurl stones at the occupation soldiers who responded with rubber bullets and live ammunition.
