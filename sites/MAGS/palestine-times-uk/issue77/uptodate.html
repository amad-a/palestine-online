Israeli soldiers shoot 40 Palestinians in Bethlehem

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (29 November)

As many as 40 Palestinians were injured when Israeli occupation soldiers opened live ammunition, rubber bullets and tear gas at Palestinian demonstrators protesting the continuing detention of Itaf Elayan, an Islamic Jihad activist on a hunger strike since here abduction by Israeli soldiers 38 days ago.

According to eyewitnesses, more than 5000 Palestinians marched through Bethlehem streets Saturday afternoon, demanding the release of Palestinian prisoners from Israeli detention.

However, when the protesters drew closer to the Bilal Mosque (Rachel Tomb), Israeli soldiers guarding the site opened fire and rubber bullets, and gas canisters, indiscriminately, injuring scores of Palestinians, young and old.

A foreign journalist at the site used the term "mayhem" to describe the situation.

"I am sure they wouldn't use this sort of violence with their own people...They (Israeli soldiers) are treating the Palestinians as inferior people.." the journalist said.

Muslims as well Christians took part in the demonstration, and followers of Fatah, Hamas, the Islamic Jihad and the leftist groups also took part.

Scores of people suffocated as a result of gas inhalation, and ambulances were transferring people to hospitals until after 4:00 GMT.

A Palestinian legislative council member, Salah al Ta'amari, was also lightly injured.

Further protests in solidarity with Elayan also took place in Hebron, Ramallah, Jenin, and Jerusalem.

In Jenin, a letter written by Elayan and leaked from Ramle hospital, where she is being detained, called on Palestinians "not to let us down."

More protests are expected for this week.

Palestinian protests continue in solidarity with Elayan

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (26 November)

Protests, sit-ins and demonstrations continued throughout the West Bank and the Gaza Strip for the fourth consecutive week in solidarity with political prisoner Itaf Elayan, the Islamist activist abducted last month by Israeli soldiers while traveling from Bethlehem to Beir Zit.

On Wednesday, Palestinians representing various political orientations held silent protests outside Red Cross offices in several Palestinian towns.

In Hebron, protesters urged human rights organization to double their efforts on Elayan's behalf, because even the life of one human-being "matters."

Islamic Jihad activists renewed earlier warning that "we would carry out a massive retaliation if something happened to sister Elayan."

The Israeli intelligence seems to take these threats seriously.

The Israeli English daily, the Jerusalem Post, quoted intelligence officials as saying they suspected that the Islamic Jihad organization was responsible for last week's fatal shooting of a Jewish settler in East Jerusalem.

On Monday, Hamas leader Sheikh Ahmed Yasin, himself a former freedom prisoner, joined Palestinian protests in Gaza in solidarity with Elayan.

Yasin said the Palestinians wouldn't rest until "all prisoners are released."

He mentioned Itaf Elayan by name, saying that "we are with you sister Itaf and we want you to know that you are not alone and that we will not let you down."

Yasin also lambasted the Palestinian Authority for "keeping Palestinian political prisoners in Palestinian jails," saying "how could we demand the release of our brothers and sisters from Israeli jails while the Palestinian National Authority continues to incarcerate political prisoners."

Further protests in solidarity with Elayan are scheduled for Saturday and Monday, in Bethlehem, Hebron and Beir Zit.

On Tuesday, Elayan sent what was described as "an open letter" to Israeli and world public opinion on the circumstances of her unlawful arrest and detention.

The letter, excerpts of which were published by Wednesday's Arabic press, said Elayan would continue her hunger strike, now entering its 34 days, until either freedom or martyrdom.

Entitled "to all those willing to listen," the letter pointed out that Elayan's detention was utterly illegal and that the Israeli goal behind it was "to break my will.. and that will never happen."

Elayan disclosed that Israeli interrogators had informed her "not to bank much on justice because all Israeli judged are inside our rear pockets."

She added that "I shall continue in my just hunger strike until I earn my freedom, and even then, I shall continue to struggle against this oppressive law that enables occupation soldiers to detain haphazardly any Palestinian without charge or trial and for as long as seen fit by the occupation authorities."

Elayan added, rather ominously, that "I am a committed Muslim and my religious convictions are incompatible with suicide, but this (hunger strike) is my only means in the face of those oppressors."

evasive tactic"

Occupied Jerusalem- PT (24 November)

Palestinian Authority officials have rejected a reported proposal by Israeli premier Benyamin Netanyahu to redeploy Israeli troops from 6%-8% of area (C) in return for Palestinian consent to enter final status talks.

Ahmed Abdul Rahman, Secretary-General of the PA ministerial council, described the proposal as "another evasive tactic aimed at absolving Israel from carrying out commitments under the Oslo Accords."

Speaking in Gaza, after a meeting of the PA cabinet and the PLO executive committee, Abdul Rahman said the Palestinians rejected the proposal outright, insisting on full implementation of the interim agreement.

The Israeli proposal, reported by the Israeli media Sunday night, stipulates also that the key and third phase of Israeli army redeployment from the West Bank (except from settlements and military positions) be canceled.

"They want to lump interim and final status issues so that at the end we would end up with a dismal deal," said Abdul Rahman.

Netanyahu has sought to stall the second redeployment altogether or reduce its scope to negligible proportions.

Earlier this year, Netanyahu suggested that the second phase of redeployment covers 2%-3% of area (b) where Israel has "security responsibility" and the PA "civilian responsibility."

Faced with an ostensibly determined Palestinian rejection of a token redeployment, and reportedly with growing American displeasure, Netanyahu has been forced to come up with his new proposal.

The proposal has a "Zero probability" of success, since its acceptance by the Palestinians would amount to the "undoing of Oslo" from the Palestinian perspective.

The PA logic has so far been that when Israel and the PA embarked on the final status talks, the Palestinians would have significant chunks of the West Bank, perhaps up to 80% of the total area of the West Bank, under their control, thus strengthening their negotiating position.

Moreover, Netanyahu faces a stiff opposition from some of his key coalition partners.

On Monday, Michael Kleiner, who heads the so-called "land of Israel front" said his front would seek to topple the Netanyahu government if he went ahead with his proposal.

"In the past months we have met with Netanyahu on several occasions and warned him that if he crosses the red line we will topple the government. We have nothing further to say to him." said Kleiner in an interview with the Israeli press Monday.

Furthermore, National Religious Party leaders made similar threats against Netanyahu.

Yitzhak Levy, of the NRP, said "his party would leave the government if the IDF left the parts of the land of Israel they are talking about."

Additional threats also came of Rahba'am Zeivi, head of the ultra-right wing party, Moledet, which calls for the expulsion of Arabs from Palestine to the Arabian Peninsula.

"We will bring down the government if Netanyahu makes good on his remarks on redeployment," Zeivi said in an interview with the Israeli radio Monday morning .

In other development, Israeli army bulldozers demolished two Palestinian homes in the Bethlehem area.

According to Palestinian sources, the two homes (sheltering two families) were destroyed allegedly because they were built without license.

Palestinians point out that Israeli refuse deliberately to grant Palestinians building licenses in order to impede Arab demographic growth, thus forcing Palestinians to build homes without licenses.

The destruction by Israel of Palestinian homes has by far been the second most callous and evil practice against Palestinians.

If a Palestinian wants to express extreme condemnation, he Says "Yikhrib Beitak," may your home be destroyed.

Hamas-PA relations troubled again

Occupied Jerusalem- PT ( 18 November)

It seems that Hamas relations with the Palestinian Authority are destined to face another troublesome episode following what was described as "the conspiratorial hand-over" on 13 November of two of two Hamas' military wing's members to the Israeli authorities. The two, Jamal al Hoor and Abdul Rahman Ghuneimat (both members of the reputed Surif cell which Israel alleges was responsible for the killing of 10 Israeli soldiers and civilians) were "abducted" in vague circumstances by a special IDF unit on the outskirts of Nablus while being transferred from Hebron to Nablus aboard a Preventive Security car.

According to informed Palestinian sources, an Israeli agent planted inside the PS, informed the Israelis of the planned transfer of the Islamist guerrillas, enabling an IDF undercover unit to snatch al Hoor and Ghuneimat just outside area (A) within which the PA has (or is supposed to have) exclusive control. The head of the Israeli army central command alluded to tacit cooperation with the PS in "capturing the two terrorists." In a prompt news conference, Maj. Gen. Uzi Dayan said an undercover unit known as Duvedevan captured the two wanted Palestinians after receiving "information from a very special source." Dayan refused to elaborate further, but observers here surmise that he may have meant a top Palestinian security official.

When the news of the incident broke Friday morning, 14 November, Hamas immediately accused the PS of "delivering our two fighters to the hands of our enemy," adding rather ominously that "you will pay dearly for your treachery."

Hamas' senior spokesman in Gaza, Abdul Aziz al Rantisi, held the PA "fully responsible for this disgraceful act of perfidy."

"Whether it was an act of connivance, a manifest hand-over, a tacit deal, or a criminal negligence resulting from Israeli informers penetrating the PS, the PA is responsible and must pay for that," he said in a telephone interview on 15 November.

However, Rantisi refused to say explicitly that the PA did deliver the two Islamist guerrillas into the hands of the Shabak.

The relatively mild reactions from Gaza apparently were not sufficient to calm-down the fury of West Bank, particularly Hebron, Islamist leaders. And for the first time, Hamas founder Sheikh Ahmed Yasin, was criticized, though not harshly, for being "too conciliatory toward the PA."

"We will not agree to sacrifice our own brothers for the sake of an illusive national unity," said a furious relative of Ibrahim Ghuneimat, during an angry demonstration in Hebron on 16 November against the alleged handover.

The demonstrators, who came mainly from the village of Surif to which the two guerrillas belong, lashed out at the PA as never before, shouting slogans like "Arafat is (Israeli) collaborator," "Jebril (PS chief Jebril Rajoub) is jasus (spy), and "sulta (PA) is an Israeli puppet."

"We want to know what exactly happened, who were involved in this treacherous act..was it a practical translation of security cooperation between Rajoub and the Shabak," said Ahmed al Hoor, a relative of Jamal al Hoor, before a crowd of more than a thousand demonstrators, along with hundreds of PA security personnel who were looking on.

He added that "we warned him (Rajoub) against transferring our brothers to Nablus because the Israelis were after them, but he refused arrogantly to listen to us."

Earlier, hundreds of Surif villagers sought to force their way into the PA headquarters in Hebron, demanding the "release of Ibrahim Ghuneimat (a third cell member still in PA detention in Hebron) and the immediate resignation of Jebril Rajoub."

On 18 November, Hamas military wing, the Izzidin al Kassam Squads, distributed a leaflet in parts of the West Bank, vowing to "respond sooner rather than later to Rajoub's disgraceful act."

"our response will be human bombs against the Zionist enemy," said the leaflet.

The leaflet said "the delivery of our brave fighters to the enemy's hands was in implementation of security coordination and cooperation with Israel...and a clear proof that the heads of PA security agencies are merely representatives of the Israeli intelligence within the autonomous enclaves."

Moreover, the leaflet reiterated earlier charges that "Rajoub went ahead with the 'planned hand-over' despite unmistakable warnings from the two activists themselves, their families, and Islamist leaders in the West Bank."

Clearly embarrassed by the affair, PA officials sought to turn the heat on the Israeli government "which always doesn't respect agreements."

"This act of piracy and thievery is indicative of the entire behavior of the Israeli government," said chief Palestinian negotiator Saeb Ereikat.

For his part, PS chief Jebril Rajoub remained reticent on the matter. On 16 November, following repeated requests for his reaction to charges of a tacit handover of the two Hamas members, Rajoub instructed his secretary to issue a laconic statement describing "the charges and rumors of a hand-over as silly and laughable."

The statement likened "the act of piracy" to the Mossad's bungled assassination attempt on the life of Hamas chief Khalid Masha'al in Amman last month.

However, the statement recognized that "there may have been a crack within our rank and file, enabling the Israelis to locate the car and abduct the two detainees."

Rajoub has been lately exposed to a mounting criticisms from fellow Fatah members who accused him of displaying "scornful behavior toward PA officials."

Last week, his membership in Fatah's higher council, was frozen for 45 days following the disclosure of a letter he had sent to fellow member, Sakhre Habash, in which he reportedly called him " a male prostitute." The letter, say Fatah insiders, was only the latest in a series of scandalous incidents in which he displayed "bullying behaviors, brutality and utter lack of civility" towards junior and even some senior PA officials.

Meanwhile, Palestinians continued to protest the continued detention by Israel of Islamic Jihad activist Itaf Alliyan who was arrested by Israeli soldiers at a roadblock while traveling from Bethlehem to Abu Dis near Jerusalem.

Alliyan has been on a hunger strike ever since her detention 25 days ago, and Palestinian sources said her health "is in real danger."

On 11 November, Israeli soldiers protecting a Jewish religious celebration outside the Bilal Ibn Rabah Mosque (known by Jews as Rachel's Tomb) shot and seriously wounded a 7-year-old child who was returning home from school.

According to witnesses, Ali al Jawarish, a first-grader, waved his hand to an Israeli soldier whom he thought was photographing him. The soldier, apparently a sniper, shot him in the head, causing irreparable damage to his brain. Jawarish, who had been in a deep coma, died on 15 November.

An Israeli army spokesman said Israeli soldiers were trying to quell Palestinian rioters when the child crossed into the line of fire. The IDF statement was another shameless attempt to escape blame for killing an innocent child on the ground that "it was the child who hit the bullet not the other way around."

The Jwarish family decided to donate their dead son's organs for transplantation. His father, Muhammed Jawarish, said he didn't care whether the organs would go to an Arab or a Jew.

Moreover, the PA Chairman reiterated earlier charges that Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy had gone to Washington without clear authorization from Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu to discuss "executive matters."

According to Palestinian sources, the PA will make a "comprehensive review" of all aspects pertaining to the talks with Israel, especially in light of the "fiasco" in Washington and recent Israeli decisions to build thousands of new housing units in Jewish colonies throughout the West Bank.

Chief Palestinian negotiator, Sa'eb Ureikat, said a comprehensive review of the talks with Israel had become "a pressing necessity so that we won't continue to rotate in an empty circle."

Arafat's and Ureikat's statements, observers say, underscore the depth of Palestinian frustration vis-a-vis Israeli equivocation and American reluctance to deal decisively with it.

On Friday, Netanyahu was quoted as saying "Israel would not make free concessions to the Palestinians," adding that the Jewish state would like to know beforehand what concessions the Palestinians would be willing to offer in return for the so-called "time-out" from settlement construction in the West Bank.

In other development, Israeli soldiers, acting as pirates, kidnapped a Palestinian college student while traveling between Bethlehem and Hebron in the West Bank.

The student, Hani Abu Sba'a, was reportedly arrested when the car he was riding was stopped at an Israeli army roadblock on the rugged Wadi al Nar road that circumvents Jerusalem which is off-limit to Palestinians.

Two weeks ago, Israeli soldiers stationing in the same area reasserted Itaf Ilayan, a 35-years old Palestinian woman prisoner who had been freed Last February in the context of an agreement between Israel and the Palestinian Authority.

Ilayan was reportedly sent to a detention camp, without charge or trial, where she started an open-ended hunger strike to protest her flagrant detention.

Family sources said Ilayan's health had deteriorated and was transferred to the military hospital in Ramle.

The Israeli occupation army controls all roads linking West Bank towns and villages and often erects abrupt roadblocks and checkpoints on these roads to "catch" Palestinian activists considered "outspoken opponents of the occupation."

Palestinians declare Washington talks a failure

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (6 November)

After three days of intensive talks between Israeli and Palestinian negotiators in Washington under American aegis, the Palestinian Authority has declared the talks "a complete failure."

Ahmed Abdul Rahman, Secretary-General of the PA administrative council, said Thursday the talks "produced absolutely nothing."

"There was no progress on any of the outstanding issues. This is what we had expected and this is what happened." Abudul Rahman said in an interview with the "Voice of Palestine," the official media organ of the PA.

Abdul Rahman accused the American administration of "not putting up real efforts to force Israel to abide by the terms of the agreements."

"The Americans would like us to talk and talk and talk, but would not take any action that would force Israel to stop its settlement exapasnion in the West Bank and to carry out the long-overdue redeployment of the Israeli army from our land," he said.

Furthermore, Abdul Rahman accused the Clinton administration of "malicious intent."

"They could, if they wanted, put an end to Israeli intransigence and insolence, but they just continue to display what amounts to be connivance with Israel."

In other development, the Israeli occupation authorities have confiscated hundreds of donums belonging to Arab peasants and farmers at the hamlet of al Burj, 32 kilometers west off Hebron.

Palestinian sources pointed out that Israeli soldiers and settlers rooted out hundreds of olive trees belonging to two Palestinian farmers, Ahmed Ismael Itbish and Ayed Shadid, at Um al Shugfan, near the village of al Burj, which is located at the 1948 armistice line.

Earlier, Israeli sources had reported that Jewish settlers were planning to build a settlement in the area for the purpose of "obliterating the armistice line and making it impossible for future Israeli governments to cede land to the Palestinians."

Exercise of futility

Occupied Jerusalem-PT( 4 November)

Even before Palestinian negotiators set out for Washington on 3 November for yet a new round of talks with Israeli officials under the direct aegis of US Secretary of state Madeleine Albright, PA officials, including Yasser Arafat, had already predicted that the talks would be a failure. Arafat, departing from his usual "optimistic" tone, told the Israeli Hebrew daily Ma'ariv on 2 November he saw very little chance that the talks would breed genuine results "because Netanyahu is negotiating for the sake of negotiation and that he is sending (Foreign Minister) David Levy without authorization to make decisions." However, when asked why he had decided to send his delegation to the talks despite the likelihood that they would fail, he said "we received an invitation from (US Secretary of State Madeleine) Albright, and we accepted the invitation."

Other Palestinian officials were even more pessimistic in their forecasts. Yasser Abed Rabbu, who holds the Culture and Information portfolio in the PA administration, anticipated that the talks would be "just a waste of time."

"These talks will produce nothing because the present Israeli government is not interested in implementing the agreements we had signed with the previous Israeli government, they (the Netanyahu government) playing stalling tactics in order to create more facts on the ground and to frustrate us into making still more concessions to them," Abed Rabbu said.

Indeed, given Palestinian pessimism, the main reason prompting the Palestinians to send its team to the Washington talks was to please the Americans and ward off possible charges that the Palestinians, not the Israelis, were creating obstacles. So, by seeking to win "the public relations strife" with Netanyahu, the PA hopes to be able to swing the ball back to the Israeli court, having been strongly thrown into the Palestinian arena following the two suicidal bomb attacks in Jerusalem in late July and early September.

However, despite everything, the American administration was still not satisfied with what was described as "the inadequate size of the Palestinian delegation." So, eventually Secretary of State Albright exerted pressure on the PA to send more negotiators to the talks in order to broaden the agenda of the talks. This was viewed as a significant Palestinian concession since the Israeli government had always suggested that substantive issues ( e.g. settlement expansion and secondary and tertiary redeployment) and procedural issues such as the sea and air ports in Gaza be lumped together.

Clearly, the underlying reason behind this Israeli stance is to try to bamboozle the Palestinians into accepting a deal whereby they would resign to delaying or even canceling Israeli army redeployment from areas (B+C) and only a symbolic "time-out" from settlement expansion in return for a modicum of progress on the symptomatic procedural issues.

In fact, this stance was spelled out by Prime Minister Netanyahu several times before Levy's departure for Washington. Netanyahu said he " would hand out any more land to the Palestinians without knowing in advance how the features of the final status settlement would look like." Obviously, the Israeli premier believes that if the Palestinians were enabled to make substantial gains, e.g. receiving a significant chunk of the West Bank, their negotiating position would be made stronger in the final status talks. Hence his reported instructions to Levy no to delve deeply into the redeployment and settlement expansion issues.

For their part, the Palestinians, at least ostensibly, refuse the deliberate Israeli accentuation of insubstantative issues, rightly considering that as "a sly ruse to evade carrying out Israeli army redeployment form parts of the West Bank designated in the agreements we had signed." More to the point, the Israeli point out that the display by Israeli of sudden enthusiasm for reaching a concordance on the interim issues (the sea and air ports in Gaza) is misleading since over an entire year of intermittent negotiations on these issues has lapsed without making any tangible progress thanks to Israeli intransigence.

With the Palestinians budging, though begrudgingly, to American demands to dispatch the heads of Joint Israeli-Palestinian committees to the Washington talks, the PA hopes the Clinton administration will eventually move to check Netanyahu's insolence in the West Bank.

The PA seems to be fairly encouraged that recent remarks attributed to Secretary Albright, in which she reportedly lambasted Netanyahu for repeatedly deceiving the Americans, would signal the beginning of a new tougher American approach toward Netanyahu.

Another source of PA encouragement is the reported American satisfaction with PA efforts to "combat terrorism." Last week, Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub visited Washington and reportedly briefed Congressmen and CIA officials on measures taken so far against Hamas and the Islamic Jihad.

Never the less, even if the PA succeeded in returning the ball back to the Israeli court, this would be no guarantee that the US would exert sufficient pressure on the Netanyahu government to force it to, e.g. abide by the letter and spirit of the Oslo Accords.

alternatives

Aware of the dim prospects of the Washington talks, the PA is exploring other alternatives. These include seeking to further mobilize the European Union to get more involved, going back to the UN General Assembly, unilaterally declaring the establishment of the State of Palestine, and, not unlikely, seeking to reignite the intifada in cooperation with Hamas.

The formal declaration of a state, say PA insiders, has lately become on the top of the Palestinian list of "emergency alternatives." A close aide to PA chairman Yasser Arafat intimated last week that "if Netanyahu continued pursuing his malicious policies and the US continued its passive connivance with him, Abu Ammar (Arafat) would formally declare the establishment of the State of Palestine."

Palestinian Official: Washington talks waste of time

 

Occupied Jerusalem-PT (3/11/1997)

A prominent Palestinian Authority official has described the current Washington talks between Palestinian and Israeli negotiators as "a waste of time."

"These talks will produce nothing because the present Israeli government is not interested in implementing the agreements we had signed with the previous government," said Yaser Abed Rabbu who assumes the culture and information portfolio in the Palestinian autonomy administration.

Abed Rabbu revealed that the PA agreed to dispatch a delegation to Washington "only to please the Americans and to ward off charges that the Palestinians are the party that is creating obstacles," he said

PA Chairman Yaser Arafat likewise expressed profound pessimism at the prospects of the Washington talks' outcome.

Arafat told the Israeli Hebrew daily ma'ariv Sunday that he saw little chance that the talks would breed genuine results.

Arafat accused Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu of sending his foreign minister to Washington without authority to make decisions.

However, when asked why he decided to send a delegation to Washington despite the negative atmosphere, the PLO chairman said "we received an invitation from (US Secretary of State Madeleine) Albright, and we accepted the invitation."

Observers here contend that the PA decision to go to Washington despite Netanyahu's perceived ill-will was meant to the ball into the Israeli court.

Indeed, the PA believes there are good chances that they will succeed this time in what has largely become a sort of public relations struggle.

PA officials point out to recent statements by Albright before a conference of American Jewish leaders in which she reportedly lashed out at Netanyahu for trying to trick the American administration.

Added to that is the reported American satisfaction at PA efforts to "combat terrorism," a clear reference to the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and Islamic Jihad organizations.

Last week, Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub visited Washington and reportedly briefed Congressmen and CIA officials on measures taken so far against Hamas and the Islamic Jihad.

Never the less, even if the PA succeeds in returning the ball to the Israeli court, this is no guarantee that the US administration will exert real pressure on Netanyahu or, indeed, force the extremist Israeli premier to reconsider his expansionist policies in the West Bank.

In light, the main and perhaps the only alternative remaining available for the Palestinians is to initiate a genuine Arab-Muslim and international campaign that would persuade the Clinton administration that American interests would be seriously endangered if current American acquiescence and connivance with Israel continued.
