Yasin's Triumphant Return to Gaza

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem - Hamas founder and spiritual mentor, Sheikh Ahmed Yasin, received a heroís welcome upon his arrival in Gaza City on 6 October with thousands of supporters and well-wishers greeting the 61-year-old wheelchair-bound Islamist leader with ìahalan wasahalanî (welcome) and ìGod is great.î

Yasin was first accorded an official welcome at Palestinian Authority headquarters on the Gaza shore. Greeting him there were Yasser Arafatís personal secretary al Tayyeb Abdul Rahim, Suha Arafat (standing in for her husband who had earlier visited the Sheikh in hospital in Amman), and top PA political and security officials along with high-ranking Hamas leaders from the Strip. Islamist leaders from the West Bank were not present due to the Israeli closure on Palestinian territories.

After the brief official reception, Yasin was taken to the al Yarmuk stadium where over 30,000 people were awaiting his arrival since the morning. There, decorated flags and banners greeted Yasin as ìour great leader.î One huge green banner read ìIn the name of Hamas, we welcome the Sheikh of the intifadah.î

Yasin addressed the jubilant crowd, appealing for national unity and urging Palestinians to be strong and united.

ìThere is no difference between this and that or big and small. We are all one people united in the face of occupation and oppression,î he said in an unflinching tone.

Yasin told the cheering multitude that ìthe future belongs to us because our cause is just and our people are alive and well and will continue to be alive and well, God willing.î

Yasin sounded as defiant as ever when he said that ìwe must continue to defy our oppressor and foil their efforts to disintegrate us into shreds.î

Earlier, Yasin, who is physically paralyzed but mentally alert, said Hamas was not contemplating a cease-fire or truce with Israel as long as ìthe Israeli soldiersí boots are pressed against our necks and chests.î

ìResistance,î he said, ìis a natural human right; Iíll fight even my own brother if he takes over my home and arrogates my rights.î

Despite his conciliatory tone toward the Palestinian Authority and its chairman Yasser Arafat, Yasin nevertheless strongly criticized the closure by the PA in Gaza last month of sixteen social, educational, and charitable institutions associated with the Islamist movement.

He said ìI call on the authority not to succumb to American and Israeli pressure, and to desist from incarcerating our sons because this is not in our interests; it is inadmissible to close civil institutions just because the Americans and the Israelis demand that to be done.î

Yasin was flown directly from Amman aboard a Jordanian Air Force helicopter following the conclusion of a hastily-made deal between Jordanís King Hussein and Israel whereby Israel freed Yasin and scores of other Palestinian prisoners in return for releasing from Jordanian custody the two Mossad agents who on 25 September attempted to assassinate in Amman the head of the Hamas political bureau Khaled Mishaíal.

The release of Sheikh Ahmed Yasin and 22 other Palestinians from Israeli detention was undoubtedly a significant morale-booster for most Palestinians who had of late been prone to mounting frustration, given Benyamin Netanyahuís determined efforts to impose the fait accompli in the West Bank and the American and international powerlessness to stop Israeli insolence. The freedom of Yasin and scores of other Palestinian detainees already released and slated to be released soon was after all earned at almost no cost for the Palestinians. ìIt was a gift of God,î said Hamas leader Mahmud al Zahar.

Moreover, many ordinary Palestinians feel Yasinís words breathe inspiration and encouragement into the hearts and minds of thousands of Palestinians who otherwise have become accustomed to seeing Yasser Arafat repeatedly give in to Israeli dictates and blackmail.

ìHere we have a leader who sticks to his principles regardless of the balance of power...only a real man like Yasin can make a real peace with Israel,î said a Hebron Islamist doctor upon seeing Yasinís helicopter land in Gaza.

Notwithstanding, the Palestiniansí joyful reaction to Yasinís freedom and homecoming was fraught with some consternation at King Hussein for concluding a deal with Israel that enabled the Jewish state to smoothly retrieve ìour big hunt,î the two Mossad agents who had made the assassination attempt on Khaled Mishaíal.

Islamist sources argued that had the King insisted, he could have secured the release of hundreds of Palestinian detainees from Israeli jails in return for the ìtwo Canadians.î

They pointed out that many of the 22 prisoners released as a result of the deal were actually Palestinian labourers who were jailed for entering Israel without valid entry permits.

Nonetheless, it doesnít seem that the dismay is deep enough to overshadow the widespread satisfaction at Yasinís release and Khaled Mishaíalís safety.

ìWe knew from the very inception that the presence of two Mossad agents in King Husseinís custody is not exactly like them being in the hands of Hamas...we didnít expect too much from Jordan anyway,î said a Hamas leader in Ramallah as news of the swap spread.

There is no doubt that the release of Sheikh Yasin will strengthen Hamas, even in real terms. Observers here believe that the Sheikhís steadfastness (he refused all along to pledge to renounce ëterrorismí in return for his release), his dignified and high-profile release from Israeli detention, and the failed assassination attempt on Khaled Mishaíal by Mossad in Amman will all make the Movement more appealing to thousands of erstwhile disenchanted Palestinian and Arab youths who are dead tired of official Arab impotence vis-¦-vis Israel.

Moreover, Hamas, thanks to the latest developments, has been able to gain renewed and badly-needed legitimacy in the region at large.

ìFrom now on, the Hamas factor canít be ignored,î said Hamas Gaza leader Dr. Abdul Aziz Rantisi, now considered the most senior Islamist spokesman in the occupied territories after Yasin himself. ìSheikh Ahmed Yasin is now viewed as second among equals vis-¦-vis Yasser Arafat,î he asserted, rather satisfactorily.

But being ìsecond among equalsî carries with it not only promises of constructive cooperation but also undesirable prospects of friction and discord, especially in an immature political environment where the leader, e.g. Arafat, is not really held to serious political accountability.

A free Yasin can therefore be either a helping hand for Arafat or a real thorn in his side, depending on how the PA Chairman deals with Benyamin Netanyahuís policy of ìtake it or leave itî towards the Palestinians.

Khaled Misha'al to Palestine Times:

Hamas has enough reasons to continue occupation resistance

By Mahmoud Al Khatib

The head of the Political Bureau of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) Mr. Khaled Misha'al accused the Israeli Mossad of the "cowardly" assassination attempt on his life in Amman on 25 September. He added that the attempt was a complete failure.

Describing the bungled attempt against him, Mr. Misha'al said he was still not sure of the instrument used by the Mossad agents which later caused serious health troubles for him.

"What happened on Thursday, 25 September was no doubt a mean assassination attempt on my life perpetrated by the Mossad agents, but thank God, I survived," Mr. Misha'al told the Palestine Times.

"I want to emphasize that the barbaric conspiracies made by the Zionist enemy will not weaken our will. As you see, I have recovered completely, thank God, and my will and determination to continue on my path have doubled after the bungled attempt," the Hamas leader said.

Mr. Misha'al pointed out that he was attacked suddenly by two Mossad agents at the entrance of the complex that contains his office in Amman. "I heard a stunning sound in my left ear and felt as if I had been electrically shocked. In the beginning, I thought I was shot but then I was not sure of what happened exactly until I began vomiting and felt dizzy and lost my balance," he said. Mr. Misha'al was rushed to the Islamic Hospital in Amman after which King Hussein ordered that he be moved to the military King Hussein Medical City where he received treatment.

A report issued by the Sunday Times on 5 October revealed that the Mossad agents carrying fake Canadian passports sprayed a poisonous chemical into Mr. Misha'al's ear. The report described the bungled attack as "one of the most embarrassing incidents in the history of a service (Mossad) renowned for its ruthless efficiency." The incident "infuriated King Hussein of Jordan (the Arab country closest to Israel), led to the withdrawal of Canada's ambassador from Tel Aviv, and prompted President Bill Clinton to urge the release of Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, the imprisoned leader of Hamas," the report said.

The Zionist Prime Minister Netanyahu admitted that there had been an "operational failure" but said the attack was essential. Mr. Misha'al paid tribute to his bodyguard "Abu Saif", who was described by an Israeli newspaper as a "real James Bond", for running after and detaining the two Mossad agents until they were subsequently arrested.

"This hero (Abu Saif) was fasting that day. Despite that, he chased the two agents and knocked them down before he was able to control them and hand them to the Jordan police. In the process, one of the two criminals attacked Abu Saif with a sharp instrument on his head which then needed 18 stitches," he said.

The Hamas leader assailed the Zionist attempt on his life saying it was an example of the Zionist terrorism which "does not target Hamas only, but also the whole of the Palestinian people."

But Mr. Misha'al dismissed a call for revenge by the Hamas military wing in response to the assassination attempt by the Israeli Mossad. "Hamas resistance is not connected to certain incidents or occasions. Hamas originally had enough justifications and reasons to continue its legitimate resistance, the most important of which is the removal of the Zionist occupation and the restoration of our people's rights," said Mr. Misha'al, adding that Hamas fights the Zionists face to face, not from the back as the Mossad agents did with him.

"They wanted to silence Hamas, but its voice had already reached the whole world. They wanted to break my will, but now I have a doubled will," Mr. Misha'al said.

KNOW YOUR ENEMY: Netanyahu

By John Hartung*

On 6 October a JERUSALEM POST editorial charged that Netanyahu is "characterized by impulsiveness, poor judgement and faulty decision-making." The next day,Yoel Marcus of HA'ARETZ was less polite: "It is difficult to avoid the distressing conclusion that the state [of Israel] is headed by a schlemiel [a fool]. The timing, the location, the performance, the goal, the method, the humiliation -- even the bungling Inspector Clouseau of the 'Pink Panther' could not have produced a more inept accumulation of negative ramifications than was generated by Bibi's power of reasoning here." In another HA'ARETZ Op-Ed column, Amir Oren imputed Netanyhu's underlying motive for attempting to murder Khaled Misha'al: "The light-minded Netanyahu envied Yitzhak Rabin, on whose orders Fat'hi Shikaki was assassinated in Malta, and Shimon Peres, who ordered the assassination of Yahya Ayyash in Gaza;" in other words, in addition to being a schlemiel, Netanyahu is a schmuck [a stupid fool].

Outside of Israel, the established press quickly followed the Israeli lead. The plan to murder Misha'al was condemned as over-zealous and shortsighted. On 9 October, Thomas Friedman of THE NEW YORK TIMES informed us that "Israeli leadership has more in common with Larry, Moe and Curly [The Three Stooges] than with David Ben-Gurion, Menachem Begin and Yitzhak Rabin," adding that "Israel today is led by people who have no clear vision and no courage." The following Sunday, Serge Schmemann was given page one of the TIMES' influential "Week In Review" to defend the use of assassination as a political weapon, noting that the Misha'al operation was simply a misuse of a useful tool "mounted in the wrong place, at the wrong time and against the wrong target."

The most intelligent comment that received worldwide attention came from King Hussein: "I am personally unable to reach a conclusion as to how the Israeli Prime Minister thinks. This makes me very worried." He should be worried. Hussein is smart enough to know that Netanyahu is far from stupid. The Massachusetts Institute of Technology does not give degrees in architecture and business administration to schlemiels, and schmucks do not qualify for their Ph.D in Political Science. Indeed, if Netanyahu is stupid, Machiavelli was an idiot. Only a fool, a propagandist, or someone who wants to fool himself, would characterize Netanyahu as stupid. So what was he trying to do in Jordan? We may never know, but we should have enough self-respect to reject the notion that Misha'al was targeted by Larry, Moe and Curly. Instead, to understand Netanyahu's objectives, we should start with the assumption that he is smart, bold, and cunning.

We should also reject the notion that Netanyahu has "no clear vision." He has a very clear vision, as described by Uzi Benziman: "to annex at least 50% of the West Bank (the Jordan Valley, the old Green Line, Gush Etzion and greater Jerusalem); to allow the Palestinian Authority no more than the status of a quasi-state (like Puerto Rico or Andora) without arms or an army; to continue controlling its borders and airspace; to be able to limit its immigration and its access to Israeli water resources; to leave most of the Israeli settlements under Israeli sovereignty; and to continue Israeli control over both parts of Jerusalem, while possibly granting some status to an Arab entity over the Temple Mount mosques" (parentheses not added, HA'ARETZ, 21 September). This is an audacious objective. It is likely to require audacious actions.

The immediate obstacle to creating Netanyahu's apartheid state is the Palestinian Authority. The more difficult obstacle is public image. Netanyahu wants to disarm and disband the PA, but how could he accomplish such a feat? Consider the context of the Misha'al operation. For months Netanyahu repeated a mantra -- the Palestinian Authority must act against 'terrorism'...the PA must act against Hamas. This became his answer to every question, to every complaint. The United States was eventually persuaded, and the PA began to arrest Hamas leaders and close Hamas institutions in the West Bank and Gaza. But Palestinian money was still being held, settlements were still being expanded, and Madeleine Albright was told, in effect, to go bake cookies. What more did Netanyahu want? He wanted more, more of the same, more PA suppression of Hamas.

Now suppose the plan to kill Misha'al had worked. Suppose he was attacked with poison by unknown assailants in Amman. He would suffer for two days before he dies. During those two days Netanyahu does two things. First, instead of refusing to either confirm or deny involvement -- the official policy in all previous Israeli assassinations -- he calmly denies that Israel was involved. He even points to the method used, announcing that not only would Israel not attack a citizen of Jordon in Jordon, but being a signatory to the international treaty against chemical weapons, Israel would not use poisonous gas on anyone. Second, he forwards all Palestinian tax money to the PA, grants permission to build an airport and seaport in Gaza, and announces the construction of a highway connecting Gaza and the West Bank. When asked why he made these concessions, Netanyahu would say, as Misha'al lay dying, that he has been greatly encouraged by the Palestinian Authority's efforts to combat terrorism. Then he could add some words about how it is important to reward actions taken in the right direction, even though there is much work left to be done.

Within an hour, the PA would have been denying the outrageous charge -- suggested by the press -- that they attacked Khaled Misha'al as part of their crackdown on Hamas. After Misha'al died, Netanyahu would deny that the money, the airport, the seaport and the road were a reward for killing Misha'al, adding that Israeli intelligence sources have no evidence of Palestinian involvement in the murder of this Hamas political leader. At that point a unique event would have occured -- the Israelis, the Americans, Hamas and King Hussein would have agreed on something. They would all agree that Netanyahu lied about one thing -- about his concessions not being a reward to the PA for assassinating Misha'al. Arafat would have been disgraced and much of the PA would have joined Hamas. The deserters would not leave their guns behind, and the ensuing crisis would be built up by the Israeli and American press.

Do you remember the Lavon Affair -- when Israel gave bombs to Egyptian Jews and had them blow up American installations in Egypt, sending notes implicating Egyptian dissidents so that the United States would turn against Egypt in 1954? Too audacious? Remember that Israel attacked an American ship in 1967, killing 34 American sailors and wounding 171, only to have the American President order the return of U.S. fighter planes that were on their way to rescue the U.S.S. Liberty. And what about the Pollard Affair? Pollard did not get away with spying on the United States, but Israel surely did. Does Netanyahu remember these events, and many others? Or is he too stupid, as the mainstream press would have us believe?

A public opinion poll published on 11 October in THE NEW YORK TIMES showed that Netanyahu had gained on Ehud Barak among Israeli voters, and "Many listeners had already noted that in radio call-in shows, the preponderance of callers rallied behind the Mossad and Mr. Netanyahu."

Could American opinion be far behind? On 14 October Abraham Rosenthal published an Op-Ed piece in the NEW YORK TIMES announcing that the attempt on Misha'al's life was a noble endeavour and that people who criticize it on moral grounds are hypocrites. So the plan to increase tension between the PA and Hamas failed, but Netanyahu has already gotten away with that failure.

Now, about the last question. Why was the assassination botched? Mossad murdered Ayyash with a cell phone triggered from a helicopter; why couldn't they gas this guy in Amman? Israel did not use agents who were officially stationed in Jordan. This operation was too secret, even for them. They began to put a special team in place on 19 August, complete with Canadian passports. By mid-September there were 8 of them. Perhaps they had enough time to get everything ready, but perhaps not. The Ayyash assassination took several months to execute. My guess is that Mossad was rushed into action when King Hussein sent a letter to Netanyahu saying that Hamas was "ready to stop its attacks on Israel in return for discussing all the issues that have to be discussed," and offering to mediate between Israel and Hamas (HA'ARETZ, 9 October). That letter supposedly got lost for 3 days, not reaching Netanyahu until after the assassination attempt. What was the problem? Insufficient postage? Incorrect address? Jewish holiday?

No. The threat of a truce with Hamas was too dangerous. A truce would ruin Israel's ability to pursue Zionism under the guise of seeking security. It could disrupt the Jewish People's covenant with their God -- a covenant which commands them to kill all non-Jews in Israel and take great and goodly cities, which they did not build, take houses full of all good things, which they did not fill, take cisterns hewn out, which they did not hew, and take vineyards and olive trees, which they did not plant (see Deuteronomy 6:10-11). Indeed, an offer to be reasonable is a powerful threat because it could reveal Zionism's underlying motivation -- and Netanyahu's.

On 10 October THE JERUSALEM POST reported that King Hussein initially thought that the attack on Misha'al was Israel's reply to his letter. Perhaps it was. And now that the Palestinian Authority has gained some credibility in the United States, a reasonable proposal for Final Status Talks would be a very grave threat to Zionism. Again, I urge Palestinians to make such a threat. Propose a new nation for the Middle East. Propose that Israel, without the Golan Heights, join the West Bank and Gaza. This new country should not be called Israel and it should not be called Palestine. Any other name would be acceptable, but I suggest New Canaan.

All current inhabitants of Israel, the West Bank and Gaza would be citizens of New Canaan, and no citizen would be discriminated against, or receive privileges, on the basis of religion. People residing outside of New Canaan who were born in Israel, the West Bank or Gaza could immigrate to New Canaan with their family and receive automatic citizenship. Otherwise, immigration policy should be determined by a democratically elected government in any manner that would not discriminate by religious affiliation.

Because the United States has been critically responsible for creating the circumstances which require such a major solution, it would be reasonable to propose that America guarantee the security of New Canaan's international borders for 15 years subsequent to its creation and grant expedited U.S. citizenship to any former Israeli citizen of New Canaan who wishes to immigrate to the United States.

It is important to know your enemy. Benyamin Netanyahu is Meir Kahane in secular clothing. He is not manipulated by Israeli right-wing Zionists -- as the Western press is fond of thinking; he is a right-wing Zionist. The best way to expose this liability is to offer a reasonable plan for peace and let the world watch his reply. A Final Settlement Plan that could be openly advocated would be a powerful force -- a force that Netanyahu could not match.

Immigration policy should be determined by a democratically elected government in any manner that would not discriminate by religious affiliation.

15 Years After the Sabra & Shatila Massacre:

The State of Palestinian Refugees in Lebanon

By Ghada Khouri*

Fifteen years ago, Munir Mohammed survived one of the worst massacres in the recent history of the Middle East. He was only 12 at the time, but the memories still haunt him.

Following the assassination of Lebanese President Bashir Gemayel and its invasion of Lebanon, Israel sent its proxiesóthe Phalanges and members of the South Lebanon Armyóinto the Sabra and Shatila refugee camps on 16 September 1982. Under the approving and watchful eyes of the Israelis stationed around the camps, the militiamen went on a rampage, indiscriminately slaughtering children and the elderly, raping young girls and women, and butchering unarmed men. About 36 hours later, approximately 2,000 civilians lay deadósome so badly dismembered that they were unrecognizable.

While Palestinian refugees gathered to commemorate the 15-year anniversary of the massacre in front of the mass grave in Shatila, Munir remembered the family he lost and his own ordeal as a survivor. He recalled how he faked death throughout the night, lying still amid a pile of bodies and listening to the sound of moans and cries from his mother Zehrah and baby sister Iman who lay beside him. They both died a few hours later. Wounded and drenched in blood, Munir made his way out of a heap of corpses the next morning and was taken to a local hospital. Eleven months later, he started a new life in the United States. Out of a family of nine brothers and sisters, Munir has one brother left, Nabil, who settled with him in America, and one sister, Najat, who still lives in Lebanon.

Memories of his childhood growing up in the refugee camp are now taken over by images of the horror he witnessed as a young boy. Yet, life goes on in Shatila for thousands of Palestinian refugees and destitute Lebanese who still live there. About 400,000 Palestinians reside in 12 refugee camps scattered throughout LebanonóEin El Helweh being the largest with 75,000 inhabitants. Although the massacre of 1982 is now no more than an event recorded in the annals of the Palestinians' painful history, another crime is being perpetrated against the refugees in Lebanonóthe crime of poverty, marginalization and negligence.

Overcrowding, poor sanitation, and unemployment are part of the daily life in the camps. While refugees in Syria and Jordan enjoy various social and civil rights, Lebanon presents a special case due to the country's delicate sectarian balance and the legacy of a 16-year civil war. Blamed by a large segment of the Lebanese population for the PLO's role in the war, the Palestinian refugees command little public sympathy.

Eid Khair Ferrawi, a 26-year-old Palestinian born in Bourj Al-Barajneh camp, described the Sabra and Shatila massacre as a "Zionist effort to rid the country of 'unwanted elements'," adding that the Palestinians' legal status in Lebanon today also marks them as 'unwanted.' While it is not pronounced publicly, Lebanese policy seems to support Ferrawi's opinion.

Prohibited from practicing 73 different professions, most Palestinian refugees work as unskilled labourers in construction and agricultureólow-paying jobs for which they compete with other foreign workers. UNRWA estimates that 60% of Palestinian refugees live below Lebanon's poverty line. They are barred from rebuilding the camps and are excluded from the Lebanese public health and education systems.

Since 1995, Palestinians are required to secure entry and exit visas to and from Lebanon. Ironically, obtaining an exit visa does not guarantee permission to return to the country. Because of this new law, several families find themselves separated. "There are cases in which a father leaves the country to work abroad and support his family still in Lebanon and is then unable to come back to his wife and children," said UNRWA spokesperson Hoda Samra.

As Lebanon continues to grapple with high unemployment, low wages, corruption and reconstruction in the wake of the devastating civil war, the Lebanese are more concerned with their own conditions than with the fate of the Palestinian refugees. During a recent visit to France, Lebanese Interior Minister Michel Murr raised a storm of controversy when he placed the presence of armed Palestinians on an equal footing with Israeli occupation in the south as Lebanon's top security issues. "Almost 20,000 Palestinian refugees are armed," he stated on 25 September 1997. "All those who commit crimes on Lebanese soil take refuge in the camps while our security forces are prohibited from entering the camps by international conventions." Only one known fugitive, Ahmad Abdel-Karim Saadi, accused of masterminding the 1995 assassination of Sheikh Nizar Halabi, is believed to be hiding out in Ein El Helweh.

While few would go as far as Murr in expressing displeasure with the Palestinian presence, most Lebanese leaders fear that granting Palestinian refugees social and civil rights would encourage their settlement in Lebanon, alleviate Israeli responsibility, and effectively render meaningless their right to return or receive compensation, as stipulated by United Nations resolution 194. They are also concerned that absorbing the refugees, who make up 10% of the Lebanese population, would upset Lebanon's sensitive sectarian balance and spark renewed civil unrest by adding a large Sunni population to the country's demographic make-up.

As a result of these political considerations, Palestinians are largely dependent on UNRWA as the main provider of education, social and health services. UNRWA runs 25 clinics and 73 schools in Lebanon. Its budget increased by 70% from $32 million in 1993 to $55 million in 1997, thus dashing speculations that the peace process would soon lead to the Agency's dissolution. In addition to providing basic services to the refugees, UNRWA also acts as an employer. About 99% of its 2,400 employees in Lebanon are Palestinians.

Despite its undeniable role in alleviating the hardships of Palestinian refugees, UNRWA has been unable to satisfactorily meet their mounting needsólargely due to a limited budget dependent on voluntary contributions from UN member states. With a $20 million overall deficit for 1997, the Agency launched a special emergency appeal to donor states last July and announced a series of austerity measures, including the imposition of tuition fees in all UNRWA schools. The proposed service cuts led to a 9-day hunger strike outside UNRWA headquarters in Beirut, which ended when the donor countries agreed to cover the Agency's deficit during an emergency meeting on 9 September 1997. Although this year's storm has passed, UNRWA's financial troubles are chronic.

"The perfect solution is to give the Palestinians everything they need, but we simply can't do it," Samra said. "UNRWA is already doing more than its mandate requires in Lebanon because of the special needs of Palestinians on the ground. For example, it is not within our mandate to provide secondary school education or hospitalization, but we are doing it only in Lebanon."

Indeed, UNRWA recently opened one secondary school and provides hospital care through contractual arrangements with 12 private hospitals. However, in 7% of the cases, including cancer treatment and brain or open heart surgery, UNRWA only covers a portion of the costsóusually less than halfóon a case-by-case basis, according to Samra. Few Palestinians are able to pay the remaining cost of these life-saving operations.

UNRWA's limits on spending have created resentment among those Palestinians who have felt the brunt of the Agency's belt-tightening approach to providing services. Samir Khalil, a father of four in Shatila camp, is still angry that his 12-year-old daughter, who suffered first-degree burns during the 'war of the camps,' was denied hospitalization. "I have been running around for years trying to get help for my daughter who is badly disfigured," he said. Reconstructive surgery is an expensive procedure which apparently falls under those hospitalization requests which are either rejected or only partially covered by UNRWA. "Our conditions here are miserable," Khalil added. "There are constant water and electricity shortages, and no way of making enough money to provide a decent life for our families. Our fate is determined by how much money UNRWA receives."

Pressures on UNRWA have been steadily increasing since the Gulf War when remittances from Palestinians in the Gulf stopped coming in and when some of those expelled from various Gulf countries returned to life in the camps. But in spite of the sometimes tense relations between UNRWA and the refugee population, Samra asserted that the Agency "will be here until a final solution is reached."

Such a solution seems as far off today as it was when the now-defunct peace process began. Indeed, the refugee problem remains one of the thorniest issues to be addressed in the final negotiations, which are nowhere in sight. "No one has the right to deny us what we deserve and that is to return to our homes in Palestine," said Abou Fadi Hammad, a Fatah dissident and camp leader residing in Mar Elias. "We do not intend to permanently settle in Lebanon, but if there is no return, are we to continue living in abysmal conditions? It's a crime."

For Munir, a new beginning in America has not erased memories of life and death in Shatila. He shares with those refugees who remain in the Palestinian camps of Lebanon the aspiration for a Palestinian state and a right to return to the land his parents fled in 1948. In the meantime, "there is only one thing that would help make the pain of the Sabra and Shatila massacre go away," said the 27-year-old Munir. "And that is knowing that the Palestinian children growing up in the camps today, as I did many years ago, can live a life of dignity and respect among their fellow Lebanese."

A cheap way to cut off aid to Israel

By Robert W. McGee*

A Washington, DC-based group called the Council for the National Interest scheduled a ìmarch on Washingtonî on 23-25 October. The theme was END AID TO ISRAEL. It is too early to tell how effective the march was, since the spigot that pours billions of dollars into Israel -- about 10% of its GDP -- continues to flow unabated. But the effort is a step in the right direction. Giving U.S. tax dollars to the oppressive Israeli government will not stop until American politicians feel they have to look over their shoulders to see if anyone is noticing their support for Israel and its anti-human rights agenda. They have to be made to feel guilty for their abandonment of their fiduciary duty to American voters to support only policies that are in America's best interests. If there is no organized opposition, the various Israeli lobbyists will continue to have complete ownership rights over the U.S. Congress. Thus, events that increase public awareness of the human rights abuses going on in occupied Palestine are important.

It is a well-known fact that American voters are among the least informed voters of any country where voting is permitted. Many of them do not have any informed opinions on major issues, and an alarming number of voters do not even know the names of the people who represent them in Congress. That being the case, marches on Washington become all the more important because of the high visibility that television coverage provides. But achieving high television visibility for an issue that most Americans don't care about is not as effective as a multi-dimensional campaign aimed at making a basic change in policy.

I have a suggestion -- a very cost-effective and non-violent suggestion --for solving this whole AID TO ISRAEL problem. The whole thing might not cost more than $100 million to $200 million, which is much less than the billions of dollars that Arab governments spend annually on national defense.

There is widespread dissatisfaction with the U.S. government among American voters. As for the arrests of Palestinian citizens, the report of the ICRC states that ìthe number of Palestinian detainees held by Israel stabilized at an average of 4,000 at the last major release of some 1,200 in January 1996 under the interim agreement.î Arrests nevertheless continued at an average rate of more than 300 per month. Thanksgiving and the Iron Lady

By E. Yaghi

A few Thanksgivings ago, before the destruction of Iraq..

In a regal room in the White House, a spacious table was set for a pre-Thanksgiving dinner. George and Barbara had invited a small group of only 500 people to their private celebration. The enticing table was spread with every delectable food the mind could imagine while stuffed turkeys jutted their legs out in tasty temptation. Glasses of dark wine sparkled while cranberries glistened under heavy chandeliers. Turkey gravy mixed with special spices blended with the turkey aroma and sifted about the savory room.

Under the table, Millie sat impatiently awaiting tender morsels to fall into her hungry mouth. She seemed dejected and disappointed that she had been denied her usual place at George's table but George had scolded her with, "Now, Millie, we'll have none of that. I've told you before, when we have company, your place must be under the table. Since you wrote your memoirs, you've become too high-handed." Millie sadly looked at George while he spoke. "Oh don't look at me with those beseeching, doleful eyes. You know you're one of the family, but in front of others, we must be more discreet."

With a naughty jump, Millie had slurped George on his pleading face and nearly licked his glasses off. George pushed her under the table, straightened his tie and fluffed his limp sagging hair. He had told Barbara (his hairdresser) to do something about his hair so it would take on more life and fill out his thin face, but it was so hard to convince her of any of his innovations.

Just then, as George was straightening himself, Ron and Nancy strode in, their arms entwined in rhapsody as though they had recently married. George's eyes ran up and down the charming couple with envy. He viewed Ron's full head of very black hair (only his hairdresser knew for sure). Imagine, not one gray strand and he was fast approaching 80! His eyes then fell on Nancy who looked stunning in her new gown. He adored the bones that protruded out on her emaciated parched face and her shriveled hand stretched out to meet his.

"Ron, Nancy! So glad you could come. I just had to have this little get-together," George said as he stood on his toes to greet Ron and then bent down to kiss Nancy's hand. "You know I have to celebrate Thanksgiving in the sands of the Gulf with the boys and I so hate cold turkey. Bar feeds me enough of that after Thanksgiving's over. My your hand feels so icy Nancy."

"We know how you feel, son," Ron said, warmly in sympathy. "But we all have to sacrifice for the sake of oil, uh, that is, democracy. You go there and tell those American boys just what it is they're going to die for!"

We'll all bestow on them the Purple Medal of Honour when they die for their country, though I don't know what the heck they want with any medals when they're dead!"

"For their families, moron. I've told you this before. You've got to learn how to play your role right," growled Ron.

"Ron, you promised you'd be polite," Nancy broke in. "So nice to see you Georgie. Sorry to hear about our Republican defeat. I guess people aren't as stupid as we thought they were."

"Thanks Nancy," George said with embarrassment. "I guess people are just frustrated because we didn't obliterate Iraq from the beginning. I'm trying to stir up more hate by claiming the hostages taken by Iraq are being mistreated so I can attack that blasted country as soon as possible!"

"We know you're doing your best Georgie," Nancy said as she patted his hand with obvious sympathy. "By the way, why is your face wet? Are you sweating? Where's Bar?"

"Oh, no, that's just one of Millie's slobbery kisses. You know how affectionate she is. But I've been firm with her. I told her she had to sit under the table, not at the table. I've been reading a book called The Strong-Willed Dog and I've even included some of the ideas into my presidential policy," he said while taking out his handkerchief to mop his face as he reddened.

It was then that Barbara made her grand entrance. All faces turned in awe at the lovely vision she created. If only she were 50 years younger! Her face was powdered pink and blue, and her silver gray hair, puffed and sprayed, while huge pearls choked the loose skin on her neck. She looked like a fresh winter breeze. Her Nile-blue gown floated about her as she glided along greeting her guests. Finally she approached her adoring Georgie, smiled and gave him a sharp little kick while she said under gritted yet smiling teeth, "George, I told you not to invite that tainted woman here to our dinner! There's word that you two are sneaking around behind my back. I don't trust either of you. You both have too much in common. Birds of a feather flock together. I heard that you promised to give her an oil well when you reclaim Kuwait as part of America!"

A dog's lick and now a wife's kick! This was too much. George clenched his teeth in pain while beads of sweat broke out on his lean face, which turned purple with chagrin. "Bar, what's wrong with you? Have you been taking too many pep pills lately? Just take it easy. Who's this tainted woman you're talking about? I don't know any tainted women except Dolly, Marilyn, Betty, Roxanne, Linda, Karen, and Tammy Baker!"

Don't play innocent with me. You know who I'm talking about. It's that horrible woman...you know, Maggie! I told you not to invite her to our Thanksgiving dinner. I'm surprised you did not obey my orders. You may rule this country, but remember, I rule you!"

George choked on his wine and said, "But Bar, darling. You know I've never loved anyone but you and 20 or so other women! Maggie's not my type. In fact, I don't think she's anyone's type, but she's the 'iron lady' dear, and she supports my campaign against Iraq 100%. If anyone's going to get an oil well, I assure you, it will be my Bar!"

"Humph," she snorted in disbelief.

They stopped their conversation as one of the maids rang the bell for dinner. The sound echoed in the huge room and all the useless chitchat ceased. The honoured guests gathered around the splendid feast with growling stomachs and hazy heads. George was by now severely frustrated by the Republican defeat in his home state as well as elsewhere and by Barbara's groundless accusations. Her sharp kick still hurt but he bent his head in a prayer of thanks and then followed this by his annual Thanksgiving speech.

"You see, it all began when our forefathers landed on the shores of America and struggled to survive in a strange and harsh country. The Indians showed them how to plant and harvest crops and raise turkeys. The pilgrims shared their first Thanksgiving with their Indian friends. Then when the settlers increased in number and grew stronger, they expressed their appreciation to their friends by killing them off and stealing their land from them. Brave Americans armed with guns, cannons and cunning, took the defenseless and trusting Indians' best land from them and forced them to live on the worst land in reservations. Not only was buffalo hunting a great American sport and pastime, but so was Indian hunting. We invented the idea of chemical warfare when we long ago infected the blankets we donated to the Indians in charity by weaving small pox germs in with the fibres. They had no resistance to this disease, as it had never appeared before the advent of the settlers. Small pox thus wiped out whole groups of people and sometimes whole tribes. Most impressive!"

"We now face an even greater task before us. We must destroy this Saddam person. He has become too powerful. His presence will destabilize the balance of power in the Middle East. We must keep our leaders and our best friend, Israel, the only powerful country in the area. Of course, we don't really want democracy to catch on there. We only want the Arabs to think they have democracy. We've got to bomb Iraq and do our duty to poor Gabbie and give him back Kuwait, "yesterday before today!"

With these words, the guests stood on wobbled legs and vigorously clapped their hands and then drank a toast to honour the brilliance of their Georgie.

Georgie smiled in victory as just then his special guest of honour, the "iron lady" strode in wearing a tweed suit to match her tweedy face. She had recently cut her hair to give her a more manly look...George glued his eyes on Maggie in admiration still trying to figure out if that darn hair was a wig or not. His vision blurred in complete infatuation for her character, her charisma and her outdated colorful ideas that belonged to the Victorian age. He was crazy about old-fashioned women. He mustn't let Bar know he really had promised Maggie some blown-up oil wells in her name. He did so adore her little bunny teeth and how she gnawed on carrots, crackers and toast. He could hardly wait to compare defeat notes with her. But, just a minute, hadn't Ron promised him that, "you can fool all the American people all of the time?" He must remember to speak to that man before he charges off to the Gulf to meet those crazy young American boys who were going to die for the great American dream, the American way in the lonely burning sands of Desert Shield. Perhaps Millie could give him some helpful hints and his scriptwriter must be sure to write the best speech ever. George had to remember to act angry and look sincere when he faced his troops. Drunk with wine, victory and imagined fame, George's head swam in confusion as he fell face down in the cranberry sauce.

Al-Khalisa Before 1948

The village was located on a low hill on the northwestern edge of the al-Hula Plain-a location that spared it from the periodic floods of Lake al-Hula. It overlooked the plain on the north and the south. Al-Khalisa was on a highway that led from al-Mutilla in the north to Safad and Tiberias in the south, near the Lebanese border. Its proximity to Lebanon and Syria gave the village special significance as a commercial center. In 1596, al-Khalisa was a village in the nahiya of Jira (lfwa' of Safad) with a population of 160. It paid taxes on a number of crops, including wheat and barley, as well as on other types of produce and property, such as beehives, water buffalos, orchards, and a water-powered mill. In the late nineteenth century, Europeans passing through the area described al-Khalisa as a small village built of stone and situated on a plain. Surrounded by streams, the village had a population of about fifty. The Bedouin of the al-Ghawarina clan were the original founders of the village and constituted most of the population. Temimi and al-Katib (1916) described the generous reception accorded to them by the head of the tribe and the large guest house with rugcovered floors in which they were received.

The houses of al-Khalisa were made of bricks and basalt stones, cut from the hillside. In 1945 its population consisted of 1,820 Muslims and 20 Christians. Al-Khalisa had a boys' elementary school, which also admitted students from neighboring villages, and a village council that administered its affairs. The villagers drew their drinking water from several springs. They derived their income from both agriculture and commerce. In 1944/45 they planted 3,775 dunurns in cereals; 5,586 dunums were irrigated or used for orchards. The villagers held a weekly market in which goods from al-Khalisa and neighboring villages were sold.

 

Occupation and Depopulation

On 11 May 1948, the Haganah rejected al-Khalisa's request for an "agreement," and Israeli historian Benny Morris reports that the villagers felt threatened and decided to leave. In addition, they would have heard that their district capital, Safad, had been captured that same day. Given that Operation Yiftach's commander Yigal Allon intended to expel as much of the area's population as possible during the offensive, it is not surprising that the Haganah rejected the offer of a truce. Zionist forces probably moved into the village shortly afterwards, as part of their general offensive in eastern Galilee, which was called Operation Yiftach. Plan Dalet had originally called for the occupation of the British police station in the village, but it is not clear when the order was carried out.

Villagers interviewed in later years by Palestinian historian Nafez Nazzal gave a more detailed picture of the occupation. They said they fled from their homes after hearing news of the fall of Safad and sought refuge in Hunin, to the northwest. But the village militia remained for a few days, until it was heavily shelled from the Jewish settlement of Manara and saw an armored unit approaching the village. The militiamen retreated through the hills-Zionist forces controlled the roads-and made their way to Hunin. Eventually, the villagers were displaced from there to Lebanon. In later weeks, a few of them decided to return in order to dig up money they had buried in their land, or to harvest some of their tobacco and grain. They reported that the Israeli forces had burned and destroyed many of the houses. "The village was in ruins," said one villager who returned briefly.

 

Israeli Settlements on Village Lands

The settlement of Qiryat Shemona (203290) was established in 1950 on the village site.

 

The Village Today

Stone rubble from the houses marks the site. The school and the Mandate government's office buildings stand abandoned, as does the village mosque and minaret. The level land surrounding the site is cultivated by the settlement of Qiryat Shemona, while the mountainous areas are either used as pastures or are wooded.
