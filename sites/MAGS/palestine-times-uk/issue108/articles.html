The Muslim David defeats the Zionist Goliath

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khaled Amayreh

After 20 years of sinister military occupation of Southern Lebanon, the Israeli army, and its puppet quislings, known as the South Lebanese Army, left Lebanon on 23 May with their “tail between their legs.”

The ignominious retreat was carried out under the cover of dark after it became apparent that the southern Lebanese militia were collapsing and fleeing to Israel to escape possible retribution from Hizbullah and certain prosecution from the Lebanese government for treason and grand treachery.

Some of the quislings, especially those who couldn’t make it to northern Palestine for one reason or the other, surrendered to Hizbullah fighters, who in turn handed them over to the Lebanese authorities.

However, the bulk of the disgraced collaborators and their families, around 6,400 of them, crossed to Palestine to become refugees, an inescapable, though small, price they had to pay for betraying their country and their people and also for the numerous crimes they perpetrated against their people, including murder, rape and torture.

Meanwhile, Hizbullah and other Lebanese resistance groups entered the villages and towns of South Lebanon almost as soon as they were vacated by the Israeli soldiers and the disintegrated militia of the quislings.

There, the Islamist fighters were given a hero’s welcome by the local population who apparently breathed a sigh of relief, having delivered themselves from the shackles of the Zionists for over two decades of occupation, persecution, repression and constant torment.

And within a short time, Hizbullah fighters, wielding their automatic rifles, were walking with their heads held high along the Lebanese side of the border with only a few metres separating them from Zionist troops on the other side of the Lebanon-Palestine border.

One Lebanese citizen screamed at the Zionists, “We’ll see you in liberated Jerusalem…today is Lebanon, tomorrow is Jerusalem.”

Freeing detainees at Khiam

In a triumph of victory, the Islamic fighters, along with ordinary Lebanese citizens, stormed the notorious Khiam Prison, liberating hundreds of Lebanese inmates who were routinely tortured by the Zionists and their SLA quislings for refusing to collaborate with Israel or on suspicion of aiding the Lebanese resistance movements.

There, the torture tools were all in place: the whipping pole, the spikes, the window grilles where prisoners were tied naked for days, the handcuffs, and the electric machines used to jolt prisoners.

The abandoned manacles were testimony to the horrible crimes that had taken place just a few days earlier, very much like what goes on in Israeli jails and detention centres inside Palestine Proper.

Shouting “Allahu Akbar” (God is the most Great), hundreds of prisoners were able to see freedom, not realizing that their torturer prison-keepers had just fled like panicking rats southward.

The scene was too overwhelming and fraught with outpouring emotions to be described by words, as many of the prisoners thought they would never see freedom again.

Khiam was one of the stories of horror, terror and sinister inhumanity that the retreating Israeli occupation left over in Lebanon.

It was a weird and terrifying place of torture, where Lebanese citizens were systematically subjected to all forms of torture for the purpose of extracting confessions from them or simply to get them to collaborate with the Zionist occupation troops against their own people.

Some of the internees suffered irreparable damage, losing a hand, a leg, eyesight or suffering a psychological trauma that will stay with them for the rest of their life.

“I was hung naked for 13 days…they put a bag on my head and threw cold water over me night and day,” said a liberated prisoner, quoted by Robert Fisk of the British newspaper, Independent, on 25 May.

According to Fisk, who visited the notorious lockup on 24 May, the handcuffs bore the trademark “The Peerless Handcuff Co., Springfield, Mass. Made in USA.” These handcuffs were used to bind the arms of prisoners before interrogation…and they wore them day and night, as they were kicked so badly that in Ramadan’s case they had to amputate his arm.

“The hoods were still there, big light-blue corduroy sacks with towels inside–some of the towels bought from Norwegian Unifil soldiers because the UN globe was embroidered on some of them–and so was the wire with which other prisoners, including women, were beaten…big, thick wire bound in blue plastic…the torturers were sadistic, often stupid men…there were pornographic magazines and cheap comics and puzzle books in their filthy quarters. Israel has admitted teaching these men how to do their job.”

Sacrifices breed victory

The Israeli retreat from Lebanon was not precipitated by a change of heart on the part of the Zionist establishment or by a sudden awakening of a human conscience among the leaders of the Israeli establishment.

The retreat was forced upon the Zionist regime by the mounting losses that the Zionists incurred in the course of their lengthy occupation of a large area of Lebanon.

Over 1,200 young Israeli soldiers have been killed in Lebanon since 1980, and several thousand soldiers and civilians were injured, prompting Israeli military leaders and ordinary soldiers to question the wisdom of staying in Lebanon and continuing to suffer a “curse” called Hizbullah.

But more importantly, the Israeli society itself grew increasingly impatient and unwilling to bear the bloody costs of its enduring occupation in southern Lebanon.

Indeed, large segments of the Israeli Jewish population began to question the logic of endangering and losing young Israeli lives especially in the absence of a convincing cause. And this feeling would always be vindicated and justified, with every televised image of Israeli soldiers brought from Lebanon in coffins.

So, Lebanon in effect became Israel’s Vietnam, a thorn in Israel’s side, and a source of perpetual suffering and death for the Zionist establishment.

And the source of all of this was a small, irregular but dedicated fighter-force of a few hundred fighters called Hizbullah, the Party of God.

This force, built around 500 core members, represented a new generation of fighting Muslims, unlike anything seen in the previous Arab-Israeli wars…well-organized, well-trained and above all well-motivated.

Indeed, what made Hizbullah’s fighters excel in the battlefield against the greatest military power in the Middle East is their almost innate and unlimited readiness to die as martyrs for a noble cause deserving all possible sacrifices.

It was Jihad, or just sacred struggle against oppression and aggression, a struggle against the occupation and rape of their homeland by an arrogant enemy that was only interested in subjugating them and usurping their human and natural rights and occupying their land…on the mere account that it was stronger than them and that might was right.

But might did not make right in the Lebanese context thanks to the Muslim David who exhausted the Jewish Zionist Goliath who found that his unwanted presence in Lebanon was not without a price and that since conditional withdrawal was not possible, given Hizbullah’s determination to keep up the struggle no matter what, unconditional withdrawal was therefore inevitable.

And the leadership of Hizbullah, in addition to its determination and resilience, was also exemplary in its integrity and steadfastness.

Sheikh Hasan Nasrullah, like his late predecessor martyr Abbas Musawi, would not allow any distance to separate him from his fighters, as his own son was among the martyrs. In short, the leader was just one of the fighters.

And he was not infected by the virus of corruption, as many other leaders in the contemporary Arab world.

That alone commanded the respect of enemies and friends alike, and made the fighters and the would-be martyrs embarking on Jihad against the Zionists do so with serene hearts, free of doubts, suspicions or grudges.

But there was more to the Hizbullah leadership.

It didn’t indulge in behind-the-curtain dealings with the Zionists, nor did it compromise on the general principles, and it didn't allow itself to be used and manipulated by the enemy.

The leadership stood by its words…that the fighting would continue till the last Israeli soldier left our homeland…and so it was.

There was complete concordance between its words and its actions, and it did not say one thing and did quite the opposite in secret, as the PLO leadership often has done and continues to do.

All this produced a clean victory, without interim agreements, without redeployment phases, without truncating South Lebanon into areas A, B and C, or H-1 and H-2, and certainly without security coordination with the occupying powers against their own people.

Will Arafat learn the lesson from Sheikh Nasrullah? We doubt it very much, for Arafat and ilk come from a different breed.

Nonetheless, it is clear that only through the route of Hizbullah and Hamas, the path of Jihad and resistance, will the Palestinians be able to regain their homeland and dignity.

Hizbullah's Gift to Palestine

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

Since its creation in 1948 Israel has never, not even once, recognized, let alone complied with, a UN Security Council resolution. Assured of unconditional U.S. support, for more than fifty years Israelis terrorized the peoples of Palestine, Lebanon, Jordan and Egypt and perpetrated the most
hideous of crimes.
Israel could afford to expand and occupy more land over the years because Arab weakness gave it the green light to do so. After several years of venturing into Southern Lebanon since 1978, Israeli political and military leaders defied all laws and norms by storming, unprovoked, deep into Lebanon in 1982. More than half of the country, including the capital Beirut, came under direct Israeli occupation. Former Israeli defense minister Sharon stood in the heart of Beirut to announce that his troops were there to stay. Indeed, nothing would have compelled the Israelis to withdraw. The UN and international law meant nothing to them and they did not believe the Arabs had the guts to do anything having failed repeatedly in the past.
Soon afterwards, the Israelis discovered they had ventured into a quagmire. Lebanon was to become a graveyard for their troops. Still over confident and blinded from seeing the implications of their adventure, the Israelis created an army of collaborators whom they thought could be relied on for maintaining a buffer zone in the south of the country. The ploy was alleged to have been aimed at protecting Jewish settlements in the north of Palestine from 'terrorist' attacks. But in time neither the army of collaborators nor the so-called 'undefeatable' army of Israel with its sophisticated weaponry and mythical reputation was of any good. In May 2000 the entire world watched with amazement as Israeli troops pulled out under the cover of darkness
leaving behind their army of collaborators to disintegrate within hours.
Hizbullah has succeeded where the Arab states have failed. The two decades of Israeli occupation of south Lebanon have exposed Israel and enlightened the world about the real nature of this Jewish colonial enclave in the heart of the Muslim world. The Israeli humiliating defeat at
the hands of Hizbullah has proven beyond doubt that the Hebrew state understands no language but that of force. This is an entity that is comprised of Jewish immigrants who came in search of the land of milk and honey and not of pain and agony. Paradise had been promised to them by those who recruited them for one of the most inhumane projects in the history of humanity. Occupying other people's lands and homes turned to hell compounded by the fact that Israeli cowardice and fear of death is met with unique bravery by their victims and love of death in martyrdom.
During the glorious days of the Palestinian Intifada (uprising), former Israeli Prime Minister Isaac Rabin wished that one day he'd wake up from his sleep to be told that Gaza had been swept away by the sea. So deep was the trouble he found himself in although his troops were the ones that broke the bones of stone-throwing Palestinians and shot at them with live bullets. At the time the Intifada had still been restricted to the use of stones and peaceful means of protest. Nevertheless, that was too much for the Israelis who grew accustomed to meeting no resistance and engaging no real fighters except for intermittent guerrilla operations by various PLO factions every now and then here and there. What had boosted Israel's confidence in the past was the fact that all previous wars with the Arabs were no real wars. In 1948, Arab troops, mostly commanded by British officers, only prepared the ground for the creation of Israel and the displacement of the bulk of the Palestinian Arab population.

In 1967, the Arabs were not in a mood for war and had not prepared the least for it. Israel took the initiative while knowing the lack of willingness on the part of the Arabs to engage it. In 1973, former Egyptian President Anwar Sadat embarked on what initially was truly a glorious conquest of occupied Sinai only to effect tactical changes in prelude to making a humiliating U.S.-brokered peace with Israel.
The Intifada made a real difference. It exposed Israel's predicament and restored confidence in the hearts of millions of Palestinians, Arabs and Muslims. Furthermore, it opened the eyes of many millions around the world to the cruelty and inhumanity of Israel. When in response to the massacre of Palestinian civilians by Israeli troops or settlers Hamas and Islamic Jihad introduced military tactics including 'martyrdom' bombings, the Israelis sensed more trouble. Now we know how much concern that escalation in resistance posed for the Israelis. We know more surely because of what has happened in South Lebanon. Simply, Israelis cannot afford to die in combat. Someone else should die but not them. If they are the ones that are excepted to pay, they'd rather relinquish their positions and retreat.

Let us try for a moment to envisage what could have happened had the Lebanese scenario been allowed to unfold in occupied Palestine. Like the Lebanese, the Palestinians were prepared to continue the struggle despite the clear imbalance of power. Like Hizbullah, Hamas and Islamic Jihad had the ability to recruit fighters whose love for death in the cause of regaining their people's rights exceeded the Israelis' love for a life of security, safety and comfort. As in the case of Hizbullah, Arabs and Muslims all over the worlds sympathized with the Palestinian struggle and were prepared to support it until it reaped its fruits. However, unlike the case of Lebanon, the Palestinian struggle was stabbed in the back and betrayed from within. Israel was presented with a golden opportunity by the ailing, corrupt and bankrupt PLO leadership. As a result of the 1993 Oslo agreement between the PLO and Israel, the task of securing Israel against Palestinian
reprisals has been undertaken by the forty thousand or so policemen and security agents recruited by the Israeli-sponsored Palestinian Authority (PA). The PA deserves credit, and has indeed been credited by the Israelis, for doing an excellent job hunting down resistance members of both Hamas and Islamic Jihad murdering them, incarcerating them or handing them over to Israel. It is also credited for opening the doors wide open before Israel to establish relations with other Arab countries and with so many nations around the world that until then had refrained from recognizing Israel in support of the PLO. Had Israel not found a helping hand from the PLO, it might have had no option but to withdraw unilaterally from the territories occupied in 1967.
Intelligence sources say that at one time, Israeli politicians were seriously considering an offer of truce by Hamas leader Sheikh Ahmad Yassin according to which Hamas would cease its military operations for an agreed period of time in exchange for a total Israeli withdrawal from 1967-occupied territories, the release of all Palestinian prisoners and the dismantling of Jewish settlements in the occupied territories. Israel did not need to spend much time considering that option when Yassir Arafat offered it peace of mind in exchange for some trivial VIP concessions.
Fortunately, it has not taken long for most Palestinians, including many of those who initially welcomed the creation of the PA, to discover the truth. The PA has turned to be nothing but a new Israeli agency of collaborators little different from the disintegrating South Lebanese Lahd's army of collaborators. Year after year since its creation, and from one round of negotiations to the other, the PA has signed away Palestinian rights. Aware of the predicament it led itself into, the PA's response to the defeat of Israel in Lebanon was a mix of disregard, indifference and denial. There is no clearer evidence than the attempt by Yassir Arafat, PA leader, to uplift the morale of Israelis following the most humiliating moment in the history of their state. As if nothing was happening in the region, he flew to Europe to attend a football match between a European team and a joint Israeli-Palestinian team in support of a peace process few people now believe in.

Clearly embarrassed by Hizbullah's historic success, Arafat insisted in a television interview that the Israelis withdrew from Lebanon not because of Hizbullah but because of compliance with UN Security Council Resolution 425, an argument the Israelis themselves found too good to believe and too feeble to present. Clearly Israel's defeat in Lebanon was also a defeat for Yassir
Arafat and his PA. In the fate of Lahd's army, Arafat and some of his lieutenants may have seen the end of their unholy enterprise. Perhaps it is time for those of them who still associate with the cause of their people to reconsider their positions before their PA is no more just as Lahd's army is no more.
Today Lebanon is free and tomorrow Palestine will be. This is the essence of the message Hizbullah has sent to the Palestinians by awarding to them its victory.

Israel’s True Perception of Arabs is Reflected
in Saleh al-Qaisyia’s Story

By Ramzy Baroud*

It was a fierce battle at Rahwa location near the Israeli Jordanian borders. Dozens of Palestinian and Jordanian fighters who defended the area were declared dead as the military compound was transformed into the ashes of khaki tents, columns of flames and human remains. On the day of 13 September 1956, the bodies of the Arab fighters were unrecognizable. But since no survivors were reported, a large mass grave and the naming of the fallen martyrs was all that was needed for that chapter to be closed. For the families of the dead, the pain and the loss was never redeemed.

What made the Rahwa battle more memorable than most of the tragic battles fought at the time was the eye witness testimony of an Arab shepherd, who declared that departing Israeli soldiers took one last survivor, blindfolded and handcuffed. Although such testimony sparked a glow of hope, the passing of the years were enough to snuff it out. Saleh al-Qaisyia was that captive who survived the Israeli raid by a miracle, yet the slow death of 44 years inside Israeli prisons was the cruel alternative.

It is difficult to be a prisoner of war. It is more difficult to be an Arab prisoner of war in Israel, where your human rights have no value or place. But what is much more difficult than all of that combined is being Saleh al-Qaisyia.

What drove Saleh’s brother Muhammad al-Qaisyia to believe that his brother had a better chance of being that captured solider is the fact that he came to the location the day after the battle ended. When he arrived, he found a hat, a small handmade hat laying away from the destroyed camp. He knew is his heart that it belonged to his brother Saleh.

The story was aired live on a local Palestinian TV station, al-Majd, where Saleh’s brother, son and grandson retrieved that past. With tears and despair, they told what they knew of the story. While the past appeared as vivid as an open book for Saleh’s brother, his son was only six months old when his father became a martyr. So for him, the only memories remaining were those of the long journey of suffering he endured to become a man without the love and care of a father. Yet he dreamt, like any child growing up under the same circumstances, that his father was alive. The fantasy became a reality one day when a man who escaped from an Israeli prison in the late 1960s told the family that Saleh had been held in an Israeli prison since his capture. Yet with few avenues available to investigate and Israeli’s refusal to cooperate, that revelation faded quietly.

While a Palestinian mother was waving goodbye to her son after their short visit ended at the Askelon Israeli prison and was readying herself to head home, she saw an old man sitting dressed in his prison uniform with a long white beard weeping alone. After approaching and trying to comfort him, the old man lamented that his tears were caused by being imprisoned for 44 years without his family knowing that he was alive. The old man was named Saleh al-Qaisyia.

Saleh’s story and tears were quickly disclosed to his family who became firm believers that Saleh was the blindfolded soldier captured 44 years ago at Rahwa. Days later, through the intervention of the Red Cross, Saleh’s son was able to hear loud and clear from the administrator of Israeli prisons that his father was alive.

Yet while the story still lacks a true happy ending, since Saleh is still incarcerated and since two thirds of his life were spent away from his family in torture, humiliation and loneliness, vital questions remain. How many Arab prisoners are secretly held in Israel? How many of them passed away and were buried without their families even knowing of their imprisonment? Why would a 70-year-old man be forbidden from meeting his family? In fact, why is he still being held years after the signing of the peace treaty with Jordan?

Saleh is a reflection of Israel’s disregard for human life and values, a fact that has not changed after the signing of many meaningless papers called “peace agreements”. Most of us already know how Israel would react if Saleh was an Israeli soldier held secretly for 44 years in Jordan or elsewhere. While we cannot expect Israel to treat its enemies like its own people, we may have hoped that years of handshakes would have brought about some change in Israel’s inhumanity. Yet when Saleh and thousands like him are enduring the worst in life and are used as bargaining chips in Israel’s manipulative peace schemes, we know that little has changed despite the ceaseless talks about peace.

Military victory and the attainment of national rights:
from South Vietnam to South Lebanon

Dr. Daud Abdullah*

The issue of Israel’s withdrawal from southern Lebanon is no longer a question of speculation. It is now only a matter of timing. Whether it takes place in July or not seems unimportant to Lebanese and Palestinians alike. While it may not be as dramatic as the humiliating American withdrawal from South Vietnam 25 years ago, the historical significance of this event would not go unnoticed: that no military power, however advanced technologically, can defeat a resolute people armed with faith in the justice of their cause.

When it finally comes, Israel’s withdrawal would be an unparalleled victory for the Lebanese resistance. This shall be the first time that a resistance movement would have forced the Jewish State to abandon occupied Arab lands. From a different position, Palestinians may rightly perceive the withdrawal as a victory. For although the 1982 Operation Peace for Galilee was intended to “rid South Lebanon of its Palestinian population”, the military occupation that followed failed to accomplish this. The “transfer” of Palestinians, which Menachem Begin suggested to a meeting of the Knesset Foreign Affairs and Defence Committee in June 1982, has since proved more difficult than expected.1

Traditionally speaking, Lebanon has always been a place where the region’s weak and oppressed sought refuge. Christians who left the Orthodox Church and adopted Roman Catholicism found refuge there in the eighteenth century. Two hundred years later, Armenians fleeing Turkish massacres also ended up in Lebanon. Others included Kurds, Chaldeans and Assyrians from Iraq, albeit in smaller numbers. None of these arrivals compared in size and impact to that of the 125,000 Palestinians who entered Lebanon in 1948. As their number multiplied to over 400,000 during the past five decades, they have on account of their religious identity and national cause determined the direction of Lebanon’s political discourse more than any other exile group. This was helped by the fact that unlike other countries in the Middle East where political power is wielded by army officers and influential families, consensus and the balance of sectarian interests have been the guiding forces behind Lebanese politics.

Race for the camps

The search for consensus in Lebanon was not only determined by conflicts between competing confessional communities. It was also defined by conflicts within individual communities as they fought to overthrow ruling elites and install new power brokers. With regard to the Palestinians, this latter factor appears to be one of the most important that may make this summer the longest and hottest in Lebanon’s modern history. The telltale signs of a struggle for the camps have become increasingly clear during the last nine months as Syria and the PLO jockeyed for position of leadership over the camps.

When a Lebanese military court condemned Sultan Abu al-Aynen, a Fateh leader and Secretary General of the PLO in Lebanon, to death in October 1999 for leading an armed group and trafficking in arms, tensions in and around the Palestinian refugee camps in southern Lebanon rose automatically. One reason is that unlike other countries in the region where they took refuge, the Palestinians in Lebanon had, after decades in the camps, successfully transformed themselves from a stream of defenseless refugees into an ocean of armed combatants. The clearest manifestation of this was the November 1969 Cairo Agreement between the PLO and the Lebanese authorities which enabled them to administer the camps and maintain an armed presence there in return for respect of Lebanese sovereignty.

Today, as Israel prepares to withdraw from occupied southern Lebanon, the central government in Beirut has come under increasing pressure to disarm the Fateh factions in the camps. The contradictory signals from the administration have not helped matters. While President Lahoud openly espoused the disarming of the camps, other officials favour a pro-Syrian leadership that will probably take the fight to Israel. The second sentencing of Abu al-Aynen in April to prison for collaborating with Israel is seen by many as an attempt to increase the pressure on Fateh loyalists. In the event of this happening, the latter have vowed to defend their positions. For its part, the PLO leadership has repeated, time and again, that the refugee issue is a Palestinian one and only the Palestinian leadership can speak on their behalf.

Admittedly, the apparent lack of chemistry and trust between Presidents Arafat and Assad has obstructed Arab coordination on the refugees, especially after Syria became militarily embroiled in Lebanon in 1976. Despite their claim of support for the refugees’ right to return, Prime Minister Salim al-Hoss confessed in a recent interview (Shu’un al Awsat, February 2000) that there is no coordination between the Arab parties on the matter.

By demanding the disarmament of the camps and expansion of a UN peacekeeping role in southern Lebanon, Beirut has unwittingly played into the hands of the Israelis. The latter believe that this arrangement would prevent the south from remaining a war zone after withdrawal. Having failed to obtain any such security guarantees from Lebanon and Syria, military strategists in Israel fear their Prime Minister is leading them into a trap. It is in this context Tel Aviv has called upon America to use its influence in the Security Council to create an expanded military role for the UN in south Lebanon. Its main task would be to prevent the Palestinians from infiltrating or using southern Lebanon as a base to launch attacks from across the border. This was, after all, the raison d’ï¿½tre of the occupation. While disarmament may be the surest way to preempt this, attempts to implement it militarily may turn out to be a bloody and costly affair, especially if Israel’s surrogate South Lebanon Army is allowed to hold on to its weapons.

In a related matter, the Lebanese argue that conditions in the camps should remain as they are because improvements would amount to a form of naturalization. This position is untenable because there are hundreds of thousands of Palestinians in Europe and the Americas who live in dignity and still have not given up their right to return.

Whatever measures the Jewish State adopts to deal with the Palestinian presence in Lebanon, nothing short of repatriation has the slightest chance of succeeding. The Israeli Ministry of Defense nevertheless has other ideas. It recently approved the construction of a 40-kilometre wall along its northern border with Lebanon. In the age of internet communication, it seems utterly ludicrous that the Jewish State should resort to the erection of a Berlin-type wall in order to prolong the division and exile of the Palestinian people. The sad irony of this situation is the same democratic States that supported the reunion of Soviet Jewish families during the Cold War are the very nations who today oppose the reunification of Palestinian families.

Military defeat and political bankruptcy

Israel’s humiliating military defeat in south Lebanon has spawned a virus of political bankruptcy among its ruling elite, hawks and doves alike. Shimon Peres, the pre-eminent dove and author of the New Middle East project, recently entered the debate concerning the withdrawal agenda and its impact on the peace negotiations. Like the rest of the Tel Aviv establishment, he joined the campaign for the postponement of negotiations on the issue of refugees (Ha’aretz, 1 May 2000). He argues that since there is no likelihood of a solution in the immediate future there would be no harm in postponing discussions of the issue.

Of course, there would be no harm for those who do not live in the derelict concrete huts that shelter dark memories of bloody massacres with stench from the sewers and rats that roam the garbage heaps. Britain’s prize-winning journalist, Robert Fisk admitted, “If I lived there, I always think, I’d go mad.” From Nahr el-Bared in the north to Rashedieh in the south, Lebanon’s 12 Palestinian refugee camps are saturated with guns and pent-up anger. Shoot-outs are commonplace both within and without the camps. This is one of the forgotten parts of the world where 40% of the children leave school before the age of 11 and 60% of the workforce is unemployed.

As far as the Lebanese are concerned, asking them to permanently resettle hundreds of thousand of Palestinians seems unfair as well as impracticable. Lebanon simply does not have the capacity to absorb the refugees. It has, besides, yet to recover from its 15-year civil war. More than 450,000 of its own citizens fled the country during the period 1975-1990. It is inconceivable, therefore, how the government will balance their return with the resettlement of the Palestinians. With a gross domestic product (GDP) of $16 billion and an external debt that exceeds $20 billion, most of the country’s earnings go toward interest payments. Yet, friendly countries that are larger and richer than Lebanon continue to pressure it to naturalize 400,000 Palestinians while they cringe at the prospect of accommodating 4,000 refugees from the Balkans.

Despite the absence of genuine incentives, Palestinians must remain hopeful that good sense, probity and justice will eventually prevail. That elected governments in the world’s leading democracies will respond positively to the call of the 103rd International Parliamentary Union Conference, which met recently in Jordan and unanimously called for the Palestinian right of return, according to United Nations resolution 194. And, that they would, likewise, respond to the United Nations non-governmental organizations (NGOs) meeting in Paris which also called for Palestinian repatriation. As a leading member of the international community and a global power with historic ties and interests in the Middle East, much attention is now being focused on the British government. Indeed, observers are waiting for the autumn when the European Convention on Human Rights will be incorporated into the United Kingdom’s law. This event, widely seen as the most significant constitutional change in Britain since the 1688 Bill of Rights, will open mechanisms for the legal debate of unresolved historical issues. These are expected to cover a spectrum that extends from Britain’s involvement in the African slave trade to its creation of Jewish Israel in Arab Palestine.

The Palestinian refugees in Lebanon have, over the years, displayed great resilience, restraint and courage. They survived attempted genocide and numerous crimes of war. They defied the prognosis of those who assumed that the expulsion of the PLO’s leadership from Lebanon would break their will and bury their cause. They continue to live for the return. This objective is one of the few remaining certainties in their daily lives. Their social, cultural and human identity revolves around it. Despite efforts to reduce it to the level of a political demand that can be traded in the negotiations of unequal partners, it remains for them a legitimate and inalienable right that must be part of a just peace.

With over 400,000 Palestinians exiled in Lebanon, Israel’s decision to withdraw from South Lebanon can only produce a temporary lull in hostilities. It can lay no claim to a final settlement or closure of the Palestinian file. Any attempt to legitimize Palestinian exile would, just as night follows day, give rise to a new leadership that will champion the cause of return.

Palestine’s Rejection of the UN Partition Plan

Dr. Musil Shihadeh*

I would like to examine here the much talked about Israeli myth that the Palestinian tragedy was a result of their rejection of United Nations resolution 181 outlining the Partition of Palestine. In other words, what would have happened had the Palestinians and the Arabs accepted the UN Partition Plan of resolution 181? Would Israel have implemented it in good faith?

To keep Israel’s image unblemished in the eyes of the world and to dismiss the contention that Israel was born in sin, Zionist apologists blame all the tragedies that befell the Palestinians on their rejection of the UN Partition Plan as embodied in resolution 181 of 29 November 1947. Such a theme would cloak Israel with importantly needed morality, justify Israel’s atrocities, massacres and the uprooting of the Palestinians in the war of 1947-1948 in order to take over their lands. In retrospect then, the Zionists claim that if the Palestinians accepted the UN Partition Plan as the Zionist leadership did, none of the miseries of 1947-1948 would have taken place–no wars, no victims, no refugees, no land expropriations–and the two peoples would have enjoyed peaceful co-existence for the last 50 years!

To minimize the speculative approach, one has to understand that when the UN Partition was adopted on 29 November 1947, the combined ownership of land by Jewish organizations did not exceed 7% of Palestine while the Jewish population (even with all the illegal massive Jewish immigration) was inferior to that of the Palestinians at a ratio of 1:2 (records of the UN Commission on Palestine, JNF records, the Israeli custodian of absentee properties established 1950, and the new historians). The most important of these are the permanent status negotiations, which have been delayed but are now said to be starting soon, and the changes taking place in the Palestinian territories ahead of these negotiations. It will be recalled that the Oslo Agreement was structured on the basis of two stages, a so-called interim or transitional period of five years, and a final phase when a permanent settlement would be drawn up between the parties. The Agreement stipulated that final status talks would start no later than the beginning of the third year after it was first signed, that is by January 1997. Not only have the talks not started, but Israel's previous prime minister tried to sidestep the interim terms altogether and proceed directly to the permanent settlement. Ehud Barak has followed on in this tradition by his latest proposal to merge the last stage of the Wye Agreement into the final status talks. He was in any case no enthusiast for the Oslo Agreement, which he thought too lax on security arrangements and whose timetable on withdrawal he regarded as ceding too much territory to the Palestinians too quickly.

In the meantime, the Israeli government has been actively involved in settlement expansion and building both in Jerusalem and the West Bank. The issue of settlements and the future of Jerusalem were supposed to be decided only during the final status negotiations. Nothing should have been done by either side to alter the status quo, because it might preempt the final outcome. In fact, the settler population in Gaza and the West Bank increased during the first year of Binyamin Netanyahu's premiership by 9.4 per cent. By 1998, it had reached 180,000 people in the West Bank and Gaza and the number of settlers in and around Jerusalem was around 200,000. The population of the nine largest West Bank settlements increased by 6 per cent during the first nine months of 1998. At the same time, a number of existing settlements were expanded by the building of additional housing, and new ones were established. On April 14 of this year, Haaretz reported that twelve new settlements had been built in the West Bank since the Wye Summit.

In February 1997, the new settlement of Har Homa to the south of Jerusalem was approved by Israel's last prime minister and in September another was established at Ras al-Amud in the heart of East Jerusalem. For Har Homa 6,500 dwellings are planned and the first 1,000 have already been advertised for sale. The current prime minister never opposed construction of either of these settlements which he regards as "housing projects." His only criticism of the Netanyahu record on Jerusalem settlements is that it attracted the wrong sort of publicity and their establishment could have been ignored by the international community had his style not been so confrontational. Barak himself regards the West Bank as part of the land of Israel where the state has every right to build settlements.

On November 13, 1998, the U.N. General Assembly voted overwhelmingly to condemn Israel for illegal settlement building, especially in Jerusalem. Of course, the U.N.vote was taken within the framework of Israel's violation of international law, and not of the Oslo Accords. Meanwhile, the timetable on troop redeployment from Palestinian territory has been subject to many delays. The Oslo Agreement stated that the final settlement would be based on Security Council Resolutions 242 and 338. These resolutions both stress the obligation of Israel to withdraw from territories occupied in the war of 1967, including the West Bank and Gaza. In accordance with this idea, a timetable for the redeployment of Israeli forces from these areas was drawn up with successive Israeli governments. But the process was incomplete and also agonizingly slow, the last withdrawal being 2 per cent of the West Bank out of the 13 per cent agreed at the Wye Summit. Furthermore, the present Israeli prime minister has already made dear that withdrawal by Israel to the 1967 borders is ruled out. Other matters that had also been agreed prior to the era of Netanyahu, such as the release of Palestinian prisoners in Israeli jails, the building of a port at Gaza, and the opening of a safe corridor between Gaza and the West Bank, remain outstanding and await possible implementation during forthcoming Israeli Palestinian talks.

It is in this framework that the Palestine state solution should be seen. In the last few years, this idea has become accepted dogma. Of course, it is not a new invention of the peace process, but it has never before enjoyed such credence. It was first raised in 1974, when the Palestine National Council (PNC) voted at its 12th meeting to establish a Palestinian "authority" on any liberated part of the Palestinian homeland. In the later 1970s, this decision was defined further to mean that a state should be set up on the liberated lands. This was not universally welcomed by Palestinians, many of whom were dismayed that the goal of total liberation had been given up. Despite this and ever since then, however, the Palestinian leadership has consistently aimed for an independent state, to be set up in the West Bank and Gaza with East Jerusalem as its capital.

After the Oslo Accords were signed in 1993, this position crystallized and found support, both tacit and overt, from the Arab world and the international community with the exception of Israel and the United States. The Arab League had in any case accepted "Palestine" as a member state in 1976. In November, 1988, the PNC meeting in Algiers formally accepted the existence of two separate states, Israel and the new Palestine. Nine years later on November 13, 1997, Yasser Arafat announced that the PLO would declare the establishment of an independent Palestinian state in 1999, that is, at the end of the interim period of the Oslo Agreement. In fact, the May 4 deadline passed without the crisis that had been anticipated by the international community The Palestinian leadership, which was cajoled into believing that a declaration at that moment would be damaging to the chances of a Labor Israeli government being elected in May, postponed the event to a later time.

Although the exact boundaries of the proposed state have not been defined, even by the Palestinians, and international support has not expressed itself in terms of square meters of land that might constitute the new state, the idea of such an entity “alongside Israel" has taken firm hold. And there is widespread if tacit acceptance of the idea even in Israel; although there has never been any official endorsement of a Palestine state, there is a feeling of resignation toward this as a probable outcome. Even the hard-line Israeli minister for infrastructure at the time, Ariel Sharon, was quoted on November 25, 1997, as saying that he recognised "with deep regret" that giving the Palestinians an entity of their own would lead inevitably to the establishment of a Palestinian state.

Today, the idea of two states so dominates the discourse about the final outcome of the peace process as to exclude all other possibilities. Yet, in the circumstances, the two-state solution for this intractable conflict seems ever more impracticable. And, even if it had been, it would not be desirable for reasons that will be explained below.

Is A Palestinian State Feasible?

If we take first the question of feasibility, we see at once that there are considerable logistical obstacles in the way of setting up a Palestinian state. A glance at the latest map of the occupied territories explains the position. This shows a West Bank pockmarked by Jewish settlements encircling Palestinian towns and separating them from each other, criss-crossed by so-called bypass roads built for the exclusive use of Israelis and breaking up Palestinian territory even more. Sharing the territory of the West Bank and Gaza with the Palestinians are 180,000 Jews and some 200,000 in and around East Jerusalem. With the latest settlement at Jabal Abu Ghoneim (Har Homa) to the south of Jerusalem, the separation between Jerusalem and the West Bank has become complete. The map thus shows no territorial continuity among the Palestinian areas in the West Bank, which are cut off from each other, from Gaza, and from Jerusalem.

If the settlements remain, then any projected Palestinian state would have no meaningful territory on which to become established. The problem is further complicated by the lack of natural resources, from which the Palestinian areas currently suffer. One of the effects of thirty years of Israeli occupation has been a transfer of resources from the Palestinian inhabitants to the settlers. It has been calculated that by 1989, 90 per cent of the West Bank's cultivatable land and 75 per cent of its water had been switched to the settlers and beyond them to Israel. Since the Palestinian economy is heavily dependent on agriculture (in 1991, it accounted for 35 per cent of the West Bank's and Gaza's GDP, as compared with 2 per cent for Israel) this depletion of land and water is extremely serious. The shortage reached crisis proportions this summer with a once-weekly availability of only drinking water to Palestinian West Bank inhabitants. To make matters worse, there was a significant lack of investment in the infrastructure of the West Bank and Gaza throughout the years of Israeli occupation. Sara Roy, who undertook a detailed study of Gaza's economy in 1995, has described the impact of Israel's occupation of the Strip as a deliberate process of "de-development." She defines this as an economic policy designed to ensure that there will be no economic base, even one that is malformed, to support an independent indigenous existence. She found that over the period of occupation, Gaza's economic resources in water, land, and public finance were extensively expropriated by the Israelis.

Unskilled labor in Israel consequently became a major economic activity for Palestinians from Gaza and the rest of the occupied territories. In 1990, nearly 35 per cent of the Palestinian labor force was working in Israel. Due to these factors, the already weakened Palestinian economy became heavily dependent on Israel. Since 1993, the economic situation in the Palestinian territories has deteriorated further as a result of the Israeli policy of closures imposed on Gaza and the West Bank and the importation of foreign labor into Israel which today houses an estimated 250,000 foreign workers. The Palestinian areas are thus disadvantaged by high unemployment (at least 50 per cent in Gaza and 20 per cent in the West Bank), imposed trade restrictions, an undeveloped industrial base, and poor natural resources. Any Palestinian state set up on this basis is obviously not viable and could only survive with a massive infusion of billions of dollars in aid. It is in this context that a federation with Jordan, which has frequently been proposed, would have the greatest value. Among other things, it is argued that this would promote free movement of labor within the federation, easing the economic problems, and Ehud Barak is on record as favoring a con-federation with Jordan.

To Give Up A World

E. Yaghi

No one should ever give up everything they hold dear in life, not for anyone. For in the end, the one who sacrificed will not be satisfied, nor will those for whom the sacrifice was made ever understand.

And so it happened, the young lady with dreams in her head and love in her heart gave up her country, her parents, and her sibling to travel to a Middle Eastern place. She gave it all up because of the great love she had for her husband and to ensure that her children would grow up free from drugs, alcohol and immoral diseases.

She never expected the drastic way her life would change or all that she would have to give up in order to try to make her dreams a reality. Looking back, Chris often wondered if she had really made the right decision. Had she done the right thing? But she always came back to the same conclusion that she would do it all over again for the sake of her children. The same children now who had become her judge and jurors. The same children who now passed sentence on her with ease of conscience. The same children who expected her to go on living a life she was not happy living so they could comfortably slot her away somewhere in the back of their minds.

Those were the children that she had struggled to keep clean, fed and free from danger. Those were the children that she had lived through several wars for when what she had really wanted to do was move back to America with them where they might have been spared the dangers of those conflicts.

How she had loved his cause and his people. How she had tried to adapt to their way of life. How she had tried so hard and got so tired doing it. Perhaps if things had been different, all this would not have mattered. She could have done all of this, made all these sacrifices. Even dying for the Palestinian cause seemed to be noble and fitting.

But somewhere along the way, once outside her country, and into her husband’s host country, Chris discovered that she suddenly was all alone. And her husband knew that she was. On the other hand, he was at his prime and surrounded by his relatives who could always encourage him to be very macho. Macho at the expense of his wife and kids. Any time he flew into a violent rage, she had no one to protect her, no one to turn to, and no one to defend her rights. In short, he was the king of his little kingdom and he enjoyed abusing his power. It was at this time that her great love for him began to wilt and die, for a flower, no matter how beautiful or how fragrant, cannot survive without water, just as a marriage cannot survive without respect and justice.

Yes, she could have been stronger. She could have stood up for her rights more. But most of what she did do was try to keep peace in her household and protect her children from their father’s wrath. She was wrong and she knew that now. And all the years of pain she had suffered and all the tears she had shed as a result were gone with the blowing of the wind.

And so it came to pass, one day after her children had all grown, Chris packed a single bag and just walked out of her home. She left it all behind her. The house she had worked so hard to maintain and save money for. The newly bought car that she would never drive again. She walked out on the support of her kids and entered a new realm. One of no money, no home of her own and no car to drive. Yet, she also now had freedom, no outbursts of violent temper from her husband and no one to try to control her every movement.

Alone on the shores of America, Chris has had much time to reflect on her escape to freedom and also what she had to sacrifice in order to gain that freedom. In so trying to be honest with her feelings, to really be honest, there was no one to listen to her. No one to help her get back up again. No one to tell her that she perhaps had done the right thing. But now she had her children to condemn her and to let her know that as long as she remained in a marriage she was miserable in, no one really minded. The moment she stepped outside to freedom and to honesty, then she was all alone.

Sometimes it is really better to be yourself even if you are hated for it than to be someone you are not and be loved. All Chris can do now is to try to make a living in a country she has not lived in for most of her adult life and to continue to write and work for the Palestinian people and for all the Arab people who do not have honesty or freedom as a way of life.

Jewish Fundamentalism in Israel

Israel Shahak and Norton Mezvinsky

Part 9

The Rise of the Haredim in Israel

Other Israeli journalists expressed similar fears and went further than did Markus in their published articles. Some attacked not only the religious but also the secular Jews who remained silent about the attacks upon them and their behavior and who would allow continual efforts by religious surveyors to brainwash systematically. Many Israeli Jews, whose opinions were represented by certain journalists, saw the activities and actual victories by religious factions as advancements towards a full-scale Jewish "Khomeinism" in Israel.

The discussion of the Aloni scandal continued for weeks in the Israeli press and became increasingly political. Nahum Barnea wrote in his October 23, 1992 Yediot Ahronot article:

Rabin encouraged the torrents of anti-Aloni propaganda by advancing the slogan "either Aloni or peace." What connection can there be between Aloni's dietary preferences and peace…

On four separate occasions Rabin summoned the leaders of Meretz [Aloni's party] to his office in order to convey to them the complaints about Aloni made by Rabbi Ovadia Yosef, the spiritual head of the Shas Party.

In his October 23, 1992 Davar article, Amir Oren censured Rabin for being subservient to Rabbi Ovadia Yoseph and for equating the rabbi's power to be equal to that of Stalin's in his time. Oren opined that the Shas Party had begun to fulfil in Israel a role analogous to that of the Shi'ites in Lebanon. In Oren's view Israel, "far from being the only democracy in the Middle East was imitating Lebanon and Iran, becoming in effect half a state of anarchy and half a theocracy."

Amnon Abromovitz in his October 23, 1992 Maariv article put a somewhat different spin on the Aloni scandal. He wrote: "The vicious use of Aloni as a scapegoat by the religious Jews generated public support for her. A repelling stench of religious zeal, fundamentalism and sexism is emanating from the harassment of Aloni." Abromovitz blamed Rabin for encouraging this harassment, but he added that despite all her talk and non-kosher eating, Aloni had granted religious institutions, especially those of the Shas, more money than had any previous Minister of Education. Abramovitz concluded: “Aloni may talk blasphemously about God, but she has been foremost in generosity to those who believe in Him.”

The leaders of the Labor Party and their non-traditionalist sympathizers answered the above expressions of fear, especially after Oslo, by arguing that concessions to the demands of the Haredim were necessary to ensure backing for the peace process. This stock answer did not satisfy many secular Israelis. What Markus concluded represented broad secular opinion:

The reason for Rabin's servility to Shas is supposed to be politics. Labor experts in skullduggery assure us that the Shas Party may leave the coalition if it finds it no longer able to withstand pressure from the other Haredi circles... The conclusion is that Labor must do its best to placate them . . Politics is important, but freedom of conscience and everyone's right to follow one's creed are even more important. Jewish secularism is a creed. The crude hypocrisy, with which the ministers fake religious devotions, leads nowhere but only damages their government 's integrity. If Shas wants to leave Rabin's coalition, it will do so by order of its rabbis. It will then not help if Rabin puts on an Haredi garb and/or if Aloni shaves her head to cover it with a coif. [The reference here is to a commandment of traditional Judaism that a woman, before marrying, has to shave her head and cover it with a coif. The Haredim attempt to enforce this rule strictly. Many Jewish, religious women cut only some of their hair and cover the remainder with wigs. Many secular, Jewish women are enraged by this rule.]

By design, Haredi rabbis and politicians select secular women in politics as the primary targets of their attacks, even though they could pinpoint -secular men as much, if not more, for transgressions of religious law. The Haredim repeatedly refer to Jewish women, engaged in politics, as witches, bitches or demons. Although a bit crude at times in the use of descriptive language, the Haredim approach mirrors to a great extent traditional Judaism's broadly based position regarding women. This position not only restricts the rights of women but in many ways holds women in contempt. Rule 8 in Chapter 3 of the Kitzur Shulhan Aruch (Abridgment Shulhan Aruch), an elementary textbook for Jews with little talmudic education, for example, dictates: "A male should not walk between two females or two dogs or two pigs. In the same manner the males should not allow a woman, dog or pig to walk between them." All Haredi boys between the ages of ten and twelve study and are required to observe this rule. (Few dogs and no pigs can be found in Haredi neighborhoods.) Traditional Judaism also prohibits women from playing even insignificant roles in politics and/or in any public activities in which they may appear to be leading males. Women are forbidden to drive buses or taxis; they can drive private cars only if no males apart from those in their own families or other women are passengers. These and many rules are followed in Haredi neighborhoods. In these neighborhoods women who are "dressed immodestly" are often insulted and/or assaulted. Many traditionally religious Jewish males in other than Haredi neighborhoods, who do not observe inconvenient religious commandments, take the lead of the Haredim in resenting and opposing participation of women in politics. These traditionally religious males regard such participation by women as a threat to their domination of their own families.

The numerous misogynistic statements in the Talmud and in talmudic literature constitute a pan of every Haredi male's sacred study. The statement in Tractate Shabat, page 152b, defining a woman is exemplary: "A woman is a sack full of excrement." The learned Talmudic Encyclopedia (volume 2, pages 255-7), written in modern Hebrew and thus understandable to all educated Israeli Jews, devotes a section to the "nature and behavior of women." In this section the proposition appears that the urge for the sexual act is greater among men than among women. The evidence presented for this is that men tend to hire women prostitutes because their urge for sex is greater than the urge of women. For that reason the Halacha punishes a wife who refuses to have sexual relations with her husband much more severely than it punishes a husband who refuses to have sexual relations with his wife. For the same reason a prospective husband is obliged to see his wife-to-be before marrying her but a prospective wife is not obligated to see her husband-to-be before marriage. After seeing his prospective bride, moreover, the prospective husband can send a messenger and conduct the marriage through the messenger. Jewish folklore contains stories describing the utilization of this procedure.

The halachic prohibition of teaching talmudic literature and/or the Bible to women has been in the past and is currently still of great importance. Studying "Torah Sheba'al Peh" (the oral law) is for the Halacha a supremely important commandment. It is equivalent in importance to all the other commandments put together. (The law, according to belief, was given by God orally to Moses and was handed down orally for many centuries before being written.) This obligation, termed "Talmud Torah" or "learning the Torah" is viewed as independent of time. Every pious male Jew is obligated to devote a portion of all days and nights, including holidays and working days, to this study. A basic talmudic rule frees women from positive obligations tat are dependent on special times and obliges women only with positive obligations that are independent of time. Women, for example, are obliged to keep the Sabbath and the holidays that last more than twenty-four hours and are thus considered to be independent of time. Women, on the other hand, are not obliged to hear the shofar (ram's horn) blown on the New Year, which only takes a short time and is thus considered to be dependent on time. (There are a few exceptions to this rule.) A woman is permitted to fulfill what she is not obliged to do; hence she can choose to hear the ram's horn blown on the New Year. This rule underlines the women's religious inferiority to men, since another talmudic dictate is that a person who fulfills a commandment because he is obliged to do so is greater and receives a greater reward from God than a person who fulfills a commandment he is not obliged to fulfill. A Jewish woman that comes to the synagogue on the New Year and hears the ram's horn being blown, according to traditional Judaism, will receive a smaller reward from God than a male who does the same, because she is not obliged to hear whereas he is so obliged. Tractate Kiddushin (page 34a) of the Talmud, however, ruled that women are not obliged to fulfill "Talmud Torah," even though it is an obligation independent of time. This ruling is part of Halacha. The rule was later amended to mean that women should learn only the special obligations that they must keep to the extent that they know what to do and what to avoid. The issue, therefore, arose: What parts of sacred studies are women permitted to learn or to be taught? The talmudic answer to this question, based upon many quotations, was given by Maimonides. In his work, Talmud Torah Laws (chapter 1, rule 13), Maimonides wrote:

A woman who has studied Torah receives a reward [from God], but it is an inferior one when compared to man's reward. This is because she is not obligated [to do so], and everyone who does what he is not obliged to do gets an inferior reward compared to [the reward given to] one who does what he is commanded to do. The woman nevertheless receives some reward. The sages commanded a father not to teach his daughter Torah, because most woman never intend to learn anything and will, because of the weak understanding, convert the pronouncements of Torah into nonsense. The sages said: "Everyone who teaches his daughter Torah can be compared to one who teaches her insipid matters." This rule, however, applies only to talmudic studies.

Although a woman should not be taught the Bible, she, if taught, would not have been taught insipid matters.

A somewhat shortened version of this is given in the authoritative compendium of the Halacha, Shulhan Aruch (Yorah Deah, rule 246, paragraph 6). In modem times the Haredim have attempted to modify those rules to some extent. They have taught and still do teach girls the easier parts of the Talmud, in which arguments between the rabbis, that are considered to be dangerous for the "weak female mind", do not occur. Similarly, the Haredim have taught and do teach girls the Pentateuch but reserve the highest level and most serious commentaries for the boys The Haredim maintain in their schools a strict separation of girls from boys and do not allow the girls to observe boys playing in the schoolyard.

Many Israeli Jews, who in their youth received thorough talmudic educations, have later in their lives reacted antagonistically against Orthodox Judaism's depiction and treatment of women. Some of these Jews in reaction have written articles that are often published in the Israeli Hebrew press but are almost never translated into English. Kadid Leper, for example, a well-known Israeli journalist who as a youth studied in a yeshiva for years before becoming a secularist, wrote in his April 18, 1997 Hai’r article under the title "Woman is a sack full of excrement," the following:

Beatings, sexual brutality, cruelty, deprival of rights, use of a woman as merely a sexual object; you can find all of this there [in the Talmud] ... For two thousand years women had a well defined place in the Jewish religion [Orthodox Judaism]; this place is different from what the rabbinical establishment describes; according to the Halacha, the place of women is in the garbage heap together with cattle and slaves. According to the Jewish religion [Orthodox Judaism] a man buys for himself a slave woman for her entire life simply by providing food and dress and granting to his wife the sexual act.

This kind of published article, together with the many published reports of rabbinical harassment of women, have not only firmed polarization in Israeli Jewish society but have contributed significantly to the growing secular enmity towards Haredim.
