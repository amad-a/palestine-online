Islamist leaders hope a million Muslims will

take part in the "al Quds in danger" contest

Occupied Jerusalem- Islamist leaders from the 1948 Occupied Palestine are planning to hold a worldwide contest aimed at increasing Muslim awareness of the dangers besetting Jerusalem and Islamic Holy shrines therein, Palestinian sources said.

Sheikh Ra'ed Salah, leader of the Islamic Movement in the 1948 Occupied Palestine and mayor of the Arab town of Um al Fahem, said he hoped that a million Muslims would take part in the contest which he said was aimed at "making Muslims around the world well aware of the looming dangers facing Jerusalem and Islamic and Christian holy places."

"We have one al Quds, and one Aqsa, and if Israel succeeds, God forbid, in making them disappear, then no Muslim would have the audacity to view himself or herself in the mirror," said Salah.

Salah said he got the idea of the contest, which would have "al Quds in danger" as the main slogan, after several visits he had made to the Islamic Shrines in Jerusalem and seeing the continuing digging by the Israeli authorities beneath al Masjidul Aqsa.

Salah said he was hopeful that there would be an overwhelming Islamic response, especially from ordinary Muslims "who do care about Jerusalem and the frantic Israeli Jewish campaign to obliterate its Islamic identity."

So far, the steering committee of the contest has contacted several prominent Muslim leaders and scholars who expressed their willingness and desire to take part.

Several Islamic thinkers have already expressed enthusiasm for the grand scheme including Mustafa Mash-hur, leader of the Ikhwan al Muslimun (Muslim Brotherhood) movement in Egypt, Abu al Hasan al Nadawi of India (a prominent Islamic thinker and scholar), Sheikh Yousef al Qardawi, Dr. Abdul Fattah al Uweisi, Kamel al Shrif of Jordan, Rashed al Ghannoushi of Tunisia, and Hamed Beitawi of Palestine.

According to the steering committee, contacts are being made with Sheikh Muhammed Hussein Fadlulah and Muhammed Mahdi Shamsuddin of Lebanon, and several prominent Iranian, Saudi, Pakistani and Turkish Islamist scholars and leaders.

Mustafa Mash-hur was quoted in the Islamist weekly Saut al Haq wal Hurriya (Voice of Truth and Freedom) on 12 December as saying, "It is imperative for all Muslims to demonstrate that our holy places are not cheap and easy targets for desecration and destruction."

Abu al Hasan al Nadawi, one of the most prominent Islamic thinkers in India and the world at large, urged "the Umma in the four corners of the world to rally behind Jerusalem and not let the Jews repeat what happened at Ayodia", when Hindu militants destroyed a historical Islamic Shrine several years ago.

Salah said the contest would, God willing, awaken Muslims to the dangers and perils surrounding al Masjidul Aqsa.

"The time for sleep is gone...so wake up Muslims! before it is too late because your first Qibla and third Holiest Mosque is in danger," Salah said.

Hamas gains in student elections

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) continued to make gains in college elections throughout the occupied territories. Last week, the Islamic block received 40 seats out of the 81-seat-student assembly in the Student Council election at the Najah national university in Nablus. The Shabiba (Fatah) block received 35 seats, the Popular Front (PFLP) 4, the Democratic Front (DFLP) 1 and the Communist party 1. The Najah University had always been a bastion for PLO support, but in the past few years, especially since the advent of the Palestinian Authority, the Islamists have been able to establish themselves as the "largest student block" at the college. Moreover, Hamas supporters are expected to win at the student election at the Islamic University in Gaza, a traditional "home turf" for the Islamists. Recent opinion polls in the occupied territories have shown a slight increase in Hamas popularity. A PA-funded Opinion Survey Center in Nablus put Hamas support at 10% and Fatah at 35%. However, results as such can't be considered purely empirical as they often reflect tendentious goals serving the PA.

In other developments, Israeli army bulldozers demolished four Palestinian homes at Asira al Shamaliyya on Monday, 15 December. Palestinian sources said the Israeli army placed the village and surrounding areas under curfew before carrying out the demolition.

The four homes belonged to the families of Palestinian suicide-bombers involved in the two suicide bomb attacks in West Jerusalem early this summer.

Human rights organizations had appealed to the Israeli authority to cancel the "demolition order" on the grounds that the families, including as many as 30 men, women and children, were innocent victims. Moreover, it was argued that the Israeli policy of home demolition proved ineffective against Palestinian resistance of Israeli occupation. The demolition of Arab homes (the IDF does not demolish homes of Jews involved in murdering Palestinians) has been an established Israeli policy since Israel occupied the West Bank and the Gaza Strip in 1967.

Israeli settlers destroy Mosque, soldiers raid Journalist's home

Occupied Jerusalem-PT - Israeli settlers demolished a Mosque at the Arab village of Um Faraj near Akka on 4 December, Palestinian sources said.

Sheikh Ra'ed Salah, head of the Islamist movement across theGreen Line (pre-1967 Palestine) said Jewish settlers from a Kibbutz called "Bne Ami" brought a bulldozer on Thursday to demolish the Mosque which was built in 1934.

Eyewitnesses said the settlers embarked on their crime under the cover of night and, consequently, the demolition could not have been filmed or photographed.

Salah expressed profound outrage at the "evil act," saying "the destruction of the houses of worship is a red line everywhere except in Israel."

He pointed out that the destruction of the Um-al Faraj Mosque was the latest incident in a series of attacks on and desecration of Muslim holy sites throughout the country.

He cited the unearthing by bulldozers of several ancient Muslim cemeteries and the desecration of numerous mosques "from Beir al Sabah in the south to Safad in the north."

Salah pointed out that in Safad alone, the Israeli authorities have destroyed 17 mosques since 1948, leaving only a small mosque and minaret intact.

According to Salah, other mosques have been turned into "dens for drug addicts, gambling houses, and places of ill repute."

A spokesman for the al Aqsa Committee, an Islamic society dedicated to the protection of Muslim holy places, lamented the destruction of the Mosque at Um Faraj by the Jews.

"It reminds me of the destruction of Jewish synagogues by the Nazis in Europe in the 1930s and 1940s...now the former victims are doing the same thing to us."

In other developments, scores of Israeli soldiers raided the home of Naim Tubasi, head of the Palestinian Journalists Union, in Ramallah on 4 December.

According to Tubasi's son, Ibrahim, as many as 30 Israeli soldiers stormed the house, located on Ramallah's southern outskirts, around 1:30 in the morning.

"They pounded savagely on the outer door, terrorizing the entire family...then they made their way to my father's small library, inquiring about his possession of certain books critical of the Israeli occupation and of Zionism."

The raid on Tubasi's home has been condemned by the Palestinian Legislative Council and human rights organizations in the Occupied Territories.

Palestinians behind bars: a cry for justice

PT staff-

Itaf Elyan went on a hunger strike in protest against her so-called "administrative detention". She was released along with other inmates a few months ago. Shobak officials claimed she was a "threat to security" because she was an activist on behalf of the Al Jihad Al Islami party. No other formal charges had been brought against her other than her association with Al Jihad.

Ms. Elyan was able to gain worldwide sympathy and draw the attention of the international public to the injustice she has been suffering since her release from detention after spending 8 years out of her 14-year sentence. Hers is just one of the many similar cases in which Palestinian citizens are held in custody for no particular reason except the whim of Shobak leaders. Prisoners are often detained without being charged or tried and remain in detention for lengthy periods of time. Some inmates have stayed for as many as five years in this state of injustice. The main obstacle facing the detainee lawyers is the fact that the Shobak officials get to have the final say regarding the release order, not the courts. Therefore, appeals for release submitted to the courts by the lawyers on behalf of their clients are "just a waste of time", as one prisoner described the process.

This blatant violation of one of the basic human rights as stated in the International Declaration for Human Rights articles (2), (9) and (10) has been a recurrent process prevalent in the Jewish State. The prisoners in Israeli jails undergo ill treatment, including incarceration without charge and then being subjected to inhumane treatment and bad prison conditions. In an attempt to improve their prison conditions (better food and health care), prisoners have been known to resort to hunger strikes. In many cases, they would be punished for their efforts by being put in solitary confinement and/or forbidden from receiving family visits. The prisoners are also subjected to various methods of torture and ill treatment during interrogation sessions. The Israeli Government has renewed, at regular intervals, the authorization to use harsher methods of interrogation in "exceptional cases"; these methods include violent shaking, abusive body positioning, beatings, hooding and sleep deprivation. Not surprisingly, such methods have been precursors to the deaths of many prisoners-45 in the past seven years.

Taking into consideration that the number of political detainees is around 3,648 (according to a recent Human Rights Watch report), the problem is obvious. The Israeli Government has not complied with the Fourth Geneva Convention article (76), which requires that protected persons be held in the territories under occupation. Prior to redeployment, the Israeli Government transferred thousands of prisoners and detainees to facilities inside Israel and has abducted, in several cases, Palestinian citizens from Palestinian Authority territories because they were "wanted" in Israel.

As Israel continues its inhumane policy towards the Palestinians by violating their basic human rights, degrading their humanity and taking every measure possible to prevent them from getting or even claiming any rights, talk of a lasting peace settlement is no more than a big lie and deception, since building peace needs trust and good intentions (which are clearly not present). The most important point that one should realize is that peace will not survive in an atmosphere of oppression and suppression.

Palestinians help Lebanese girl injured by Israelis

Special to PT

Relations between Palestinians and Shiites in Lebanon have not always been positive. Over the past decade, thousands of Palestinians were killed defending their refugee camps in Lebanon by Shiite soldiers led by Nabi Berri and the Amal militia. Many Shiite Lebanese in the south blame many of their problems with the Israeli "scorched earth policy" on the provocative behaviour of armed Palestinian resistance groups there. Despite these divisions, a recent case of a Lebanese Shiite girl being treated in the U.S. by a Palestinian-American relief group has shown that the problems between the two communities are in the past and that a new relationship based on mutual cooperation and respect can emerge.

16-year-old Miri Zeineddin does not know the politics of her community well. Brought up in the small village of Safat Batik near the Israeli-occupied zone in south Lebanon, what she does know about is war and suffering. As a small child, she watched in horror as an Israeli officer pulled a gun and shot to death her father in front of her home. There had been an attack on an IDF patrol, and it seems the murder of Miri's father was an act of revenge. This was just the start of the young girl's nightmare.

In April 1996, Miri and her family were fortunate to escape alive from the Israeli war machine's invasion of South Lebanon. The "Grapes of Wrath" attacks in Lebanon left over 100 Lebanese civilians dead and many more injured. "We were lucky to not suffer the same fate as Qana," Miri recalls. Qana is where the Israelis killed over 100 women and children seeking refuge in a United Nations post.

Following the Qana massacre, a monitoring team was set up by the French and U.S. to ensure that Israelis and Hizbollah, the Shiite resistance movement fighting Israeli occupation in the south, did not attack civilian targets. For six months following Qana the people in South Lebanon were spared the usual bombings and attacks by Israeli artillery, warplanes and soldiers. In August 1996, however, several Israeli shells hit the home of Miri and her family in Safat Batik. It was the first cease-fire violation since Qana.

"I had just come home from school," the young girl recalls, "when the first shell hit. It blew off our roof and part of the wall fell on me." Relatives quickly dug Miri out and took her to a hospital, where doctors had to amputate her leg above the knee. "I am lucky," Miri says. "Our home was hit again just after the people had taken me from the rubble. Another minute and I would have been hit again and killed."

Enter the Palestine Children's Relief Fund, a non-political, non-profit U.S.-based relief group that arranges free medical care in the U.S. for injured Arab children who cannot be treated in the Middle East. It was set up by a group of concerned Americans who wanted to help relieve some of the great suffering Arab children endure in war-torn Lebanon and occupied Palestine. "We do not discriminate against any child in need of our help," says PCRF treasurer Farid El-Asmar, who lives in New Jersey. "We have helped kids not only from Palestine, but also from Jordan, Syria, Iraq and Lebanon. It is an honour as Palestinians to give something back to these people, instead of always taking or needing their help."

In October 1997, Miri flew to the U.S. for a quality artificial leg built at the Shriners Hospital in the U.S. The PCRF took care of her ticket and all expenses for her travel and are housing her with a local Palestinian family in Philadelphia. "We do not think of Miri as Shiite or Lebanese," explains Nizar Al-Warasneh, who has opened his apartment to take care of the girl he never met before. "It is a duty as a human being to help these kids. They are suffering the same in Lebanon like we are suffering in Palestine, and we have to help them whenever we can. It is the right thing to do."

Miri is currently having a new leg built in the U.S. and is happy to have the chance to walk again. While Nizar and his wife Samia have opened their home and hearts for this girl, they hope that others will see their work as an example and do the same for other children in need. "There are so many suffering Arab children throughout the Middle East," says Nizar. "We need more people to work with the PCRF to help house and take care of those kids. It is our duty as Palestinians and as human beings."

Political prisoners go on hunger strike in PA jail

Occupied Jerusalem (17 December) - Scores of Palestinian political detainees have started a hunger strike at the PA lockup in Dahairiyya, 20 kilometres west of Hebron, Palestinian sources said on 17 December.

The usually reliable sources said the mostly Islamist detainees decided to go on a hunger strike after the prison administration failed to make good on a promise that had been made to the detainees to charge them or release them.

Most of the detainees on the hunger strike are reportedly from the Nablus area.

Palestinian sources revealed a partial list of the detainees, including Eyad Marwan, Sami Jabarin, Ala'a Humeidan, Ra'ed Kukah and Fahim Dawabshe.

The above-mentioned were rounded up by various PA security agencies following the two bomb attacks in West Jerusalem last summer.

The detainees reportedly included a number of pro-Hamas students studying at the Najah National University in Nablus.

In other developments, the PA (mukhabarat) Intelligence Service has been interrogating leaders of the teachers' union in the West Bank.

According to a source close to the union, the interrogation centred on their role in a teachers' strike earlier this year protesting teachers' low salaries.

The source, who agreed to speak on condition of anonymity, said PA interrogators accused the teachers of "mixing education with politics and of having been politically motivated" in the said crisis.

The source added that a security file was opened for each teacher participating in the strike.

The teachers' union is an elected body representing more than 1,500 teachers.

An average Palestinian teacher receives a monthly salary not exceeding $300.

Netanyahu: No further redeployment until

PA accepts our conditions for final-status settlement

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu has vowed that "Israel will not carry out any further redeployment in the West Bank until the Palestinians accept our conditions for the final settlement."

Speaking during a television interview on 21 December, Netanyahu said his Government would not transfer any more territories to the Palestinian Authority until "we know the features of the final settlements beforehand and the Palestinians accept our conditions for the final settlement."

He added that the PA would have to carry out all its commitments stipulated in the Oslo Accords before Israel could start carrying out the second phase of redeployment from parts of the West Bank.

Netanyahu's remarks are considered the clearest signs so far that the present Israeli Government will not comply with the stipulations of the Oslo Accords.

The Accords originally envisaged a three-phase Israeli redeployment from the West Bank save "military positions, settlements and Jerusalem," which would be left for the final-status talks.

The former Labour Government carried out the first phase by pulling out from most of the Gaza Strip (which was a painful thorn in Israel's side) and seven towns in the West Bank, leaving more than 90% of the territory in Israel's hands.

However, the present right-wing coalition headed by the extremist premier Benyamin Netanyahu has adamantly refused to carry out the second and third phase of the redeployment, arguing that it is not bound by agreements to which it was no part.

On 22 December, Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy accused his colleagues in the Government of thinking they can procrastinate endlessly about carrying out the second phase of redeployment in the West Bank.

According to Israeli sources, Levy complained that as a result of government procrastination and equivocation, the United States might exert pressure on Israel causing the latter to lose ability to manoeuvre.

Netanyahu and most of his ministers have sought to seek pretexts to evade the implementation of the second phase of redeployment.

Normally, Netanyahu would cite the "security issue" as the main hurdle impeding progress in the redeployment issue.

However, when the PA leadership last month consented to a US-brokered "security cooperation agreement" with Israel, the Israeli premier started talking about the "final-status settlement" and "Israel's security interests."

It is expected that Netanyahu will continue to try to evade the real issues even after his upcoming meeting with US President Bill Clinton slated for late January or early February.

Israeli sources said Netanyahu would seek to meet with as many US senators and congressmen as possible in the hope that they would convince President Clinton not to exert pressure on Israel.

Clinton is also expected to meet with PA Chairman Yasser Arafat in late January.

Both meetings with Netanyahu and Arafat were decided on after US Secretary of State Madeleine Albright failed to extract from Netanyahu (during their meeting in Paris on 18 December) any commitment to carry out a credible redeployment in implementation of the Oslo Accords.

Netanyahu: I want to see Palestinians at each other's throats

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu has expressed renewed discontent with the "memorandum of understanding," which contained terms of "security cooperation and coordination" between Israel and Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority.

In statements published in leading Israeli dailies on 25 December, Netanyahu said the document was incomplete and needed to be revised. "We are talking only about a draft copy at the moment. The document has yet to be approved. Our instructions to our representatives were that they should go and listen to the other side and tell them of our demands that they fulfill their commitments. The security document at the moment is still only one of principles and is not a detailed plan," Netanyahu said. The Israeli premier didn't spell out what he would like to see added to the document.

However, Israeli sources indicated that Netanyahu would like to put in the document clauses obliging the PA to, inter alia, hand over Islamist detainees in PA-run jails to Israel, escalate the policy of administrative detention (incarceration without trial for prolonged periods), ravage the infrastructure of the Islamic movements, exercise tighter scrutiny over the content of Friday sermons, exclude anti-Israeli references from the Palestinian media, and close down all Palestinian institutions that are run by individuals or groups deemed anti-Israeli.

Netanyahu said the existing document had many shortcomings and inadequacies, adding that drafts were defective, which necessitated that they be resubmitted to the Palestinian Authority.

Netanyahu's statements contradict assertions by key PA officials that the document was signed under the supervision of CIA representatives.

On 24 December, Muhammed Dahlan, head of the Preventive Security Service (PSS) in Gaza vowed that the PA "will not agree to change even a single word of the document."

He said the document was acceptable to the PA because it contained the element of reciprocity, a claim the Israelis have strongly denied.

The PA has complained to the United States about the release on 22 December of an Israeli army officer held in an Israeli lockup. Last year, the officer killed an Arab child, Helmi al Shawashe, of the village of Husan near Bethlehem, by smashing his head with his personal firearm.

Sa'eb Ereikat, the chief PA negotiator, protested during a radio interview with the PA radio station, "The Voice of Palestine," that "how could they dare demand that we round up Hamas activists when they quietly release the murderers of our children from jail only a few months or weeks after committing their crimes."

Ereikat's lamentation, however, is not likely to be translated into a strong Palestinian stance in the face of Israeli blackmail.

Observers note that the sole decision maker in the PA is Yasser Arafat, a man often reputed of caving in to Israeli and American demands, especially those pertaining to repressing the opponents of the Israeli occupation, particularly the Islamists, within the autonomous enclaves.

Palestinian Christian leaders: Christian-Muslim relations are excellent,

Jewish persecution is behind Christian emigration

 

Occupied Jerusalem - From Khalid Amayreh

In response to recurrent Israeli misinformation about alleged Muslim harassment of Christians in the West Bank, three prominent Palestinian Christian leaders have described Muslim-Christian relations in the Holy Land as "excellent," arguing that the enduring and indiscriminate Israeli oppression of all Palestinians was the main reason behind Christian emigration.

The three are: Professor Bernard Sabella of Bethlehem University, Albert Aghazarian, Head of the Public Relations Department at Beir Zeit University, and Afif Safieh, PLO ambassador to the UK.

In a booklet published last month entitled "Christian Voices from the Holy Land," the three described Israeli insinuations about Christian-Muslim relations in the West Bank, and in Palestine at large, as "malicious," pointing out that Christians and Muslims alike were recipients of Israeli torment, oppression and persecution.

Sabella, a sociologist, pointed out in his article "Palestinian Christians: Challenges and Hopes," that owing to the Israeli military occupation of the West Bank and East Jerusalem, Christian emigration from Palestine increased drastically.

He added that the Christians were especially susceptible to the practices of Israeli occupation authorities as more than 12,000 of them left East Jerusalem, the West Bank and the Gaza Strip to go abroad.

In a paragraph entitled "Excellent Christian-Muslim Relations," Sabella contended that there was no need to reaffirm the traditionally excellent relations between Christians and their Muslim neighbours.

"This tradition of good Christian-Muslim relations", he wrote, "has evolved through centuries of co-existence and exchange in the cities of Jerusalem, Nazareth, Bethlehem, Ramallah and in the rural areas such as Zababdeh, Bir Zeit and other towns and villages where Muslims and Christians live side by side and interact in their pursuit of daily preoccupations and concerns."

Sabella cited several factors as contributing to the good relations between Muslims and Christians including the Palestinians' common experience of dispersal and loss of homeland, the recognition by Islam of the many aspects of the Christian faith as crystallized in "al Uhda al Umariyya", which guaranteed the safety of Christians and their holy places in 638.

Sabella, answering the question of "Why do Christians Leave?", said Palestinian Christians were more sensitive than the rest of the population to bad political and economic conditions, particularly if they perceive that the prospects for advancement are not forthcoming.

He added that there was "a clear relationship" between the higher rates of departure and the overall bad or worsening economic and political situation in the Occupied Territories.

The result, he said, was more emigration, citing the dwindling Christian population in East Jerusalem, which is today only 35% of what it was in 1944 (around 11,000).

Albert Aghazarian, in his article "Zionism, Jerusalem, Multiculturalism and a New Palestinian Vision," sought to refute many of the Jewish myths, which Israel succeeded in circulating around the world, such as "the 3000 anniversary of the foundation of Jerusalem."

He pointed out that if David showed up 3000 years ago, the Canaanites (who were Palestinians, of course) were around 2000 years longer than that.

He pointed out that Israel is bringing torment to the Arabs and no real security to Jews.

As for Jerusalem, Aghazarian pointed out that since 1967, Israel has unilaterally declared the city "reunified." He said that by using terms such as uniting, dividing, reuniting and redividing, implied by common sense, either bringing two or more parts together or setting them apart. He said no such thing has happened in the case of Jerusalem. In reality, he said, the town remained more religiously, ethnically, culturally and politically divided than ever.

Finally, Afif Safieh presents a moving personal account, an epitome of the overall Palestinian plight, of the dispersal of his own family to the four corners of the world.

He described himself as "a wandering Palestinian, which I still am."

"Having finished high school in 1966, I left for Belgium to the University of Louvain, the oldest Catholic University in the world...the 1967 war made me the wandering Palestinian I still am. >From Belgium, I moved to Paris to pursue my studies then lived and worked in Geneva, Beirut, Belgium again, Harvard, the Hague, London..." he wrote.

Safieh related how he was repeatedly subjected to Zionist harassment and intimidation whenever he sought to speak out against the Zionist oppressors.

"...At the entrance of the conference centre, around 50 right-wing supporters of Likud and a few Dutch fundamentalist Christians were shouting anti-Palestinian slogans. To my surprise, one of the slogans was 'Safieh-Satan'..I had thought that the usage of such concepts was restricted to the third world. I sent a friend to tell them (half) jokingly: 'Please do not compare me to Satan; you risk making hell look less unattractive than it was intended to be'."

Safieh added, "I don't know what they are shouting down there but I hope it is 'Safieh go home'."

Safieh related another incident illustrating the extent of Western ignorance of the Palestinian question.

He said "in April 1996 I was invited by friends to address 150 workers in the field of international development from the different dioceses around the UK. Shimon Peres had just launched an unnecessary war in Lebanon and the Qana massacre was only a few days old. I was revolted, angry, and emotional and I believe, moving. During the coffee break an elderly lady, obviously a pro-Israeli, angrily approached and shot at me: 'since when are you a Christian?' I could read through her. She probably believed that one day Yasser Arafat and his colleagues met in clandestine underground and decided that I should convert to Christianity so that I would sensitize audiences who might be more sympathetic if they were to learn that there are also ...Christian Palestinians. She­and I­knew that Western audiences could have that type of unchristian attitude. Very quietly, I answered: 'Madam, I was born a Christian. My family goes back in Jerusalem as far as the archives exist. Do not forget, Christ and the Christian faith was born in Palestine. So I am a historic Christian. You can even say, a pre-historic Christian'."
