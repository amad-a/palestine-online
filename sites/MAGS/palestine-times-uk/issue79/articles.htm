Second Anniversary of Martyr Ayyash's Assassination

By: E. Yaghi

January 5 marks the second anniversary of martyr Yahya Ayyash's death. It was on this day that a cellular phone he was using, exploded in his face while he was talking on it. The phone had recently been given him by his friend's uncle when his previous cellular had stopped working.

Yahya was known as "The Engineer" and was adulated by hundreds of thousands of people who admired not only his ingenuity but his courage to fight in the resistance for his homeland.

The Engineer was born in Rafat of the West Bank in 1966. Yahya studied chemical engineering at Bier Zeit University in the West Bank in the mid-80s. Those who were close to Yahya knew him to be a devout scholar of the Koran. In 1992 he was married and when he died, he had two sons, one newly born just before Yahya's death.

Until his assassination, Yahya was known to be a leader of the military wing of Hamas and was well known for his proficiency as a bomb maker. The Engineer did not come to the notice of Israeli authorities until the end of the Intifada, the Palestinian uprising at the end of 1987.

Over the following months, Yahya was involved in a number of bomb attacks. The first successful attack occurred in April 1994, when a suicide bomber blew himself up next to a bus in Afula, and Israeli town just north of the West Bank. Five suicide attacks then followed and Ayyash was also linked to a double suicide bombing near Natanya in early 1995 which killed 22 Israelis, mostly soldiers.

Since the last weeks of 1995, Hamas had promised the PA leader Yasser Arafat, not to launch any more military operations from the autonomous areas of the West Bank and Gaza Strip. And even though The Engineer had kept this promise, he was still on the most wanted list of the Israeli intelligence.

Even his enemies acknowledged that Yahya was not only courageous but very intelligent and proficient as well. He became a symbol to the Palestinians and lived a life of constant danger and excitement as well.

He was a larger than life hero and made a difference to his people. Though his enemies may have regarded him as a terrorist, his people will long remember him as a freedom fighter and a man who was brave enough to resist the cruel and ruthless occupation of his beloved country. There have been freedom fighters from the beginning of man's first injustice and oppression against his fellow man. And there have been heroes like Yahya who have inspired their fellow countrymen to carry on the battle long after their deaths.

Sir William Wallace, the Scottish hero against English oppression did more for Scots in death than he was able to do in life. His cruel execution by order of the English king, Longshanks, only served to inspire and finally

unite Scottish nobles and peasants to overthrow British rule under the newly crowned Robert the Bruce. Yet no one called Sir William a terrorist for he has gone down in history as a man who died for his ideals and fought to free his country from occupation. Yahya was no different. He died serving his country and died for the taste of freedom.

Hundreds of thousands turned out for Yahya's funeral. He was given a martyr's burial and will suffer the pain and agony of this world no longer.

There are thousands of Yahyas and the powder keg of tyranny will only produce a more determined resistance. The Palestinians are for the most part poor and starving. They have lost their land, their homes, their whole way of life. They have little else to lose and freedom is that cherished treasure that so many are willing to die for.

Yahya lives on in the minds and hearts of his people. There are heroes in the seaweed and there are children in the morning. They are leaning out for love, they will lean this way forever.

History is made by those who hang heroes. Yahya was a warrior-poet. He fought for the freedom of his country and one day those poor and starving Palestinians will regain their land, their dignity and their freedom!

Sheikh Yasin: Oslo Accords mysterious; Israel explains them according to its interests

Resistance should continue as long as there is occupation

Gaza- (Quds Press)- Interview by Abdel Ghani Shami

Sheikh Ahmed Yasin, the spiritual leader of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) said that the Oslo Accords were born dead and that the Palestinian People should adopt Jihad as the method of resistance to the occupation.

Sheikh Yasin described the negotiations with the Israelis as a "waste" of time. Israel deceives the world by these negotiations in order to make them believe that it wants peace, he said. The Islamic leader assailed the Palestinian Authority for the mass punishment it practices against its own people who had committed no crime and who have been arrested for their political affiliations.

Following is the full text of the interview made with Sheikh Yasin in his modest house in Gaza:

Q. What is your comment on the current developments in the Palestinian arena?

A. What is going on in the Palestinian arena is the inevitable result of the Oslo Accords. These Accords were mysterious and gave the Israelis an opportunity to explain their provisions in a way that serves the interests of the Israelis and in accordance with their power. Therefore, the settlements continued and the Israeli government turned its back to the world and did not honour the agreements it signed publicly. In addition, Israel continued the closures, which made the Palestinian People prisoners in a mass prison.

Because the Palestinian Authority vowed in the Oslo Accords to fight "terrorism", which means fighting anyone resisting the Israeli Occupation, the PLO turned from a basic enemy to Israel to an advocate in Israel's favour, although the Israeli government has not fulfilled the provisions of the Oslo Accords. Therefore, the PLO lost all their cards in confronting the Israelis who are known for not keeping their promises and pledges. The fact is that the PA punishes the Palestinian People and Hamas collectively, a thing which we strongly reject. Those who are arrested did not commit any crime. They are held only for their political affiliations.

Q. If the Israeli-PLO negotiations failed, what would be your alternative?

A. The Oslo Accords were born dead. Therefore, we still believe that the resistance of the occupation through the use of Jihad is the method that the Palestinian People should adopt. The present negotiations are a "waste" of time and deceive the whole world as they project Israel as a country that seeks peace even though it is piling up nuclear and chemical weapons to launch new wars against the Arab and Islamic Nation.

Q. Do you support the freezing of the armed operations against Israel?

A. I said that the resistance should continue as long as there is an occupation. But the Mujahideen can choose the appropriate time for their operations. This means that the freezing of the military activities against the Israeli Occupation should not be mentioned under the circumstances of our people in the Occupied Territories and in the Diaspora.

Q. How can the national unity be reinforced? And how do you assess your participation in the National Dialogue?

A. We are one people with one language and the majority of us embrace one religion. We all stand in one trench against one enemy that does not differentiate between a Palestinian and another. All Palestinians are targeted by the Israeli enemy. We also have one goal, which is to liberate the Palestinian territories and to repatriate the Palestinian People in Diaspora to their homeland. We should make these facts the basis for the unification of our ranks against the Israeli conspiracies that aim to sow the seeds of dispute among us.

As for our participation in the National Dialogue, it is continuous. But we hope that this dialogue is not ceremonial but real in order to protect the freedom of the Palestinian people and their right to their homeland.

Q. What is the position of Hamas concerning the current circumstances?

A. Hamas has a firm stand. It insists on continuing its Jihad against the occupation. It is willing to sacrifice until the occupation is removed and the Palestinian State is established on the whole Palestinian soil with Jerusalem as its capital. Hamas rejects the accords "Oslo" produced, which the international observers believe to be dead.

Israel, the United Nations, and Resolution 194

by Dr. Daud A. Abdullah*

This is a woeful tale of injustice, betrayal, and failure. Though unfinished, we are recalling it at this time because 11 December represents an important turning point in its chronology. For it was on this day 49 years ago that the United Nations General Assembly formally adopted Resolution No.194 (III) recognizing the Palestinians' 'right to return' to their homeland. Since that fateful day, 11 December 1948, the Assembly has reaffirmed this right on more than 100 occasions, only to be spurned and disregarded by Israel. Being the only state in the world which came into existence by the summons of the UN, and having been accepted into the world body on the condition that it implemented Resolution 194, one would have expected Israel to honor its treaty obligations and respect international law. Instead, it has by its defiance, undermined the credibility of the international body and destroyed its image among the community of nations.

Expulsion through terror

In reality, Resolution 194 was neither a gift nor a favor from the United Nations to the Palestinians. It was essentially the ratification of a natural right which imposed an indisputable duty upon the party that caused the problem. While Israeli leaders have denied whenever necessary their responsibility for the dispersal of the Palestinians, they did on certain occasions actually admit this responsibility. Staff Officer Yigal Yadin recalled that he prepared the nucleus of Plan Dalet in 1944. The key targets of the Plan according to Yadin were 'the main Arab villages.' Former Foreign Minister Moshe Sharett boasted that 'The most spectacular event in the contemporary history of Palestine - more spectacular in a sense than the creation of the Jewish state - is the wholesale evacuation of its Arab population.' Elsewhere he recalled that the 1948 war had given the Zionists the best opportunity to solve 'the most vexing problem of the Jewish state' by expelling the Palestinians. The disclosures of David Ben Gurion, Israel's first prime minister was no less significant. As early as 1937 he wrote to his son informing him that when the Jewish state was created, 'We will expel the Arabs and take their places.' Ten years later he instructed that 'In each attack, a decisive blow should be struck, resulting in the destruction of homes and the expulsion of the population.'

Is there any need for further evidence to dispel the pseudo-academic arguments that the Palestinian exodus was caused by an 'irrational panic syndrome' and 'loss of community infra-structure'? Would it not be an insult to history and the United Nations itself to deny that the Palestinians only left their towns and villages after they were invaded by Zionist terrorist groups which subjected them to a reign of terror? The truth of the matter as corroborated from the above quotations is that the majority of Palestinians were forced to abandon their lands, property and homes as a result of armed aggression. They were not, however, spontaneous or haphazard attacks but instead the brutal enactment of an ideology aimed at creating maximum living space for the Jews. Hence the destruction and depopulation of over 400 villages between 1947 and 1949.

The right of return and self-determination

The existence of the majority of Palestinian people in exile since 1948 is a direct result of their inability to exercise their 'right to return.' At the same time, the Israelis' refusal to comply with UN Resolution 194 throughout the period has apparently stemmed not from a conviction that it is wrong or illegal, but rather because they fear the consequences of its implementation. They know very well that the 'right to return' in the case of the Palestinians bears with it certain civil as well as political obligations. For whether it be enforced individually or collectively the Resolution recognizes, in a civil sense, their right to ownership and compensation for their property and suffering. Likewise, from a political point of view, it upholds their national right to self-determination.

Notwithstanding Israel's obstinacy, the General Assembly has since the early 1970s adopted a number of resolutions acknowledging and underscoring the link between the Palestinian 'right to return' and self-determination. Resolution No. 3089 of 7 December 1973 declares that: 'full respect for and realization of the inalienable rights of the people of Palestine, particularly its right to self-determination, are indispensable for the establishment of a just and lasting peace in the Middle East, and that the enjoyment by the Palestine Arab refugees of their right to return to their homes and property, recognized by the General Assembly in resolution 194 (III) of 11 December 1948, which has been repeatedly reaffirmed by the General Assembly since that date, is indispensable for the achievement of a just settlement of the refugee problem and for the exercise by the people of Palestine of its right to self-determination.' These same principles were subsequently upheld in the preamble of resolution No.31/20 which was adopted on 24 November 1976. A few years later, 10 December 1981, the Assembly went even further and approved resolution No. 36/120 reaffirming its 'rejection of those provisions of the [Camp David] Accords which ignore or deny the inalienable rights of the Palestinian people, including the right of return, the right of self-determination and the right to national independence and sovereignty in Palestine...' Since 1981 only two members of the UN, Israel and the US, have voiced their opposition to this resolution whenever it came up for renewal.

Admittedly, Israel's determination to prolong the Palestinian refugee problem was not confined to its non-compliance with Resolution 194. Other treaty obligations were similarly breached. Hence, even after signing the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights on 19 December 1966, the Israelis stubbornly refused to ratify the document until 3 October 1991. One may naturally ask, why on earth did it take the Jewish state 25 years to ratify the Covenant? The answer is quite simple. It wanted to evade article 12 of the document which stipulated that : 'Everyone shall be free to leave any country, including his own...No one shall be arbitrarily deprived the right to enter his own country.' In this same context some observers pointed out that Israel apparently delayed its ratification in order to avoid responsibility for the estimated 300,000 Palestinians and their descendants who were expelled in 1967. In this fierce world of international relations motives seldom count for anything. For it is here, perhaps more than in any other sphere, 'actions speak louder than words.' Thus, Israel's refusal to fulfill its duties toward the refugees after ratifying the Covenant in 1991 establishes beyond any doubt a flagrant breach of its international obligations.

Three claims

Yet another question arises. Why has this situation persisted even after the solemn proclamations at Madrid? In a real sense certain sections of the international community appear equally culpable in preserving the status quo. They have contributed to this by overlooking the fact that the same ideological considerations which led to the expulsion of the Palestinians in 1948 and 1967 are the same doctrines which today deny them the right of return. And by so doing, they made it not only easy but even necessary to reward the likes of Shimon Peres with the Noble Award for peace. This was clearly his reward for denying Israeli responsibility for the refugee problem which he claimed 'can not be resolved.'

Throughout the past 50 years Israel's policy toward the refugees was founded upon three premises. Firstly, that they were not responsible for the problem. Secondly, that there was a 'population exchange' in which Jews from the Arab lands were settled in Israel, and thirdly that the Arabs would not live in peace with their Jewish neighbors if they returned. Having already dispelled the first argument we may briefly consider the other two.

To begin with, the majority of Jews who migrated from Arab lands did not participate in the 1948 war. Similarly, they were not expelled from these lands as was the case of the Palestinians from their ancestral land. And, furthermore, there was actually no 'exchange' because the Palestinian Arabs did not go to countries like Iraq and Yemen from where most of the Jews came. The vast majority of them remained in the West Bank, Gaza or Jordan. Shimon Peres' attempt therefore to compare Israel's 'generous' acceptance of Jewish 'refugees' from the Arab world can not be matched with the refusal of the Arab states to naturalize the Palestinians; a policy he criticizes as being unnatural for people of the same race. In effect, the two situations can not be equated because the rights of the Palestinian people is one matter and the obligations of Arab states to Israel is fundamentally another.

The third aspect of Israeli policy has been based on the condition stipulated in Resolution 194; that the returning refugees should live in peace with their neighbors. Their interpretation of this has not changed since the days of Ben Gurion. He told the UN Conciliation Commission for Palestine in 1949 that; 'The government of Israel believes that there is a strong and definite link between the resolution of this problem and the resolution of the suspended issues between Israel and the Arab states; and that it is not possible to accomplish this except after peace has been restored to the Middle East.' While this rationalization may appeal to those who blindly support Israel, it certainly would not stand the test of impartial reasoning. For the instability and turmoil that has gripped the region since 1948 was not caused by the refugees themselves, but was rather the result of the denial of their national rights.

Conclusion

Several factors have combined to make the Palestinian refugee problem really unique. There exists today a wide gap between their geographic dispersal on the one hand, and their legal unity on the other. Their expulsion and continued exodus to every corner of the globe has resulted neither in the loss of their national identity nor assimilation in their places of refuge. Of course, it is practically impossible for them to exercise their national right of self-determination without first realizing their right to return. The UN General Assembly has, therefore, recognized this principle on numerous occasions and has, indeed, enacted it with respect to the refugees from the war in the former Yugoslavia. After fifty years of exile, torment, and deprivation the Palestinians' right to return would seem all the more obligatory since it has been proven that Israel was founded on the falsification of history and the claim that 'Palestine was a land without a people.' Besides, it should not be forgotten by the powers that be that the Palestinian 'right to return' was not only legally tied to the birth of Israel, but even its admission into the United Nations.

Surely the physical existence of the Palestinian people is a fact that can neither be disproved nor ignored. The denial of their right to exercise sovereignty over their land by Israel will not, in any way, change this reality. True, there may be differences of estimates concerning the numbers of Palestinians in their places of refuge. Yet, there is absolutely no doubt that the overwhelming majority of the Palestinians live outside of their ancestral land in exile. Israel's claim that the Palestinian exodus took place too long ago and it would be impracticable to allow their return is a non-starter. The fact is, the wrong done to the Palestinians is so grave that the passage of time does not rectify it. And since they, the victims, have neither abandoned, forgotten nor surrendered their right to return, it is the legal duty of Israel and the member states of the UN to respect and guarantee this right. No amount of pious excuses and protestations shall absolve them from the collective guilt of betrayal and injustice to the Palestinians.

Finally with specific reference to the UN, its adoption of Resolution 194 in 1948 seemed in every respect a recognition of its accomplicity in the offense. Besides, its display of unlimited tolerance with Israeli transgressions has not gone unnoticed; especially when it hastens to punish and reprimand other regional culprits. The need for Arab unity and cooperation can not, therefore, be over-emphasized. When all has been said and done, are we still to assume that the future return of the Palestinians depends entirely upon their acquisition of powerful friends in the Security Council?

 

* The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre - London and editor of the Return Review

Is Netanyahu's Downfall Imminent?

by Khalid Amayreh

After only 16 months in office, Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu has probably scored the highest number of crises and scandals than any other Israeli premier in such a short period of time. The list is long and impressive. There was the tunnel crisis, the Bar-On affair, the Azzam-Azzam affair, the Misha'al affair, the debacles in Southern Lebanon (which resulted in the killing of over 100 Israeli soldiers this year) and, now, the brewing crisis besetting his leadership and endangering the delicate internal unity of the Likud and, not unlikely, the survival of the ruling coalition itself, a heterogeneous federation of right-wing and religious parties headed by Netanyahu.

The latest crisis within the Likud, as nearly always, is basically rooted in the largely personal rivalry and bad chemistry between Netanyahu (often described as a mean-spirited and conspiratorial-minded character who would seek to outsmart his opponents rather than co-opt them to his side) and prominent Likud figures such as Benyamin Begin, Dan Merridor, Yisrael Katz and, to a lesser extent, Limor Livnat.

Added to this is the chronic "confidence crisis" with Foreign Minister David Levy, leader of the Gesher party whose constituency is largely made up of Jews from North Africa and other "oriental Jews" known as the "Mizrahim." In fact, it has become all too well known recently that Netanyahu's relationship with Levy is more of "a fragile modus vivendi" rather than of "a coordinated teamwork operation." Last month, Levy reportedly blamed Netanyahu for causing him to have a minor cardiac attack after he had become utterly fed up with ignoring and side-stepping matters that fall squarely under the responsibility of the Foreign Ministry.

Earlier this month, the internal tensions in the Likud resurfaced. More than 2,700 delegate members, who met at the Likud headquarters in Tel Aiv on 10 November, shouted down and humiliated the Likud leadership, particularly Prime Minister Netanyahu and his closest adviser, Avigodor Lieberman. The acrimonious convention, fraught with mutual recrimination and invective accusations, centred on the issue of the "Likud primaries", which Netanyahu and supporters wanted to be scrapped, or at least indefinitely postponed.

Jeered, whistled and booed at by the rowdy delegates, Netanyahu couldn't even complete his speech before his own party's convention.

The delegates, worried that the Prime Minister was plotting to concentrate power in his and his aides' hands, lambasted him for "trying to be despotic within the party."

Yisrael Katz, the rebels' candidate for Chairman of the Likud Central Committee, received a stormy applause from the audience when he told Netanyahu that "all we want is for you to hear us out. These delegates are the Likud. They put you where you are today. Don't prevent them from making decisions as they see fit."

With the party at a loss, and trying to limit the damage, Netanyahu declared that the meeting was "over" and that a resumption of the discussion over the primaries would take place at a later date.

However, the evasive ruse, which seemed somewhat expedient, later proved ineffective. The rebels continued their campaign against Netanyahu and even succeeded in gaining new important converts such as Tel Aviv Mayor Roni Millo and Jerusalem mayor Ehud Olmert, who both reportedly threatened to form their own break-away party.

As the implosion in the Likud continued with heightened ferocity, Avigodor Lieberman, Director-General of the Prime Minister's Office and one of the most controversial pivotal figures in the Netanyahu administration (also the source of indignation for the rebels' camp) suddenly announced his resignation on 23 November.

Netanyahu expressed "great sorrow" at Lieberman's resignation, while the opposition viewed it as "another nail in the Netanyahu-government coffin."

The next day, 24 November, Lieberman, in a much-publicized news conference in West Jerusalem, lashed out at Netanyahu's opponents within the Likud, accusing them of "having caused the failure of the party in the 1992 election and of trying to bring down the Government now."

"What happened here while the Prime Minister was out of the country was too reminiscent of countries in South America and South Africa. Anyone who considers himself a worthy alternative to the Prime Minister can challenge him openly in the primaries, but not knife him in the back," he said.

Lieberman called Netanyahu's opponents "ring-leaders" who were trying to topple the Government and terminate Likud rule in Israel "in connivance with the Shabak and Jebril Rajoub."

But Lieberman's vociferous offense soon turned into a defense. Only a few hours after he delivered his speech, the police recommended that he be indicted for theft, fraud and embezzlement.

According to Israeli sources, Lieberman, who had once been accused of being a KGB agent, reportedly didn't pay NIS 80,000 of a NIS 110,000 he had borrowed from an organization called "Gesher Aliya," active in attracting Jewish immigrants to Israel from the former Soviet Union.

It was not clear if the charges were genuine, or if they were, as Lieberman claimed, concocted by his political enemies for the purpose of "destroying him and further undermining the Prime Minister."

Undoubtedly, the resignation and possible indictment of Lieberman will hurt and harm Netanyahu, at least for the time being. But the fall-out should not be overestimated. In fact, Netanyahu may now feel relieved, having, almost at no price, got rid of a close friend and ally, who had become a Likud liability accused by the rebels and many other Likudniks of being the source of discord and potential disintegration within the party.

Is the storm over now? Not by any means. The rebels will likely continue to "narrow Netanyahu's horizons" until he completely succumbs to their demands. However, they are not going to exert "too much pressure" on the Prime Minister, e.g. the kind of pressure that would force him to contemplate forming a national unity Government with the Labor party.

This prospect (pushing Netanyahu towards Labor) is anathema not only for the Likud refuseniks but also for the leaders of Gush Emunim, represented in the Knesset and the Government by the National Religious Party, the Mifdal.

Those leaders, who are themselves part of the so-called "Land of Israel Front", have been making threats that they would bring about the downfall of the Netanyahu Government if he went ahead with his proposal for carrying out Israeli army redeployment from 6%-8% of the West Bank.

However, as many pundits here argue, the threats are not all sincere, and this is what the chairman of the Likud Bloc in the Knesset alluded to on the weekend.

He said, "If they succeeded in bringing down the Government, they know all too well that the next Government would offer more concessions to the Palestinians."

Doesn't this make "blackmail" the real title of the crisis?

U.S. Mideast policy- from failure to failure

by Munir Shafeeq*

There's an Arab and international near-consensus holding the U.S. Government as the first culprit for the breakdown that has beset the 'peace process' recently.

Total bias by the Clinton administration in favour of Israeli policies under both the Labor and the Likud governments have left it semi-isolated on the official Arab level. Other great powers, especially France and Russia, seized the opening to play ever-growing roles in the very region that U.S. foreign policy always wanted to set apart solely by and for itself.

But recent developments have shockingly demonstrated that even when the U.S. throws all of its weight behind it, a Mideast settlement purely on Israeli terms is NOT a feasible proposition.

Moreover, recent developments have shown that the American role in the region has begun to shrink, and that U.S. prestige has begun to break down.

Three fine examples of recent American foreign policy blunders in the region illustrate the point:

First, there's the dismal lack of success of the State Department in harnessing meaningful official Arab participation in Ad-Doha's regional economic conference. Instead, all it got from the larger Arab states such as Saudi Arabia, and especially from Egypt and Morocco, was a big NO. Yet that only made Albright stand shaking behind the podium in Ad-Doha without finding anything to say except blazing Iraq! And that was an act bereft of courtesy vis-¦-vis the Qatari hosts as well as the few other Arab delegations participating in the convention. Then, very shortly after her speech, Albright deserted the convention which she had before braced herself to lead.

The second foreign policy failure was the collective official Arab refusal last month to partake in the U.S. administration's plan to deal Iraq a devastating military blow.. Subsequently, U.S. diplomacy retreated before and grudgingly accepted a Russian initiative to resolve the festering crisis with Iraq. That was a retreat that can't be diluted by the fact that the [Russian] initiative incorporated the American condition of a full return of U.N. inspectors to work, because the U.S. really wanted to resolve the crisis differently, i.e., by force with official Arab blessing. Neither does it make the U.S. happy to see Russian diplomacy snatch the initiative, or for Russia and France to set the Iraqi issue on a different track from that which was previously imposed by the governments of the U.S. and the U.K.

But the third foreign policy blunder is perhaps the cruelest. It stems from the extraordinary success of the Islamic Summit in Tehran, Iran, regarding both the resolutions adopted and the reconciliations, or dual agreements, that took place behind the scenes there. That failure was further highlighted by the stark contrast between the relative positions of the U.S. administration and the Iranian leadership in the week during which the summit took place: The U.S. administration seemed deprived of its closest allies in the Islamic world while 54 Islamic states crowded all around Iran in an atmosphere of friendship.

Thus the success of the Islamic Summit in Tehran simply put the U.S. administration in an unenviable position as it lost the initiative while the momentum began to turn against it. Additionally, that may carry the probable consequence of encouraging other hitherto pliant States to defend their interests and to seek a more just world order, instead of submitting meekly to an unjust Zionist world order that is run by a U.S. administration that thrives on arrogance and wallows in double-standards. That should be good news, even to Iraq as well as Libya, Syria and Sudan.

Undoubtedly the U.S. administration is busy right now trying to extract lessons from and to contain the consequences of the three aforementioned failures. Several statements made by Clinton a couple of weeks ago hinted that these recent U.S. foreign policy failures may be attributable to the stalemate in the 'peace process'. Some analysts henceforth interpreted Clinton's statements as indicative of dissatisfaction with Netanyahu's policies, thus rendering them their due share of blame for the official Arabian scattering away from the American line on the Middle East, and for the growing Iranian, Russian and French roles in a traditionally American dominion.

Nevertheless, if we assumed that the logical conclusion of Clinton's statements will manifest itself in a serious and credible attempt to distance American from Israeli positions, we have to first figure out how Clinton may pull off such a policy shift while remaining a powerless prisoner in the grip of the Zionist lobby in his National Security Council, his administration, Congress, and the Media!! We also can't forget the Attorney-General and the big file full of scandals he's got on the Clintons.

Yet, and in spite of all that, if Clinton doesn't make a significant foreign policy change, his administration will continue to leap swiftly from one Mideast failure to another.

 

* A Palestinian writer and thinker and former director of the PLO Planning Department.

Mideast: Misleading Promises

by Ruba Farkh

About seven years after the start of the peace process in Madrid, it seems that the only thing gained by the people involved was false promises. Promises of prosperity, independence and a flourishing economy turned out to be mere propaganda. No one received anything tangible. On the contrary, this propaganda caused severe damage to the economy and even to the environment since all it really did was lead the people to believe that the coming years would witness an unprecedented demand for land, busy tourism seasons, the establishment of many investment projects and the opening of larger markets. But on the ground, none of these took place, at least not in the sense that the people were prepared to handle.

Initially, land prices in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank went sky high. As fast as they went up, they came down, and not even to a level where investors could recover their debts by selling their precious land leaving many with dashed hopes of a dream not realized. Heavy construction works took place, and in many cases turned out to barely cover the expenses. In Bethlehem, shop and hotel owners were deeply frustrated last Christmas since all the tourists were arranged by Israeli tourist companies and most were staying in Tel Aviv hotels; this is just the opposite of what they were expecting and what they had prepared for.

In Jordan, it was the same story all over again. Many factories that had been established based upon the promises of new markets soon closed down after being unable to export their products to Palestinian and Israeli markets.

In response to the predictions of increased tourism, many hotels were built in Amman, Aqaba and Petra. The small number of tourists resulted in some of these establishments facing the difficult task of even covering their expenses. In another instance, one alarming consequence of the false promises was caused by a Jordanian while attempting to prepare a piece of his land to attract tourists. In the 1997 summer issue of "Environews", a UNDP environment news update, an article entitled "An eye on the environment" pointed out that "with the expected boom in the tourism sector, Jordan has started promoting investment on the eastern shore of the Dead Sea. This development scheme, which could bring economic benefits for Jordan, can also bring threats to the special environment of that area, the first of which is the diversion of the course of the Zara waterfall. This thermal waterfall originates from a 60C spring, as well as some 50 other thermal springs. An investor in that area has diverted the course of that waterfall, cut all the native trees and planted exotic flowers to construct a recreational spa without conducting any environmental assessment studies on the consequences of the project."

As the promises continue, so will the damage to all aspects of life, since one can never tell how the investor or the layman will react in response to misleading promises, especially when no constraints are made to control reactions.

In the summer of 1968, daily television broadcasts of the war in Vietnam helped turn American public opinion around from overwhelming support of the war to massive opposition.

The intifadah, however, was different.

Everyday, Americans turned on their television sets to witness heavily armed Israelis firing live ammunition at women and children civilians who only carried stones.

Unlike in Israel, American forces are prohibited from firing on civilians, even when the civilians throw stones. The one exception was the murder of four students at Kent State University by the Ohio National Guard, which was immediately condemned!

Using rocks found strewn in their streets, Palestinians confronted the soldiers and pressed their demands for liberation and independence.

Daily images of a Palestinian child courageously standing in the line of fire of an Israeli soldier while throwing a stone were permanently etched in the American conscience.

The Intifadah did in weeks what the "Revolution" had failed to do in 20 years.

Americans quickly saw parallels between the Intifadah and the Biblical confrontation between David, armed only with a slingshot and a stone, and his oppressor, a heavily armed behemoth named Goliath.

Americans did not like the idea that their taxes were paying for the armour and the weapons that Goliath used against the young David.

While the Intifadah succeeded in destroying Israel's once "heroic" image in the United States and in other countries, the failure of Arab Americans to support the Intifadah allowed Israel to counter the damage.

Let us commemorate the successes of the Intifadah.

But let us not forget the shortfalls that prevented it from winning ultimate Palestinian independence.

Let us enjoy this Ramadan, help others and ourselves and bring back the true meaning and spirit of Ramadan.

There was no answer but the sound and smell of nature, his sole companion. Peace On Earth

by:Erik Paul

It's Christmas!!! There is magic in the air. You can hear bells ringing and children singing Christmas carols. The true meaning of Christmas is to spread peace and joy to all parts of the world. In some homes, there will be no joy and little peace and no warm fireplace nor gifts under the tree.

Think twice this season as you have a MERRY CHRISTMAS and a HAPPY NEW YEAR!!!

The politics of Islamophobia

By Yaqub Zaki

(2- 3)

Dickens' outburst of Islamophobia was mild compared to what was being expressed on the spot by British commanders. Brigadier Nicholson said: "Let us propose a Bill for the flaying alive, impalement, or burning of the murderers of the women and children at Delhi. The idea of simply hanging the perpetrators of such atrocities is maddening. I wish I were in that part of the world [he was at that moment in the Punjab] that if necessary I might take the law into my own hands."

As a matter of fact, a Bill was not necessary, and Nicholson headed a siege-train from the Punjab to besiege Delhi. There followed a massacre on such a scale as to stand in comparison with the sack of Baghdad by the Mongols. What happened is best described in the words of British eyewitnesses. Mrs. Saunders said: "For several days after the assault every native that could be found was killed by the soldiers­women and children were spared." The lucky ones were shot. Here is another eyewitness; Lieut. Majendie describes a Muslim being burned alive: " the horrible smell of his burning flesh as it crackled and blackened in the flames, rising up and poisoning the air­so in this 19th century, with its boasted civilization and humanity, a human being should lie roasting and consuming to death while Englishmen and Sikhs, gathered in little knots around, looked calmly on. No one will deny, I think, that this man at least, adequately expiated by his frightful and cruel death, any crimes he may have been guilty of." All this, note, was in 1857, when the agitation for the abolition of the slave trade was at its height, when the sanctimonious Wilberforce was spouting in Parliament against the inequity of the trade and Mrs. Fry was worried about conditions in British prisons. So when the Jews demand an apology from Germany for the Holocaust, why don't we demand an apology from Britain for this?

Although Nicholson fell at the Kashmir Gate thereby entering the annals of imperial hagiography, he needn't have worried; without benefit of a Bill all his proposals were taken up and executed­no pun intended­to the letter: hanging, burning alive and impalement. Many people languish under a misapprehension, thinking that impalement consists in having a stake thrust through your midriff from the back. Nothing of the kind! The stake is thrust up through the rectum in such a way as to bypass all the vital organs until it emerges at the shoulder. It is then planted upright in the ground. A letter home from a British officer asks: "How much longer are we to be outraged by the sight of writhing humanity on stakes?" Beheading was also resorted to. Apart from burning alive and merely being hanged, there were other methods. The phrase in Lawrence's directive, to be blown away from guns, calls for amplification. Here is what it is like to be fired from a cannon, described by Mrs. Coopland in her own immortal narrative, A Lady's Escape from Gwalior: "Many prisoners were hanged after the battle, and as it was discovered they did not care for hanging, four were tried and sentenced to be blown away from guns; accordingly, one day we were startled by hearing a gun go off, with an indescribably muffled sound . An officer told us it was a most sickening sight.One gun was overcharged, and the poor wretch was literally blown into atoms, the lookers-on being covered with blood and fragments of flesh: the head of one poor wretch fell upon a bystander and hurt him."

Speaking of one mutinous regiment, the 55th, that other figure of imperial martyrology, the hero of many a Victorian history book, Sir John Lawrence, records his humanity in the following terms:

" they were taken fighting against us, and so far deserve little mercy. But, on full reflection, I would not put them all to death. I do not think we would be justified in the eyes of the Almighty in doing so, and 120 men are a large number to be put to death. Our object is to make an example to terrify others. I think this object would be effectively gained by putting to death one fourth to one third of them. I would select all those against whom anything bad can be shown­such as general bad character, turbulence, prominence in disaffection in the fight, disrespectful demeanour to their officers during the few days before the 26th and the like. If these did not make up the required number, I would then add to them the oldest soldiers [note the utilitarian sentiment, right on the heels of the Evangelical sentiment]. All these would be shot or blown away from guns, as may be most expedient. The rest I would divide into batches: some to be imprisoned ten years, some seven, some five, some three."

To the unsystematic pillaging of drink-maddened soldiers there succeeded the systematic pillage of the prize-agents. They dug up the floors of the houses in search of buried treasure. Delhi, the glory of Mughal civilization, was depopulated. Such of the inhabitants as survived the sack were driven out into the countryside, whilst proposals were debated for the city's future. There was a demand that it should be razed to the ground, like Carthage­echo, or, rather pre-echo, of Peregine Worthsthorne. To the proponents, it does not seem to have occurred or mattered that Delhi contained some of the finest specimens of architecture in the world. Another proposal was that only the mosques be demolished. Delhi fell in September. Only in January, 1858, did people begin to trickle back; at first only Hindus were allowed to return. "The purpose of this business," Lord Roberts said, "is to show those rascally Musulmans that, with God's help, Englishmen will still be masters of India."

Roberts, who was at that time a subaltern, wrote home to his sister: "You must not think, darling Harriet, that I pity the sepoys or blackguards who are rebelling against us. On the contrary, few are more unrelenting than I am. When a prisoner is brought in, I am the first to call out to have him hanged." Another reports with zest: "I spent that night on picket at the Masjid above-mentioned, much of our time being passed in shooting or hanging prisoners taken during the day . Many a poor wretch breathed his last at this spot, dying, for the most part, with a calmness and courage worthy of a better cause." On 23 September, exactly a week after the fall of Delhi, Mrs. Coopland, already quoted, writes with unction that the provost marshal had put to death "between 400 and 500 wretches since the siege. The soldiers, inured to sights of horror, and inveterate against the sepoys, were said to have bribed the executioner to keep them a long time hanging, as they liked to see the criminals dance a 'Pandies' hornpipe', as they termed the dying struggles of the wretches." Her host, one Captain Garstin, had just come from the hanging of the Nawab of Jhujjur and remarked that "he was a long time dying."

General Neill's instructions were specific: "Certain guilty villages were marked out for destruction, and all men inhabiting them to be slaughtered. All sepoys of mutinous regiments not giving a good account of themselves were to be hanged. The town of Futtehpore, which had revolted, was to be attacked and the Pathan quarters destroyed with all their inhabitants. All heads of insurgents, particularly at Futtehpore, to be hanged. If the Deputy Collector is taken, hang him and have his head cut off and stuck upon one of the principal (Mohammedan) buildings of the town." Women and children were burnt to death in the villages torched by Neill's column as it advanced on Cawnpore.

After the people, it was the turn of the buildings, particularly the mosques. Although Delhi was not in the end razed to the ground, an entire quarter, that containing the residences of the Mughal nobility, was razed. The Jami' Masjid had a narrow escape. First, it was to be demolished, but it was too valuable as a Sikh barracks and stabling for that. Next Egerton, the City Magistrate, proposed that it be converted into a church, with each floor slab inscribed with "the name of one of our Christian martyrs."

Finally it was sold to a Hindu, and from hiwas bought back by the Muslim community five years later. Other mosques were not so lucky. The Akbarabadi Mosque, where Shah Waliullah had taught, was demolished. The Fatehpuri Mosque was used as shops and offices for 20 years. The Zinat al-Masajid, built by Aurangzebe's daughter, might have remained a bakery for good had it not caught the eye of Lord Curzon about 1900. We should be grateful to Lord Curzon: after 50 years of so-called independence, three Islamic monuments in Pakistan are still Christian churches.

I don't need to recount the story of the capture of Bahadur Shah's sons by Hodson at Humayun's tomb, or their murder. What is less well known to my audience, I am sure, is that the scene of the exploit­they were proud of it­is depicted on a marble relief on the huge cenotaph the Anglican authorities erected to Hodson's memory at Lichfield Cathedral. Bahadur Shah's sons were stripped naked first to humiliate them. Their bodies were displayed on the stone terrace in front of the Kotwali for three days; they were then thrown into the river. "In 24 hours," Hodson gloats in a letter to his wife, "I disposed of the principal members of the house of Timur the Tartar. I am not cruel, but I confess I did rejoice at the opportunity of ridding the earth of these wretches. I intended to have them hung (sic.), but when it came to a question of 'they' or 'we', I had no time for deliberation." Twenty-one royal princes were hanged a few days later. 1,400 residents were marched to the Yamuna, where they were beheaded and their bodies cast into the river. Some women jumped into wells to escape dishonour; others were killed by their husbands before they slew themselves. Montgomery Martin, in a letter to the Bombay Telegraph, attests that "All the city people found within the walls when our troops entered were bayoneted on the spot; and the number was considerable, as you may suppose, when I tell you that in some houses 40 or 50 people were hiding. These were not mutineers, but residents of the city, who trusted to our well-known mild rule for pardon. I am glad to say they were disappointed." Kaye wrote: "Eight dead carts daily went their rounds from sunrise to sunset to take down the corpses, which hung at the crossroads and the market-places." He estimated that 6,000 people had been so disposed of. Wilfred Scawen Blunt, during a visit in 1883, was informed by the Chieftan of Loharo that about 26,000 were killed by soldiers, or hanged or blown away from guns during the eight months following the fall of the city. It is unlikely that these figures are exaggerated. Greathed said, "The sacrifice of 500 villainous lives for the murder of two English is a retribution that will be remembered." Hundreds of Qur'ans were burned; the Ulama' were hanged. The anti-Islamic frenzy in India was if anything exceeded by the anti-Islamic frenzy of the British Press, which surpassed itself in calling for merciless reprisals. Government by gallows lasted right into the following year.

What are we to make of all this? First, that Islamophobia is endemic in the European psyche, but periodically it becomes epidemic. It, I dare to hazard a guess, may be even more virulent this time round, for now Europe is united, whereas in the 19th century it was divided. This much can be deduced from the way in which Britain dragged a reluctant Germany in her wake over the Bosnian War when Britain held the chairmanship of the EC.

Injustice: cause and effect

Most people in the world have a basic understanding of the scientific terms "cause" and "effect". Logically, if something occurs, there will be an effect. For example, if a stranger walks up to you in the street, and he slapped your face, then he caused what will happen next. There are a variety of possibilities for what could follow. You could walk away and forgive the man. Or, you could tell the police and take him to court. Or, you could hit him back. Most humans have a natural defense system that will act upon itself as a reflex to the event, just as when a doctor taps one's knee and his leg kicks out. So, most of us would probably hit the man back for hitting us. Thus, we would have given the effect.
