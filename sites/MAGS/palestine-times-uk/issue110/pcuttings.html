Press Cuttings

Ha’aretz, 21 July 2000

One of the most unpleasant inconveniences facing political leaders is the fact that they have nations to govern - nations with aspirations, entities that also have the final say. (Israeli) Prime Minister Ehud Barak and Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat might have been able to work things out between themselves if they had been alone. But, nations, for some stupid reason, want their leaders, like their basketball teams, to come home with the trophy - no excuses and no mercy for the losers.

At one moment of truth, Barak said to Arafat, "If we don't finish the job now, at the next meeting, I will no longer be prime minister." Whereupon, Arafat replied: "If I give in on Jerusalem, I will be killed and then you will have to negotiate with [Hamas leader Sheikh] Ahmed Yassin."

The Guardian, 21 July 2000

The Arafat-Barak talks are focusing on ideas first formulated by negotiators Yossi Beilin and Abu Mazen back in 1995. Involving land swaps, expanded city boundaries, flagpoles and local autonomy in east Jerusalem, they provide the trappings and symbols but not the old-fashioned, fully fledged sovereignty to which Mr Arafat aspires. In return for this and other compromises, he is offered Israeli recognition of his state-to-be. Mr Barak digs in; Mr Arafat hesitates.

This is the hinge upon which Camp David's door to peace now swings open, now shut. On this, the whole package rests. It must not be allowed to collapse.

The Chicago Tribune, 20 July 2000

The deadlock in the Camp David summit followed weeks of Palestinian demonstrations and Israeli political uproar that reminded Palestinian President Yasser Arafat and Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak, time and time again, that they had little room to compromise on Jerusalem and other issues dear to their peoples' hearts.

Even before President Clinton departed for Japan without a deal, Palestinian protesters wound through the streets of this Arab town north of Jerusalem Wednesday, giving a hint of the pressure on Arafat and how some of his people would react with relief and others with frustration and anger to any major development.

As the opposition leftists paraded past, Ashraf Hamdan scowled and turned his back on the 50 or so demonstrators, most of them young boys, who were shouting, "Ya, Arafat, stop surrendering to the Israelis!"

The Financial Times, 20 July 2000

The (Camp David) summit met with wide skepticism from the start, with both sides lowering expectations of a historic breakthrough, which would include agreement on the fate of the 3.6m Palestinian refugees, the borders of a Palestinian state and the future of Jewish settlements.

But with both Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak and Palestinian Leader Yasser Arafat politically weak, neither leader can afford to go home empty-handed.

If in the coming days they fail to overcome their differences, they must map out a strategy that limits the damage of failure and maintains the momentum for peace.

Remaining elements of previous peace agreements, including a third Israeli redeployment from the West Bank, must be implemented to prevent Palestinians from falling into despair.

The Chicago Tribune, 14 July 2000

Amid the shroud of secrecy the Clinton administration has placed around the Mideast peace talks going on at Camp David, information--like water in a leaky hose--is finding unpredictable ways of getting out.

"Welcome to the daily press blackout," State Department spokesman Richard Boucher said Thursday as he kicked off a daily briefing on the peace summit devoid of any substantive detail.

..The Camp David news blackout stems from a real belief that day-to-day press coverage that exposed negotiating positions or proposals could scuttle the entire summit by roiling public opinion in Israel and the Palestinian territories.

Speculation in the Israeli news media prior to Barak's departure led to a rupture that nearly toppled his government.

The Independent, 11 July 2000

Ehud Barak claimed to have a mandate for peace from the Israeli public yesterday, putting a brave face on his government's collapse, and defiantly prepared to try to make history with Yasser Arafat, the Palestinian leader, at a Camp David summit.

But, to add insult to political injury, the Israeli premier was forced to delay his departure for Washington in order to defend himself against two humiliating motions of no confidence at a stormy session of parliament, which he narrowly survived.

The political hiatus in Israel meant that he is entering eight days - and possibly longer - of potentially historic negotiations at President Clinton's country retreat without a foreign minister, or an interior minister, and with only a third of the Knesset behind him.
