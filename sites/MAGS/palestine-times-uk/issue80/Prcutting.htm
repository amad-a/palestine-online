Yasser Arafat at the US Holocaust Memorial Museum? That was the brainstorm of Dennis Ross and Aaron Miller, the State Department's top Middle East negotiators. They wanted Arafat to visit America's foremost memorial to the victims of the Nazis in the belief, The Washington Post reported, that it would help him ''understand the fears of an adversary he has experienced primarily as a regional superpower.'' Arafat agreed to go - if the museum rolled out the red carpet and welcomed him as a head of state. But the Palestinian Authority that Arafat rules is not a state, and the museum declined to treat him as more than a private visitor, albeit one who would be ''received with the greatest courtesy.'' In a huff, Arafat said he wouldn't go. The Boston Globe, January 22, 1998

 

An Israeli court ruled Wednesday that a Jewish ultranationalist accused of plotting to throw a pig's head onto one of Islam's holiest sites be released to house arrest pending trial, a court official said. Avigdor Eskin, arrested several times in the past on suspicion of incitement, was ordered to post $143,000 bail, the official said. Jerusalem police have said the alleged plan to throw the pig's head at an area of Muslim shrines in the holy city's Temple Mount complex (Al Aqsa Mosque) would have sparked widespread bloodshed. The Chicago Tribune, January 22, 1998

 

Amid the rocks of the Wadi Selouki (south of Lebanon) lie three dead men. Rajeh Aidi, Samer Balluli and Saleh al-Jadaa have been there since 24 October, the day when their Islamic Jihad guerrilla operation against Israeli occupation troops went fatally wrong. All three - Aidi was Lebanese, the other two Palestinian were shot dead in a gun battle and within hours, the United Nations and the International Red Cross asked the Israelis for permission to retrieve the bodies for burial. Despite repeated requests, the UN says it never even received a reply. The Red Cross confirms it was refused permission to collect the corpses for "security" reasons. The Independent, January 24, 1998

 

The Clinton administration pressed Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat yesterday to accept an American-drafted package of security measures to halt political violence against Israel, and President Clinton committed in return to insist that Israel hand over at least 10 percent of the West Bank in the coming phase of troop withdrawals, according to participants in the top-level meetings and other sources. Clinton's territorial pledge was the most specific by the president to date and put him squarely at odds with Israeli Prime Minister Binyamin Netanyahu, who left Washington Wednesday night without agreeing to the double-digit American benchmark. But accounts from U.S., Palestinian and Israeli officials suggested that Arafat, too, is resisting Clinton's bottom-line proposals on security, which are being cast as part of a package deal. The Washington Post, January 23, 1998

 

Meanwhile, the Israeli daily Haaretz reported that the United States is working on a proposal for Israel to pull out of 12 percent of the West Bank in three stages over an 80-day period in return for a Palestinian crackdown on Islamic militants. Netanyahu and Arafat each met with President Clinton in Washington last week to try to bridge gaps over implementing last year's U.S.-backed agreement, in which Israel pledged three more troop pullbacks in the West Bank by mid-1998. The Chicago Tribune, January 26, 1998

 

President Clinton's sexual behaviour, it now seems, may tilt the balance from peace to war in the Middle East. Every front-page headline in the Arab world has grasped this point. True, President Clinton might try to distract Americans by launching yet another strike against Baghdad for its non-compliance with UN weapons inspectors; even if he found military reason to do so, however, no one in the Middle East would believe that the timing was coincidental. Yet far more serious is the fact that the US president -who never did more than complain privately to Israel about its wish to destroy the Oslo agreement - no longer has the slightest leverage over the Israeli prime minister, Benjamin Netanyahu. The Independent, January 27, 1998
