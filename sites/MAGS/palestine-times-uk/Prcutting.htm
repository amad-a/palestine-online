The Pentagon's stealthy purchase of 21 advanced MIG-29 fighter jets from the downsizing military establishment of Moldova, disclosed last week, was an ingenious, cut-rate method for protecting the national security. Depriving Iran of Moldova's MIG-29s may be defined as addition by subtraction. Subtracting from Iran's arsenal this means of delivering nuclear weapons adds to the security of all the countries around the Persian Gulf as well as Turkey, Pakistan, and Israel. Buying the best of Moldova's decommissioned air force is less complicated and less risky than having to take some preemptive military action against Tehran a few years from now. To cope with the proliferation challenge that has become the primary threat to US security, the Pentagon has invested in the proverbial ounce of prevention. From the editorial of The Boston Globe, November 10, 1997.

 

A PALESTINIAN informer who unwittingly delivered a booby-trapped mobile telephone which killed the notorious Hamas bomber "the Engineer" is suing Israel's Shin Bet security service for 15 million compensation. Kamal Hamad, a contractor from Gaza, claims he is owed the money because of the financial losses the murder of Yahya Ayyash has caused him and the ever-present threat to his own life. The law suit, the biggest against the Shin Bet, has been filed in a Tel Aviv court and follows fruitless negotiations between the two sides since Ayyash was blown apart by the rigged cellular phone in January 1996. The Guardian, November 11, 1997.

 

HENRY KISSINGER the former American Secretary of State, said yesterday it is time for Israel to realise that a Palestinian state is inevitable. His declaration came in a memorial lecture to mark the second anniversary of the assassination of Yitzhak Rabin, the Labour Prime Minister. Dr Kissinger told a packed auditorium in Tel Aviv: "The big decision has to be made. One cannot possibly hope there is a way around it." He said that while he had advocated a gradual, "step-by-step process" to ending the Arab-Israeli conflict, he now believed it was time for giant strides. The Times, Tuesday, November 11, 1997.

 

The conflicting reports about Arafat's health have created even more concern than usual about the stalled Israeli-Palestinian peace negotiations and raised fears of a struggle for succession. Talk of Arafat's physical vulnerability has promoted jockeying among people hoping to inherit power in the Palestinian Authority, which rules parts of the Gaza Strip and West Bank, Palestinian analysts said. The authority's democratic structures remain embryonic, and some Palestinians have voiced concern that a post-Arafat power struggle would involve a trial of strength among the heads of several Palestinian police and intelligence agencies. The Washington Post, November 15, 1997.

 

Fifty years after the war that established Israel and forced many Palestinians off their lands into refugee camps or exile, Palestinians almost instinctively support anyone who will oppose Israel, said Haidar Abdul Shafi, a prominent Gaza politician and former Palestinian chief negotiator with the Israelis. Unfortunately but unavoidably, he said, "the Palestinian masses reacted from their own deep sense of injustice, to the point of hailing the missiles falling on Israelis" in 1991. Raed Habet, a man in his twenties who cradled his infant son last week in the Gaza Strip's Shati refugee camp, said Palestinians' frustrations have grown with this year's breakdown of the peace process that once offered hope for a Palestinian homeland. "We are driven by our emotions over what we have lost," Habet said. " The Washington Post, November 17, 1997.

 

A Middle East conference intended to encourage peace and economic prosperity ended Tuesday with calls for Israel to respect its agreements with the Palestinians and move toward lasting peace. Most Arab states boycotted the three-day conference to protest the policies of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, whom they blame for the current impasse in the peace process. The six to participate voiced strong support for the Palestinians, who did not send a delegation. "There is no future for cooperation, development and stability if we do not protect the peace process from collapse and unite in our efforts to force Israel to make good on its commitments," said Tunisia's foreign affairs minister, Alsadeq Abalahu, at the closing session. The Chicago Tribune, November 19, 1997.
