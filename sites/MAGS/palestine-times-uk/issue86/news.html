PA says talks with Israel produced nothing

Occupied Jerusalem- After several meetings of direct talks between PA and Israeli officials, held at the urging of U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright, Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat declared on Wednesday, 22 July, that talks with Israel didn't achieve any results.

Arafat said the Palestinians would hold no more high-level talks with the Israelis following the failure of the 19 July meeting between Israeli Defense Minister Yitzhak Mordechai and Yasser Arafat's deputy, Mahmoud Abbas.

PA spokesman Nabil Abu Rudiena said the talks with Israel were going nowhere, adding that Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu was only seeking to "sell old wine in new bottles."

"So far, the Israelis have shown no signs of accepting the American initiative," said Abu Rudiena.

The Palestinian official added that Mahmoud Abbas would not hold another meeting with Mordechai owing to continuing Israeli intransigence.

Meanwhile, the Israeli Defense Minister called on the American Administration to dispatch a high-ranking American diplomat to try to bridge the gaps between Israel and the PA.

Mordechai said American intervention was needed owing to the PA refusal to accept recent Israeli proposals concerning the implementation of the second phase of Israeli army redeployment in the West Bank.

Described by the Palestinians as a "take it or leave it" offer, the said proposals suggest that the Israeli occupation army pulls out from 10% of the West Bank while the remaining 3% stipulated in the American initiative be turned into a natural reserve off limits to demographic expansion.

Mordechai said, "It was imperative that Dennis Ross or somebody else come to the area and strive to achieve something in a very short period of time."

However, State Department spokesperson, James Robin, reacted negatively to Mordechai's call saying, "It was up to the parties to solve their differences ... and the United States would intervene if and when the parties achieved meaningful progress in their direct talks."

Meanwhile, Netanyahu was coming under pressure to conclude an agreement with the Palestinians before the end of July.

According to Israeli sources, the leader of the Third Way party, Avigodor Kahalani, threatened to leave the Netanyahu Government if an agreement with the Palestinians was not reached before the end of July.

Moreover, two other coalition partners also threatened to topple the Netanyahu Government if Netanyahu accepted the American initiative (e.g. agreeing to redeploy from 13% of the West Bank).

The two parties are the Mifdal (National Religious Party) and Tsomet whose support is vital for the survival of the Israeli Government.

Israeli sources said Netanyahu might well seek a way out of the current deadlock by dissolving the Knesset and calling for early general elections, which he hopes to win.

Fatah says American initiative is "filthy trap"

Occupied Jerusalem (25 July)
Fatah, the mainstream PLO faction has lambasted Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat for "dragging his feet" and "procrastinating" on the formation of the new PA cabinet.

In a leaflet distributed on Friday 24 July, the organization accused Arafat of "placing the vital interests of our people in the hands of Benyamin Netanyahu."

The leaflet pointed out that the PA was "in a state of paralysis and seems to be utterly powerless to extract the Palestinian cause from the political dead-end it was forced to following the election of Netanyahu as Prime Minister of Israel."

The leaflet also questioned the real reasons behind Arafat's reluctance to form a new cabinet that would win the support of the Legislative Council and the Palestinian people as a whole.

Arafat has postponed several times naming a new cabinet, claiming the task needed more consultations.

The leaflet is seen as a sign of discontent within the rank and file of young Fatah activists due to the stagnation of the peace process and the widespread corruption permeating throughout PA institutions.

Meanwhile, Islamist opposition figures and legislative council members accused the PA leadership of "deliberately suspending local and municipal elections due to diminishing support for Oslo among the Palestinian people."

Sheikh Jamal Salim, an Islamist leader from the Nablus area, described as "nonsense" PA claims that the postponement of the elections was being impeded by the Israeli occupation authorities.

"The real reason behind the freezing of the election is PA fear of the results," said Salim.

He pointed out that "the head of the Preventive Security Force in Gaza, Muhammed Dahlan issued last year a secret recommendation to Yasser Arafat suggesting that the elections be canceled lest the opposition scores a sounding victory which would send the wrong message to our people and the world."

More to the point, Salim argued that Arafat himself and the legislative council were elected in the shadow of the Israeli occupation, adding that the PA was only looking for a pretext to freeze the democratic process awaiting "a certain political achievement" that would be used to enhance Arafat's image.

"This is a flagrant example of opportunism and anti-democracy," he said.

Husam Khader, an independent-minded Fatah councilor, also lashed out at the PA for "seeking to confiscate the people's choice."

He pointed out that the Legislative Council adopted a unanimous decision to organize local and municipal elections.
He added however, that Arafat utterly ignored the decision for "personal political calculations."

Palestinian State in focus

Occupied Jerusalem- PT
The Oslo peace process has effectively transformed major Palestinian population centres in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip into "isolated Bantustans"; this is the conclusion reached by three Palestinian political figures who spoke during a seminar organized by the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine in Tulkarm on 22 July.

Ali al Jarbawi, Professor of Political Science at Bir Zeit University said, "the automatic extension" of the "transitional period" would only perpetuate the present "tragic situation" in the occupied territories where the Israeli occupation completely controls our life and land.

Jarbawi opined that there are several indications suggesting that the Palestinian Authority will eventually agree to extend the transitional period (due to expire in May 1999), despite its recurrent threats to declare the formal establishment of a Palestinian State by May of 1999.

"The threats to proclaim a Palestinian State as of May 1999 is largely rhetorical and meant only to exert pressure on Israel to redeploy its troops from a larger chunk of the West Bank."

Jarbawi said the unilateral proclamation of a Palestinian State by PA Chairman Yasser Arafat would have mainly a psychological effect but would have very little affect on the situation on the ground.

"In a certain sense, Benyamin Netanyahu would be glad to give Arafat 10% or 51% of Palestine and call it a Palestinian State...even Israel would recognize a State as such."

Jarbawi described the continual rounds of talks between Israel and the Palestinian Authority as "without substance and a waste of time."

"These talks have produced nothing and contribute only to lowering further the ceiling of Palestinian demands and expectations."

Another participant, Taysir Khalid of the DFLP, pointed out that the Palestinian people were facing three choices: continuous futile talks with Israel without a time-limit, conclusion of an agreement with the Israelis before the end of the transitional period, and, third, proclaiming a Palestinian State.

Khalid opined that the last choice was the safest way out of the Oslo predicament, suggesting, however, that proclaiming a State was one thing and establishing a viable State was quite a different matter.

The DFLP official suggested that the PA immediately enter into a serious dialogue with populist forces in the occupied territories in Palestine and the diaspora for the purpose of reaching a "common approach concerning the paramount issue of Statehood."

Khalid urged the PA to immediately terminate all forms of normalization with the enemy "because others won't come to our aide when they see us in a business-as-usual situation with the Israelis."

For his part, Khalil al Shekaki, Director of the Nablus-based Palestinian Studies and Research Center, opined that the slated proclamation of a Palestinian State by May of 1999 was the only viable choice available.

However, Shekaki warned that in order for that proclamation to be successful, the PA must put the Palestinian house in order, as well as launch a relentless campaign aimed at acquiring international recognition.

Shekaki, brother of the late Islamic Jihad leader Fathi al Shekaki who was assassinated in Malta by Mossad agents three years ago, warned that the price for Statehood would be immense.

He pointed out that the Israeli occupation authorities would seek to annex large areas of the West Bank as well as "close off Palestinian population centres ... and might even reoccupy the autonomous enclaves completely."

Hamas says Israeli account of "foiled bomb attack" is nonsense

Occupied Jerusalem (20 July)

The Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, has vehemently denied Israeli claims that a former Hamas activist was planning to carry out a car-bomb attack in West Jerusalem on 19 July, describing Israeli claims to that effect as "sheer nonsense."

The Islamist source, who spoke on condition of anonymity, disclosed that Jalal Rummaneh, the person the Israeli authorities allege was planning to blow up his car in a crowded West Jerusalem street, was in fact a construction contractor carrying several plastic containers of diesel fuel for his concrete mixers.

"You certainly don't use diesel oil when you plan to carry out a bomb attack," said the source.

The PA also cast doubt on the "car-bomb theory."

Nabil Abu Rudiena, spokesman for the PA Chairman, said in a radio interview on 20 July, "I believe the whole story is concocted by the Israelis."

Abu Rudiena also accused the Israelis of "spreading misinformation on the eve of the resumption of talks between Israel and the PA for the purpose of blackmailing the PA into giving more concessions."

"There is something suspicious about this incident; the Israelis know that the 'car-bomb story' is a fabrication."

So far, Israeli police have not produced any credible evidence corroborating the claim that a car-bomb attack was being carried out, aside from the allegation that the driver was associated with Hamas in the past and that he entered Jerusalem without a valid permit.

Moreover, the Israeli authorities, which imposed a blackout on the investigation into the incident, never claimed that "explosives" were found inside the car.

Meanwhile, Israeli police were put on high alert to thwart any possible attacks by Islamist guerrillas following the incident in West Jerusalem on 19 July.

Moreover, Israeli soldiers carried out meticulous random searches of cars and urged Israelis to inform the police of any suspicious objects.

The new measures caused widespread traffic jams throughout pre-1967 Palestine.

Amnesty: Israel's report to UN Committee fails
to address abuses in Occupied Territories

London- PT
Amnesty International expressed its concern to the UN Human Rights Committee about a wide range of human rights violations, including extrajudicial executions , torture , arbitrary detention and unfair trials , committed by Israel.

Last month the Israeli Government presented its first report -- long overdue -- to the Committee. The report describes how Israel is implementing the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR). Members will question the Israeli delegation and make concluding observations.

A statement by Amnesty pointed out that "Israel's report to the Committee fails to discuss the human rights situation in the Occupied Territories -- the West Bank, the Gaza Strip, South Lebanon and the Golan Heights - even though Israel is also bound to apply the ICCPR in these areas, as well as in Israel."

Amnesty criticised Israel because it failed to address in its report to the Human Rights Committee the implementation of the Covenant in the territories which it occupies, especially given the scale and seriousness of the human rights violations perpetrated against their populations.

Amnesty International requested that the Committee asks Israel to submit a supplementary report on the implementation of the Covenant in the Occupied Territories. The human rights organisation also asked the Committee to make strong recommendations to the Israeli Government on how it can fully implement the ICCPR.

Amnesty International's concerns, expressed in a memorandum to the Committee, include:

Extrajudicial killings and unlawful killings: With flagrant disrespect for the right to life, the use of extrajudicial executions to combat "terrorism" appears official policy for the Israel Government. Additionally, each year the Israeli security forces shoot dead dozens of Palestinians, frequently using excessive force and even violating the Israeli army's own Open Fire Regulations.

Torture and ill-treatment: Secret official guidelines authorize the General Security Service (GSS) to employ torture and ill-treatment during interrogations. The GSS uses sensory deprivation methods, position abuse and violent shaking systematically.

Torture is not only official policy, it is effectively legalized: Israel's Supreme Court has, until now, failed to give legal protection to detainees subjected to such methods. In Khiam Detention Centre, a prison in South Lebanon run by the South Lebanon Army (SLA), Israel's proxy militia in the area, in cooperation with Israel, Lebanese detainees are also systematically tortured.

Arbitrary detention: Israel currently holds about 90 Palestinians in arbitrary detention without charge or trial as "administrative detainees". In addition, it holds 21 Lebanese nationals in administrative detention as hostages in return for information about Israeli soldiers missing in action in Lebanon. In November 1997 the Israeli Supreme Court held that it was legal to hold these detainees as "bargaining chips".

The Israeli army and the SLA illegally arrest Lebanese nationals in South Lebanon, taking them either to Khiam Detention Centre or, occasionally, to Israel. About 100
Lebanese detainees, including children, are imprisoned in Khiam, which operates completely outside any legal framework. None has been charged or put on trial. Some
detainees have been held in this condition since 1985.

Unfair trials: Palestinian detainees receive unfair trials in the military courts set up by Israel for the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. Convictions are almost invariably based exclusively on the accused's confession, usually extracted by the use of torture and ill-treatment. It appears to Amnesty International that there is a presumption of guilt rather than innocence in these courts.

The UN Human Rights Committee is a body of 18 experts elected by the States Parties to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights to monitor the way these states implement their obligations set out in the Covenant. Israel ratified the Covenant in 1991. The experts act in their personal capacity and do not represent their government. The experts regularly review written reports from states on how the Covenant has been implemented by questioning government representatives and issuing written conclusions.

ISRAEL: Endless Human Rights Violations

Amman- Special to PT
For two days there was extensive debate over Israel's second periodic report to the Committee against Torture convening in Geneva, wherein the Committee identified a range of interrogation methods used by Israel that violate article16 and constitute torture as defined by article 1 of the Convention. The Human Rights Watch, in a memorandum submitted to the Committee, noted that "Despite the Committee's repeated recommendations of concrete steps Israel should take to bring its laws and practices into compliance, Israel has consistently disregarded these recommendations and continues to use torture and cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment during interrogations of Palestinian detainees".

The Israeli record, which is full of gross human rights violations despite the wide international condemnation of its practices towards the Palestinians, will be filling up with even more violations since "Israel is currently considering legislation that would give legal cover to secret interrogation procedures", including those already identified by the Committee as being in violation of the Convention. A recent draft of the General Security Services (GSS) Law allows the adoption of secret guidelines for interrogation, and grants GSS agents immunity from criminal liability if they are acting "in good faith and in a reasonable manner in the course of carrying out their duties." The bill also makes it a criminal offense for any member of the GSS or its Knesset overseer committee to disclose information without authorization. If enacted, this bill would provide a legal basis for the use of "moderate measures of physical pressure" by GSS agents during interrogations-including practices already recognized by the Convention as constituting violations of article 16 and amounting to torture as defined in article 1.

Of course, it goes beyond mentioning that "this trend toward expanding immunity from prosecution for abuses committed by the State is also evident in Israel's draft Law Concerning Handling of Suits Arising from Security Force Activities in Judea, Samaria and the Gaza Strip. The law would severely limit the right of Palestinians to seek compensation for wrongful injury or death caused by Israeli soldiers. The legislation would disqualify most suits by unduly broadening the definition of "combatant activity"-situations for which no compensation could be claimed-and by exempting from consideration injuries that are not serious and permanent. If enacted, this law would eviscerate what has been one of the few means of holding the Israeli army accountable for abuses: civil suits in Israeli courts.

One of the main issues that drew a lot of controversy, being described as inhuman and unjust, was "Israel's use of prolonged administrative detention and hostage-taking" since people should never be used as bargaining chips. Under administrative detention, detainees are held without charge or trial, sometimes for years at a time. According to Human Rights Watch, "Although Israel recently released some of the longest held administrative detainees, including Ahmad Qattamesh, who was held for almost six years without charge, an estimated 150 individuals remain in custody". It also stated that "Israel admitted for the first time in 1997 that it used administrative detention to hold Lebanese nationals as hostages to secure the release of Israeli servicemen."

Despite the Oslo accords signed by Israel, which should have enhanced its respect for the rights of Palestinians, Israel is still acting in a manner very much in contradiction to its alleged pursuit for peace and coexistence.

It is evident that Israel is not willing to uphold a commitment to honour or respect the rights of the Palestinians, let alone giving them any of their legal rights. Its attitude as such has caused a volatile atmosphere, one that is ready to explode at the slightest friction.

Hamas urges PA to release political prisoners

The Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas, has called on the Palestinian Authority to release all Hamas leaders and supporters detained in the PA jails, namely Dr. Abdel Aziz Rantisi, Dr. Ibrahim Maqadmeh and Emad Awadhallah, who were detained following the assassination of Hamas military leader Muhiel Deen Sharif.

A communiqué issued by Hamas referred to the report on the results of the investigation made by the PA's Legislative Council on the assassination of Sharif as "a diplomatic ploy adopted by the investigation committee in order not to condemn the self-rule authority for hastening to fabricate charges against Hamas members." The committee formed by the Legislative Council last April to investigate the assassination of the Hamas military leader concluded that the PA had no sufficient evidence proving its accusations against Hamas members. It also stressed the necessity to release all Hamas prisoners detained by the PA because of the assassination.

"We, in Hamas, stress that the Zionist Enemy is responsible for this crime, along with the involvement of some PA security bodies who implemented the crime. They are influential parties who seek to get away from the consequences of the crime by all means," Hamas said.

Hamas pointed out that its members in the PA detention places are subject to continuous physical and psychological torture in order to be forced to admit to a crime they never committed or to provide interrogators with information leading to the arrest or liquidation of Adel Awadhallah, a prominent military leader of Hamas.

"We appeal to all free people of this nation to intervene in order to put an end to the arbitrary practices of the PA's security bodies. We also believe that it is time to open all detention places and free the Palestinian hostages in the PA jails, especially after the PA proved unable to regain the legitimate rights of the Palestinian People. Its security conduct has proved that it preserves the security of the Israeli occupation instead of protecting the security of the Palestinian People, while Jerusalem is being Judaized and the American sponsor (of peace) proved to be biased to the Zionists," Hamas concluded.

Palestinian prisoners stage hunger strike in Nafha jail

Gaza - Palestinian and Arab prisoners in the Israeli desert jail said the Israeli jail authority escalated its arbitrary measures against them. In a letter they sent to the Palestinian Prisoners and Freed Detainees Society, prisoners revealed that they live in seriously poor living conditions including, but not limited to, overcrowding, poor ventilation, lack of proper health care, and mass punishment. They added that one of their inmates, Martyr Yousef Ar'eer, died last month owing to the absence of proper medical care in the jail. The prisoners said they decided to stage a hunger strike as a means to stop the Zionists' arbitrary measures against them. The prisoners complained of the mass punishments, the tear gas attacks and of being detained in cells with Jewish criminals.

Palestinian prisoner dying of cancer

Gaza - The Palestinian Human Rights Centre warned the Zionist occupation authorities of the continued detention of Jamal Khamisi of the Maghazi refugee camp in Gaza. Mr. Khamisi, who has been detained since September 1989, is now lying in the hospital of Ramleh jail suffering from liver cancer. He was sentenced by an Israeli military court to 25 years imprisonment on charge of murdering a Palestinian detainee in an Israeli jail suspected of collaboration with the Zionist authorities. The Palestinian Centre asserted that Khamisi's health reached a critical situation, and he is only expected to live for another four months. The Centre urged the Israeli authorities to promptly release Khamisi and provide him with the required medical treatment.

Israel demolished 2,003 Palestinian homes since Oslo accords

Gaza - A report issued by the Palestinian self-rule authority accused Israel of demolishing 2,003 Palestinian homes since the signing of the Oslo accords in September 1993. The report added that 757 of these homes have been demolished since the Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu came to power in mid 1996. The demolished houses belonged to Palestinians in occupied territories, especially Jerusalem, the West Bank and Gaza, the Palestinian report said.

Palestinians need $90 million to renovate Bethlehem

Bethlehem - Palestinian Christian authorities in Bethlehem of the West Bank estimated the funds needed to renovate Bethlehem and to prepare for the celebrations for the second millenium of The Christ's birthday, slated to start next year, to be $90 million. But Nabil Qisais, the general coordinator of the project, expressed satisfaction because the local authorities have collected $43 million to date. The funds will be allocated for the reconstruction and maintenance of roads, and the construction of new water networks and public parks as well as several hotels. Among the foreign countries that gave donations for this project are Sweden, Japan, Belgium, Italy and Norway, as well as the World Bank and the European Investment Bank.

Israel's exports to Far East reduced by 30%

Occupied Jerusalem - A report issued by the Israeli Central Statistics Bureau revealed that Israel's exports to the Far East countries have been reduced by 30% during the first half of this year, which valued about $570 million. The report added that Israeli exports to these countries consist of 16% of the total Israeli exports. It pointed out that Israeli jewel exports to the Far East was reduced in the first half of this year by 40%, compared with the exports in the same period of 1997. This reduction was valued at about $420 million. The same report pointed out that Israeli exports to Japan was reduced during the same period by 16%; to South Korea by 30%; and to Singapore by 34%.
