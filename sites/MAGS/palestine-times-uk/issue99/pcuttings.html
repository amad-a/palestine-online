Press Cuttings

The New York Times, 15 August 1999

(Israeli)Prime Minister Ehud Barak of Israel permitted the truck transport of a huge metal turbine part on the Jewish Sabbath on Aug. 14, defying an Orthodox party that forms a crucial part of his coalition government and raising concerns over a possible government crisis.

On Friday, Eliahu Suissa, the minister of national infrastructure and a leader of the Orthodox Shas party, tried to prevent the transport -- Jewish law prohibits vehicular travel on the Sabbath. He threatened to take his party's 17 seats in Parliament and leave the government.

The Washington Post, 13 August 1999

..Ehud Barak, Arafat's intended, accuses him of "inflexibility" and trying to "put up obstacles" to peace. Arafat, unable to go home to mother, calls his friend Bill at the White House to complain.

Arafat is miffed. Clinton is confounded. The irony is thick. For three years, Clinton's Middle East hands prayed and maneuvered for a Labor government in Israel, one they assumed would docilely walk in the path of peace as prescribed by Washington. Now they have it. Beware what you wish for.

..It is only August, and we have already hit the first stone wall on the path to peace: Barak is reluctant to carry out the Wye River agreements, forced on Netanyahu by Clinton himself.

Why is Barak balking?

Ha'aretz, 13 August 1999

The government of Prime Minister Ehud Barak is entering the second month of its tenure without having taken a single step towards implementing the Wye agreement, the suspension of which by the previous government caused a profound crisis in the peace process. At the end of yet another week characterised by a plethora of contacts at various levels, there is no sign of any real change in the positions held by either side. Neither the prime minister's meeting with Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat and Arafat's deputy, Abu Mazen, nor the contacts between lawyer Gilad Sher and Arafat and Dr. Saeb Erekat have led to an understanding regarding the changes Israel wants to introduce into the agreement.

The Guardian, 12 August 1999

The Daily Telegraph, 11 August 1999

ISRAEL plans to release the memoirs of Adolf Eichmann, the Nazi war criminal, to international scholars and his family, the Justice Ministry said yesterday.

Eichmann, mastermind of the Holocaust, wrote the 1,300-page manuscript during his trial in Israel in 1961 and up to his execution a year later. The document, which has been kept in the vaults of the state archives in Jerusalem, is believed to be Eichmann's vindication of his role in the genocide.

Release of the manuscript was initially blocked for fear that it would be used by neo-Nazi groups to deny the Holocaust.

The Washington Post, 5 August 1999

Lt. Gen. Ehud Barak, then Israel's top army officer, was taken by surprise when he learned of the Oslo peace deal between Israel and the Palestinians six years ago, and he expressed grave reservations.

Two years later, as a freshman government minister, Barak was equally skeptical when a follow-up accord required Israel to go ahead with pledged troop withdrawals from large chunks of the West Bank. In the cabinet vote on the agreement, he abstained.

..Much as Barak balked at the sequence of Israeli withdrawals set forth in the original Oslo accord, he is hesitating now to carry out the land transfers mandated by last October's Wye River agreement, the latest attempt to give the Oslo process a shove forward.
