Israel continues murderous war on Palestinian children

Occupied Jerusalem- Khalid Amayreh

Last month, an Israeli court in West Jerusalem acquitted an Israeli settler of murdering a 10-year-old Palestinian child, citing insufficient evidence and "extenuating circumstances."

The murderer, a Jewish settler named Nahum Korman, killed Helmi al-Shousheh of the Arab village of Husan, four miles south-west of Bethlehem, while chasing a group of schoolchildren whom he claimed hurled stones at his car.

The incident, which took place in October 1996, was described by the Palestinian and Israeli press alike as "a cold-blooded murder for which no justification can be found."

Shousheh's friends testified then that they saw Kolman step on the child's throat with his army boots, beat him on the back of his head with the butt of his gun until the child lost consciousness.

Shousheh was transferred to hospital with a massive brain haemorrhage where he succumbed to his injury.

Initially, Kolman cited "self-defense" as justification for the gruesome crime. However, he later retracted his testimony, claiming that Shousheh's head injury resulted from a fall, not from (his) gun strikes.

The judge, Ruth Orr, who is reportedly affiliated with the Israeli far-right, wasted no time in embracing Kolman's concocted story.

She dismissed the testimonies of Shousheh's school peers, Ibrahim and Taher Shawash, as children’s "fantasies" saying, "There is no plausible reason to believe that the children told the truth."

Orr also rejected a testimony by the Israeli Chief Coroner, Yehuda Hiss, who testified that after watching a recreation of the witnesses' version of events, he was convinced that the child's death had been caused by the gun blows and the stomping.

In a nutshell, one more Jewish murderer of a Palestinian gets away with impunity, and one more Palestinian, a schoolchild this time, is added to the long list of the helpless victims of Zionist savagery.

Age-old racist attitudes prevail
The case of Helmi al-Shousheh actually epitomizes official Israeli policies toward the Palestinian people as a whole, which emanates from age-old racist attitudes toward non-Jews.

Nahum Korman, a security officer at the settlement of Hadar Betar near Bethlehem, was trained and indoctrinated in the Talmud, the Jewish holy books that treats "goyem" (derogatory for non-Jews) as beasts whose lives are almost worthless, especially if and when compared to a Jew's life.

According to Yisrael Shahak's "Jewish History, Jewish Religion: The Weight of Three Thousand Years," the Talmud orders Jews to "kill the best of the gentiles…whose flesh is as the flesh of asses and whose issue (of semen) is like the issue of horses."

This Talmudic injunction, needless to say, has always been relevant in the annals of Zionist savagery toward Palestinians and Arabs.

The scandalous thing is that it is still applied in a variety of sinister ways, not only by the murderous settlers, who view the murder of innocent Palestinians as a "Mitzvah" or "good deed with which God is pleased," but also by the Israeli army, justice system and the overall apparatus of the Israeli State.

Earlier this year, an Israeli soldier who murdered a Palestinian labourer from the village of Dahiriya, 15 miles south-west of Hebron, was detained for only five days before he was set free by the Israeli army commander of the West Bank. The commander cited "good conduct" as a reason for pardoning the soldier. It was not clear what good conduct the commander had in mind, but it was amply sufficient that he didn't view the murder itself as "bad conduct."

More to the point, the Israeli justice system, which practices judicial apartheid, often adds insult to injury when "prosecuting" Israeli soldiers and settlers for murdering Palestinians.

For example, a Tel Aviv court in 1996 fined two Israeli undercover soldiers two agorot (roughly one quarter of a US cent) each for killing a Palestinian labourer north of Ramallah while on his way to work inside Israel.

Needless to say, the court decision, besides giving a definite green light to murder Palestinians, carried a bluntly-racist message that an Arab life was not worth even one US cent!

In fact, persons guilty of murder, even mass murder, against Arabs are allowed to rise to high public positions. An illustration of this, which Shahak mentioned in his book, is the case of Shmeul Lahis, who was responsible for the massacre of 50-75 Palestinian peasants imprisoned in a mosque in the village of Dawaymeh after it had been conquered by the Israeli army.

Following a pro-forma trial, he was granted complete amnesty, thanks to Ben Gurion's intercession. The man went on to become a respected lawyer and in the late 1970s was appointed Director-General of the Jewish Agency, the executive tool of the Zionist movement.

Children treated as terrorists-in-the-making
If the murderer of Helmi Shousheh felt no scruples at killing his little victim while returning home from school on the ground that "he is just another Arab," the Israeli army likewise seems to treat Palestinian children as a whole as "terrorists-in-the-making."

The Hebrew press reported last week that the Israeli army General Command in the West Bank and Gaza Strip decided to toughen punishments for Palestinian children involved in stone-throwing incidents.

According to the new amendments, the minimum age of prosecutable Palestinian children will be reduced from 14 to 12, meaning that 12-year-olds will be viewed and treated as adults bearing full responsibility for whatever they do.

The "culprit" children here will not appear before a juvenile court, or anything of this sort, nor will they be questioned by a special officer who would treat them, as children everywhere should be, with emphasis on behavioural reform rather than punishment.

The children in this case will be interrogated by regular Shin Beth interrogators whose modus operandi is physical and psychological torture (Israel is the only country in the world where torture is sanctioned by the law).

This necessarily means that filthy-smelling sacks (or what is known as the hooding technique) will be placed on the child’s head for as long as needed to "straighten up their minds." (Hooding is always the first order of business in the interrogation of Palestinians.) It could also mean that they will undergo another notorious torture technique—shaking—in addition to other routine forms of torture, such as sleep deprivation, sexual molestation, painful positioning and beatings.

On 19 August, the very same day the Israeli government decided to free the longest-serving Palestinian prisoner, Khalil Ra'ei, the Israeli army arrested 15 Palestinian children at the Arroub refugee camp near Hebron for throwing stones at Israeli army vehicles.

These children are almost certain to undergo a nightmarish experience at the hands of Shen Beth's sadistic torturer.

The question which everyone is prompted to ask in light of this barbarity is: "How will those children be for the rest of their lives?"

In other words, isn't Israel ultimately responsible for the "suicidal-bomber phenomenon" as much as anybody else, perhaps more than anybody else?

The Wye River Agreement…Back to Square One!

By Mahmoud al-Khatib

Until now, attempts have been exerted by some Arabs, and Palestinian Authority officials in particular, to convince us that Israeli Prime Minister Barak is not a carbon copy of his predecessor Netanyahu. For the first time since the coming to power of General Barak, the PA’s negotiators lost their nerve and became frustrated with the prospect of achieving any progress in the negotiations between the PA and the Israeli government on the implementation of the Wye River Agreement signed in October 1998 under the patronage of the U.S. President. Several statements by the chief PA negotiator, Saeb Ereikat, and other PA officials admitted that there was no genuine difference between the terrorist Barak and the terrorist Netanyahu.

Before the two sides reached what they called a “partial agreement” on the release of a limited number of Palestinian prisoners in the Israeli jails, the PA officials said they have returned back to square one since the resumption of the negotiations between the Israelis and the PA that began after the victory of General Barak in the Israeli elections last May.

According to the modest partial agreement, the Zionist regime will release about 500 Palestinian prisoners in two batches—the first in the beginning of September and the second on 8 October. There are more than 3,000 Palestinian political prisoners, mainly Hamas supporters, in the Israeli jails. The Israelis insisted that they would not release any Palestinian who had his hands “stained” with Jewish blood. Therefore, most of those who will be released are criminal prisoners who were detained for ordinary crimes. This is exactly what happened with the first batch of prisoners (250 Palestinians) who were freed by Netanyahu in November 1998. That batch included only 100 political prisoners belonging to pro-Oslo groups, especially Fatah, and 150 prisoners who were arrested for ordinary crimes such as car thefts and infiltration to 1948 Occupied Palestine, so-called Israel, for work without permits from the Israeli government.

Excepting this partial agreement, the PA officials failed to convince Barak’s government to implement the Wye River agreement from the point Netanyahu stopped at, which is the redeployment of the occupation troops from 13% of the West Bank and the construction of a “safe passage” between the West Bank and the Gaza Strip.

The PA’s tragedy was that it found itself forced to negotiate with the Israelis on issues that have been already agreed upon and included in authenticated agreements. Every time the PA officials sign an agreement with the Israelis, they were forced by the Israelis to reach a new agreement to implement the original one! When the Cairo Agreement (Oslo 2) was signed, they went to the United States to negotiate the implementation of that agreement which ended with the Wye River agreement. Now they are negotiating a new agreement to implement Wye River! With every agreement, the Palestinian Authority is the only side that is required to introduce relinquishments and to abandon more legitimate rights of the Palestinian People.

The Israelis’ tactics in their negotiations with the PA officials are very clear. They are aware of the fact, as the Israeli Haaretz daily put it, that Chairman Arafat and the Palestinian leadership have become exhausted and tired and therefore they want to reach any agreement with the Israelis no matter what the cost and even if this solution will not survive! For Arafat, it is a matter of racing against time in order to establish his authority; it is not a matter of legitimate rights of the Palestinian People, which should not be sold out.

Anyone who observes the reaction of the PA to the recent Jewish settlement activities in the West Bank, which Netanyahu started last October and which were blessed by Barak later, becomes certain that the PA has no concern for the Palestinian rights that are confiscated by the Jewish settlers every day. The settlers have built about 20 settlements on the West Bank since last October. In addition, the Israeli government is now planning to expand and fatten the already existing settlements. Barak’s government has already given the green light to the settlers to start construction works at Ras Al-Amoud and to resume the works in Abu Ghneim (Har Homa) settlement in Occupied Jerusalem. All this is being done even though all agreements between the PA and the Israeli government state that Jewish settlement activities in Occupied Territories should be stopped until an agreement about the final status of the settlements in the West Bank and Gaza is reached.

Barak does not want to implement the Wye River agreement with the PA because the longer the current situation continues, the more Palestinian land can be confiscated by the Jewish settlers. He is not interested in signing a deal with the Palestinians. Rather, he is interested in making deals with the Syrians and the Lebanese, which are expected to be reached in one year.

By: E. Yaghi

(Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness’ sake: for their’s is the kingdom of heaven.)

If the dead could speak, this is what I would say to you. My name is Helmi Shousheh. On October 27, 1996, I was murdered by the armed Israeli settler, Nahoum Korman. At the time of my death, I was only 11 years old.

Perhaps you might say that I, a Palestinian child, deserved to die. Maybe I was throwing stones at an armed settler or at heavily armed Israeli troops. But this is not the case. I was on my way home from school and more than anything, wanted to see my mother and tell her about my day learning about the world in my classroom. Instead, this killer of children chased me for no reason other than the fact that I was a Palestinian child and unable to defend myself. First he hit me and then kicked me in the head. I fell down bleeding. The cause of my death was repeated blows to my head by Korman’s rifle butt. I screamed out begging the man to stop beating me, but I knew he was out to end my life and all my screams and all the blood that gushed out of my head did not stop him. I want to emphasize that I never hated anyone in my life and never wished harm to any person, including my killer.

There were eyewitnesses who testified to Korman’s brutality and these eyewitness accounts were supported by the medical evidence of the Head of the Israeli Institute of Forensic, Medicine, Dr. Yehuda Hiss.

What is surprising to me is that not only was Korman acquitted on 16 August, at the Israeli District Court in Jerusalem, but that the judge was a female, Judge Ruth Orr. That a female could take the death of a child so lightly is in itself a tragedy. Judge Orr preferred to believe that, inspite of eyewitness accounts and the medical evidence provided by Dr. Hiss, I had hit my head on a stone and thus died. Did she think that the stone was so vicious to cause my death? She should have seen my body, especially my head then after the cruel beating that no human or even animal should be forced to endure.

I remember my own mother and how she was so sensitive, soft and tender and how when I was buried in my small shroud, stayed crying at my grave and asking why did I have to die such a terrible death. But apparently, Judge Orr has no soft heart nor is she sensitive to human pain like most women are.

During the past 11 years, 300 Palestinian children have been murdered by Israelis. Few of these killers have ever been charged and fewer still are convicted or if convicted, have ever served their full prison sentence. How strange that confessions extracted by torture from Palestinians are used as evidence against them and sends them either back to prison, or to death.

The acquittal of Korman is a serious matter, not only for Palestinians, who can be hunted down and slaughtered like big game in Africa, but for Israelis too, particularly since Israel supports the presence and arming of Jewish settlers in the Occupied Palestinian Territories.

Whenever civilians are armed, regardless of the reason, there is bound to be conflict.

This acquittal will also encourage any Jewish settler to open fire or hunt down any Palestinian adult or child they please and this is backed by the new Israeli policy to prosecute children accused of stone throwing. Stones are hurled by the hands of the small against the oppression and occupation. We, the Palestinian children, are the Davids of today and the Israeli occupation is Goliath. This Goliath has no mercy or heart.

My innocent blood indelibly stains not only Korman’s hands but Judge Orr’s too and all those who make certain that Palestine remains occupied by an unjust force that has no legal, historic, or moral right to be there. My tomorrows were gone before I even had a chance to plan or dream or live. I shall not enter heaven until I take the hand of my mother once she passes away and together, we shall live in a place where there is no war, no hate, and where children regardless of whether they are Palestinian or not, are free.

Occupied Jerusalem (August) - Sheikh Dr. Yousuf al-Qaradawi is one of the most erudite contemporary Muslim scholars. His ability to find Islamic answers to many of the problems facing Muslims nowadays gained him a clarion reputation throughout the Muslim world and even in the West. Answer: The conflict will continue; its smouldering embers will not be extinguished and its mill will not stop moving as long as there is a usurper and oppressor on the one hand and an oppressed and victim on the other hand. The oppressor may gain the edge for some time, owing to his military strength, but time is not on his side and won't be in the future.

The regimes may give in under pressure and surrender, especially if they happen to be defeated from within. However, the peoples and their vital forces, particularly the Islamists, will not succumb to oppression and will not lend recognition to the illegal usurpation. This is a matter of faith; it is also the seed for victory in the future.

In fact, there are many signs and glad tidings in the Qur’an that the Zionist power will recede sooner or later. Today, they are basking in their arrogance of power, but this won't last for a long time, and once this rope is severed, they will fall down, because their rope connected with God was severed a long time ago.

The Zionists will incur the heavenly wrath as they did in the not-too-distant past. So if they returned to bloodshed, oppression and mischief on earth, as they are doing now, the Almighty will visit them with destruction and vengeance again, and that is what will undoubtedly happen.

Question: How do you view normalization between Muslims and the Zionist regime?

Answer: The processes of normalization with the Zionist regime are prohibited and unacceptable from the Shariah standpoint. This normalization necessarily involves a recognition by Arabs and Muslims of Zionist sovereignty over the lands that they usurped and arrogated from the Muslims. It also involves a recognition on our part that our beloved towns and villages, such as Haifa and Akka among others, are no longer Arab, and this is unacceptable and shouldn't be tolerated. If this normalization was in the context of a truce, then it would be arguable, but this normalization is part of an imposed settlement—similar to capitulation—and true Muslims shouldn't despair of God's victory, no matter how long it is deferred.

Question: What do you think about foreign military presence in the Arab world? What are its goals and long-term effects?

Answer: The foreign military presence in the Arab world is in fact a new imperialist invasion. Some people project it as taking place with the consent of the governments concerned, but in reality it is imposed on them. The foreign presence doesn't serve the interest of the peoples of the region. On the contrary, it is there to achieve certain goals for Israel first, and for the interests of the imperialist States themselves and finally to exert pressure on the governments and closely monitor their moves and thwart any kind of unity among Arabs and Muslims in this region. So, the foreign presence constitutes a grave danger to our independence, development and unity potentials. The scandalous thing about it, however, is that it is sustained at our expense as we pay the bill at the end of the day.

Question: How do you view the Islamic resistance in Lebanon and Palestine, including the martyrdom operations?

Answer: The Islamic resistance in Lebanon and Palestine represents the glorious face of the Muslim Umma and serves as an example to that effect. As for the martyrs, I have issued a religious edict blessing the martyrdom operations in which a given Muslim fighter turns himself or herself into a human bomb that casts terror in the hearts of the enemy.

As for the edicts that were issued against the Mujahideen, it is important to point out that they were tendentious and made by unreliable ulema who don't possess genuine knowledge of Islam; they are the ulema of the palace who often act as mouthpieces for the regime, so they mislead people while they are misled themselves.

These heroes are carrying out the duty of Jihad on behalf of the entire Umma. The liberation of Jerusalem and al-Masjidul Aqsa is a collective and individual duty which no Muslim can claim doesn't concern him. This duty is not biding for the Palestinian people alone but concerns all Muslims everywhere in the world.

Indeed, if every Jew the world over considers himself responsible for the survival of and well-being of Israel, likewise every Muslim should be responsible for the liberation of al-Aqsa Mosque.

If we can't carry out acts of Jihad ourselves, we at least should support and prop up the Mujahideen financially and morally so that they will be steadfast until God's victory.

Jihad and Resistance is our fate and destiny; it is a duty upon us. We should counter Menachem Begin's slogan, "I fight, therefore I exist," with Sheikh Ahmed Yasin's motto, "I resist, therefore I exist."

This resistance is a divine Sunna (way) and without it the forces of arrogance and aggression will have the upper hand and the forces of truth and justice will suffer and be defeated.

Nobody should doubt any effort in this regard. The lowest degree of resistance is the economic, social and cultural boycott of the enemy; we should reject all forms of normalization with the Zionist regime, we must make sure that the psychological barrier stays there. We must also educate the Umma about the Zionists and their dangers and expose those among us who seek to foster despair and defeat in the ranks of Muslims.

Finally, we must warn the Palestinians against pointing their guns against each other, because the common enemy is there and the fundamental contradiction is and should remain with the Zionists.

Question: What is the future of Islam in this changing world?

Answer: I see that the future belongs to this "wasati" (moderate) Islamic movement, because the Islamic umma is inherently a moderate umma as indicated by the Qur’an. The Islamists shouldn't give in to hopelessness, for a Muslim lives perpetually with the hope for a better tomorrow. The source of this hope is God himself.

Furthermore, the Qur’an and the traditions of the Prophet (May the peace and blessing of God be upon him) contain clear signs that the future belongs to Islam. In any case, the present situation of the Islamic movement is better than it was a few decades ago and is getting even better. This a slow movement, but its success is certain in the long run.

Question: How do you see the relation between Islam and pan-Arabism?

Answer: Arabism has a strong relation with Islam; the Arabic language is the language of the Qur’an and the ritual language of Islam. Moreover, the land of the Arabs was the cradle of Islam and there the sacred mosques and places are located. In the final analysis, an Arab is one who speaks Arabic, and in many parts of the Muslim world, especially in the Maghrib countries, there is no difference between being Arab and being Muslim.

However, contradictions arise when Arabism takes a secular, non-Islamic direction. But there are extremists in the Arabist and Islamist camps. And while it is absolutely unacceptable that Arabism be placed above Islam or takes precedence before it, it is also equally unacceptable on the part of some Islamists to brand all Arab nationalists as "kafir" or disbelievers. A constructive dialogue between the two camps is always positive, and this direction should be encouraged.

The PA cultural conspiracy

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem- Since its establishment over five years ago, the Palestinian Authority of Yasser Arafat has been steadily trying to spread “modern culture” among the mostly conservative and religious Palestinian people.

However, for the PA, “modernity” hasn’t meant, as one would expect, such positive features and values as fostering a culture of democracy, human rights, civil rights, civil liberties, nor, indeed, the establishment of the rule of law whereby everybody from the President downward is subject to the authority of law.

Indeed, these things are still rare species in the PA political and cultural dictionary. Instead, the PA’s “modern culture” has chiefly meant the encouragement of moral and behavioural laxity (especially among the youth) such as alcohol consumption, prostitution, sexual deviance, belly-dancing, casinos and many of the things that otherwise are considered promiscuous in the conservative and Islamic culture. (It is said that in Nablus alone the PA gave licenses for 45 bars in which alcoholic beverages from the locally-produced Arak to the fine Scottish whisky are served.)

This summer, the West Bank and Gaza Strip have witnessed as many as ten “carnivals,” an increase by 50% compared to 1998. Most of the events bore the titles of “artistic” and “cultural.” In reality, however, the carnivals featured little or no meaningful cultural activity one can speak of. Indeed, Palestinian critics, including people associated with Fatah, often complained that naked thighs and naked breasts were more conspicuous than anything else. This indicated that the carnivals had more in common with the nudity culture of Sau Paulo and Rio de Janeiro than with the conservative Palestinian culture of Nablus, Hebron and Bethlehem.

Besides the brazen display of feminine flesh, the “fa’aliyat” or activities were very low in quality. This led some of the PA commentators and columnists to dismiss this year’s carnivals as “scandalous.”

Hafez al-Barghouthi, editor-in-chief of the PA semi-official newspaper, al-Hayatul Jadida, described the atmosphere at one of the carnivals in Ramallah in these words: “There was a crescendo of noise, harmful to the ears—disharmonious and discordant—and we still dare call it art.”

Barghouthi lashed out at the organizers of the events, arguing that instead of promoting the cultural awareness among Palestinians, the carnivals actually did the opposite. He cited the “almost promiscuous intermixing of young people of both sexes in ways that debase and disgrace our national dignity and pride.”

PA responsible for corrupting youth
It is amply clear that the Palestinian Authority, probably in connivance with Israel and the CIA, is responsible for the dissemination of this cultural promiscuity among Palestinians. And the reason, or ill-motive, behind this is no less than a sinister endeavour to corrupt the Palestinian youth and the Palestinian population at large in order to make them forget “Palestine” and enter the new Israeli age.

The PA claims, falsely of course, that its goal is the liberation of Palestine and the establishment of a free and independent Palestinian State with Jerusalem as its capital. However, one is tempted to wonder how can this sublime goal be served by hosting bands of barely-dressed belly-dancers and other symbols of seduction whose only message to the Palestinian youth is “don’t worry, be happy!!”

In other words, the Palestinian Authority seems to be convinced that the way to liberate Jerusalem and the Aqsa Mosque is by mobilizing an army of singers, belly-dancers and by spreading the culture of “don’t worry, be happy.”

This is not an overstatement in any way. The Authority, which opened the largest casino in the Middle East in Jericho only twenty minutes’ drive from the Aqsa Mosque, will only promote the “casino culture,” which really means prostitution, drug-addiction, fornication, adultery and other forms of permissive lifestyles.

But there is a clear political dimension to the PA policy in this effect. For while Jewish settlements continued to expand at the Palestinians’ expense, the morally bankrupt and politically discredited Palestinian Authority continued to display total impotence in the face of continued Zionist insolence.

On 5 August, a PA Culture Ministry official insisted on opening the “Halhul Cultural carnival” on time, despite the fact that the Old Town of Hebron was languishing under curfew. When asked why the Ministry didn’t identify with the people under curfew, the official said, “We would like to show the Israelis that we are not affected by their repression.”

The Ministry of Culture is in fact the chief culprit in PA efforts to introduce and deepen cultural estrangement among Palestinians. Indeed, the Minister of Culture, Yasser Abed Rabbu, on many occasions attacked the Palestinian Islamic culture, labeling it as “dark” and “backward.”

Abed Rabbu, a former communist-turned-liberalist, who is often called “Yasser Abed Yasser” or “Yasser slave of Yasser (Arafat)”, remarked numerous times that “with the liberation of Palestine, the Palestinian people have the right to be happy and to dance in order to spit off the Zionists who don’t want us to be happy or look happy.”

But happiness, in Abed Rabbu’s views, has not meant delivery from Zionist shackles (which have never been tighter), nor freedom from the spectre of arbitrary arrest and prolonged internment without charge or trial in order to please and appease the Zionists. It only meant “the culture of love and joy,” in their very negative sense.

Indeed, that culture of love and joy, as advocated by the PA, is based first and foremost on arousing the sexual lusts of young men and women so that satiating these lusts will eventually become the youth’s main or only preoccupation.

That culture, needless to say, has already bred its first fruits in the form of several incidents of rape and sexual assaults (rape was nearly absent in the Palestinian society before the advent of Yasser Arafat’s Authority).

More to the point, by inviting scores of famous Arab singers to the self-rule areas, the PA is actually acting as “middle man” for normalization between the Zionist regime and the Arab world. This is shameful, to say the least, and there is no other word to describe it except that it is “treachery.” It is clear to everyone that any Arab wishing to come to the self-rule enclaves has to check first with the Zionists who control the borders between the autonomous enclaves and the outside world. So, the PA is procuring in essence Arab normalizers with the Zionists, and at no price.

PA only serves the enemy
It is lamentable that the Palestinian people have reached such a disgraceful and despicable state of affairs under the PA junta. This should awaken the Palestinian people to the reality of and truth about the PA, the revolutionary vanguard that turned into a quisling gang in the service of the enemy.

Certainly, the liberation of Jerusalem doesn’t need misfits like Mustafa Kamar and Ragheb Alama and other symbols of cultural and moral degeneration. The liberation of the Muslim holy shrines in Jerusalme requires real heroes like Salahuddin al-Ayyubi, Sultan Qutz and Muhammed al-Fath. It needs real Mujahideen, not drunken punks dancing till the later hours of night.

Before the catastrophe of 1967, a large sector of Arab youth in Syria, Jordan, Egypt and Palestine was classified in accordance with their preference for and identification with a given contemporary Arab singer. Thus, there were the “Abdul Halim Hafez camp”, the “Farid al-Atrash camp”, the “Um Kulthoum camp”, etc. The result of this tragic craze was seen in the early morning hours of 5 June. Everybody knows the rest of the story.

No Memorials in Israel for Bernadotte

By Dr. Daud Abdullah *

Less than 24 hours after submitting his report to the General Assembly, the first United Nations mediator to Palestine was dead. Folke Bernadotte was shot at a roadblock in Jerusalem by Jewish extremists on 17 September 1948. His killers told the Tel Aviv bureau of Agence France Presse that all United Nations Observers in Palestine were members of foreign occupation forces which had no right to be in their territory. There was much more behind the crime than hostility toward the UN. Bernadotte’s insistence on the unconditional right of the Palestinian refugees to return to their homes was, apparently, the immediate and real cause for his murder. His tragic memory will, therefore, endure as long as the Palestinians remain Stateless and scattered throughout the world.

At the outset of his mission, Bernadotte appeared well disposed to the Zionists. Influenced by events in Europe during the Second World War, he, like most Westerners, cultivated a noticeable sense of sympathy for the Zionists. He saw in their settlement of Palestine the fulfilment of biblical prophecy. As a result, Bernadotte’s original views on key political issues, such as partition and Jerusalem, were radically compromised during the four months between his appointment and his assassination.

On the question of partition, evidence from the Mediator’s diary confirms that he disapproved of the plan before his arrival in the region. Bernadotte initially believed that the partition plan was a gross mistake that was bound to lead to war. He, therefore, favoured a unitary State with ‘far-reaching rights’ for the Jews. This view was, however, noticeably altered in his 27 June 1948 report which called for a union of two members, one Arab, including Transjordan, and the other Jewish. By the time the UN Mediator submitted his final report on 16 September, he had again shifted from his union proposal to that of recognition of Israel’s de facto existence.

In the same manner, Bernadotte made a similar 180-degree turn on the crucial question of Jerusalem. Prior to his departure for the Middle East, he intimated to the French Foreign Minister, Georges Bidault, his considered opinion that Jerusalem should be included in Arab territory and not given international status. By September, however, Bernadotte had substantially changed this view. This was reflected in his last progress report which recommended that the city of Jerusalem “should be treated separately and should be placed under effective United Nations control.”

Notwithstanding these about-turns, the one outstanding area in which the Mediator remained resolutely consistent was that regarding the Palestinian refugees. He had absolutely no doubt that their uprooting and exile was caused by the systematic “terror and ravages of war.” Indeed, if ever he demonstrated any change on this issue, it was the fact that he became increasingly uncompromising and insistent on their right of return. For whereas in June his report merely stated that the “refugees should be entitled to return to their former places of residence”, his September recommendations had become decidedly urgent and specific. It read: “The right of the Arab refugees to return to their homes in Jewish controlled territory at the earliest possible date should be affirmed by the United Nations, notwithstanding the views expressed by the provisional government of Israel.”

 Those were the days
Although Bernadotte stepped out of tune with the Arab position on partition and Jerusalem, his stand on the refugee problem did strike a distinct chord with popular sentiments and official policies throughout the region. By demanding that the refugees be allowed to return ‘at the earliest possible date’, he was in effect underlining that their return was neither dependent upon the conclusion of a formal peace nor on the engagement of negotiations with the Zionists. Those, however, were the days when there was consensus and resolve among Arab States that there should be no direct negotiations with Israel unless and until the refugees were allowed to return. Today, the situation is incredibly different. Arab consensus has now become a relic of history as regional leaders cue, openly and secretly, to negotiate with Zionist Israel. Hence, the one reality that has not changed since is the tragic and inhuman circumstances of the refugees.

When compared to the statements of previous Israeli leaders, the public declarations of the Barak regime offer virtually no hope to the dispossessed and exiled Palestinians. In a letter to Bernadotte dated 1 August 1948, the country’s first Foreign Minister, Moshe Shertok, promised ‘when the Arab States are ready to conclude a peace treaty with Israel’, the question of the refugees will be raised as part of a general settlement. Speaking to the American public in an NBC interview recorded by Tim Russert on 14 August 1999, Israel’s Prime Minister, Ehud Barak, declared “I don’t think the refugees will be able under any circumstances to come back into Israel.” Count Bernadotte must surely have turned in his grave when he heard the celebrated soldier-politician add: “But a better solution for them should be found in the countries where they are living now.”

Given the shameful reluctance of the United Nations to commemorate the 51st anniversary of Bernadotte’s assassination, Middle East watchers hoped, albeit against hope, that Mr. Barak would have used his visit to New York to lay the foundation of a Folke Bernadotte Memorial or institute for refugee and forced migratory studies. Surely such a gesture would have signalled the commencement of the “fresh start” which he so piously promised in his earlier meetings with Messrs Mubarak and Arafat. Besides, it would also have been a timely and most appropriate way of expressing thanks to the UN for its unparalleled role in creating the Jewish State in Palestine. And, above all, it would have been a truly honourable way of paying tribute to the 4.5 million Palestinian refugees who, against all odds, preserved their national identity and kept their dream of return alive.

Furthermore, at a time when international terrorists, military dictators and war criminals are hunted down on every continent and brought to book, Mr. Barak could even have used his high-profile visit to New York to announce a reopening of the Bernadotte murder case. Did the chain of command begin and end with the two Polish immigrants, Nathan Yellin-Moor and Matitiahu Schmulevitz, who were arrested two months after the killing? Or, did it extend to the co-commander of the Stern Gang, Yitshak Shamir, who was himself directly implicated in the crime? Certainly a judicial review of this outrage would be a welcomed giant step toward the peace of the brave which Barak publicly calls to.

Red Lines
Back in the Middle East, diplomatic encounters with the new Israeli administration have all been depressingly farcical. None of the regional leaders who met with Barak have shown the courage to admit their disappointment, frustration and misgivings. They have, instead, all appeared before the world’s media telling tales of ‘constructive’, ‘positive’, ‘meaningful’ and ‘historic discussions’. There was nothing in their press statements that indicated a willingness to call Mr. Barak’s numerous bluffs. The Palestinian leadership was given one such golden opportunity during the “heart-to-heart talk” between Chairman Arafat and Prime Minister Barak. The latter, it was reported, used the occasion to emphasize the need for both leaders to be truthful and avoid deluding their peoples. According to Ha’aretz (16/7/99), the Israeli leader told Arafat that “We also have ‘red lines’ that we are not prepared to cross, and we will have to obtain the agreement of both our nations for every move we make.” This is all well and good. For every Palestinian acknowledges that the right of return is their RED LINE that cannot be retracted. Will Mr. Barak, therefore, allow the 4.5 million refugees to exercise this right if they choose to do so?

While the new Israeli leadership has refused to consider a return even to Palestinian administered areas, waves of Jewish and non-Jewish immigrants continue to flood into occupied Palestine. The Central Bureau of Statistics in the Jewish State confirmed that with the arrival of 29,000 immigrants during the first half of 1999, there has been a 14.5% increase over the same period last year. Notwithstanding, Israeli researchers Gershon Shafir and Yoav Peled believe that according to the ultra-orthodox definition of who is a Jew, 20% of those who arrived at the beginning of the 1990s were non-Jews. Additionally, 60% fell into that same category by the mid-1990s.1 Given his obvious determination to maintain this status quo, it is no wonder that Mr. Barak should have appealed to President Clinton to act only as a ‘facilitator’ and not as an ‘enforcer’ in the future negotiations with the Palestinians.

When stripped of their diplomatic garb, the terms ‘facilitator’ and ‘enforcer’ mean nothing to the Israeli political establishment. For what is the difference between Bernadotte as ‘mediator’ and Clinton as ‘facilitator’? Or, on what basis would they accept the latter after they have repudiated the legacy of the former? Was it not Bernadotte who said, “I interpret my role as Mediator not as one involving the handing down of decisions on the future of Palestine, but as one of offering suggestions on the basis of which further discussions might take place and possibly counter-suggestions be put forth looking toward a peaceful settlement of this difficult problem.”2

Yes, the UN Mediator may have failed in his objective to bring about the demilitarization of Jerusalem and the return of the refugees. Yet, he did succeed in one respect. As he intended, Bernadotte succeeded in putting forward recommendations which, 51 years after his death, have remained the internationally accepted legal basis and point of reference for any settlement of the refugee problem. They were enshrined in UN resolution No. 194 in 1948 and have since been reaffirmed every year by the General Assembly. “That the refugees wishing to return to their homes and live in peace with their neighbors should be permitted to do so at the earliest practicable date.”

The right of return as envisaged by Bernadotte is one, which under the present circumstances only requires affirmation and implementation. It is not a privilege that demands debate or recognition. And, herein lies the fundamental difference between Bernadotte the ‘mediator’ and Clinton the ‘facilitator’. While the former maintained that the unconditional right of the refugees to make a free choice should be upheld and guaranteed, the latter believes that it should be defined and negotiated. Because of their unqualified support for the latter, the Israelis will certainly offer no memorials to mark the 51st anniversary of Bernadotte’s murder.

*The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and editor of its Return Review.

Securing Occupation: The Real Meaning
Of The Wye River Memorandum

Dr. Norman G. Finkelstein

(2-3)

 Wye Memorandum

The Wye memorandum is basically divided into two parts, “Further Redeployments” and “Security.” (A third section takes up miscellaneous “Other Issues.”) Bits and pieces comprising some 40 percent of the West Bank are to come under “full” (Area A) or “partial” (Area B) Palestinian jurisdiction before final-status negotiations begin. According to Savir, Rabin was prepared to relinquish “roughly 50 percent” on the eve of final-status negotiations.11 The putative ideological rift between Labor and Likud amounts to perhaps 10 percent of the West Bank. Indeed, the various final-status maps of the Likud all fall within the parameters of Labor’s Allon Plan, retaining for Israel roughly half the West Bank.12 For those who care to hear the truth, the Israeli press has been reporting for years that “there’s almost no difference between Netanyahu’s and Peres’s concepts of the permanent agreement,” indeed, “Sharon and Peres are not far from each other in their perception of the permanent settlement.”13 This pragmatic convergence between Labor and Likud points up, incidentally, that partial withdrawal was the maximum Israel could have hoped for at Oslo. Israel did not effect a “historical compromise” with the Palestinians; only with reality.

Technically, Wye marks a regression from “prior agreements.” At bare minimum the 1995 Israeli-Palestinian Interim Agreement on the West Bank and Gaza Strip (hereafter: Interim Agreement) stipulated a “complete redeployment of Israeli military forces from Area B” before final-status negotiations (Article XIII), placing 30 percent of the West Bank in Area A. The memorandum, however, puts only 18 percent of the West Bank in Area A. Yet, this quibbling over percentages is ultimately beside the point. The “Palestinian Authority” exercises no substantive authority anywhere in the West Bank — except as Israel’s surrogate. The “security” provisions of the Wye Memorandum make this abundantly clear.

The security section of Wye initially observes that “both sides recognize that it is in their vital interests to combat terrorism and fight violence.” Yet to implement this protocol, Wye specifies an action plan only for the Palestinian side: “The Palestinian side will make known its policy for zero tolerance for terror and violence. ... A work plan developed by the Palestinian side will be shared with the U.S. ... to ensure the systematic and effective combat of terrorist organizations. ... In addition to the bilateral Israeli-Palestinian security cooperation, a U.S.-Palestinian committee will ... review the steps being taken to eliminate terrorist cells. ... In addition to the bilateral Israeli-Palestinian security cooperation, a high-ranking U.S.-Palestinian-Israeli committee will ... address the steps being taken to combat terror and terrorist organizations,” and on and on. One would never suspect from this document that, according to the Israeli human rights organization, B’Tselem, many more Palestinians have been killed by Israelis than Israelis by Palestinians since the onset of the Oslo process (356 Palestinians as against 251 Israelis through October 1998). The “vast majority” of killings by Israel, according to Amnesty International, were “unlawful.”14

Wye also repeatedly emphasizes Palestinian responsibility for the vigorous “investigation, prosecution, and punishment” of “terrorist suspects.” Yet according to Amnesty, “there continues to be almost total impunity for unlawful killings of Palestinians” by Israel: “[I]nvestigations are inadequate. The officers responsible rarely appear before an inquiry; if they do so they are rarely punished; if they are punished the sanction is trivial in relation to the loss of life.” To illustrate this last point, Amnesty cites the case of four soldiers convicted of killing a Palestinian motorist: “The court fined each soldier one agora, equivalent to about US$0.03.” Indeed, although Israeli soldiers have killed fully 1,500 Palestinians since the beginning of the intifada, not one has served any prison time.15

The Wye memorandum reeks of the rancid Israeli (and American) discourse on terrorism. Terrorism is a self-generating force. It originates in the “terror support structure,” “terrorists and their structure,” “terrorist organizations and their infrastructure,” “terrorist cells and the support structure that plans, finances, and supplies and abets terror,” “organizations (or wings of organizations...) of a military, terrorist, or violent character,” and — lest we forget — the “external support for terror.”

Detached from its Israeli environment, Palestinian terrorism is always the cause but never the effect of evil: assaulting Israeli innocents is by definition unrelated to Israel’s brutal rule. Thus, to understand terrorism, it is irrelevant that, since the Oslo accord, more than 600 Palestinian homes have been demolished and 140,000 dunums of Palestinian land confiscated. It is also irrelevant that, due primarily to Israel’s illegal imposition of closure on the eve of Oslo, the Palestinian standard of living has fallen by nearly 40 percent, with fully 30% of the workforce unemployed and fully 40 percent of the population living at or below the poverty line.16

Given that terrorism is an implacable negative force, the only means to combat it is an implacable positive force: repression. And in this Manichaean struggle between good and evil, the more repression the better: any restraints will impede the struggle. Accordingly the Wye memorandum gives short shrift to human rights concerns, despatching them in one sentence: “without derogating from the above, the Palestinian Police will ... implement this Memorandum with due regard to internationally accepted norms of human rights and the rule of law. ...” Presumably on account of its exemplary human rights record, Israel is not called upon to do even this much. Indeed, the record does impress. According to Amnesty, even after Oslo, Israel continued to engage in “mass arrests of Palestinians;” place “thousands of Palestinians” under administrative detention without charges or trial, sometimes for “years on end” (“many may have been prisoners of conscience”); “use torture systematically on Palestinian political suspects ... its use was effectively legal, an internationally unprecedented state of affairs” (“this legalization of torture has, over the past five years, if anything, become a more entrenched part of the system in which Palestinian detainees find themselves”); resort to “brutality, amounting to torture or ill-treatment ... at checkpoints”; and conduct “unfair trials ... convictions are almost invariably based exclusively on the accused’s confession, usually extracted by the use of torture and ill-treatment.”17

The Almost Longest Held Prisoner

By Edna Yaghi

(Dedicated in memory of Ziad)

It’s as you said Cherrit, lend not your strength to that which destroys you.

My name is Ziad. Soon, I have heard, I am to be released just as the longest held prisoner was released before me a few weeks ago. My companions in prison have been cockroaches that grow as big as nightmares, mice and weevils. For a long time, I was held in solitary confinement so I had to devise ways to keep myself sane. Perhaps I am not so sane after all, for how could any human hold on to sanity after spending 30 years in an Israeli prison?

It all began during the Battle of Karami, 21 March 1968. I was a newly enlisted soldier with the Jordanian army and the oldest of 10 children. I had never eaten army food before or really had to obey any orders other than those of my father, who at the time, I felt, was too hard on me. Little did I know the real meaning of hardship or cruelty or suffering.

It was not as if I did not love my parents. I loved them dearly. It was not as though I did not care for my younger brothers and sisters. It was only that in my secluded and protected world, I saw myself as an abused young man who was expected to help out with my father’s booming business in his butcher shop right in the heart of Amman, Jordan. My father really did try to make me a man; it’s just that at 20, I really had no conception of what the demands of manhood were. Certainly not incarceration, certainly not torture, and certainly not the loss of my own humanity.

Having served only 1 month in the Jordanian army, while on a short weekend leave, I stormed into my father’s shop and declared, “That’s it! I’m not going to eat army food any longer.”

My father gave me an allowance that would enable me to buy chocolate, cookies and other kinds of food that would sustain me so that I, spoiled as I was, would not have to eat army food. But even this did not satisfy me enough to keep me from deserting.

I was born in Palestine in 1948, just before all Palestinians on the coast were either forced out of their homes and land or were frightened away. Atrocities such as those committed at Deir Yassin by the Israeli Haganah, where an entire village was barbarically wiped out, were still fresh in the minds of my grandparents and parents. Only a few months old and carried in my mother’s arms, I was oblivious and unaware of that sad and tragic journey that separated my family and their beloved orange groves and their homes that they had lovingly built with their own hands. And so, I spent part of my youth growing up in a refugee camp in Jericho, the lowest place on earth and also one of the hottest.

My father was strong and young and brave. Even though he lost all his land and home, he learned how to get milk from a bird and I survived and soon my other brothers and sisters followed in childbirth and my mother learned how to skimp and crawl on her hands and knees when her strength gave out, and how to make silk out of a sow’s ear.

We moved to Jordan and my father raised beef for his butcher store and we did well. Very well. So well that I soon forgot the fact that I was a refugee. But I had to help out with feeding the cattle and the butchering. I thought I was too good for that kind of work, so, in order to escape my duties as a son, I ran away to enlist in the Jordanian army. Army teaches people discipline, and I really did not want to be disciplined. I just wanted to escape home duties and be free from all responsibility. To my dismay and shock, I was not free at all. I had to eat army food and obey army rules. This definitely was not a life for such as me. So I ran away from the Jordanian army and during the Battle of Karami, went over to the Palestinian Freedom fighters in the valley of Karameh, Jordan, who had joined forces with the Jordanian army and together in unity, we defeated the invading Israeli army.

But in total glory of my first real victory, I was defeated and captured by Israeli soldiers. Gone were my days of youth and freedom, gone was my family. I never again would hear my father yell at me, “Come on Ziad, you overslept again! Get up and come and help me open the store.” In fact, never again would I hear my father’s voice at all.

Deep in an Israeli dungeon, unknown to the rest of the world, after being tortured by Israeli soldiers on a daily basis until I fainted, I would recall scenes of home and my mother and father and younger siblings. Their images were all that kept me alive in the beginning.

I knew my father would leave no stone unturned to find me. I knew that he would look everywhere. I heard rumours that he in fact did. But, my name was never given out to the Red Cross by the Israeli authorities and as far as all the world was concerned, I did not exist. My father soon gave up hope of ever finding me and I was told by a fellow prisoner who knew my father that he dedicated his life to raising my other siblings and had given me up for dead.

Beatings and torture made me rebellious. I kept getting into trouble in those Israeli dungeons deep under the ground, and solitary confinement became the norm for troublemakers like myself. I grew a beard. At 30, I felt and looked like an old man.

The only solace I had in the sweltering summer heat was to fall asleep dreaming of my family and how beautiful my life had been when I was with them in Jordan. Why didn’t I appreciate what I had then? Why did I run away from the Jordanian army and why did I, more than anything, run away from my parents, the people who loved me more than themselves? Why had I been such a careless fool?

Later, after spending years and years in Israeli prisons and being shifted from one Ansar hellhole to another, I tried to be a bulwark for newly captured Palestinian youths who faced torture and some certain death. I tried to teach them ways so that their torture would not hurt so much; but no matter how brave you are, no matter how you brace yourself to resist pain, there is no escape and no mercy and even an old pro like me would end up screaming and cussing at those animal-like and brutal torturers who left no trace of anything human in helpless and defenseless prisoners such as myself and others.

I prayed to God to release me from my bondage, to free me from my pain, to let me die to escape the living death I was forced to endure. I am 51 years old now. It is not as though I have celebrated my birthdays, only kept track of my days spent in prison to prove to myself that I am still sane. But I seriously doubt my own sanity and I have no grasp about life outside prison walls at all. I am not used to the kind of laughter that shakes your belly when you laugh.

My mother will not recognize me. If she has grey hair, my own is white. If she is bent with age, my body is bent from affliction. Prison friends told me that my father died a few years ago from cancer and that all my brothers no longer live in Jordan. My eyes, still blue but faded, are unused to sunlight and the whites have browned from the tears and pain that they have witnessed. It is rumoured that I am soon to be set free. But where will I go, and what will I do? No woman will marry an old man like me and even if I were ever able to find someone foolish enough to marry me, I am too weak to work to support a family or even myself. My life has been destroyed and I have nothing to live for at all, not even the hope of being set free at last.

Though I am a prisoner of war and have been one for 31 years, I was never treated like one. Forget about the Geneva Convention. I cannot turn back time or erase all that I have suffered. But my greatest wish is that those who persecuted me for no reason, those who so inhumanely tortured me, would one day feel and know what it is like to lose your humanity.

The Jewish History, Jewish Religion

Political Consequences

(19 -19)

By Israel Shahak

The persistent attitudes of classical Judaism toward non-Jews strongly influence its followers. Orthodox Jews and those who can be regarded as its continuators, zionists. Through the latter it also influences the policies of the State of Israel. Since 1967, as Israel becomes more and more ‘Jewish’, so its policies are influenced more by Jewish ideological considerations than by those of a coldly conceived imperial interest. This ideological influence is not usually perceived by foreign experts, who tend to ignore or downplay the influence of the Jewish religion on Israeli policies. This explains why many of their predictions are incorrect.

In fact, more Israeli government crises are caused by religious reasons, often trivial, than by any other cause. The space devoted by the Hebrew press to discussion of the constantly occurring quarrels between the various religious groups, or between the religious and the secular, is greater than that given any other subject, except in times of war or of security-related tension. At the time of writing, early August 1993, some topics of major interest to readers of the Hebrew press are: whether soldiers killed in action who are sons of non-Jewish mothers will be buried in a segregated area in Israeli military cemeteries; whether Jewish religious burial associations, who have a monopoly over the burial of all Jews except kibbutz members, will be allowed to continue their custom of circumcising the corpses of non-circumcised Jews before burying them (and without asking the family’s permission); whether the import of non-kosher meat to Israel, banned unofficially since the establishment of the state, will be allowed or banned by law. There are many more issues of this kind which are of a much greater interest to the Israeli- Jewish public than, let us say, the negotiations with the Palestinians and Syria.

The attempts made by a few Israeli politicians to ignore the factors of ‘Jewish ideology’ in favour of purely imperial interests have led to disastrous results. In early 1974, after its partial defeat in the Yom Kippur War, Israel had a vital interest in stopping the renewed influence of the PLO, which had not yet been recognised by the Arab states as the solely legitimate representative of the Palestinians. The Israeli government conceived of a plan to support Jordanian influence in the West Bank, which was quite considerable at the time. When King Hussein was asked for his support, he demanded a visible quid pro quo. It was arranged that his chief West Bank supporter, Sheikh Ja’abri of Hebron, who ruled the southern part of the West Bank with an iron fist and with approval of then Defence minister Moshe Dayan, would give a party for the region’s notables in the courtyard of his palatial residence in Hebron. The party, in honour of the king’s birthday, would feature the public display of Jordanian flags and would begin a pro-Jordanian campaign. But the religious settlers in the nearby Kiryat-Arba, who were only a handful at the time, heard about the plan and threatened Prime Minister Golda Meir and Dayan with vigorous protests since, as they put it, displaying a flag of a ‘non-Jewish state’ within the Land of Israel contradicts the sacred principle which states that this land ‘belongs’ only to Jews. Since this principle is accepted by all zionists, the government had to bow to their demands and order Sheikh Ja’abri not to display any Jordanian flags. Thereupon Ja’abri, who was deeply humiliated, cancelled the party and, at the Fez meeting of the Arab League which occurred soon after, King Hussein voted to recognise the PLO as the sole representative of the Palestinians. For the bulk of Israeli-Jewish public the current negotiations about ‘autonomy’ are likewise influenced more by such Jewish ideological considerations than by any others.

The conclusion from this consideration of Israeli policies, supported by an analysis of classical Judaism, must be that analyses of Israeli policy-making which do not emphasise the importance of its unique character as a ‘Jewish state’ must be mistaken. In particular, the facile comparison of Israel to other cases of Western imperialism or to settler states, is incorrect. During apartheid, the land of South Africa was officially divided into 87 per cent which ‘belonged’ to the whites and 13 per cent which was said officially to ‘belong’ to the Blacks. In addition, officially sovereign states, embodied with all the symbols of sovereignty, the so-called Bantustans, were established. But ‘Jewish ideology’ demands that no part of the Land of Israel can be recognised as ‘belonging’ to non-Jews and that no signs of sovereignty, such as Jordanian flags, can be officially allowed to be displayed. The world through the eyes of the media

By Rana Dajani

Reading through Newsweek magazine (3 May 1999), an article about Sudan caught my attention. It was entitled "Out of Bondage". It related how a group of Christians (CSI: Christian Solidarity International) were attacking slavery in Sudan by putting a price tag on freedom. It also related accusations against the Islamic-fundamentalist regime in Khartoum of arming and financing local Arab militia that prey on blacks in the southern war zone, claiming that these tribes in the name of Islam were abducting women and children and treating them brutally without mercy.

My initial reaction as a Muslim and an avid supporter of Sudan was defensive, but as I read through the article, I became convinced that something wrong was going on in Sudan. I tried to analyse the facts to create an explanation that would satisfy my previous beliefs and incorporate the new facts. I could not reach a satisfactory conclusion other than that slavery was being practiced in the name of Islam with no plausible explanation. This conclusion left me somewhat skeptical and not very comfortable.

After a week or so, I happened upon another article in a magazine which I frequently read, namely Al-Mujtama (issue No. 1347, 27 April-3 May 1999 [Year 30]), addressing the same issue. The article contained a larger number of facts and went back a couple of years reviewing the history of the problem and the institutions involved, explaining and giving a comprehensive and satisfactory view of the issue—an approach very different from Newsweek, which article had, for the most part, related a few stories and quotes, leaving the readers to draw their own conclusions without giving enough facts.

The Al-Mujtama article explained that the abductions and looting were a result of tribal feuds and had nothing to do with religious differences. It also went on to say that the Sudanese government was fighting these practices and had called a press conference to clear up the accusations by the human rights groups. The article shed light on the Christian Solidarity International (CSI) organization, which had a long history of fighting Sudan because it is an Islamic country. The article went on to explain that by focusing on the issue of slavery, CSI conjured to gather sympathy and credibility for itself and resentment and evidence against the Sudanese government. This was achieved by pouring money into Sudan supposedly to free slaves while to the contrary it fueled the trade and encouraged more slavery among the tribes in the South.

One's reaction at first to these two seemingly contradictory reports is: “Who is telling the truth?” But as one reads more and uses one’s common sense and logic, one can develop a coherent picture of what is really going on. Nevertheless, two points should be taken into consideration whenever one is analysing news: first, the power of the media in forming people’s views of current and international issues and second, who is in control of the media.

To avoid such misconceptions among the general public in the future, and to allow people to develop a true picture of what is really going on, the following points are of utmost importance:

  1. More coverage of events supported with more facts;
  2. More writers reflecting various points of view;
  3. A wider distribution of news to reach a wider range of people.

Bringing the truth to the public is a responsibility hanging upon the shoulders of every journalist who has a conscience.

Mr Brown, live up to your promise of last year, just ignore the word “countries”. You did in April for the Kosovar refugees. Do it for the Palestinians, too.
