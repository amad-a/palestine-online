Why do so many Palestinians object to the Wye Plantation agreement?

The Wye Plantation agreement, signed in an impressive White House ceremony on 23 October, was the consummate outcome of 18 months of intermittent talks between Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority and Benyamin Netanyahu's Israeli Government. It stipulates a redeployment of the Israeli occupation army from 13.1% of the West Bank, 3 % of which will be converted into a "nature reserve" under joint Palestinian-Israeli control. Moreover, the Palestinians will not be free to build houses in the nature reserve, suggesting the Israeli withdrawal is only a token and symbolic in nature.
When and if the agreement is implemented, the area under PA exclusive and partial control will increase to 40% of the West Bank, including 17% (area A) and 23% (area B). Now, the PA has full control over only 3% of the West Bank.
In return, the agreement requires the PA to take several draconian measures against Palestinian forces opposed to the Oslo Accords, particularly the Islamic Resistance Movement, Hamas. According to Palestinian and Israeli sources, the security annex attached to the agreement stipulates unprecedented draconian measures against "terrorist groups" and their infrastructure.
This conceivably includes charitable, educational and youth institutions run by or associated with the Islamist movement and other anti-Oslo organizations. Predictably, this could bring the originally uneasy PA-Hamas relations to an unprecedented level of tension. What makes the prospects of a collision more likely than anytime in the past is the fact that the agreement makes a conditional relationship between the phased Israeli redeployment and the measures taken by the PA against Hamas. Thus, according to the agreement, the Israeli redeployment will be carried out in several phases, depending on the extent and scope of PA measures against the opposition as stipulated in the Wye Plantation accords.
Thus, aside from the security pact, which many observers believe will result in serious human rights violations in the occupied territories, most Palestinians seem to be already indignant at the "submissive terms" of the agreement, including the following:
First: The third phase of redeployment. The agreement virtually ignored the third phase, considered the most important of the three redeployment phases stipulated in the Oslo Accords. Faced with adamant Israeli refusal to withdraw from further territory in the West Bank, the Palestinian leadership apparently succumbed to Israeli intransigence, effectively agreeing to phase out the third phase and go straight to the final status talks.
The dilution or obliteration of the third phase was done in a way enabling both sides to claim victory. The Palestinians claim they didn't give in on the third phase, arguing that a bi-lateral committee would discuss the matter. For their part, the Israelis insist they wouldn't withdraw from more than 1% of the West Bank in the context of the third phase, a clear trivialization of the matter. Nevertheless, the consent by the Israelis and the Palestinians to begin final status talks before and without the third phase suggests that the Palestinians caved in. According to the Oslo Accords, the Israeli army is supposed to vacate the entirety of the West Bank by the end of the third phase, with the exceptions of East Jerusalem, the settlements and the military positions.
Theoretically, this would have left the Palestinians with as much as 80% of the West Bank ahead of the final status talks. In other words, the Palestinians would be in a strong negotiating position vis-à-vis the final status talks. However, now with the third phase practically phased out, the Israelis have succeeded in forcing the Palestinians into an inferior position ahead of the final status talks. Hence, Palestinian disappointment and indignation.
Second: The Palestinian National Charter. The Wye Plantation agreement obliges PA chairman Yasser Arafat to convene a special session of the Palestine National Council (PNC) in order to formally rescind the National Charter. The rescinding of the Charter at this time amounts to stripping the Palestinian people of the remaining cards still in their hands. What makes this especially lamentable is the fact that there is no Israeli reciprocity in return for the canceling of the Charter. For example, Israel has not recognized the right of the Palestinian people to freedom, Statehood and self-determination. In short, the annulment of the Palestinian Charter can thus be considered a serious concession by the Palestinian Authority.
Third: The Palestinian prisoners. According to the Wye Plantation agreement, Israel will release only 750 Palestinian prisoners out of over 3,000 prisoners languishing in Israeli jails. This means that as many as 2,250 prisoners will remain behind bars in Israel, most of whom are identified with the Islamic movements and other secular groups opposed to the Oslo Accords. According to the Hebron Agreement, Israel should free virtually all Palestinian prisoners before the beginning of the final status talks, which will begin in the first week of November, according to the Wye Plantation agreement.
Fourth: The Palestinian State. For many months, Yasser Arafat has been threatening to formally declare an independent Palestinian State by 4 May 1999. However, it seems that the Palestinian leader has abandoned plans to that effect, which suggests that Arafat perhaps didn't really mean what he was saying to that effect all along. The implication is very real. If Arafat agreed to lay aside this strategic goal of the Palestinian movement, then he likewise can be expected to extend the term of the Oslo Accords beyond 4 May of next year. Indeed, with only seven months to that designated date, it seems unlikely that the interim agreement would be finalized by then. This suggests that the talks will be dragged out for many months, even years, during which the Palestinian leadership would be exhausted into giving the Israelis more and more concessions.
History
The Wye Plantation agreement should not be understood as an historical breakthrough or anything of this sort. Indeed, the agreement doesn't even constitute a concordance on interim issues that ought to be settled ahead of the final status talks, which will tackle such insurmountable subjects as the future of Jerusalem, the settlements, the refugees, water and the borders of the would-be Palestinian entity.
The Palestinian-Israeli conflict, after all, is not over 10% or 13% of the West Bank, but rather over Palestine itself.
Indeed, one could say candidly that the agreement, if implemented, will only bring the two parties, and the world at large, closer to the real issues, the nitty-gritty, of the conflict.
