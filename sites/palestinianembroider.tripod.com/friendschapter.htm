Friends

The American Connection

By Michael S. Ladah

In elementary and middle school I had a few but very good friends. Two of them in particular were friends whom I knew not only in school, but also after school in our neighborhood and in their homes and in our home. Three of us, Ghassan, Maurice and I formed what we called a cultural club when we were 10 years old. During the week, at least two of the three members would do homework together in the home of one of us, have a snack, go out to play for a while and then at dusk each would go to his home which was only a few short blocks away.

Our weekend in those days was a split weekend; we went to school Monday through Thursday, were off on Friday, went back to school on Saturday and then took a day off again on Sunday. The idea behind this split weekend was so that both Moslems and Christians would have a day off on their respective day of the Sabbath, while the other day was an extra day off. Membership in our little cultural club was not based on any religion but the three of us happened to be Christian. So, our weekend get-togethers took place on Fridays since each of us wanted, and were required, to spend Sundays with our respective families. On Fridays we had a lot of time to read, exchange books and discuss the philosophy of life in general. The most popular publication in our club was an Arabic language weekly children's magazine published in Egypt under the title of Sindibad Al-Bahri (Sinbad the Sailor). Each one of us had a set of issues of Sindibad magazines and we looked forward to our weekend meetings to discuss the different feature stories and exchange different issues. We also read books in these meetings, sometimes reading the same book taking turns reading aloud while the others listened. Since the mother tong of each of the three of us was Arabic, we read Arabic books and publications in the beginning; but then we started to read in English those books which did not have Arabic translation, especially later as we were required to read in English for school. The three of us also contributed books to the club for use by any of the club members. Maurice and I did not contribute a lot of books to the club, but Ghassan was always bringing all kinds of books that we found very interesting. Some of the books which I remember from Ghassan's collection was Mother by Maksim Gorky and From the House of the Dead by Vyodor Destoyvsky. Most of the books which were contributed by Ghassan, we later realized, reflected his interest in Russia and the Communist Revolution. This interest was later to cause him a lot of grief with the authorities in Ramallah and later cause him continuous "administrative" detention by the Israeli authorities during the Israeli occupation of the West Bank. This chronic detention in jails administered by the Israeli authorities, and not fit for humans, caused Ghassan to fall ill and eventually die at a young age.

Ghassan, Maurice and I all went to the same school, the National College otherwise known to many in Ramallah as the Abu Rayya School. It was my first exposure in Ramallah to a non-Catholic school. Although Abu Rayya was a "private" school, it was relatively inexpensive and its tuition was negotiable. The principal, who was an Arab American, used to make frequent visits to the United States and solicit donations for maintaining his school and for new community projects in Ramallah. Many of the students who attended his school did not pay any tuition, or paid a very small portion only, especially those who were judged by the principal as deserving a break, either because of their economic condition or because of their high intellect.

Besides Maurice and Ghassan, I had two other close friends in middle school; these were Vanto and Sari. Because of where they lived, however, Vanto and Sari did not hang out with the rest of my friends in my neighborhood. We usually went to the movies together and talked about these movies in school. Vanto always managed to keep our interest in Hollywood very high by bringing magazines with articles about movie stars, movies and upcoming Hollywood events. Vanto and Sari continued to be close friends until I changed schools one more time at the end of my elementary schooling.

One summer, three years after I started Abu Rayya School, my father heard that one of the best private secondary schools in the Middle East, which was located in Ramallah, was accepting Palestinian refugees and had an arrangement with the United Nations Relief Works Agency (UNRWA) for receiving a discounted tuition directly from them for such students. My father immediately enrolled me into the Friends Boys School in el-Bireh, Ramallah's twin city. I was not very excited about changing schools especially that I had to leave my friends behind in Abu Rayya, until I found out that Ghassan was also moving to the Friends.

There were two Friends Schools in the Ramallah area, the Friends Boys School in el-Bireh and the Friends Girls School in Ramallah. According to the history records of the Ramallah City Hall, the Friends Girls School was established in Ramallah in 1889 and the Friends Boys School in el-Bireh in 1901. The two cities were side by side, one starting where the other ended, and most didn't know exactly where the dividing line was in most areas. Rashed Haddad, the father of the original Ramallah clans, came to Ramallah from the east Jordan Mountains of el-Shoubak, and settled in Ramallah in 1550. The twin cities are about the same age, inhabited for hundreds and possibly for thousands of years. Actually the settling of Ramallah by Rashed Haddad and his descendants was the latest settling known. When I lived in Ramallah I used to go to places which we used to discover in the surrounding fields and mountains which seemed to have been inhabited in ancient times and which must have been abandoned for hundreds and perhaps thousands of years. Some of our family friends in Ramallah once took us to a field just outside Ramallah in what today is al-Tiereh neighborhood. In that field, in between the bushes and under the dirt and tree leaves, they uncovered a hole in the ground just barely large enough for a person to squeeze through. The hole had a hinged wooden cover on it that looked extremely old for the wood looked old and rotten. As we opened the door, we found a set of stairs, made of crudely arranged stones, leading down to a cave-like structure. We went down and saw a large area, which could hold about 50 people, all carved into the dirt and rocks with stones on the floor all around the walls of the structure arranged as benches, which must have been used for seating. At one end of the cave there was a large stone with flat smooth top standing in the middle of the end wall. There was evidence of oil and candle wax strewn all over the stone and all around the floor by the end wall. A simple wooden cross was hung on the wall over the stone. The cross was carved of olive tree wood and was smoothed, but only to a crude finish. The place must have been a church used by early Christians during the time when Christians were persecuted during one or another of the persecution campaigns in the first few centuries AD. Our friends jokingly explained that this was their find and considered it their hideaway church. One of the ladies told us how she liked to come to this 'church' alone sometimes and sit there and pray, with no one around and no light whatsoever except the light of the candles which she brought with her or kept there for future 'service'. There were many sites like this one that we occasionally stumbled on or were taken to by friends.

The twin cities of Ramallah and el-Bireh go way back when the area was last settled by two families (tribes or clans) around 1500, one Moslem family in el-Bireh and the other, Rashed Haddad and his bunch, a Christian family, in Ramallah. There were no Moslems living in Ramallah ever since Haddad settled the town, and perhaps never before. That continued until approximately 1967 when additional refugees left their hometowns and villages on the West Bank closer to the borders of Israel and moved into the area of Ramallah and el-Bireh. But until then, no Moslems had lived in the city of Ramallah. Similarly, no Christian families had lived in el-Bireh except the Rafeedi family. The Rafeedis were Christians but decided to settle in el-Bireh hundreds of years ago, but much later than 1500. Judging by their name, the Rafeedis probably came originally from Rafeedia, a small town near Nablus. When I lived in Ramallah, every Sunday morning one could time the clock by the sight of the entire Rafeedi family strolling down the el-Bireh street. They would cross the center of Ramallah, later built up as a fountain and called al-Manarah, down the Latin street and into the Latin (Catholic) church which was the site of my first elementary school in Ramallah. This must have continued for many years because el-Bireh had no churches and had no plans to build a church for just the one Rafeedi family. Conversely, Ramallah had no mosques and had no plans for the construction of such when no Moslems even lived in Ramallah in the fifties and until the mid 1960s..

Israel then occupied the West Bank in 1967 and caused a change in the demography of Ramallah. Now there were many Moslems living in Ramallah and there were many Christians living in el-Bireh including the Rafeedi family. The Rafeedis worked hard on trying to convince the powers to be in el-Bireh to build a church there, now that the Rafeedis multiplied in numbers and had other Christians living in el-Bireh with them. But those powers won't budge. Finally, an opportunity presented itself to the Rafeedis. They learned that the Moslem population in Ramallah was requesting Ramallah's city council for a permit to build a mosque there. Rumors had it that the two cities got together and agreed that a mosque could be built in Ramallah and a church could be built in el-Bireh. That agreement unfortunately was not implemented, or simply implemented one way. As of this writing, there are approximately 15 mosques in Ramallah but not one church in el-Bireh. The Rafeedis and their Christian neighbors, who by now must be a large minority in el-Bireh if they are still there, still have to go down the el-Bireh street, crossing the center of Ramallah by al-Manarah, down the Latin street and into the Latin (Catholic) church which was the site of my first elementary school.

The two Friends schools, the boys school in el-Bireh and the girls school in Ramallah, were run by the Quaker missions from Richmond Indiana. The two schools were the most popular schools in the West Bank and East Jordan, and among expatriate Palestinian and Jordanian families who worked in other Arab countries. Because of this, and while I was attending the Friends Boys School, both schools had a large boarding population which kept the schools buzzing with activities all day long, well after classes were over. The faculty of both schools was mixed, some American volunteers who came from the U.S. on rotation assignments and some Arab and third country nationals, most of them graduates of American universities.

The Friends Boys School had two main focal personalities. The first was the Head Master or Principal of the school who traditionally was a U.S. person. The Head Master's job was rotational, changing incumbents every few years, and must have been easy because we, as students, had very little interaction with the Head Master and hardly saw him except during chapel assembly three mornings a week. My contemporaries were Dr. Reynolds and then Mr. Smuck. They were known for being disciplinarians but did not have the reputation of their predecessor Dr. Jones who, legend has it, kept a whip for those who were difficult to discipline. The legend of the whip continued on, well after Dr. Jones left. Although the effects of the legend of the whip faded with every passing year, that whip which none of my contemporaries ever saw continued to be a deterrent to those who may have otherwise contemplated acts unbecoming of a Friends student.

The second focal character was the Dean of the Friends Boys School, a job that was most powerful on campus because it was a permanent position and which interacted with students on more than one level. In addition to being the academic dean, the Friends Boys School Dean was a disciplinarian and a teacher. Our Dean was Mr. Farid Tabri, who at our time appeared to be, and upper classmen and alumni confirmed it, a permanent fixture of the Friends Boys School. When I arrived at the Friends, Mr. Tabri had been there almost thirty years and had been in the position of Dean for many years. No matter how much one can try to describe Mr. Tabri, words can never even start to describe him. It would be difficult to describe in words his serene personality, his loyalty to the school, his commitment to teaching and his admiration for those with whom he worked at the Friends. Mr. Tabri held special admiration for and often spoke very kindly of Dr. Totah who had been an Arab American benefactor and contributor to the school. It is easy for me now to remember Dr. Totah because Mr. Tabri had an oil painting of Dr. Totah hanging in the hallway outside his office. Under the picture was a chair which was specifically designated for trouble-students to sit in and wait to be called in to see Mr. Tabri whenever a student was sent to the dean by one or another of the teachers for discipline.

Mr. Tabri taught us Arabic and Arabic Literature, and was the most renowned authority on the Arabic language throughout the West Bank and East Jordan, and possibly beyond. He was also an expert on the Holy Koran, and had memorized it. He was an expert on the interpretation of the Holy Koran and Hadith (Tradition of the Prophet Muhammad), although he always refrained from making any such claims or from making religious interpretations deferring to those of the Moslem faith. He could recite every chapter and verse from the Holy Koran and always recited verses in support of his language teachings and explanations in the class. Mr. Tabri was a Palestinian Christian whose love for Islam and the Arabic language lead him to join the Al-Azhar University, the famous university in Cairo, Egypt, renowned for its specialty in Islam, Islamic Studies and the Arabic Language. Farid Tabri was accepted to Al-Azhar under the cover of Islam. He often told us that non-Moslems were not accepted to Al-Azhar because of its specialty subjects. Nonetheless, Farid Tabri got himself admitted to Al-Azhar in spite of the university's prohibition on the admission of Christians. In spite of his wealth of knowledge and expertise on the subject, Mr. Tabri was the most humble person I ever met. He used to tell us stories about his adventures while he was at Al-Azhar, but he never bragged or seemed to imply that he knew more about the subject than other teachers. His humility often led to some challenges from other faculty members who pretended to be experts on the Arabic language and put him in an awkward position with his students, because he did not like to tell us that another faculty member was 'wrong'. He quietly asked us to refer to earlier lessons and to verses from the Koran to bring his point across without ruffling anybody's feathers.

One of Mr. Tabri's unique teaching styles was his insistence that each of his students acquire and keep a standard pocket size notebook, or diary, in which the student must record any vocabulary or synonyms or explanations that Mr. Tabri would dictate, and he did that frequently. It was amazing how quickly we acquired and maintained that diary especially that most of his quizzes where from the notes he dictated to us and because of the dire consequences that he outlined for not maintaining the diary. When it was time, in his opinion, to clarify a matter in the Arabic language, he would say mufakkerah (diary), and in response every one in the class would have to lift his diary up high above his head to make sure every one had it before he started dictating. Those who did not have their diaries had to see him after class to explain and, when the explanation was not satisfactory as it usually was the case, to receive a lecture on that old whip of Dr. Jones or receive instructions for the appropriate detention. The diary was an excellent idea; we learned so much about the Arabic language, much more than students in other schools, especially about idioms that were commonly misused to mean one thing when in reality they meant the exact opposite. Mr. Tabri coordinated an Arabic poetry competition annually for the entire school, similar to the competitions that the al Jahiliyyah (pre Islam) Arabs held in Souk Okaz (the market of Okaz).

Another of the highlights of Mr. Tabri's teaching activities was his ability to integrate the etiquette of visits and table manners, an activity which culminated in the annual dinner which he hosted for the graduating class at his home. Mr. Tabri used to enjoy watching and listening to his students imitate him and make fun of his teaching style during school gatherings. He had two lovely daughters who worked at the school and were prominent members of the school choir, were musically talented and contributed so much to the mornings chapel assemblies through piano concerts and recitals which made chapel much more pleasant and more bearable. Mr. Tabri also had a son in school who was one or two classes ahead of me and whom I knew very casually but did not see after he graduated. Thirty some years later, in the mid 80's as my family and I were returning to Dhahran from a vacation in the U.S. Virgin Islands through New York's JFK airport, we transited the then Pan Am terminal to connect with a Pan Am flight to Dhahran. As I handed the tickets and passports to the agent at the check-in counter, I got busy organizing my documents in my brief case. With my head in the briefcase I heard a voice ask, "Are you the same Michael who attended the Friends Boys School in Ramallah?" I couldn't believe my ears, nor could I believe my eyes when the agent introduced himself as Wadi' Tabri, the same son of Mr. Tabri whom I casually knew as the son of my great teacher and Dean at the Friends Boys School in Ramallah. Mr. Farid Tabri has since passed away but I know that his memory will always live on, in the minds and hearts of those Friends Boys School alumni who knew him.

One other old timer among the faculty of the Friends in Ramallah was Mr. Khouri, another Christian Palestinian from Jiffna who always wore an Arab thobe. When I arrived at the Friends, he also had been there for a long time, possibly over thirty years. Mr. Khouri was a boarding teacher. He taught lower grades Arabic and Arab History and he taught me Religion (Bible) during my first year at the Friends. During the Religion period, our class was split into two groups, the Christians studied Bible with Mr. Khouri and the Moslems studied Koran with someone else. Mr. Khouri was one of those great teachers who would exaggerate and dwell considerably on a subject to bring a point across. I did not have much contact with Mr. Khouri except during Bible class, or when he substituted for another of our teachers, but that was enough to convince me that his was a no-nonsense teaching style and that he was a very capable and kind character. He called every one of his students with the Arabic adjective 'shater' (clever or good boy). Any one who would raise his hand in class was certain to get the standard response from Mr. Khouri "Eish ya shater?" which literally meant 'What is it, clever?'

Mr. Adib Musallam, another of the great high school teachers of Palestine was one of those teachers who, I imagined, was following me wherever I went. He had taught me mathematics at Abu-Rayya, the National College, and as I was surprised to see my friend Ghassan at the Friends on the first day of school in September of 1954, I also discovered that Mr. Musallam was going to be my math teacher at the Friends. While I was not especially excited about Mr. Musallam's quizzes, finding a teacher whom I already knew was definitely a relief. Mr. Musallam had a very familiar and a very typical face, very comfortable to look at, one that would make a person wonder after seeing him for the first time 'where have I seen this face before?' He was a relaxed, kind teacher, the dream of every reasonably intelligent student, but he was a very firm disciplinarian, especially with a few trouble-students in class. Mr. Musallam had been teaching for so long, that there were very few families in Ramallah and its surrounding area who did not have one or another of its members having been taught at one time or another by Mr. Musallam. Most of his students at the Friends had family who had had Mr. Musallam for a teacher at one of the Ramallah area schools. Mr. Musallam knew his students by name and knew their relation to students whom he had taught in earlier years. He used only one threat in class when any of my classmates gave him a hard time: "Wala, I taught your brother", Mr. Musallam would admonish the troublemaker. Or, he would say: "Wala, I taught your mother" or "Wala, I taught your father." This was enough for the troublemaker to behave once and for all, for it was obvious that Mr. Musallam knew the nuclear secrets of that student and at least one other member of his family. Most of us in class imagined that all that Mr. Musallam had to do, to put us in our place, was simply send a message to our relatives whom he had taught in earlier years and complain about our behavior. That was deterrent enough to stop us from committing any mischief in his class.

Nevertheless, there was one students who always seemed to get in trouble with Mr. Musallam and irritate him; that was Makram, who had no clue what math was all about and was not about to have anyone, let alone Mr. Musallam, pull a fast one and change that. With Makram, when it came to math, ignorance was bliss and he wanted that to stand. Makram used to always carry a tennis racket with his books, although he played tennis infrequently; in class one was always under his desk with the books. Because he was bored during math class, he would pick up the racket and pretend it was a guitar that he would pretend playing while Mr. Musallam was writing on the board. Makram would get in trouble when Mr. Musallam would turn around quickly to make a point, and would get extremely irritated to see Makram playing around. During one of his tests, Mr. Musallam was careful about what to expect from Makram. After he gave out the test sheets, Mr. Musallam faced the class for the first few minutes then he walked over to the bookcase that was next to the chalkboard. We thought he was looking at the titles in the glass-covered case with his back to the class. Soon enough, and before he even turned around, Mr. Musallam called on Makram: "Wala, Ana shayfak minelazaz." Which meant, 'I can see you in the glass'. Makram was in trouble for he was caught asking his neighbor to let him cheat.

Mr. Musallam was an excellent math teacher. He took his teaching job seriously, used to come to class prepared, and was always willing and ready to demonstrate how to construct geometrical figures and would be carrying all kind of tools, colored chalk and pieces of string. Everything I learned about math, in high school and in college, I owe to Mr. Musallam. He is one whose children I came to know well later especially the kind Diana and the pleasant Nabil. He is one of those teachers whom I and my classmates will never forget.

One day, I think it was during one of Mr. Musallam's classes while he was going through the proof of Pythagorean theorem, there was a knock on the door. The class was in the building adjacent to the auditorium and the classroom door had a small glass window in the middle of the door. Most of us who were politically aware could recognize just outside the door two infamous mabaheth (detectives) who were very well known and despised by the students in Ramallah; both of them were natives of two villages outside the city. The school secretary was with them. She came in, whispered in Mr. Musallam's ears and he called for Ghassan, my friend and my colleague in our little cultural club. Ghassan got up, went with the school secretary and walked out the door to be hand cuffed and led away to jail. That incident had the most sobering effect on most of us students who were politically active in school and outside school. It made us realize that political activity came with a price and those who were not willing to pay that price had better stop their games. It was the last time I was in class with Ghassan. Although he came out of jail a few years later, he spent a considerable part of his life in jail accused of being a communist. If he was tried, it must have been in one of those kangaroo courts where one would have no chance if accused of political activities "contrary to the national interest" with the "intent of demeaning the government."

Ghassan's arrest was a typical abuse of political activism and individual liberties, not only by the Jordanian government, but also by most governments of the Middle East. Like most students in the West Bank during the 1950s, Ghassan had beliefs and opinions that did not necessarily conform to those held by the ruling class. When he was arrested, he was a political prisoner. He was not a criminal. He did not carry weapons, he did not plant bombs nor was he involved in any physical activity that caused or threatened any physical harm to the life, health or property of any individual or group. All students, and most of the population, believed that their government at that time was going in the wrong direction. There were no civil liberties, there was no respect for human rights, and there was no sharing of governance by legitimate representatives of the people. The Parliament, which at that time was a joke, had been suspended and martial law had been declared, as this had been the government's escape whenever it encountered heavy political opposition and the people demanded political reform. Political parties were banned in their infancy and as soon as they appeared to threaten the ruling few. Public gatherings (gatherings of three or more individuals) were banned for they formed a threat to those who were in control.

The relation between the government and the people was adversarial, one of "them versus us". The secret police, generally referred to throughout the Arab World as the 'mabaheth' or the 'mukhabarat', were in control of the streets. They knew everything that was going on and everyone that was doing it. They often arrested a person because he or she was suspected of "subversive" activities. Those who were in power defined what constituted subversive activities. For them, subversion consisted of every activity that even remotely resembled any expression of dissatisfaction with the present rule or appeared, in the minds of the mukhabarat, to threaten the governance of those who were in power. The mass media was controlled by the ruling power. What was transmitted to the eyes and ears of the public was shaped by the interest of the government. Hence, the distribution of leaflets, by political activists, that quoted international news media regarding events in Jordan was at that time labeled as subversive. The distribution of leaflets analyzing the governments stand or action on certain issues of interest to the public was "subversive activity". The desire by any one or group to share in the governance was interpreted by those in power as a threat to their power and a challenge to their authority. They knew that if they shared their power of governance, they would stand the chance of loosing their power.

The governments of the Arab World in the 1950s fought the "subversive activities" with the power of the secret police and the military, instead of fighting it with ideas and information, because they knew their control of the people was not legitimate and the truth was their enemy. As the governments cracked down more on the protests of dissatisfaction, the people got angrier and angrier and peaceful protests turned into mass hysteria. I will not forget the protest marches and demonstrations that were organized in Ramallah, Jerusalem, Nablus, and other West Bank cities when I was in high school. I will not forget one demonstration that I found myself leading from the Friends Boys School to the town center to meet other marches that had been started in other parts of the town and which converged on al-Manarah, the town center. As the demonstration got bigger and bigger, it got out of hand and turned into chaos and anarchy in spite of all good and peaceful intentions. As part of the demonstration arrived a building that had been rented by the US Information Service and converted to an 'American Library', the protest march got totally out of hand. Bands of the demonstrators attacked the building, snatched all of the books, bookshelves and other furniture, piled them up outside the building and torched them to express their dissatisfaction with the support of the United States to those who were in power and their dictatorial powers under the monarchy. I also remember the curfews that were imposed immediately thereafter and the troops, the Bedouin forces of the Arab Legion of the Jordanian Army, and the army tanks that occupied the town center. There were lives that were lost; there were lives that were destroyed, there were families that lost their breadwinners for years and there were more children made street beggars.

As a political prisoner, Ghassan was quickly whisked away to far away jails in the eastern desert and no one could dare communicate with him lest that person fall under suspicion, by the authorities, of being "subversive" also. Arab rulers and Arab governments in the 1950s had absolute power over the people and were determined to keep that power. Unfortunately, little has changed in most of the Arab World, and what little now seems to be changing is changing in the wrong direction. As more and more Arab rulers remain in power for decades, they are afforded the opportunity to change the individual dictatorships and one-party rules into dictatorial dynasties and perpetual one-party dictatorships.

Mr. Musallam taught me for two years and then I had different math teachers in upper classes. After I left the Friends in 1958 I did not see Mr. Musallam until 1971 in Detroit, when he was visiting his son Nabil and his daughter, Diana, who lived there. For me it was a very emotional re-union.

At the time I attended the Friends, there were even some third country nationals who were teaching there as their presence in Palestine was in connection with the UNRWA. One such example was Dr. T. John, an Indian national who was in Ramallah with his wife; she was a medical doctor on the medical staff of UNRWA overlooking the health of the public at the refugee camp in Jalazoun. I can remember Dr. John's face very clearly as if I saw him yesterday; he had dark colored skin typical of the natives of southern India and islands of the Indian Ocean. His eyes had dark pupils with very white whites; he had round face, his nose was small with a flat tip, probably because he liked to rub it with the palm of his hand while he spoke. He had wavy hair, straight and upright posture and wore very baggy pants which I later came to realize, as later in my life I became acquainted in the Arabian Peninsula with many Indians, was typical of Indian dress habits. He always wore long sleeve shirts with the sleeves kept high on his arms with metal elastic bands around his biceps. Occasionally he wore a jacket or a sweater over his shirt but never a tie. Dr. John, if I remember correctly, had a doctorate degree in history and a doctorate degree in divinity, at least one of them from Colombia. He was a wealth of knowledge for us at the Friends Boys School. He taught me for four years, and if I had to listen to him teach me many more years, I would have never been bored of the things he had to teach or the stories he had up his baggy sleeves.

Dr. John taught me the history of ancient civilizations, the history of the Roman Empire, the history of the Holy Roman Empire and the history of the Unification of Italy and Germany. When he spoke, he was so interesting and enlightening, one could hear a pin drop in the class for he captured everyone's imagination. Dr. John also taught me religion, which in his class was the great religions of the world, and taught me about the great personalities of history. He not only taught us about Buddhism, Hinduism, Confucianism and other religions and reform movements, but he also taught us about the great personalities of history, human rights activists and great scientists and inventors. He taught us about Jesus Christ, Buddha, Muhammad, Toyohiko Kagawa, Booker T Washington, Confucius, Abraham Lincoln, Mahatma Ghandi, Benjamin Franklin and many others whom he admired and magically transferred that admiration into our minds and hearts. With his persuasive presentations, low-key manner of speech and the typical Indian movements of his hands and his head, he made us share his admiration for, and fall in love with the acts of, each of his heroes whom we by now have adopted as our own. He taught us how an emperor walked for miles, in the snow, and knelt on his knees in front of the Pope to ask for forgiveness. He taught us how Garibaldi, humble as his start may have been, worked against insurmountable odds to unite a divided Italy after years and years of intra national bickering and infighting. He taught us how in his country, Mother India, passive as they may have been ,the people of India were still able to resist the British arrogance, prejudice and occupation with passive resistance under the leadership of Mahatma Ghandi. He taught us about the American Revolution, the Declaration of Independence and the framers of the American Constitution. He taught us about many people and many things that left an effect and an impression on us that are not likely to wear off till the day some of us die. He taught us to speak freely, never to be afraid of consequences of the truth. It was his teachings coupled with the political events of the time in the Arab World that shaped my awareness of my surroundings, social and political. Dr. T. John was my inspiration to rebel, as many in the Arab world did, against social injustice, the absence of civil liberties and the arrogance of those in power.

Dr. John's teachings reinforced my feelings about and awareness of the incompetence of those who were in power and their disregard for decency. Why couldn't they be like Thomas Jefferson? Why couldn't they be like Mahatma Ghandi? Why couldn't they act like Benjamin Franklin? Why couldn't they believe in the principles taught by Confucius, the humility taught by Buddha, the forgiveness taught by Christ, and the tolerance taught by Mohammad. Why did they pretend to know it all and insist on telling me, and others like me among the people, that they knew better than the writer or the poet, the carpenter or the blacksmith, the banker or the broker, the laborer or the farmer? It was Dr. John's teachings that made me feel obligated to do something, anything, about the society in which I lived and the state of affairs that existed at that time. Like most people, I could feel that there was something wrong with what was going on in that place I called home, something terribly wrong, where the instruments of the governing few were abuse and more abuse of the rights of the many, and absolute disregard for the rights of the individual. I could feel there was something wrong when units of the army roamed the streets to put down peaceful demonstrations. I could feel there was something wrong when tanks rolled down the streets into Al-Manarah, the town square, to stop the peaceful and passive resistance. I could feel there was something wrong when members of the mabaheth sneaked up under the cover of darkness and hid from sight under the windows of peaceful families to eavesdrop and listen in to what was going on in these homes. The mabaheth picked up, detained and interrogated anyone who was caught tuned in to Sout-el-Arab, "Voice of the Arabs" radio broadcast from Cairo. Anyone who tuned in to that radio station was assumed to be a troublemaker who should be detained. I could see that there was something wrong when I saw the instruments of oppression pick up my friend Ghassan from school because of his political beliefs. I could feel there was something wrong when I saw soldiers fix their bayonets to their rifles in the streets of Ramallah, as a sign of readiness to quell peaceful gatherings. But I could also feel there was something wrong when the remedies sought by many of us turned into ugly hysteria, confrontations, and anarchy.

Everything that Dr. John taught in class seemed to enforce my feelings that there was something wrong because every principle he taught seemed to be violated constantly by my country's government and its agents. The teachings of Dr. John reinforced my feelings that everything good that was supposed to be happening to my people was, for some reason, not happening to me or to my people. I convinced myself, and I was out to convince anyone I could, that that reason was the government and its policies and its instruments of oppression. Dr. John's teachings inspired me to participate in, and sometimes lead, peaceful demonstrations. His teachings inspired me to be an activist, to organize peaceful opposition and to disseminate information that the people had been denied. His teachings inspired me to join in the secret distribution of political leaflets in the vegetable market of Ramallah, in the painting of anti-government political slogans on public buildings in the middle of the night and defying the ban on public gatherings.

Dr. T. John's teachings also reinforced my feeling about religious harmony among the various religions of the world. He taught compassion for those who did not have the same beliefs as we did, and emphasized tolerance to those beliefs that differed from our beliefs. His teachings reinforced my tolerance, as a Christian, to other peoples and religions especially Moslems and Islam, the only other people and faith with whom I had daily contact. It also reinforced my tolerance, as a Christian, to Jews and Judaism in spite of the suffering that was inflicted upon me as a Palestinian by people who were Jews. I had learned tolerance from my father, whose three closest friends were three Jewish brothers. He always taught me that Jews did not mean any harm to Palestinians, no more than Christians meant any harm to Jews in Europe. My father used to say, "It was the Nazis who caused the aggravation and grief for the Jews in Europe, and it was the Zionists who caused us our grief as Palestinians. Only a small uninformed group on either side look at these conflicts as conflicts of religions." I had learned, during the years of my growing up in Palestine, and especially in Ramallah, that I could and should be proud to be a Christian Arab and that no one ever had the right to question my belief. I also learned, and Dr. John's teachings reinforced that, that I could be proud of my Arab culture that was shaped by Islam. I definitely was. I was proud to have Christianity as a faith that established my relation with my creator, and have Islam as a cultural background that established my relations with Arab history and culture. I felt I was more fortunate than non-Arab Christians, and more fortunate than Arab Moslems. I felt that I was fortunate to have had the best of both worlds. I will never forget Dr. John for all that he taught my schoolmates and me at the Friends Boys School.

Among the Friends Boys School teachers I also remember the science teachers, specifically Mr. Fouad Zaru who taught Chemistry at the two Friends Schools in Ramallah, and who much later, after I left the Friends, became the school principal. Another Mr. Khouri who taught me at the Friends was Mu'een Khouri who was a mentor and a political activist in the Baath Party, and was always present at most of the Party functions which I attended, especially protests and demonstrations. But most fondly I remember those young Palestinian teachers who had just completed their education in the United States and returned home to teach at the Friends. One I will always remember is Mr. Badee' Nijim. My classmates will definitely remember Mr. Nijim because he was light hearted in teaching and because of the examples that he used to cite, and the stories that he used to tell in class. He taught us European History, from the collapse of the Roman Empire to Napoleon. He brought to class many examples of the corruption and decadence of the Roman emperors and their wives. One particular example that we used to like him to tell us repeatedly was the stories about the wife of Emperor Claudius. He told us stories of her sexual adventures and how she used to challenge young Roman army generals into sex marathons in the age of decadence in which she lived. He used to know that we, at the age of 15 and 16 used to like that stuff and he took advantage of that fact to exaggerate and bring his points across his young audience. I was destined to meet Mr. Nijim again later in my life, this time in Saudi Arabia, and we both laughed when I reminded him of his European History stories.

The two Friends Schools were completely segregated except on certain days of the week when we had a common chapel or on special occasions. The girls came to visit the boy's school during special athletic events, when outside speakers were invited or during common holiday festivities such as during the Christmas pageant. Students came to the Friends from all parts of the West Bank and East Jordan and some came from many countries of the world. When I was at the Friends Boys School, we had students from the UK, USA, Iraq, Lebanon and, I am sure most of my contemporaries would remember, one student who came from Ethiopia. The Friends school atmosphere was calm, the local and foreign faculty was nice and friendly and the students were all easy to know that it took me only a short while to feel at home there. Also, the walk to school in the morning from my home to the Friends was much more pleasant than the walk to Abu Rayya. The walk to the Friends took me to areas very close to many girls' schools and it was nice to see smiling pretty faces in the morning. From my home I could take one of two different roads, which then converged by the main movie theater on Main Street, which then was a straight shot all the way to el-Bireh and the Friends Boys School. The first road took me up the Friends Hill that went right by the Friends Girls School. Going that route I could see most of the Friends' girls going to school in the morning and that was nice especially that there were a few of them I liked, some of them a lot. The other route took me up through the vegetable market by the Greek Orthodox Church, up a narrow street where my friend Jalal worked in his father's bakery. Jalal sometimes would join me the rest of the way. We would then go up the Latin Street by al Ahliyyah School and close by the girls' school of St. Joseph. Up at the top of the hill was one of the Jerusalem bus stops where many of the students arriving from Jerusalem or some of the surrounding villages would start their walk to their schools. The walk up that route was just as pleasant for I could see most of the St. Joseph School girls walking to their school, most of them were good looking and especially few were knock outs. It was a great pleasure at that age to be able to exchange glances and, if one is bold enough, to pass notes back and forth.

It was during my years at the Friends Boys School that I learned to love and dream about America. The school activities and the interaction with the faculty and staff, many of whom were Americans, made me interested in the United States. Some of us who were politically active would sometimes challenge the American faculty members to an informal debate about the 'imperialist ambitions' of the United States in the Middle East and about the role of the superpowers and democracy. Many of us had mixed feelings about the United States of America. On the one hand, it was a country of those who revolted against British colonialism, the country of the American Revolution against the oppression of the British. That history in itself was enough for me and many of my friends to establish a bond. The Palestinians suffered the consequences of the Ottoman, then the British, colonial rule in Palestine and developed a feeling of mistrust, and hate for the colonial power's deceit and duplicity. That common grievance with the Americans against the British gave me the feeling of comfort in talking to and dealing with Americans. Then there was the American Constitution and the Declaration of Independence, and what they stood for in terms of the liberties and the individual's rights to life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness. We quickly learned that the United States was protecting some of the Arab rulers out of political expediency and not because the Americans sanctioned dictatorship, but we never accepted that as legitimate. We learned from our American teachers, although we did not accept the argument, that America in many instances had no choice but to support such dictatorships in spite of their abuse of civil liberties and human rights. That was something we could not understand in those days, and many of us have never able to understand until this very day. We questioned our American teachers about the unqualified support and protection, which their country gave to the Zionists, those who took our country and our homes and made refugees out of us. That, they could not defend; they could only cite that the American public opinion was shaped by the influence of the pro-Israel lobby.

During the last two years at the Friends, most students were talking about what they would do after school and where they would go next. Among the well to do students, the American University of Beirut (AUB) was a very popular, though expensive, choice. Among those who were not so well to do, the United States was top choice because, as most American teachers would tell us at that time, after the first year of college there the student could work and be able to make a living while going to school. But even that was out of the question for many of us, for we had to have the means to get there and survive for one year first. Outside the conversation on education, it seemed that every conversation among the students was centered on the United States. Even those who were planning, and could afford, to go the American University of Beirut, were talking about their dreams of eventually going to the United States to live there in the American heaven on earth as portrayed by the Hollywood images of the 1950s. I wanted so badly to go to college in the U.S. but I knew I could never afford it. Although my father by now was doing well enough to provide for a family of ten, and educate all of his children through high school, he could never afford the tremendous financial burden of travel and college tuition and other expenses for a whole year. I was told in no uncertain terms that we could not afford to have me go to school in the United States. My dreams about travel to America were squashed by financial reality.

After I graduated from the Friends Boys School, I didn't know what to do. Jobs for high school graduates were very limited and the pay was hardly enough to cover pocket expenses. I worked for my father in the machine shop for a while and then I saw many of my friends from school leave the country. Many of them went on to college at the AUB or in the United States, Egypt or even Turkey, assisted by their parents or by some scholarship which required grades much better than I had. Others left the country in search of work in other parts of the Arab world, especially the oil rich countries of the Arabian Peninsula. I was itching to leave too, and it was not long after that, that I too left.

At the prodding of a German customer of my father, I decided to go to Germany to learn German, work and go to college. That was the first failure of my life, which cost me one year of wasted time. As I arrived in Germany and enrolled into the Goethe Language Institute in Murnau, right outside Munich, I started to communicate with German colleges. They promptly and politely advised me that there was approximately two years waiting time for admission of foreign students, and politely recommended that I enroll into some work-study program in the field of my aspiration until I could be admitted, a very time consuming proposition. I very enthusiastically took their advice, and as soon as I completed the language course, approximately ten weeks after I arrived from home, I went to Wiesbaden and worked in a factory that made mechanical handling systems. The pay was good enough to cover my expenses but I did not stay there long. I realized, after soul searching, that my admission into college even after two years was not guaranteed and it was conceivable that I could stay there working in the factory for two years, simply to find out that admission was not possible. Actually, some of my colleagues who were in the same situation ended up working without going to college until they left Germany several years later, some of them to Austria but most of them to the United States. After about six months from the time I left home, I returned with the agony of defeat, and back to square one. I stayed in Ramallah for a few months pondering the situation and in the summer of that year (1959) I decided to go look for educational opportunities in Damascus, Syria. There, I quickly found out that Syrian universities were overcrowded with a long waiting list and most of their high school graduates who wanted to continue their education had to go elsewhere. While in Damascus, I stayed with some friends at the office-and-guest-house of the Baath Arab Socialist Party (of Jordan) for two months. When I gave up on educational opportunities in Damascus, I left to Beirut in search of the same opportunities, which until then had eluded me. While in Beirut, I met one of my classmates from the Friends Boys School who had heard about an American junior college, run by Armenian Americans, which was giving scholarships to Palestinians. I immediately went to Haigazian College and applied for admission and for financial aid. I was asked to take an entrance exam, which I took and passed, and based on my transcript from the Friends and the results of the entrance exam, I was admitted for immediate enrollment. Based on the fact that I was a Palestinian refugee, my admission was with full financial aid. As I had no place to stay, and because the college was not in session yet, Haigazian staff arranged for me to stay, free of charge, at the home of one of the Armenian families in Al-Hamra district within a few blocks from the college. I stayed there for a couple of weeks until school started at which time I moved into the Haigazian dorms in a brand new building.

My Beirut experience was enjoyable and very rewarding and I very much appreciated the opportunity, which I felt was God sent. When Haigazian opened its doors, I found out that I was among ten or so students from the Jerusalem/Ramallah area, a few of them I had already met back home. Also, my Friends connection came into play, for there was one other Friends graduate at Haigazian and were many Friends students attending the AUB, just a few blocks down the road from Haigazian. Two of the students who were closest to me in Beirut were Isa, a classmate from the Friends, and Munir who was another friend from Ramallah and was rooming with Isa. I knew that if I were to continue my education after Haigazian, I had to be serious about school and study hard. I knew that meant that I should de-emphasize my involvement in politics and emphasize my focus on getting good grades. That I did, although it was difficult for me to distance myself from politics, at least temporarily. I was so obsessed with getting good grades that I studied most of the time and had little free time for anything else.

Based on my placement exam, I was placed in College Physics, English, Philosophy and Remedial Math. I was very upset about the Remedial Math and felt that I should have been in Calculus. I went to the chairman of the Math Department, who was teaching Calculus, and pleaded with him to move me into Calculus. I explained that I had left high school a whole year earlier and was somewhat rusty on my math. He told me that the results of my entrance exam indicated that I belonged in Remedial Math as a step before Calculus, and the fact that I was rusty proved that. However, he continued on to explain, if I were to do very well on another exam that was to be given at the end of the week in remedial math, and prove that I 'was the star' of my math class, then I could be moved to Calculus. I started to review my math by getting books from the library and practicing various college algebra problems. I must have practiced on several hundred problems by exam time at the end of the week. I took the exam on Friday morning and felt good about it. On Monday morning, the second Monday of the school year, the two math teachers called me in for a joint meeting with both of them, and I was moved into Calculus, because I had a perfect score on my exam. That action gave me the confidence that I needed to do well while at Haigazian. I worked hard in all of my classes, especially in Physics. Anise, a fellow Palestinian student from Qalqilyeh, whom I met upon my arrival in Haigazian, went complaining to some of my friends that I was studying too hard. He was concerned that I may have emotional problems because he felt that I was spending too much time thinking and gazing at a ruler during study hour, and that he was worried about me. When some of my friends confronted me, I had to explain to them that I was not flipping from too much studying, and that I was not gazing at a ruler in study hall. The "ruler" Anise was talking about was actually my slide rule, which I used in solving my physics problems.

I also knew that I had to keep my focus on the United States. My rationale was as follows: Haigazian was essentially a two year college after which I had two choices, either go to the AUB which I could not afford, or go to the United States and earn a living while I was going to school. I studied hard at Haigazian and completed the year successfully. I am grateful to Haigazian and the Armenian American community who supported the school. I owe them my fruitful year in Beirut for they were responsible for my having been able to gain the confidence to go to the United States of America where I studied, worked and earned my engineering degrees.

Right next to Haigazian, separated by a hurricane fence, was the Armenian Evangelical School, an Armenian high school. Little did I know that the love of my life, whom destiny would have me meet many years later, was attending that school.
