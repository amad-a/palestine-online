Pulmonary edema:

Clinical assessment

Symptoms

  • Acute dyspnoea
  • Cough, frothy sputum

Signs

  • Tachycardia
  • BP variable
  • Lung crepitations
  • Other congestive signs
    • JVP elevated
    • Peripheral oedema
  • Cardiac enlargement - apex beat displaced
  • Gallop rhythm - apical third heart sound, S3

Past history - may indicate

  • previous subacute or chronic heart failure
    • Dyspnoea on exertion, orthopnoea, nocturnal dyspnoea
    • Peripheral oedema
    • Decreased exercise tolerance
  • causative or precipitating factors
    • Angina or myocardial infarction
    • Hypertension
    • Valvular heart disease
    • Heavy alcohol consumption
  • precipitating factors
    • Respiratory infection
    • Arrhythmias
    • Anaemia
    • Hyperthyroidism
    • Volume overload (transfusion)
  • relevant medication
    • Cardiac depressant drugs
    • Non steroidal antiinflammatory drugs

Investigations

 

  • CXR (heart size, pulmonary oedema vs infection, consolidation)
  • ECG (brady or tachyarrhythmia, AF, ischaemia, previous or silent MI, LV hypertrophy)
  • Arterial blood gas (hypoxia, metabolic acidosis)
  • Blood biochemistry (Cr, Na+, Ksps+)
  • Hb, WBC and differential
  • Thyroid function tests ("unexplained" AF and HF in elderly)
  • Echocardiography after acute treatment and stabilisation (LV size, function, wall thickness, valvular and other abnormalities)
    • Lack of compliance with previo

Acute care

Initial measures

  • Sit patient upright with legs down ('armchair' position)
  • Oxygen at 4-6 L/min (maintain SaO2 > 90%)
  • Morphine 2.5 mg iv over 2-3 minutes, repeated once if necessary (monitor respiration)
  • Frusemide 40-80 mg iv repeated as necessary
  • Glyceryl trinitrate sublingual tab or spray, repeated dosage according to blood pressure or alternatively by iv infusion (local protocols)

Subsequent treatment

After review, generally in CCU, ICU

  • Digoxin for ventricular rate control in atrial fibrillation and flutter, loading dose 1.0-1.5 mg iv or oral in divided doses over first 24 hr, then 0.25 mg daily (less in the elderly and with renal impairment)
  • Dobutamine iv infusion up to 10-15 mcg/kg/min (inodilator effect)
  • Dopamine iv infusion up to 5-10 mcg/kg/min if persistent hypotension (inoconstrictor effect)
  • Continuous positive airway pressure

Post acute management

Review clinical presentation and investigations including echocardiogram to determine remediable causes or precipitants and to guide management decisions:

  • LV global ischaemia (pulmonary oedema resolving, ECG ST-T changes, relatively normal LV size and function on echo).
  • Occult valvular disease - AS, MS
  • Uncontrolled hypertension
  • Hyperthyroidism in the elderly
  • Previous medications

Plan further investigations / referral / intervention for the above if required.

Plan maintenance treatment

  • Diuretic/ACE inhibitor combination for HF with LV systolic dysfunction.
  • Digoxin for AF and an optional addition for patients in SR.
  • Aspirin for all patients with coronary disease.
  • Selective anticoagulation for high risk patients with AF and those with marked LV impairment

Other important aspects of care

  • Education and counselling measures for patient and family, general, diet and medication related.
  • Plan integrated follow up with primary care.
