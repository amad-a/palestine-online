Torment in Surif

Occupied Jerusalem- Following the discovery and arrests of the "Surif Cell" members, responsible for a number of attacks against Israelis in the West Bank and Israel, including the suicide bombing at a cafe in Tel Aviv on 21 March, the entire village of Surif has been facing collective punishment. The village has approximately 15,000 residents. Collective punishment has taken the form of repeated violations against the personal human rights of the villagers who have NOT been charged or suspected with illegal activities. This is in addition to the demolition of the ìSurif Cellî family member homes.

Curfews have been imposed on the village from 21 March through 6 April and again from 16 through 21 April (during the Eid ul Adha holiday). On 23 April, yet another home was demolished. The entire area has been declared a closed military area up til the end of April, thus entering and exiting from the village is prohibited. Journalists are also prevented from entering. This has been the most brutal and lengthy curfew since the signing of the Declaration of Principles in September 1993.

Strict curfew and beatings

The curfew was interrupted every few days for two hours to allow villagers to obtain food and water. According to testimony collected by Bassem Eid of the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, the interruptions in the curfews did not allow the villagers enough time to obtain food supplies, since all village entrances and exits were closed off by the IDF. Furthermore, residents were beaten by border policemen in the streets as they rushed to prepare for the re-imposition of the closure. Bilal Muhammed Arar, age 22, was attacked on 14 April during a break in the curfew. A jeep with three border policemen on board stopped next to Arar and two other Palestinians. The driver of the jeep got out and accused him of throwing stones. At the time, there was no disturbance in the village. The policeman held Arar's head between his legs, and beat him on his back with a helmet. Then four other policemen spread him on the floor and began to hit him, claiming that he was throwing stones. After being hit with a rifle butt, Arar lost consciousness and woke up two hours later at the clinic in the village. The Red Cross complained to the border police, and two soldiers will be tried for this offense, according to Israel Radio.

Ishak Ismail Ghneimat, age 19, left his house at 11:30 a.m. on 28 March to pray, in violation of the curfew. Ghneimat and other villagers were attempting to assemble at the mosque. He was stopped by a border police jeep and driven around the village for two hours while two policemen beat him in the back of the jeep. Afterwards, the jeep went to the school in the centre of the village and threw him on the ground. Another border police jeep arrived and eight border policemen began hitting him. Two and a half hours later, he was released, but not before being threatened that if he leaves his house again, he will be run over by the police.

Denial of urgent medical care

Residents were not allowed to leave even to seek urgent medical treatment. A Jordanian tourist, Khalid Mustafa Arar, aged 61, who was visiting relatives in the village, died after being denied exit to a hospital.

On 14 April, Arar decided to end his visit and return to Jordan. As he was leaving, he encountered a disturbance in progress and fell unconscious from tear gas inhalation. Residents gathered around him to provide first aid, and the soldiers fired more tear gas at the gathered people, with the unconscious Arar in the centre. Residents tried to evacuate him through the Beit Ummar checkpoint, but soldiers refused to let him pass. The delay lasted for nearly two hours. After a lengthy argument, the residents forced their way through the checkpoint. Arar died on the way to the hospital. Gas inhalation caused his heart attack, although Arar might have been saved if his evacuation was not delayed for so long.

In another instance, a pregnant woman approached the authorities two days in a row, begging for permission to leave the village and give birth in a hospital. On 10 April, Ifrikiya Ahmed Irnimat, age 18, called for an ambulance since she was in labour. The village midwife examined her and determined that she should be taken to the hospital, but the hospital explained that the soldiers were preventing ambulances from entering the village. On 11 April around 8 a.m., Irnimat tried to leave the village, but the police turned her back. Again in the afternoon she tried to leave, and this time she succeeded. However, after giving birth in the hospital, the baby was born dead.

Complaints of brutality and violence

The entry of the soldiers to the village was accompanied by brutal and unnecessary violence and destruction of property. At least 60 homes were entered and private property destroyed by the soldiers. Hazem Ghneimat, age 31, was arrested on 10 April at 7 a.m. Border policemen damaged kitchenware in his house during their search and arrest. The police knocked over a container of paint on the floor of another house. This onslaught of violence included the destruction of rooftop water cisterns shot by soldiers during disturbances in the village. Dozens of windows were shattered, and the residents reported that border police shouted insults and obscenities at them during the night through loudspeakers mounted on jeeps, and shock grenades were detonated in narrow streets and alleys for the purpose of disturbing and intimidating the residents.

At least 45 villagers were arrested at the beginning of the curfew and remained in detention after the members of the "Surif Cell" were captured. Twelve of the detainees remained in administrative detention without being charged of any crime or link to the Cell. The administrative detention orders last for 2-6 months.

Denial of education to the children

During the imposition of the curfew, the children of Surif were unable to attend school, losing four weeks of education. At the same time, 60 teachers employed by the Surif schools, but living outside the village, were unemployed as a result of the curfew. Seventy-two teachers living in Surif and employed in other villages were unable to leave to tend to their classrooms, thus denying hundreds of children outside Surif of their teachers.

The residents of Surif are furious because of the collective punishment. During the intifada, the villagers suffered similar measures, but largely as a result of participation in the mass uprising. This time, the suspects wanted by Israel and the Palestinian Authority were apprehended, and the measures taken against the rest of the village constitute illegal violations of human rights.

Recommendations

The success of the Israeli army in arresting the Surif Cell was a result of cooperation between the Palestinian Authority and the Israeli security forces. The PHRMG has a problem with such security cooperation (ostensibly aimed at upholding the law) being used to violate human rights and international law. The destruction of houses, the use of violence and extended curfews, the denial of medical treatment and of education, as well as administrative detentions are all violations of human rights and international law. We call on the Palestinian Authority to condition their cooperation with Israel, on the assurance that human rights will be respected, and that information provided will not be used to violate human rights and impose collective punishment.

Israel must understand that in the context of the ongoing political process in which the Palestinians are partners in fighting "terrorism," that the imposition of collective punishment only creates more distrust and hard feelings. Israel must cease the arrogant treatment of Palestinians, and recognize that they are not the collective enemy that must be collectively punished. We call on the international community to serve notice to Israel that human rights violations are not justified by the need to fight "terror", especially not when that fight is being assisted in dramatic ways by the Palestinian Authority. (This article is published by courtesy of The Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group(PHRMG)

After 42 months in Israeli prisons

Rantisi assails security cooperation between the PA and Israel

Hebron- From: Mohammed Halaika

A prominent leader of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) who was freed last month after spending more than 42 months in an Israeli prison declared his opposition to any reconciliation with the Palestinian self-rule authority. "There is no common ground between Hamas and the Palestinian Authority," said Dr. Abdul Aziz Rantisi from his home in Khan Yunis refugee camp south of Gaza City.

Dr. Rantisi, who was the spokesman for the 400 Hamas deportees in Southern Lebanon in 1992, criticized a decision made by the Hamas leadership to participate in a national reconciliation conference between the main Palestinian factions organized last February by the PA in Nablus in the northern part of the West Bank. "I would only agree to such a national dialogue if its aim was to prevent bloodshed between Palestinians," Dr. Rantisi said.

Commenting on statements made by Chairman Arafat's adviser, Nabil Abu Rediana, who denied security coordination between the PA and Israel, Dr. Rantisi told the Palestine Times that coordination had not stopped. "The PA should release three Hamas activists from Surif who were accused of kidnapping and murdering an Israeli soldier, Sharon Edri, if, as they claim, security coordination had stopped," he added.

Asked whether the leakage of information to the Israelis by the PA about the activities of the Hamas military wing has any impact on the Palestinian national unity, Dr. Rantisi said that Hamas is the one that always pays the price of national unity. "Despite this fact, Hamas will not direct its guns against any Palestinian because Zionists are its only enemies," he added.

Dr. Rantisi, however, said that Hamas was keen not to push the Palestinian Authority into the "enemy's trench" because the Zionists will blackmail it and force it to give more relinquishments which would only further harm the interests of the Palestinian people.

Dr. Rantisi makes no secret of the possibility that he could be arrested by the Israelis again even though he is living now in a Palestinian self-rule area. "The Israeli authorities arrested Sheikh Hassan Yousef, a Hamas leader in the West Bank, when he was crossing the bridge to perform his pilgrimage duty although he was living in Ramallah which is also a Palestinian self-rule area," Dr. Rantisi said, adding that occupation still exists.

Remembering his last moments in prison, the Hamas leader said that the Deputy Chief of the Israeli Bearsheba prison told him "leave, your detention is over, but do not force us to arrest you again."

He expressed concern over the fate and living conditions of Palestinian detainees in Israeli prisons. "They are sad and disappointed because Palestinian officials do not give priority to their problem."

Dr. Rantisi had founded, with Sheikh Ahmad Yaseen, the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) in late 1987, one week after the eruption of the Palestinian uprising (Intifada).

In 1988, he was put in administrative detention (without trial) for 30 months on the charge of leading Hamas. He was released in 1990 but arrested again and remained in administrative detention for 12 months. On 17 December 1992, he was also arrested but this time to be deported with more than 400 Hamas leaders and activists to Marjel Zuhour in Southern Lebanon. When Israel allowed the deportees to return home in 1993, he was arrested before reaching his home. On 20 April and after 42 months of detention in various Israeli prisons, he was released. Rantisi, 50, is a podiatrist and a lecturer in the Islamic University of Gaza

Hamas leader calls on Palestinians to escalate uprising

against Israeli occupation

Occupied Jerusalem-The head of the political bureau of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) Khalid Misha'al called on the Palestinian people in the occupied territories to escalate their popular uprising in order to confront Israeli occupation and settlement policies. He also vowed to continue Hamas' armed struggle against Zionist occupation.

Misha'al said in a letter he addressed to the Palestinians under occupation that the Palestinian question is now at a cross-roads. "The question is either to continue our Jihad and resistance against Zionist plans by escalating our popular intifada which has spread to all Palestinian cities, villages, and camps or to enter into an endless circle of negotiations," the Hamas leader added.

"The (Israeli) enemy is establishing its aggressive entity and completely overrunning our land and holy places amid international silence, while America, in collaboration with Israel, is benefiting from the so-called peace settlement which crushed the rights and hopes of our people and legalized the usurpation of Palestinian land. It also allowed the enemy to complete usurping the parts it was not able to seize by force."

Misha'al stressed the importance of National Unity adding that after 50 years of suffering under Israeli occupation "our people in the West Bank and Gaza Strip have started a new blessed intifada: a rebellion that has dissipated the enemy's delusions, ruined its plans to liquidate the people's cause and halted its malignant growth."

The Hamas leader said that unity among the Palestinian people should be based upon resisting and facing the Zionists. "The uniting with Palestinians in diaspora completes the circle of resistance and supports our people in the West Bank and Gaza," he added.

"The Palestinian people have succeeded in maintaining their unity by Godís grace and by the wisdom and awareness of Hamas and other resistance factions who prefer being patient rather than waging a civil war among Palestinians."

The Hamas political leader emphasized that the "experience obliged our people to continue their Jihad and their resistance and to reject all promises of a divided homeland that would realize the interests of Israel and achieve its security."

"The events proved that the Zionist enemy only understands the language of force. Therefore, Hamas will continue with its Jihad and its members will keep on fueling the fire of the popular revolution."

Misha'al accused the Palestinian Authority of attempting to abort the new intifada."Neither did they make an effort to close the tunnel under al Aqsa Mosque nor did they try to stop the settlement in Jabal Abu Ghneim," he said.

Hamas Boycotts Dialogue Session; Israeli Repression Continues

Occupied Jerusalem: PT- By: Khalid Amayreh

Palestinian Authority efforts to co-opt and rally the Islamist opposition behind Chairman Yasser Arafat suffered a serious set-back on 28 April when Hamas boycotted a follow-up national dialogue session in Nablus. It is still unclear why the main Palestinian Islamist opposition organization chose to stay away since earlier indications had prognosticated the movement's active participation. A popular Islamist leader from the Hebron area told the Palestine Times (PT) on 29 April that Hamas was "increasingly discovering that Arafat is not really serious about conducting a meaningful dialogue with the opposition." The Islamist representative accused the PA leadership of "only seeking to reap propagandistic fruits" from the dialogue.

Jamal Mansur, a prominent Hamas leader from Nablus said the PA reneged on the "confidence-building measures" which the Palestinian leadership had promised to take following the preliminary national dialogue session in Nablus nearly two months ago. The said measures reportedly included, among other things, promised freedom for incarcerated Islamist activists in PA custody. Mansur also hinted that the PA was trying to manipulate the "dialogue with Hamas" as a means to regain some of its lost popularity in the Palestinian Street.

According to confidential Islamist sources, Hamas is increasingly upset by Arafat's apparent willingness to resume full security and intelligence cooperation with Israel. Hamas reportedly was furious at PA Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub for colluding with Israel in apprehending the so-called "Surif Cell" which reportedly stood behind a series of attacks on Israeli targets, including the suicidal bombing at a Tel Aviv coffee shop several weeks ago on 21 March. According to Israeli sources, confidential information passed on to the Israeli General Security Service, the Shabak, by the Preventive Security facilitated the apprehension and arrest of a dozen Hamas activists in Surif, 15 kilometres north-west of Hebron.

The small town itself has been under siege and intermittent curfew for the past six weeks. Palestinian sources in Surif reached by the Palestine Times spoke of Israeli soldiers routinely raiding Palestinian homes during the night, beating people and causing incalculable damage to house utensils and appliances. Human rights activist Basem Eid, who was able to visit the town on 23 April, pointed out that the IDF was "inflicting severe collective punishment on the local population." Eid cited one example of Israeli soldiers deliberately damaging as many as 130 water tanks on rooftops in Surif, depriving the population of a readily-available water supply.

Israeli draconian measures against Palestinians outside the "liberated" towns found expression in the killing on 27 April of 18-year-old Mahmud Abu Amiriyya in the nearby village of Kharas. Palestinian sources pointed out that Abu Amiriyya was killed by a random bullet in the head fired by an Israeli soldier whose jeep was stoned by village children. Three additional youngsters were also injured in the incident. An Israeli military spokesman admitted that Abu Amirriya "may not have been involved in the stoning," adding, rather laconically, that the matter was being investigated.

In the village of Deir Samet, 20 kilometres to the south, Israeli troops raided many homes on 27 April, arresting five people and causing extensive damage to furniture. At least on one occasion, occupation soldiers threw stun grenades into one home causing serious injury to an elderly woman.

In the meantime, Israeli soldiers manning roadblocks and checkpoints intensified the practice of arresting Palestinians traveling on West Bank roads. Palestinian sources told (PT) that as many as 30 Palestinians were arrested at checkpoints and roadblocks and sent to the Meggido Detention Centre without charge or trial.

According to Isa Karakei, Head of the Prisoner's Club, as many as 400 Palestinians were arrested by the Israelis during April. Karakei said most detainees are subjected to intensive interrogation before they are sent to Meggido where hundreds of Palestinians are kept as hostages under the rubric of the so-called administrative detention. Human rights organizations point out the utter illegality of the administrative detention as detainees have no way of knowing when they will be freed since the Israeli prison authorities routinely extend by six months the detention terms of inmates.

Feverish Israeli campaign to destroy Hamas infrastructure

Hamas assails PA for security cooperation with Israel

Occupied Jerusalem- Israeli security sources said that the Internal Security Service (Shin Bet) is investigating alleged links between a Hamas cell, arrested last month in the West Bank village of Surif, and a number of prominent activists of Ezzul Deen al Qassam Brigades, a military wing of Hamas in the Gaza Strip.

According to Israeli sources, Shin Bet had arrested two members of the cell suspected of having contacts with two military leaders of al Qassam Brigades. The leaders were identified as Muhyel Deen Sharif (known by the Israelis as Engineer No. 2, a reference to Yahya Ayyash who was known as the Engineer) and Mohammed Dief, a Hamas military leader in the Gaza Strip. Sharif and Dief are at the top of the Israeli securityís most-wanted list of Hamas leaders. Until now, the security has failed to identify their whereabouts believed to be somewhere in the West Bank or Gaza.

According to Haaretz newspaper, Israeli sources accused the Hamas cell in Surif of carrying out a series of attacks against Israeli targets during the last two years which resulted in the killing of 11 Israelis and wounding more than 60 others.

The Israeli sources said that Shin Bet would reveal new information about the Surif cell pointing out that the Palestinian Authority had arrested four other members of the cell. They added that the Palestinian security services had provided the Israeli Shin Bet with important information which led to the discovery of the place where an Israeli soldier had been buried in the village of Surif seven months before.

The Israeli sources alleged that questioning members of the Hamas cell would lead to further arrests among Hamas members in the West Bank, including the Palestinian self-rule areas. They also boasted that the arrest of the cell aborted a series of attacks which were to be carried out in the 1948-occupied Palestinian areas and against Jewish settlers in the West Bank. The sources pointed out that Israeli security is preparing for a widespread campaign to crack down on Hamas in the Hebron area, in cooperation with the Palestinian Authority.

On 21 March, the Israeli occupation troops imposed a strict curfew on Surif, which has a population of 13,000. They also arrested many of the young people in the village.

Meanwhile, the leader of the rightist Moledet movement, Rahba'am Zaefi, called on the Israeli government to impose strict mass punishments on Surif and even to "remove it from the face of the earth". Yuli Eldeshtine, Israeli Minister of Immigration (Israel B'aliya party), called for expelling all residents of Surif.

In another instance, two brothers of Martyr Musa Ghneimat of Surif said that Shin Bet interrogators have been savagely torturing them since their arrest following the Tel Aviv bombing carried out by their brother. Ghazi and Mohammed Ghneimat submitted a petition to the Israeli Supreme Court saying that the Israeli interrogators were using all kinds of systematic torture including "violent shaking" and keeping their hands and legs tied for long periods. They requested the court to issue an order preventing the interrogators from torturing them.

The Israeli occupation authorities had arrested the parents of Martyr Ghneimat, his wife, and four of his brothers on 21 March after the Tel Aviv bombing. All of them, except Ghazi and Mohammed, were later released.

The two detainees complained that the Israeli interrogators kept them naked for long periods in very cold rooms and prevented them from sleeping or resting by playing loud music. They also complained of being denied the right to see a lawyer.

Ghazi was allowed to see his lawyer only once, at Eshkelon prison. The Israeli interrogators did not allow the lawyer to see her client again. During that sole meeting with his lawyer, Ghazi told her that the interrogators tried to force admissions from him and his brother about an alleged relation with the suicide bombing.

In another development, Andrie Rosenthal, an Israeli lawyer and human rights activist, expressed his fears over the fate of his client Ayman Qufaisha of Hebron who has been detained by Shin Bet since 5 April on the charge of having links with the Surif Hamas cell. Appealing to the Israeli Supreme Court, Rosenthal feared that his client might be tortured by his interrogators

The Israeli prosecutor replied that Ayman was suspected of being the link between the Surif cell and the military leadership of Hamas. He also accused Ayman of taking part in the attacks carried out by the same cell since 1995, including last September's kidnapping and murder of Sharon Edri, an Israeli soldier. He alleged that Shin Bet had a strong foundation to assume that the Palestinian detainee was withholding important information which, if revealed, would protect Israel and its people from serious danger.

The court finally accepted the justifications of the Israeli prosecutor to prevent Ayman from seeing his lawyer which would give the Israeli interrogators more time to use violence to force information from the prisoner.

Meanwhile, in two separate communiquÈs, the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and its military wing (Ezzul Deen al Qassam Brigades) assailed the intelligence cooperation between the PA's security services and the Israeli Shin Bet which led to the arrest of the Hamas military cell in the West Bank last month.

Hamas accused the Palestinian Authority of misleading the Palestinian people by threatening to halt security coordination with the Israelis if construction was not stopped in the Abu Ghneim settlement. "The arrest of a number of 'our heroes' directly after the meeting between Chairman Arafat, the Chief of the Zionist Shin Bet and a CIA official after the Tel Aviv bombing was to Jerusalem ëa stab in the back' over which the PA's officials are now lamenting. It was also a stab in the back to all Muslims, Arabs and Palestinians who are staging an uprising and sacrificing everything in order to defend Jerusalem," Hamas said. It condemned the "suspicious role" of the U.S. Administration saying it has been directly involved in suppressing the Palestinian people. "Such a role is a dangerous development that foreshadows extraordinary developments in the future," the Islamic movement added.

Hamas called on the self-rule authority to release all detainees at once and to stop security and intelligence cooperation with the Zionists.

On the other hand, al Qassam Brigades described the arrest of several Hamas activists by the PA and the help it offered to Israeli occupation authorities to identify the burial place of the Israeli soldier as " a real crime" against not only Hamas but also against all Palestinian people. Al Qassam Brigades warned that they would not be "scapegoats" for the breakdown of the "so-called peace accords which resulted in our people being lost and torn apart."

Al Qassam Brigades stressed that the "shameful crime" committed by the PA would neither undermine its abilities nor stop its 'Jihad'. It called on the political leadership of Hamas to suspend any contacts or dialogues with the PA until the latter "expiates its sins" by releasing detained Hamas supporters.

PA's Security detains Palestine Times Correspondent

American Committee Protests PA Harassment of Journalists

Occupied Jerusalem- The New York-based Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) has protested to Yasser Arafat concerning recent Palestinian Authority police harassment of Palestinian journalists in the Hebron area. On 11 April, CPJ informed Arafat that it was "deeply disturbed by the actions of a Palestinian police officer responsible for inciting physical attacks against journalists covering Palestinian demonstrations in Hebron."

The Committee pointed out that Wasfi Abu Ayman, a force-17 member, publicly provoked a group of youngsters to attack 10 Palestinian journalists whom he labeled "collaborators" with Israel. Furthermore, the protest letter pointed out that both local and foreign correspondents on the scene were subsequently pelted by a hail of stones thrown by the youngsters in the crowd. Ayman also attacked an unidentified cameraman, punching him in the head, and threatened to break the camera of Associated Press correspondent Nasser Shoukhi.

CPJ argued that harassed journalists were targeted for carrying out their professional duties. Such actions, the letter went on, represented clear violations of the right to free expression guaranteed by Article 19 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights of the United Nations, and exacerbates an already dangerous situation for journalists covering the clashes in the West Bank between Palestinian demonstrators and the Israeli army.

Observers in the autonomous enclaves have cited growing police intolerance and intimidation of the few remaining "non-conformist" journalists who refuse "to go with the flow."

On 23 March, Preventive Security Chief, Jebril Rajoub, ordered his men to briefly detain and interrogate Palestine Times Correspondent Khalid Amayreh for "distorting Rajoub's image and communicating inaccurate information."

Amayreh was questioned at the Preventive Security offices in Dura, 10 miles south-west of Hebron, about a televised report he had dispatched to the United Arab Emirates (Sharja Satellite Television) on a meeting in Jerusalem between Israeli Intelligence Chief Amy Ayalon and Rajoub and his Gaza counterpart Muhammed Dahlan. Rajoub accused Amayreh of "tendentious reporting" when the Palestinian journalist reported that "Ayalon demanded that Rajoub and Dahlan immediately put Hamas activists and leaders behind bars."

The following day, 24 March, Rajoub said on a phone-in programme aired by "Sawt Falastin" (Voice of Palestine) that "first of all Khalid Amayreh is insane" adding that "we didn't arrest the great ëmunadelí or fighter for freedom (he used the words sarcastically)...we just questioned him briefly concerning a big lie in a television report he prepared for UAE television in which he said that the Preventive Security was beginning to arrest Hamas leaders in Gaza." Rajoub went on saying that "we didn't do anything incompatible with the law; we have to protect our national interests. Amayreh and others can always write against Oslo." At the end of the tirade, the presenter, seemingly mesmerized by Rajoub's words, said "indeed, we have to protect our national experiment and interests."

Again, the CPJ wrote to Arafat about the incident. According to the media law promulgated by the PA and signed by Arafat, "no security agency has the right to detain, arrest, question or interrogate journalists on matters relating to their profession."

3,000 Palestinians and Arabs still detained in Israeli prisons

Gaza- The Palestinian Human Rights Centre said more than 3,000 Palestinians and Arabs are still detained in Israeli prisons under harsh conditions despite the peace accords signed between the PA and the Israeli government. The centre added that only a very limited number of detainees have been released since the signing of the Oslo accords while many others have since been detained. The Palestinian Centre accused the occupation authorities of escalating the arbitrary measures against the Palestinian and Arab prisoners by imposing harsh restrictions on visits from families and meetings with lawyers. "These measures are considered a violation of the 4th Geneva Convention regarding the protection of civilian residents during war. Article 116 of the Convention states that ìany detainee should have the right to receive visitors regularly, especially his relatives," the Centre pointed out, adding that such measures "weaken the trust between the Palestinians and Israelis which was established by the peace process." The Centre warned that prisoners' lives were endangered after the rulings by the Israeli Supreme Court which allowed Shin Bet interrogators to use violence and torture against the detainees

B'Tselem reports

Quiet deportation- Israeli residency policy in East Jerusalem

Policy aims to create new demographic and geographic reality

B'Tselem is an Israeli independent, non-governmental organization established in 1989 to monitor human rights violations in the occupied territories. It publishes reports, conducts advocacy campaigns, engages in public education and serves as a resource centre.

ISRAELI RESIDENCY policies have caused the displacement of thousands of Palestinian families from East Jerusalem and threaten thousands more. The forced eviction of Palestinians from East Jerusalem is clearly in breach of international human rights and humanitarian law. Beyond this, however, is the acute human suffering of people who have moved out of East Jerusalem - temporarily, they thought -for a variety of reasons (the following are just some examples):

- Study or work abroad.

- The acute housing shortage for Palestinians in Jerusalem, a result of deliberate Israeli policies.

- The refusal by Israeli authorities to allow a "non-Jerusalemite" spouse to dwell in the city.

- Normal population movement from the heart of a city to its suburbs.

People belonging to any of these categories are at the risk of loosing their right to "residency" in Jerusalem.

This means that they may not enter their hometown, let alone work in it, unless by special permit, and that they are no longer entitled to social benefits, such as national insurance, child benefits, pension and health insurance.

It should be stressed that these policies affect a Palestinian moving 50 yards north of the municipal boundaries, but not a Jewish Jerusalemite moving to West Bank settlements in the vicinity.

Such a person suffers no loss of freedom of movement, residency rights or social benefits.

What is frustrating to us as human rights activists is that these grossly unjust and inhumane policies, which have already affected thousands of Palestinians, and may affect tens of thousands more, are carried out discreetly, through invisible, bureaucratic procedures backed by Israeli laws and court decisions.

There are no trucks, no weeping women and children dragged by brutal soldiers, no physical violence- in other words, little for the media to "cover."

But the actual effect resembles what the most extremist political factions in Israel have advocated: large-scale displacement of Palestinians which threatens to permanently alter the demographic character of Jerusalem.

We believe that for these policies to be halted, public opinion and decision makers, both in Israel and internationally, must be made aware of them and the damage they cause.

The goal of Israeli residency policy in East Jerusalem is to reduce the number of Palestinians living in the city, and to create a demographic and geographic reality that will preempt any future effort to challenge Israeli sovereignty in East Jerusalem.

Some 170,000 Palestinians holding Israeli identity cards reside in East Jerusalem.

For the past two years, Israel's Interior Ministry has been revoking the residency rights of Palestinians in East Jerusalem who at some stage of their lives lived outside the municipal borders.

Consequently numerous Palestinians have been required to leave their houses and families and move to areas outside the Green Line, the demarcation line of Israel at the outset of the Arab-Israeli war.

It is estimated that some 120,000 Palestinians (70 per cent of the Palestinian population of the city) may lose their right to live in Jerusalem because of this new Israeli policy.

Following the war, in contravention of international law, Israel annexed East Jerusalem an area of 70 km2 in East Jerusalem, and applied Israeli law in the annexed territory.

Immediately after the war, Israel conducted a census in East Jerusalem and granted the status of permanent resident to everyone present in East Jerusalem at the time of the census. Israel also declared that residents of East Jerusalem could receive Israeli citizenship upon request, but for political reasons, most East Jerusalem Palestinians did not request citizenship.

Israel's Supreme Court held that the Entry into Israel Law applies in determining the status of Palestinian residents in East Jerusalem, and that the identity card issued to them is comparable to the permit to permanently reside in Israel granted under that law.

The court also held that when a permanent resident settles outside Israel, the permanent residency status expires. "Expiration" of the permit, contrary to revocation, leads to the loss of residency without explanation and without any procedure that would enable the subject to appeal.

The law stipulates that a person is considered to have settled outside Israel if he or she remained abroad for more than seven years, received a permit to permanently reside in another state, or became a citizen of that state.

The Supreme Court held that residency may also be revoked if other facts indicate that the individual settled outside of Israel even where the period of residency outside Israel was less than seven years.

By applying the Entry into Israel Law to residents of East Jerusalem, Israel relates to them as immigrants, even though the families involved have lived in the area for scores of years, and it was; Israel who entered the area, and not vice versa.

The legal status, described above, reflects only a small part of the picture. The Interior Ministry uses unwritten criteria and unclear procedures in revoking residency status. Human rights organizations and attorneys have failed in their attempts to determine the applicable criteria and procedures.

The ministry refuses to publish its criteria for issuing or revoking residency permits, or data regarding the number of persons whose residency has been revoked.

Not only Palestinians who have moved abroad are threatened by this policy. Over the years, many Palestinians have moved to neighbourhoods and villages outside the Jerusalem municipality.

This migration is the result of the following Israeli policies designed to entrench Israeli sovereignty in East Jerusalem:

1. Israel has greatly restricted Palestinians in residential building, causing overcrowding due to the serious housing shortage.

2. Prior to 1994, Israel rejected requests for family unification submitted by Jerusalem Palestinian women on behalf of their spouses who are not Jerusalemites.

The Israeli policy compelled these women to leave the city in order to be with their husbands.

East Jerusalem Palestinians living outside the city borders customarily went to the Interior Ministry's office in Jerusalem to renew their exit permits, thereby restarting the seven-year counting period.

The Interior Ministry's policy had been that only a continuous seven-year stay outside of Jerusalem would result in the loss of the right of residency. In the past two years, however, Israel changed its policy retroactively, and those who have not lived within the Jerusalem Municipality continuously lose their right to live in the city, even if they lived outside the city for less than seven years and even if they did not become permanent residents or citizens of another country. Persons who require the services of the Interior Ministry in a variety of matters, such as replacing an identity card, registering a child, or receiving an identity card for the first time at age 16, are required to provide documentary proof that they live in Jerusalem.

Those unable to provide suitable documentation lose their right to live in Jerusalem.

This requirement is excessive and unduly severe, and serves as an additional bureaucratic tool to reduce the number of Palestinians holding Israeli identity cards.

The requirement of proving residency in Jerusalem applies both to Palestinians living in the United States and to those living in Al Ram, which lies only a few kilometres from Jerusalem's municipal borders. Since the residency status "automatically expires," denial of residency status occurs without a hearing, without explanation, without any notice of the right to appeal, and without being informed that the policy had changed.

The authorities have also taken Jerusalem residency status from East Jerusalem Palestinians who moved to the Jerusalem suburbs (which are within the West Bank).

Loss of Jerusalem residency status has significant implications. Residents of East Jerusalem are not subject to the military government, as are residents of the rest of the occupied territories.

Those without Jerusalem residency status are not allowed to enter freely and work in Israel, and require special permits.

Loss of residency also results in the immediate cessation of national insurance benefits and health insurance through Israeli sick funds, as well as access to education and other benefits.

The Israeli Supreme Court has approved the policies of the Interior Ministry. Therefore, no domestic remedies are available to the victims of these policies.

Since the Interior Ministry does not publish its criteria for revoking residency status, East Jerusalem's Palestinians are uncertain about their status. Consequently, many do not utilise the services of the Interior Ministry, fearing that their residency in Jerusalem will be questioned and that they will be found not to be entitled to an Israeli identity card.

Within the next six months, the Interior Ministry intends to replace the identity cards of all Israeli citizens and residents. Palestinian residents of East Jerusalem will then have to go to the Interior Ministry, where the clerks will check whether they are entitled to an Israeli identity card.

According to estimates, some 70 per cent of Jerusalem's Palestinian residents are liable to lose their residency status.This would have severe consequences in terms of this populations ability to enjoy civil rights as well as economic and social benefits. In addition, Israeli residency policies are liable to dramatically change the character of the City.

Palestinian teachers in West Bank end 18-day

strike under threats from PA security

Ramallah (West Bank)- Special to PT

On 23 April, West Bank teachers ended a strike against the Palestinian Authority after Chairman Arafat authorized the release of 25 striking teachers and promised to offer the teachers a pay raise.

A coordination committee of the striking teachers decided to end the strike and return to work on 24 April ending an 18-day walk-out which involved more than 18,000 teachers and kept more than 200,000 pupils out of their schools.

Palestinian police allowed 15 of the detained striking leaders in Ramallah out of prison to meet with their colleagues in order to reach a settlement to end the strikeóthe first to be staged by PA employees since Chairman Arafat came to power in the Palestinian territories in 1994.

However, the police retained the identity cards of the released leaders in order to return them to prison if they failed to convince their followers to end the strike.

Sources of the strike committee said they had been promised by the Chief of the Preventive Security Service in the West Bank, Colonel Jibril Rujoub, that all detained teachers would be freed. But the sources feared that a number of the detained teachers would remain in prison for a further unlimited period.

The coordination committee had accused the Palestinian Minister of Education, Yasser Amr, of escalating the situation by issuing an "arbitrary decision to refer 19 Palestinian teachers to disciplinary council". The teachers were later dismissed from their work after being charged with committing violations and "harming the public interests." But the Minister reconsidered his decision by suspending their dismissal. The coordination committee said the teachers were punished because of their 'distinctive' activities in the committee which defends the rights of more than 20,000 teachers in PA schools in the West Bank.

The committee had called on the PA to dismiss Minister Amr and his Under-Secretary Dr. Naem Abul Hummos for their role in the "destruction of education" in the self-rule areas.

PA teachers in the West Bank earn about 250 Jordanian dinars a month (about $350) while the monthly cost-of-living for an average Palestinian family is estimated at $750, the committee says. The teachers sought a 200 per cent rise in salary, while the PA officials offered them only a 10 per cent pay raise which was viewed by teachers as insufficient. They complained that their salaries were lower than their contemporaries in other ministries.

Teacher sources said that the teachersí leaders agreed to end the strike under the threat from Palestinian police and education ministry officials of further arrests and dismissals if they did not give in.

The teachers denounced the corruption in the PA civil service. "Millions are being squandered on cellular phone bills for PA officials and on their luxurious cars whereas the Education Ministry does not have a budget to meet its operating expenses," a statement by the teachers said.

The teachers had staged partial strikes during the past few months calling for a salary increase of 200 per cent. But the PA only agreed to increase their salaries by 10 per cent. The teachers also demanded improvement of retirement rules and regulations. They said they were surprised to know that instead of meeting their demands, the Education Minister had dismissed 19 of their colleagues, hence their decision to strike.

Dr. Naem Abul Hummos, Under-Secretary of the Education Ministry, pointed out that the PA had taken over a totally destroyed infrastructure which included the education sector. "We need more than 1,200 classrooms and 1,500 new teachers to accommodate the increasing number of Palestinian pupils," Abul Hummos said.

The Palestinian official said his Ministry needs more than $200 million a year to maintain its schools, an amount which is not available now.

Dr. Abul Hummos added that the PA spends about $450 million a year on salaries for its employees besides $200 million spent on social activities.

Meanwhile, the head of the education committee in the PA's Legislative Council (parliament), Abbas Zaki, supported the teachers' demands saying that the Council sees these demands as "fair" because of the low wages the teachers receive. "We live in a crisis as a result of failing to assess our political and economic policies and as a result of accepting a low-ceiling peace agreement with Israel,î Zaki added.

17 April: A Date for Solidarity in the Palestinian Calendar

By : Ruba Farkh

The prisoners of conscience have always been one of the constants in the story of a nationís salvation, just like martyrs. Both are the tax paid for freedom and independence.

Prison walls all around the world would tell all sorts of stories about heroes who spent their lives behind bars because of their undying belief in just causes and their efforts to attain lofty dreams.

In the Israeli prisons, there are more than 3,000 Palestinian prisoners, of which 120 are children and 400 others are a combination of the elderly and the sick. They are all distributed throughout 12 jails and charged with patriotism to their country.

The Israeli authorities have blatantly ignored international laws governing human rights protection mechanisms and continue to systematically torture their Palestinian prisoners whether or not an actual crime has been committed. What is even more disturbing to people of conscience is the fact that these atrocities are carried out under the guise of Israeli law and approved not only by the ministerial committee for intelligence affairs in 1994, but also by the Israeli Supreme Court in 1996.

The permission to use torture has resulted in the killing of 114 prisoners, either directly by shooting or indirectly as a result of various methods such as violent shaking, beatings, hangings, sleep deprivation and the conscious decision to withhold medical treatment. The human rights abuses by the Israelis go beyond physical torture; the prisoners are also isolated from the outside world. This is accomplished by the constant relocation of detainees from one prison to another causing family members great difficulties in tracking down their imprisoned relatives. Even if the prisoner is successfully located, the Israeli authorities impose strict visitation constraints which limits visitors to first-degree family members (parents and wife), while brothers, sisters and children are allowed only if they are under the age of 16 or over the age of 40.

Despite the harsh conditions Palestinians endure in Israeli jails, the high morale of the prisoners and amazing solidarity among them (for instance, sit-ins and hunger strikes) has enabled them to procure some of their human rights; they were able to improve the quality of their food and to have a sick prisoner moved to a hospital to receive appropriate treatment. Unfortunately, there were plenty of failures by their noble efforts which cost some inmates their lives in the process.

17 Aprilóthe day of solidarity with the Palestinian prisonersóbrings back sad, yet bitter, memories of all those who died behind bars and those whose sentences have made sure they will never see the light of freedom again.
