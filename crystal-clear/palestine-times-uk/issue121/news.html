Sharon wants “cease-fire in return for nothing”

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

As the Israeli propaganda machine continues with its vociferous misinformation campaign against the Palestinian Authority, particularly its Chairman Yasser Arafat, the Israeli occupation army and para-military Jewish settlers have continued their own concerted campaign of terror and rampage throughout the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

The latest escalation culminated on 24 June, when Israeli intelligence agents murdered a Fatah activist in downtown Nablus.

According to Palestinian police sources, Osama Fathi Jawabra, 29, was killed when a booby-trapped public telephone in downtown Nablus exploded in his face as he picked up the telephone handset.

The murder, which was apparently a fulfilment of an earlier decision by the Israeli inner cabinet to resume the “assassination policy” against intifada activists, infuriated the Palestinian public and forced Fatah leaders to warn that “this cease-fire is killing us,” and that “ death will be met with death from now on.”

“This was a brazen violation of the cease-fire; it comes on the eve of Sharon’s visit to the United States; this is unacceptable and the world community must realize that it is the Israelis who are breaching the cease-fire,” said Anan al-Attiri, a spokesperson of Fatah in the Nablus area.

During Jawabra’s funeral on 25 June, in which thousands of Palestinians took part, Fatah leaders vowed to avenge the murder.

“The criminal Sharon should understand that Fatah will not allow its cadres to be assassinated under the rubric of the cease-fire…the cease-fire can go to hell.”

Since the outbreak of the intifada nearly nine months ago, the Israeli army and intelligence services assassinated as many as 40 Palestinian activists in what several human rights groups around the world termed “extra-judicial executions.”

Meanwhile, Israeli army bulldozers once again visited with utter destruction the small refugee camp of Yibna in Rafah at the southern tip of the Gaza Strip, demolishing as many as 20 homes and leaving their indigent occupants homeless.

As usual, the brutal ugliness of Israeli racism manifested itself in ways defying even linguistic description.

“After midnight, the tanks and bulldozers arrived. They yelled at us to leave the house; we took the children and fled, and they flattened it,” said Hasan Burhom- a victim of the latest savagery- with a whiff of cynicism mixed with a heavy dose of frozen rage.

“They only gave us five minutes to get the children out; they told us the bulldozers would crush them if we didn’t get them out in five minutes,” said his cousin, Muhammed.

Asked where he and the other victims were going to sleep that night, he said “somewhere between the land and the sky.”

Another victim, Ahmed Burhom, described the demolition of the houses as “a massacre of buildings.”

“An army that storms civilian neighbourhoods in the quiet hours before dawn, destroying residential homes and terrorizing sleeping men, women and children without any justification, is not an army of soldiers but an army of thugs and criminals…if something with this ugliness and brutality happened anywhere in the world, it would be called a war crime, yet the world is silent at this Jewish Nazism,” said Burhom, who lost a home and an olive orchard, in an angry but serene tone.

As daylight broke, the victims, some distraught by the traumatic experience while others raised their hands heavenward asking God to avenge Israeli oppression, wistful-looking men and women, rummaged the concrete block piles of what was left of their homes.

Children carried away kitchenware and dragged away half-torn mattresses, or simply searched for a toy they couldn’t bear to see crushed.

An Israeli army spokesman said in a terse statement that they were carrying out “engineering work” for the purpose of widening a road used by Israeli army patrols along the Egyptian borders. Asked why the army chose to carry out the operation after midnight, the spokesman said, “Can you suggest a more suitable time?”

However, the rabid destruction of the houses was in reality a collective reprisal against innocent and defenceless civilians for the death of two Israeli soldiers a day earlier by a Palestinian martyr-bomber. Hamas claimed responsibility for the operation, saying it was “in retaliation for continued Israeli killings and repression of our people.”

The agricultural “victim”
The barbarity of the wholesale home-demolition in Rafah was actually being matched with another equally affronting barbarity, namely the wide-scale burning by Jewish settlers of Palestinian fields and orchards.

Palestinian sources and foreign journalists have described the torching of the fields as “phenomenal,” pointing out that thousands of acres of Palestinian orchards, fields of almonds, and wheat crops have been reduced to ashes by settler fires.

So far, the settlers have concentrated their rampage in and around villages lying between Nablus and Ramallah, many of which are still outside the jurisdiction of the Palestinian Authority.

In some cases, many villagers lost everything, having seen the source of their livelihood, which they spent their lifetime tending and caring for like they rear their children, turn to ash in a matter of a few hours.

The Israeli army, which normally guards the settlers “while at work” against Palestinian resistance, often blocked firefighters from putting out the fire.

In several cases, occupation soldiers themselves take part in setting Palestinian fields on fire as happened at the villages of Abud and Sawiya south of Nablus on 24 and 25 June.

The rampage of arson and vandalism is carried out by messianic Jewish settlers who believe that Palestinian villages and towns should be burned down in the same way the old Israelites dealt with Canaanite tribes during biblical times.

Sharon continues repression policy
In light, it seems amply clear that the Israeli government has neither the willingness nor the inclination to significantly reduce the level of repression meted out to the overall Palestinian population. This, in turn, makes it extremely difficult for Yasser Arafat to persuade his tormented people to suspend their resistance of Israeli repression.

In short, Sharon seems intent on retaining virtually all the repressive measures against the Palestinians while at the same time continuing to demand a “complete” cessation to “violence.”

Needless to say, the experience of the last two weeks shows that this is quite impossible.

Precarious cease-fire

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

With the Israeli blockade of Palestinian towns and villages remaining effectively in place, and with Jewish terrorist attacks on Palestinian civilians continuing unabated, Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat is having difficulties convincing the politically-educated Palestinian public to end the intifada, or even to stop responding in kind to settler attacks and vandalism.

Indeed, despite Israeli propaganda and often outright misinformation about relaxing the suffocating siege, it was clear that the “relaxation” lacked in substance and did not alleviate substantially the overall plight of over three million Palestinians who are more or less confined to their immediate places of residence, thanks to the draconian blockade.

In some instances, the Israeli army did remove concrete slabs off some roads, but no sooner had the television cameras disappeared than the Israeli army re-established the roadblocks.

In some other cases, the stationary roadblocks were simply replaced by mobile roadblocks, a ruse aimed at tricking the world public opinion into believing that the blockade had indeed been relaxed, all while maintaining the same level of repression against the Palestinians.

In fact, far from relaxing the siege, the Israeli army introduced even more stringent measures in several localities, particularly in the Hebron and Tulkarm regions, apparently for no other purpose than to further torment the Palestinians and make their daily life as unbearable and harsh as possible.

For example, the Israeli army deployed a number of tanks and armoured vehicles to the Tarousa Knoll, just outsideDura, 12 kilometres south-west of Hebron, cutting the town off from several surrounding villages and forcing villagers to use donkeys and mules instead of cars as their chief means of transportation.

There are no Jewish settlements in the area, and the onerous presence of the Israeli occupation soldiers only a few hundred metres from the confines of a Palestinian town of 30,000 can only be construed to mean that the Israeli army is looking for trouble.

In addition to causing an atmosphere of virtual claustrophobia in shut-off Palestinian towns and villages, the effect of the Israeli blockade can be deadly.

Last week, a Palestinian man with a chronic kidney illness died at an Israeli roadblock near Nablus, having been denied access to the hospital on the other side of the roadblock. The man is added to a score of Palestinians who succumbed to their illnesses at Israeli checkpoints and roadblocks since the beginning of the intifada nearly nine months ago.

Settlers’ Role
If the Israeli army is entrusted with tormenting the Palestinian people at the macro level, the heavily armed fanatic Jewish settlers are left to unleash their own virulent violence at the micro level, namely against Palestinian villages and hamlets contiguous to the estimated 150 Jewish settlements and settlement outposts dotting the West Bank.

The settlers’ wanton violence assumes two main aspects: ambushing Palestinian motorists on the roads, especially the so-called bypass roads linking the settlements with Jewish metropolitan centres in Israel proper; and the habitual practice of violently marauding through Palestinian villages, opening fire randomly, setting wheat fields and olive orchards on fire, and vandalizing as much property as they can get.

Interestingly, this senseless rampage very often takes place in full view of Israeli occupation soldiers who apparently have orders to “let the settlers vent their anger.”

And “venting their anger” also means murder.

On 14 June, the settlers opened fire on a Palestinian lorry outside Jerusalem, killing 42-year-old Awni Abdul Raouf al-Haddad, a father of six children, of Hebron.

The ambush took place near the village of Hizma only a few hundred metres from an Israeli army camp, known as “Anatut.” As usual in such circumstances, the Israeli army sought to deflect attention from the settlers, claiming that “probably non-Jews were responsible for the incident.”

The killing came less than 24 hours after an Orthodox clergyman was ambushed in a similar manner, less than a hundred metres from an Israeli roadblock outside the settlement of Ma’ali Adumim, east of Jerusalem. No arrests have been made in connection with the two incidents.

Interestingly, the two murders came only a few hours after Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon told Likud supporters that “we should surprise them (the Arabs) on the roads and make them feel unsafe.”

And that wasn’t all. Three more Palestinians, all of them children, were also killed by the Israeli army soldiers in Khan Younis (Gaza Strip), prompting the indignant Palestinian public to question the wisdom of maintaining a cease-fire while the big boots of the Israeli army were still pressed against the chests and necks of the Palestinian people.

The children are 12-year-old Ali Murad Abu Sahawish, shot dead by an Israeli sniper near the Tuffah junction south of Gaza town, and Suleiman al-Masri, also 12, killed by an Israeli soldier in Rafah at the southern tip of the Gaza Strip.

A third boy, identified as 16-year-old Adel Hasan Muqanan, died on 18 June of wounds he had sustained on 16 June near the Tuffah junction. A fourth Palestinian, an elderly man aged 81 years old, was run over by a Jewish settler on 18 June while crossing the road near Qalqilya in the northern West Bank.

Another Zionist killing of an innocent Palestinian took place on 21 June, when Israeli occupation troops opened fire on a group of Palestinian labourers while bypassing an Israeli army roadblock near the village of Na’alin north-west of Ramallah.

The Zionists killed 30-year-old Oudhe Ahmed Nafie’, a father of six, who was returning home from a hard day’s work.

The Israeli army said it was investigating the incident, a euphemism for consigning the story to history.

The continued killing and repression of the Palestinians by the Israeli army and Jewish settlers alike prompted Arafat on 16 June to appeal to visiting UN Secretary-General Kofi Anan to see to it that international monitors are deployed in the West Bank and Gaza Strip “to establish who is violating the cease-fire.”

However, Anan is unlikely to push for international monitors to be deployed in the Occupied Territories as long as the Americans reject the idea.

This pushed Arafat to warn that the American-brokered cease-fire would collapse if the international community did not force Israel to abide by the Mitchell committee report and Tenet work-plan. Arafat’s warnings did not seem to be tactical.

Indeed, several high-level security meetings between Israel and the PA in recent weeks ended in failure due to adamant Israeli refusal to establish a direct linkage between the security and political aspects of the current crisis, a linkage without which the PA would not have accepted the Mitchell report in the first place.

And the intifada fighters are not giving the cease-fire the benefit of the doubt.

On 18 June, Palestinian guerillas killed two Jewish settlers in two separate incidents outside a Jewish settlement in the Nablus area.

A third Israeli, a high-ranking Shin Bet officer bearing the rank of Lieutenant Colonel, was killed and his bodyguard was badly injured by a repentant Arab collaborator whom the officer met at a pre-arranged rendezvous south of Jerusalem. The Palestinian was subsequently killed by a third Israeli soldier who was present at the spot.

Responsibility for the three attacks was claimed by the Aqsa Martyrs Regiments, the military arm of Fatah.

Fatah, along with the Islamist and other nationalist organizations, has displayed a cautious posture vis-�-vis a cease-fire, arranged by CIA Director George Tenet and accepted under duress by Yasser Arafat.

This posture assumes the form of maintaining fully the popular aspect of the intifada while retaining a modicum of armed resistance, if only as a response to attacks by Israeli soldiers and settlers.

“Let us not fool ourselves; any negotiations with Israel would be a waste of time if not backed up by action in the field,” said Marwan al-Barghouthi, Fatah leader in the West Bank.

And this is not only Barghouthi’s thinking.

In fact, the vast bulk of Palestinians seem to think likewise.

Palestinians welcome BBC documentary,
say it is time to try Sharon as war criminal

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Palestinians have lauded a BBC documentary that was broadcast Sunday night, 17 June, and concluded that there is sufficient evidence to indict Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon as a war criminal for his involvement in the Sabra and Shatilla massacres of Palestinian refugees in 1982.

PA official Ahmed Abdul Rahman voiced satisfaction that the world “is finally beginning to hear our crying after so many years of massacres of our people by the Israeli State.”

“We have been crying and screaming to the world for more than five decades that Sharon and other Zionist leaders are war criminals who ought to be treated like the Nazi war criminals…It gives us some solace that the wold is beginning to listen to us.”

Abdul Rahman urged “all justice-loving people and human rights organizations around the world to start legal action against Sharon now, making use of this significant international documentary.”

The Palestinian official dismissed the view that starting legal procedures against Sharon now would undermine peace efforts and harden Israel’s already hostile posture towards the Palestinians.

“The opposite is true. Peace can only be achieved when the criminals are brought to justice and tried for their crimes against humanity.”

He added, “We owe it to those children who were slaughtered like sheep; the young women who were raped, humiliated and slaughtered; the helpless souls who were murdered so callously; we owe it to them to try the criminals.”

However, while welcoming the screening of the documentary 22 years after the massacres, most Palestinians harbour no illusions about the vast distance between indicting Sharon in a television documentary and prosecuting him before an international tribunal.

“We know that Sharon is a big criminal; the world knows that Sharon is a big criminal; the United States knows that Sharon is a war criminal; the question is not whether Sharon is a war criminal or not, the real question is whether the world, particularly the United States, has the political will to try Sharon as a war criminal,” said Hanna Issa, a Palestinian expert on international law based in Ramallah in the West Bank.

Issa argued that the condemnation of Sharon as a war criminal by a television documentary was late but not too late.

“Crimes against humanity don’t lose relevance with the passage of time, and it is never too late to try Sharon and other Zionist leaders for their hideous crimes against the Palestinian people and other Arab peoples.”

Issa suggested that legal steps by Arab States, the Palestinian Authority and human organizations be taken immediately against Sharon.

“It would be self-defeating and humiliating for the Arabs and the Palestinians to stop at this documentary and celebrate victory; they must pursue this paramount issue, because if they don’t, Sharon and the other Sharon’s who will come after him will repeat the Sabra and Shatilla massacres once, twice and thrice as long as they think they can get away with it.”

“The matter (prosecuting Sharon) will take time, a long time,” said Issa.

“Justice without power is powerless, and power without justice is oppressive,” he added.

The BBC Panorama programme featured Judge Richard Goldstone, former chief prosecutor for the UN criminal tribunals for both the former Yugoslavia and Rwanda, who concluded that Ariel Sharon should be tried for war crimes in connection with the 1982 massacres.

“If the person who gave the command knows, or should know…that there’s a situation where innocent civilians are going to be injured or killed then that person is responsible, in my book even more responsible than the people who carry out the orders.”

The programme also contained comments from international legal expert Richard Falk, Professor of International Law at Princeton University, suggesting that Sharon could be indicted for his role in the massacres.

The Israeli government condemned the BBC for screening the documentary, accusing it of “anti-Israeli bias” and of being “pro-Palestinian.”

A statement by the Israeli foreign ministry, released on 17 June, criticized the timing of the documentary saying, “It gives the Palestinians additional ammunition in the propaganda war with Israel.”

However, the BBC rebuffed the Zionist criticisms, arguing that the job of a reporter is to deal with the facts, and the facts of Sabra and Shatilla are deeply shocking.

In its statement, the BBC said it stood by the story which it called “a legitimate analysis of a human rights issue.”

“At Panorama we are simply being true to a basic principle. It is a duty of journalists to question the actions and record of those who wield power.”

Fergal Keane, the BBC journalist who reported the piece, repudiated claims by Ramaam Gissen, a spokesman for Sharon, that the documentary was an insult to Sharon.

“Contrary to inaccurate statements put out by some Israeli officials, Mr. Gissen was fully aware of the subject when he gave the interview.”

“When the interview was over he left us on friendly terms. In fact some days later his officials provided us with a letter to help us transit easily through Ben Gurion airport with our tapes.”

Massacre survivors testify
On Monday, 18 June, an important event took place in Brussels, as the dossiers of 28 Palestinian and Lebanese survivors from the Sabra and Shatilla massacres were deposited before an investigative judge.

The case was officially opened against Sharon and any other Israeli or Lebanese persons responsible for “war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide.”

It is expected that a decision on whether to indict Sharon by a Belgian court will be made by the end of June 2001.

The Martyrdom weapon: The Palestinian balance of terror

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

On 1 June, around midnight, a Palestinian martyr/bomber, with explosives strapped to his body, blew himself into bits and pieces at a Tel Aviv nightclub, killing as many as 18 Israeli youths and injuring over 100 others.

The result: 18 families are added to the hundreds of Palestinian and dozens of Israeli families who are mourning the loss of a child, a parent or brother, killed or murdered in the course of the current Palestinian uprising against Israeli military occupation.

Of course, the matter is not as simple as people getting killed on both sides. The Zionist regime is, after all, the tormentor, occupier, oppressor and usurper, while the Palestinians are the decimated underdogs, struggling to free themselves from the shackles of Zionist occupation and domination.

The attack itself was described as the worst in five years and the bloodiest since the outbreak of the intifada in late September of last year. Israeli leaders, enraged by the bombing, decided to take a new set of reprisals against the Palestinians, including the hermetic sealing off of the West Bank and Gaza Strip, the closure of all border crossings from and to the autonomous enclaves, as well as barring Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat from leaving Ramallah, effectively placing him under town arrest.

In other words, the “Shtakhim” or “territories” or “areas” as dubbed by the Israeli media, were effectively converted into a huge open-air detention camp, or mini-camps, cut off from each other and isolated from the rest of the world.

More to the point, the Israeli inner cabinet reportedly adopted a set of “practical measures” against the Palestinians, an ostensible reference to military reprisals against “targets” in Palestinian towns in the West Bank and Gaza Strip. Indeed, a high-ranking Israeli military commander was quoted as saying that the “cease-fire” with the Palestinians was over and that Israel was amassing more troops to the “front lines.” Needless to say, the term front lines is scandalously misleading since Israel is still occupying the West Bank and the PA-administered towns are encircled by the Israeli army and no Palestinian can enter or leave these cities unless allowed by the occupation forces.

For its part, the PA–forced into a defensive position in the Public Relations showdown with Israel–declared its willingness to accept and observe an unconditional and immediate cease-fire in order to stop “this terrible bloodshed.”

In this context, Arafat did issue clear instructions to his security subordinates to strictly adhere to the cease-fire and refrain from firing unless it is in self-defense.

Nevertheless, it is hard to give “the cease-fire” (an inaccurate wording describing a case of popular resistance against a military occupation) the benefit of the doubt, as long as Israel refuses to truly accept the Mitchell committee report, especially those clauses demanding a freeze on Jewish settlement expansion in the West Bank, including the so-called “natural growth expansion.”

Indeed, while Israeli Foreign Minister Shimon Peres, the ultimate public relations officer for the war criminal Sharon, continues to preach Israeli “acceptance” of the report, other ministers, and even Sharon himself, continue to say “No”, though somewhat sparingly, to a complete settlement freeze in the occupied territories.

And it is not only verbally. On 28 May, Housing Minister Natan Sharansky told an Israeli radio station that he had approved construction bids for 496 new settler units within Ma’ale Adumim, the largest illegal Jewish settlement in the West Bank, where there are hundreds of empty flats awaiting occupants and where home sales reportedly plummeted since the start of the intifada.

Sharanksy had also approved bids for 217 additional settler units in Alfei Menashe, near the Palestinian town of Qalqilya.

Needless to say, the Israeli government’s increasingly apparent intent on keeping up Jewish settlement aggrandizement exposes naked its colonialist designs in the West Bank, which can be summarized in one word: a fait accompli.

Indeed, imposing the fait accompli on the Palestinians, and forcing them to succumb to it, seems to be the undeclared policy of the Sharon government.

Sharon repeatedly said in no unmistakable terms that he would not agree to dismantle any settlement and that Israel would keep the bulk of the West Bank in the context of any possible “arrangement” with the Palestinians.

And Sharon seems to mean what he says, and worse, he has demonstrated characteristic willingness to back up his political belief with lethal military force.

On 18 May, he sent his F-16 fighter-bombers, the second deadliest weapon at Israel’s disposal after its sizeable nuclear arsenal, to rain havoc and death on several Palestinian towns.

The lethal combat aircraft dropped munitions as large as 250 kilograms on a prison, a police dormitory and a TV relay station in Nablus, Tulkarm and Ramallah, as well as the Gaza Strip, killing, mutilating and incinerating over a hundred people.

The F-16 bombing only culminated a spate of wanton killing targeting civilians, activists and especially Force-17 cadres whom Israel accuses of taking a leading role in armed resistance against Israeli occupation.

The killing was carried out through a variety of means, including often rabid and indiscriminate artillery bombardment of civilian neighbourhoods which on 7 May killed four-month-old Iman Hijjo while in her mother’s lap in their Khan Younis home.

It also included murderous ambushes and assassinations such as the cold-blooded killing by undercover Israeli troops on 14 May of five Palestinian policemen who were manning a guard post near Beitunia just south of Ramallah. That particular killing, many Palestinians felt, was a vile, treacherous act, as the five victims were assigned to liaise with their Israeli security counterparts in the area.

“This is a vile and unethical crime and Israel will not get away with it,” said an angry Yasser Arafat, pointing out that the victim’s guard post was established in coordination with the Israeli army.

“The whole thing was unexpected; it never occurred to us that the Israelis would murder the very people with whom they liaise and coordinate on a daily basis…this is a massacre, a real massacre, except in this case, policemen were killed.”

The Israeli army only belatedly recognized that a mistake had been made, but even then, it did virtually nothing to ensure that the “incident” would not be repeated.

Boomerang effect
This Israeli policy toward the Palestinians, based as it is on persecution and subjugation, has not been without a boomerang effect. Palestinian radicals from Hamas and the Islamic Jihad and, for the first time, the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP), stepped up their car-bomb attacks in Israel’s urban centres, sending waves of terror and panic throughout the Israeli Jewish population.

The martyr-bombing in Netanya on 18 May and the 2 June bombing at the Tel Aviv disco were only examples of what the Palestinians term as “their ability to strike deep into Israel and create a semblance of a balance of terror vis-�-vis the third strongest military power on earth.”

But the bombings, the retaliations, the retaliatory bombings, and then the fresh retaliation, all underscore the fundamental bankruptcy and futility of the Zionist policy, a policy based on bullying the Palestinians to come to terms with Jewish apartheid.

The Palestinians do realize that their struggle, even the martyrdom operations, will not defeat Israel at the strategic level. However, Palestinian resistance is succeeding in persuading the Zionist regime, including its fascist clique comprised of the likes of Sharon and Katsave, that sustaining apartheid in Palestine is not without a cost, and the cost is big and growing.

Palestinian journalists in the danger zone

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT
Covering the current Palestinian uprising has never been an easy task, particularly for the largely unprotected Palestinian journalists and cameramen who are often viewed by the Israeli army and Jewish settlers as “representative of the enemy.”

Indeed, for the average Palestinian journalist, it doesn’t seem to make much of a difference whether he or she works for WAFA (the Palestinian News Agency), the Abu Dhabi Television, or for that matter, Middle East International, for, in the final analysis, all Palestinians, including journalists and correspondents, fall within the “enemy category,” as far as the Israeli army and settlers are concerned.

If some foreign journalists are treated by the Israelis with a modicum of hostility because of the “message”, Palestinian journalists are treated with double and sullen hostility because of both the “message” and “the messenger.” The message is hostile and the messenger is the enemy!

On several occasions, Israeli hostility to the Palestinian media culminated with aerial bombardment of transmitters of the PA radio station (Voice of Palestine) on 12 October, and the PA television station in Gaza during the winter. Moreover, the offices of the al-Hayatul Jadida newspaper in Ramallah were twice strafed with machine-gun fire, after being accused of fanning incitement against the Israeli occupation.

For journalists in the field, the sullen hostility is expressed in various ways, ranging from feeling the onerous and often menacing presence of ready-to-shoot Israeli soldiers and their rough treatment at checkpoints and roadblocks to receiving one or two rubber-coated bullets or even a few “warning shots” in the lower or upper part of one’s body.

In fact, Israeli assaults on journalists, especially cameramen, became a recurrent affair ever since the outbreak of the intifada more than eight months ago.

The New York-based Committee to Protect Journalists (CPJ) released an initial report on the subject as early as November, documenting more than two dozen cases of journalists having been injured or harassed while covering the intifada.

Then, CPJ stated that it was particularly disturbed by seven cases in which journalists either charged that they were intentionally targeted by Israeli forces or where the circumstances of the shootings raise concerns that the Israeli defense forces were at least guilty of extreme negligence.

As the uprising raged on, assaults on and harassment of Palestinian and to a lesser extent foreign journalists received minimal or inadequate coverage in the Western media.

For example, the killing of the Palestinian journalist Aziz Ibrahim al-Tanah was hardly mentioned in the major Western news outlets, especially in the United States.

Al-Tanah, 32, was gunned down by an Israeli sniper while covering the news of the intifada in Bethlehem in late October. He was the head of the WAFA bureau in Bethlehem.

The serious wounding of a French journalist who, like the rest, had “PRESS” clearly inscribed on his back preceded the killing of Tanah. The French authorities did protest the incident, but that was it.

The wounding, beating and harassment of journalists by the IDF, and especially by Jewish settlers, continued unabated, reflecting a pattern combining criminal negligence, recklessness and, yes, malicious indifference towards journalists.

CPJ Executive Director Ann Cooper wrote in an appeal to Israeli Prime Minister Ariel Sharon on 15 April that “we remain deeply concerned about the IDF’s record of harassing, intimidating and attacking journalists, despite the Israeli government’s stated commitment to press freedom.”

On 11 June, a delegation from CPJ met with Israeli ambassador to the U.S., David Ivry, to express its deep concern about the cases of more than a dozen journalists wounded by Israeli gunfire during the course of the intifada.

The delegation reportedly expressed its concern that in some cases, journalists may have been deliberately targeted.

It pointed out that at the very least, the IDF acted recklessly in firing live rounds or rubber-coated steel bullets that injured journalists.

“Unless the Israeli government takes immediate steps to put a stop to these incidents, it is only a matter of time before another journalist is killed,” said CPJ board chairman Gene Roberts at the meeting.

Of the nearly 15 journalists who were shot and wounded by the Israeli army, 3 sustained moderate-to-serious wounds.

On 15 May, a reporter for the French Television channel, TF1, Bertrand Aguirre, was hit in the chest by a live round fired by an Israeli border policeman. Film footage of the incident showed the Israeli soldier, with a cigarette dangling from his mouth, shooting indiscriminately in the direction of unarmed civilians and journalists. Aguirre’s bulletproof vest apparently saved his life.

On 20 April, Layla Odeh, correspondent for Abu Dhabi TV, was hit by a bullet in the right femur while filming with her camera crew in Rafah at the southern tip of the Gaza Strip. Eyewitnesses testified that the area then was quiet and witnessed no violence.

Furthermore, three Reuters correspondents had been shot at by IDF troops near the Mintar junction on 18 March and the incident was described by IDF spokesman Oliver Rafowicz as “warning shots,” claiming the journalists had put themselves at risk by approaching the IDF outpost.

A really flagrant attack on Palestinian journalists took place in Hebron on 6 June when a mob of Israeli settlers attacked four cameramen covering a settler rampage in the Old Town.

According to eyewitnesses, rabid settlers who were attacking Palestinian civilians and vandalizing their property in full view of on-looking Israeli soldiers, turned on journalists and cameramen, beating them and smashing their cameras.

An AFP photojournalist, Husam Abu Allan, suffered a fractured jaw resulting from being hit repeatedly by a settler using the butt of his M-16 rifle. Abu Allan had been seriously wounded by IDF bullets while covering Palestinian protests in downtown Hebron two years ago.

Cameramen Nayef Hashlamoun, Mazen Da’ana, Laui Abu Haikal and Imad Said all sustained various injuries resulting from being beaten.

The previous day, two other Palestinian cameramen, Tareq Kayali and Amer Abedeen, who work for a German television station, were held up at the notorious IDF Tarqumya junction, and severely beaten. The two men testified that an Israeli army jeep actually attempted to run them over.

On the same day, Israeli troops shot an Associated Press photographer in the leg, using rubber-coated steel bullets.

The frequent attacks and harassment of journalists are obviously aimed at intimidating them in the hope that they would refrain from covering events deemed unfavourable by the Israeli army and settlers, such as settler violence and vandalism against Palestinian civilians. Moreover, it seems the Israelis hold journalists and cameramen, especially those who don’t show “understanding” of the Israeli “viewpoint” at least partly responsible for some of the unfavourable coverage Israel receives in the West. This is a classical case of shooting the messenger for communicating a bad message.

On the Palestinian side, an isolated incident briefly marred the otherwise impeccable Palestinian record of good treatment of foreign journalists and gave Israel a propaganda munitions to besmirch the Palestinian struggle.

On 29 May, two Western journalists (an American and a Briton) and their Arabic interpreter and driver were “kidnapped” and released two hours later by two individuals associated with the Fatah movement. The incident, which took place in Gaza, blown out of proportion by pro-Israeli media, was actually an individual act that was totally condemned by all Palestinian factions as “uncharacteristic of the Palestinian people and detrimental to our noble struggle.”

One of the two journalists kidnapped, Joshua Hammer, head of Newsweek’s Jerusalem bureau said, “We were treated very well.”

The two journalists even refrained from referring to the time spent at a Palestinian house as kidnapping, saying they would stay in Gaza for the rest of the week, according to previous plans, to finish their work.

Israeli army admits using prohibited
munitions against civilians

Occupied Jerusalem - The Israeli occupation army admitted using internationally-prohibited munitions against Palestinian civilians.

The Israeli State-run radio quoted a senior army officer as saying that the army “has been using tank-fired flechette anti-personnel shells” against Palestinians.

This lethal type of munitions killed three Palestinian women from one family from south of the Gaza Strip on 9 June, and claimed many other lives in the course of the 8-month-old Palestinian intifada.

The Israeli officer said the use of the deadly shell against individuals was “legitimate,” claiming the army was authorized to use it “outside populated areas.”

The Palestinians, however, strongly dispute the claim, stressing that hundreds of flechette shells were fired on residential areas.

Hospital sources in Gaza, especially in the Rafah area, spoke of hundreds of civilians wounded by the darts released when the shell explodes.

According to Jane’s Defense Weekly, the Israeli army is using a modified version of the M949 105 mm APERS-T round provided by the U.S. in the 1970s. According to a U.S. manual, the round is “designed for close-in assault against massed infantry assaults and for offensive fire against exposed enemy personnel.”

The Israeli army fitted the M949 shell with the Reshef Technologies OMEGA M127 electronic fuse, which is set, before the round is fired. At the set range, the forward section of the M949 round ruptures, releasing approximately 5,000 small flechette darts and a dye marker. The flechettes are dispersed in a cone-shaped pattern, which is 300 metres long, and about 94 metres wide.

Palestinian security official Abdul Razzaq al-Majayda described the use of the flechette shells against Palestinian civilians as “a manifestly blatant war crime.”

“Their aim is to maim, cripple and kill as many people as possible,” said Majayda.

Palestinian human rights group reveals
torture of activist by Shin Bet

Occupied Jerusalem - A Jerusalem-based human rights group charged that Israeli Shin Bet interrogators beat and tortured a Palestinian human rights activist, who has been detained by the Israeli occupation authorities for a month without charge or trial.

The Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group (PHRMG) said Abed Rahman al-Ahmar, a well-known human rights activist, had been tortured and shackled in utter disregard for human rights and dignity and despite an Israeli Supreme Court injunction against torture.

Al-Ahmar, who works as a field researcher with the PHRMG was arrested and beaten by Israeli security forces on 24 May and has since been held in detention, first at Gush Etzion military detention centre and since 30 May at the notorious Russian compound detention in Jerusalem.

On 10 June, attorney Allegra Pacheco discovered that al-Ahmar had been tortured, shackled in tight handcuffs to a slanting chair for the entire day.

This torture method, known as “Shabah,” was outlawed by the Israeli Supreme Court on 6 December 1999.

Al-Ahmar had been imprisoned several times in the past and had been tortured repeatedly by Shin Bet agents while detained at the Russian Compound and, consequently, still suffers from a hiatal hernia and several back problems. He has been adopted as a prisoner of conscience by Amnesty International.

Zionists admit: We killed three women in cold blood

Occupied Jerusalem - The Zionist regime belatedly and begrudgingly admitted its occupation troops murdered three Palestinian Bedouin women in Khan Younis south of Gaza on 10 June, claiming the killing was “a mistake.”

The three women were killed by a deadly shell containing flechette darts fired by an Israeli tank crew who said they were “firing on terrorists.”

However, Israel’s war minister Benyamin Benalizer said the target the soldiers intended to destroy was more than 1,500 metres away from the tent in which the three women were asleep.

The incident is added to scores of cold-blooded murders perpetrated by nervous and trigger-happy Israeli occupation soldiers unworried about any accountability for their actions.

Racist Zionists warn of Arab population growth

Occupied Jerusalem - Racist Zionists in the apartheid Israeli regime continued to warn against Arab demographic growth in Palestine, calling on the Israeli government and army to seriously take the “looming danger” into account.

A new study by a University of Haifa Professor warned that the Arab population within mandatory Palestine would soon outnumber Jews.

Arnon Soffer was quoted as saying that “we are at a crossroads where the situation, if it continues, will deteriorate on the demographic and socioeconomic fronts which will be like a time-bomb just waiting to explode,” he said.

According to the racist professor, there are today 5 million Jews and 5 million non-Jews in Palestine. The latter figure is composed of 4.5 million Arabs and the remainder are non-Jewish immigrants from the former Soviet Union.

Soffer predicted that by the year 2020, there will be around 6.4 million Jews, based on population growth and an average of 50,000 immigrant settlers a year. As to the Arab growth, Soffer said he expected the Arab population to reach 8.5 million.

300 Palestinians ready for martyrdom operations

Paris - French diplomatic sources have disclosed that 250 to 300 Palestinians were ready to launch martyrdom operations at any time whether inside the Zionist entity or in the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

They added that President Chirac of France had expressed his understanding of Palestinian Authority chairman Yasser Arafat’s inability to control the security situation because of this information he had.
