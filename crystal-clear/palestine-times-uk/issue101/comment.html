Comment

Arafat Must Resign Now!

It is increasingly clear that Yasser Arafat’s mental ability has considerably diminished of late, so much so that serious questions ought to be raised about his fitness in fulfilling his obligations regarding the leadership of the Palestinian people.

According to confidential sources, Arafat is exhibiting several signs of early senility, most likely resulting from brain degeneration. These include severe memory loss, inability to focus, to speak clearly or to move about with ease as well as increasing nervousness.

In order to blur these signs, mainly from the eyes and minds of his people, Arafat has refused to hold a single press conference in the autonomous enclaves for over two years now, and whenever he is asked by journalists to comment on or respond to important events, he usually gives extremely terse and poorly worded answers before he is whisked away by his aides.

In one particular recent event in Ramallah, Arafat spoke only for three to four minutes, with clearly slurred tones, and then a cheer was enforced before he was taken away.

Arafat’s shivering lips, cheeks and hands suggest that even his physical health is questionable.

In any case, what is important here is his deteriorating mental ability, which most directly affects his decision-making process.

This is doubly important because Arafat is an autocrat that solely and single-mindedly takes all the decisions affecting the Palestinian Authority, whether it is a decision on the peace process or permission to repair a car or a request for vacation, as Edward Said pointed out in a recent article.

No one else knows where the money is, where it came from, and where it is spent except he.

In the final analysis, it would not be an overstatement to say that Arafat is effectively the Palestinian Authority.

Given his autocracy, senility and ostensible indispensability (he has made himself indispensable by eliminating other credible alternatives to him in case of a power vacuum), Arafat’s continued leadership could spell disaster for the Palestinian people and the Palestinian cause, especially if he is allowed to take a central role in the upcoming final status talks with the Barak government.

The so-called agreements he has signed with the Israelis recently, particularly the Sharm el-Sheikh and the Safe Passage protocols, suggest that there is something clearly abnormal with Arafat’s mental ability and the way he takes his decisions.

Take, for example, the sensitive prisoners’ issue.

Arafat reportedly rebuked and nearly fired his chief negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat who was insisting that Israel carry out the Wye River clauses affecting prisoners.

Indeed, even Fatah leaders, although they speak in indirect terms, have recently spoken of the necessity of preventing the President from “drifting away too much” and “crossing our national red lines.”

However, it is amply clear that those “leaders” lack the courage and integrity to stand up to the President, even if he chooses to cross the red lines.

Recently, Sakher Habash, a high-ranking Fatah official in the West Bank, resorted to silence when he was asked what he and the rest of his colleagues in the Fatah leadership would do in case Arafat accepted an Israeli-dictated deal whereby a Palestinian State would be established on a small part of the West Bank and without Jerusalem as its capital.

The fact that Habash and his colleagues began looking at each others’ faces, instead of giving a straightforward answer, suggested that they cannot be relied upon to restrain Arafat from selling out Palestinian rights.

The Israeli government, especially the intelligence services, are well aware of Arafat’s mental deterioration and his vulnerability and “ultimate willingness” to concede on sensitive issues such as Jerusalem and the refugees.

Hence, the Israeli premier Ehud Barak, acting on recommendations from the Shin Bet intelligence service and the Mossad, proposed in a recent interview with the Jerusalem Post that Israel should enter into high-gear intensive talks with the PA before Arafat is dead.

The rationale behind the sudden Israeli interest in accelerating the “peace talks” apparently stems from the conviction of the intelligence apparatus that no other Palestinian leader would grant Israel a better deal, one that would presumably virtually liquidate the Palestinian question, including the paramount refugees’ cause.

Arafat has for some time made the Palestinian cause his own, so much so that one can now speak about the two interchangeably. And given this, and in light of his deteriorating mental ability, there is no doubt that his “personal circumstances” will compel him to sign whatever the Israelis and the Americans order him to sign.

Indeed, if Arafat were to realize what he has done and what the enemy—the peace partner—expects him to do during the final status talks, he should step down in dignity.

However, since this is unlikely given Arafat’s egotistic obstinate mindset, the Palestinian people, including Islamist and nationalist forces, ought to say “no” to Arafat.

Moreover, an unmistakable warning should also be made to the United States and to Israel that any agreement or deal signed with Arafat under these conditions would be null and void and unbinding upon the Palestinians.
