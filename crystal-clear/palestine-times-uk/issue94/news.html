Netanyahu displays more extremism ahead of elections

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Israel escalated its sabre-rattling against the Palestinian Authority this week after PA President Yasser Arafat threatened to resume the intifadah, or popular uprising, if the Jewish State sought to deprive the Palestinian people of their right to self-determination and Statehood.

Israeli officials, from Prime Minister Benyamin Netanayhu down the line, issued a plethora of statements warning the PA against “misreading Israel’s resolve”.

On 20 March, a statement came out of Netanyahu’s office warning Palestinians that Israel would take “strong and swift action” in case the PA unilaterally declared Palestinian Statehood.

The statement did not explain what “strong and swift action” would entail, but the Israeli Hebrew daily, Ha’aretz, pointed out in its 21 March edition that Israel would annex the vast bulk of the West Bank in the case of a formal Statehood declaration by Arafat.

In addition to preparing to annex 70% of the West Bank, Israel is also preparing contingency plans to over-run the seven Palestinian towns currently under PA administration.

According to the Israeli press, the two plans, code named “Operation Thorn Field” and “Musical Fascination”, involve stationing snipers at rooftops of high buildings, as well as deploying special forces inside the towns in addition to using helicopter gunships to quell violent protests.

The renewed Israeli warnings surrounding the subject of Palestinian Statehood came a few days after the latest row between Israel and the European Union over the status of Jerusalem, following the recent EU acknowledgement that the whole of Jerusalem was, as far as Europe is concerned, a corpus separatum or “a separate body” belonging to neither Israel nor the Palestinians.

Netanyahu, appearing inclined to make the subject of Jerusalem the main issue in the Israeli election campaign, attacked the EU “for daring to interfere in Israeli internal affairs.”

“Israel”, he said, “will never allow any country or group of countries to dictate the boundaries of our capital.”

Utterly ignoring the fact that Arab East Jerusalem was occupied by Israeli troops in 1967, as was the rest of the West Bank, Netanyahu suggested that Jerusalem was not subject to negotiation with the Palestinians or anybody else.

“I believe the final status talks on Jerusalem would be very brief, because there will be nothing to talk about as far as we are concerned”, said Netanyahu in a Jerusalem press conference on 18 March.

With the Israeli elections only 55 days away, it is clear that Netanyahu wants to exploit the general Israeli Jewish consensus on the town to bolster his election prospects.

In this context, Netanyahu reacted nervously to a visit to the Orient House (formerly the headquarters of the Palestinian Delegation to the Madrid Peace Conference) on 19 March by some ten Western diplomats who were received by three PA officials—Faisal Husseini, Hanan Ashrawi and Ziad Abu Ziad.

Netanyahu, who earlier had sought to prevent the convening of the meeting through his Foreign Minister Ariel Sharon, responded to “the provocation” by stripping the three PA officials of their VIP status.

This implied that the three Jerusalemites would be frisked and humiliated by Israeli soldiers manning roadblocks and checkpoints at key intersections throughout the West Bank and between the West Bank and the Gaza Strip, just like the rest of the Palestinians.

Husseini described the Israeli reaction to the meeting as “provocative and probably meant to serve the election considerations of Netanyahu.”

Ashrawi labelled it a sign of “political bankruptcy”.

The draconian measure taken against the three Palestinian leaders is consistent with Netanyahu’s express policy of “lowering Palestinian expectations” regarding Jerusalem and the rest of the Palestinian territories occupied by Israel in 1967.

From Netanyahu’s viewpoint, lowering Palestinian expectations clearly implies Palestinian acceptance of the fact of Israeli occupation of at least 50% of the West Bank and 30% of the Gaza Strip.

It also implies that Palestinians, particularly the Palestinian Authority, will come to terms with Jerusalem as “Israel’s eternal and united capital” as well as the “indelible” 206 Jewish settlements in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip.

In short, Netanyahu expects nothing short of capitulation from the Palestinian Authority, and his way of doing that is first by insisting on his stance and resisting international, especially American, pressure.

Postponement of declaration
Meanwhile, Arafat seems to have taken a decision to postpone the declaration of Palestinian Statehood until at least after the Israeli elections. It is not clear yet if the postponement will be open-ended, as the Americans seem to insist, or for a limited period of time, as the Palestinian public opinion demands.

That postponement decision was actually widely expected, given the American opposition to the unilateral declaration of Palestinian Statehood. The American administration reportedly made clear to Arafat that “unilateral actions” were unacceptable whether taken by Israel or the Palestinians. However, it is all too clear that when applied to the Palestinians, “unacceptable” means “active opposition” whereas it means “passive opposition” when applied to Israel, e.g. the expansion of Jewish settlements and the unilateral Israeli suspension of the Wye River memorandum.

Needless to say, the Palestinian leader had been hoping, to no avail, that the United States would reward or at least compensate him for taking such a difficult decision, which is quite unpopular among Palestinians who had been made to believe that the fourth of May was a watershed signifying Palestinian Statehood.

Now, Arafat, along with many others in the Middle East, must wait to see the outcome of the Israeli elections before taking his next step.

Palestinians: UN report on human rights
doesn’t tell the whole story

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

Human rights observers in the Occupied territories criticized a recent UN report on human rights violations in the West Bank and Gaza Strip for “understating the severity of human rights violations by both Israel and the Palestinian Authority.”

The report, prepared by UN investigator Hannu Halinen of Finland and published in early March, claimed that Israel had discontinued the most flagrant torture methods, but failed to specify which methods were actually discontinued and when.

The report argued that “while there is a positive development to be recognized on the Israeli side—such as the decrease in administrative detention and the discontinuation of ‘the most flagrant torture methods’—it appears that such violations are increasing on the Palestinian side.”

The report did mention, though, that Israel was continuing to use a wide range of physical and psychological methods of torture against Palestinian detainees as well as depriving Palestinians as a whole of their economic and other basic human rights.

Halinen didn’t say what made him conclude that there had been a certain mitigation in the severity of torture methods used by Israeli interrogators, nor did he explain the reasons for “reduced administrative detention”, namely the fact that fewer Palestinians are now living under Israel’s direct military rule.

However, it seems amply clear that what he had in mind is a set of petty “improvements”, which Israel reportedly introduced as part of its torture methods of late.

These included the substitution of the filthy-smelling sack placed on detainees’ heads for prolonged periods of time by another less rancid one, and also the substitution of hard-pressed metal handcuffs by others made of rubber.

Basem Eid, Director of the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, described Halinen’s report as “understating the truth about human rights violations by Israel and the Palestinian Authority.” Eid said that while the report certainly contained useful information, it didn’t reflect the depth and extent of the human rights tragedy in the occupied territories.

“He (Halinen) didn’t produce any evidence supporting his claim that the brutality of torture by Israeli interrogators against Palestinian detainees had been reduced or mitigated in any significant manner.”

Eid argued that Halinen failed to ascertain the veracity of the “vague Israeli claims” that some of their most sinister and flagrant torture methods were no longer used by Shabak interrogators.

Moreover, Eid questioned whether the present data concerning the use of torture by Israeli interrogators justify Halinen’s conclusion that “there is an improvement on the Israeli side.”

“There is a blackout on what is going on inside the interrogation chambers, and no serious human rights investigator can or should take statements to that effect by torturers or their bosses at their face value.”

Eid pointed out that there was no evidence suggesting that the use of the notorious “shaking technique” (which killed at least one Palestinian detainee, Abdul Samad Hureizat) was discontinued.

Eid’s assertions are in fact corroborated, even vindicated, by the report itself.

Indeed, far from presenting credible evidence supporting his conclusion that there has been “a positive development to be recognized on Israel’s side”, Halinen did in fact cite another little-known but no less sinister torture method employed by Israeli interrogators.

That method, he explains, involves the cutting off of a detainee’s blood circulation by placing him in extremely tight handcuffs whereby the resulting strong pressure on the upper arm causes the person to lose consciousness in approximately three minutes.

The use of this method, which reportedly is becoming widespread, can hardly be considered an improvement, particularly when viewed in the context of the other “traditional” forms of torture that are sanctioned by the Israeli High Court.

These forms include, inter alia, moderate beating (the task of defining what is moderate is left to the interrogator’s own discretion), the above-mentioned hooding, sleep deprivation, handcuffing, blindfolding, fettering, confining detainees in agonizing positions for many hours, placing opaque dark glasses on detainees’ eyes (which could inflict irreparable damage to their eyesight) and the shaking technique. Added to that is a variety of psychological forms of torture, including sexual humiliation, all designed to demoralize detainees and break their will to resist. Unfortunately, the UN report didn’t sufficiently underscore the nightmarish nature of these torture methods and the flagrant human rights violations inherent in employing them on Palestinian detainees.

Israel considers the use of “moderate physical pressure” against Palestinian detainees an indispensable method in combating Palestinian, especially Islamist, resistance.

However, it is sufficiently clear from victims’ testimonies that Israeli interrogators use torture as the “norm” rather than the “exception” to punish Palestinians for their ideas, mainly their ideological orientation and affiliation with certain organizations, e.g. Hamas and the Islamic Jihad. Last year, the Israeli lawyer Lea Tsemel, who has defended Palestinians before Israeli courts since 1967, testified that the employment of torture by Israeli interrogators was a routine practice that is aimed first and foremost at punishing Palestinian detainees.

The Israeli government and its Shin Beth interrogators (Shin Beth is the main torture agency in Israel) remain unmoved by local and international protests against the legalization, let alone the use of torture against Palestinians.

On 2 March, the Israeli High Court of Justice ruled in favour of keeping the torture methods employed by the Shin Beth secret, a very telling step about the complementary relation between Israel’s judicial system and its security apparatus.

UN report ignored by Israeli authorities
“The UN report on human rights violations in the occupied territories”, as it is officially termed, was generally ignored by the Israeli authorities. David Bar Illan, head of the political planning department at the Israeli Prime Minister’s office, criticized the report for “failing to take Israeli security needs into account.” He indicated that Israel was trying to minimize the use of torture, a claim which, as we saw, doesn’t seem to be genuine.

Likewise, the PA said nothing about what the report described as “the increasing violation of human rights and civil liberties by PA security organizations.”

One official at the PA Ministry of Justice said, “We aren’t allowed to comment on these matters.”

Normally, when PA officials are confronted with human rights violations within the autonomous enclaves, they retort with a shrug saying “we are not living in Sweden or Denmark.”

Outbreak Of Violent Protests Indication
Of Palestinian Dissatisfaction

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

Some of the most violent protests ever against Palestinian President Yasser Arafat’s self-rule government broke out on March 10 after a local Palestinian man from Rafah, south Gaza, was condemned to death for slaying a policeman from the Palestine Authority.

Hundreds of demonstrators rioted in the Yabna refugee camp after a security court in Gaza City condemned a local resident, from a prominent Rafah family; Ra’d al Attar, to death for the February 1 killing of a policeman in Rafah. Attar, 25, a member of a PA security force, was accused of acting as a double agent for the Islamic group Hamas. An associate of Attar, Mohammed Abu Shamallah also 25, was sentenced to life in prison and a third man, Osama Abu Tahar, 24, was jailed for 15 years hard labor for involvement in the shooting. This has been the first time that the PA has handed down the death sentence against a suspected member of Hamas.

In the camp of Yabna, PA police opened fire when hundreds of protesters threw stones at them and blocked roads with burning tires to protest the sentences. PA police killed two teenagers, Ala’ Al Hams and Khamis Mahmoud Salama, both 17. Three other persons were also wounded, one a 10-year-old boy. Police quickly withdrew from the camp streets after the shooting.

Later, during a 1000 strong funeral procession for Ala’ Al Hams, people stoned local police stations when the cortege passed by, which in turn prompted officers inside to fire in the air. Further unrest followed after the burial. News of the rioting let President Arafat cut short a scheduled visit to Amman, Jordan.

Attar, Abu Shamala and Abut Taha were charged with killing PA captain Rifa’at Joudeh after he tried to get the three men to surrender to him. The three men tried to flee and in their escape attempt, they also hit and killed two children, one of whom was a 6-year-old girl, with their speeding car.

According to PA sources, Attar, Abu Shamallah and Abu Tahar were recruited into the PA police force three years ago after declaring they had quit the armed wing of Hamas, Izzedin Al Qassam. Prosecutors accused the three of secretly continuing to work for Hamas and when they were discovered and agents moved in to arrest them in Rafah on February 1, they shot and killed a captain in the Palestinian internal security service, Rif’at Judeh. However, Hamas officials stated that the three men had quit Hamas and that the shootout that occurred was the result of a dispute between rival branches of the Palestinian security apparatus.

Subsequently, President Arafat ordered a review of the death sentence against Attar. Mahmoud Hussein, Secretary of Arafat’s Fateh movement in Gaza Strip, told reporters that, “President Arafat met the killer’s family and granted their wish to review the death sentence handed down to Ra’d Al Attar and the sentences given by the court to his two accomplices.”

Arafat’s decision to review the sentence against Attar brought calm to the streets of Rafah on March 13 and shops and schools reopened and residents went about their business as normal witnesses stated. In a further attempt to ease tension, Arafat promised the families of the two 17-year-olds slain when PA police opened fire on the stone-throwing protesters, that the circumstances surrounding the killings would be investigated.

The passing of the death sentence and the subsequent rioting have intensified the growing and even sometimes violent rift between supporters and opponents of Palestinian President Yasser Arafat and the peace process itself.

Meanwhile, a Palestinian human rights group operating in the Palestinian autonomous enclaves criticized the death sentence which a PA Gaza court passed earlier on an erstwhile Hamas member, Ra’ed Attar, on suspicion of killing a PA security officer in Khan Younis last month.

Basem Eid, Director of the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, described the court verdict as “unjust and unlawful”, saying the trial of the defendants in the Judeh’s case lacked the internationally observed procedures.

Eid said the court failed to establish beyond any doubt that the Preventive Security Force officer, Judeh, was killed by Attar.

Eid accused the PA courts of “issuing sentences under street pressure”, saying that “this is not justice; it is vengeance.”

Eid argued that the trial of the three suspects in the killing of Judeh should have involved more investigation and forensic testing.

So far, PA courts have passed 27seven death sentences; 5 of them were actually carried out.

On Tuesday, 16 March, a high-ranking PA official quoted Yasser Arafat as saying, “We can’t establish the authority of law because we don’t have the money to employ competent judges.”

Fatah supporters demonstrate against PA

Occupied Jerusalem – Special to PT

Hundreds of Fatah supporters demonstrated in Ramallah on 16 March, to protest PA failure to bring to justice the murderer of Wisam al-Tarifi, who was killed by a stray bullet during inter-Fatah disturbances in Ramallah last year.

The demonstrators shouted slogans against Musa Arafat, who reportedly gave orders to open fire on a peaceful march of Fatah activists, resulting in the death of Tarifi.

The protesters called on Arafat to see to it that the authority of law is respected.

They vowed to “take the law into their own hands” if the PA failed to punish the murderer.

Last week, Tarifi’s father criticized the court where two suspects were being tried, calling it “a court of injustice.”

This is the third time in two weeks Fatah activists have taken to the streets to demonstrate against the PA.

Recently, supporters of Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub demonstrated in East Jerusalem against Tawfik Tirawi, head of the PA General Intelligence Force.

At least three Palestinian citizens in the West Bank and Gaza Strip were killed in March in shooting incidents involving Palestinian Authority policemen.
On 22 March, one Palestinian, a Fatah activist from Ramallah, was killed when a random bullet, apparently fired by Fatah supporters who were celebrating a certain anniversary, hit him in the head.
Two persons in Gaza also died earlier last month, one of them a policeman, as a result of the "misuse of firearms" by policemen.
The Palestinian human rights organization, al Qanun, or the "law” denounced "the wide misuse of firearms by PA policemen" and "the use of firearms by security cadres in during squabbles and quarrels."
Al Qanun pointed out that five Palestinians were killed in the last five months in shooting incidents.
The PA issued regulations banning the use of firearms in public, but police largely failed to enforce the law mainly because there are as many as ten independent security agencies operating in the Palestinian autonomous enclaves with little or no coordination among themselves.

In another development, four Palestinian Legislative Council members participating in a symposium on “Democracy in Palestine” in Jenin on 17 March, unanimously agreed that Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat was the chief factor impeding the development of democracy in Palestine.

The four councilors, Ahmed Irsheid, Burhan Jarrar, Fakhri Turkuman and Jamal Shati, pointed out that Arafat very often neither respects nor carries out the decisions of the Legislative Council.

They pointed out that Arafat’s political thinking is incompatible with such democratic principles as civil society, constitutional rights and separation of powers.

Shati pointed out that there was only one way out of the “impasse” facing our country, namely the recognition by the executive authority, e.g. Arafat, that it is not above the law, and that its powers are not absolute.

Shati accused Arafat of “making concerted efforts to sideline the Legislative Council.”

These attempts, he said, were taking many forms, including disregarding the Council’s decisions and recommendations, undermining the immunity of the Council, corroding the independence of the judiciary, and fettering press freedom and expression.

Shati argued that “what we are ending up with is an autocracy, where the president takes all the decisions and controls everything.”

 Arafat gets Netanyahu’s blesses

The Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu praised the PA for foiling an alleged military operation by the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) against Israeli targets.
Speaking in a brief press conference following his return from a visit to Russian, the Ukraine, and Georgia, Netanyahu said "what the PA did was commendable and admirable."
However, he demanded that the PA do more in the field of combating Palestinian resistance against Zionist occupiers.
Moreover, Netanyahu criticized the PA for adopting the "revolving-door policy," an allusion to the release from PA jails of a few political detainees who had spent up to three years in PA custody without charge or trial.
Observers and human rights activists in Palestine believe the PA incarcerate hundreds of Palestinian activists and opposition supporters for no reason other than pleasing and appeasing Israel.

 Corruption

Palestinian jurists criticized the Palestinian leadership last month for "personalizing the entire Palestinian Authority in the person of Yaser Arafat."
The jurists, who included law professors and observers monitoring the quality of governance under the PA regime, pointed out that the concentration of nearly all powers in the hands of Arafat created a situation where an average Palestinian citizen became unable to distinguish between Arafat and the Palestinian Authority.
The cited the allocation of financial assistance to Palestinian local councils as a flagrant example of "the patriarchal system being fostered under the PA ."
They pointed out that all financial allocations paid to local councils, and other institutions and societies are offered under the title "generous grants from the President" when in fact they come from the budget of the Palestinian authority.

Political figures in West Bank criticize
Arafat for freezing democracy

Occupied Jerusalem - Several Palestinian political and civic leaders lambasted Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat for “freezing the democratic process in favour of narrow political gains.”

Speaking at a seminar entitled “Defense of civil freedoms” in Nablus on Wednesday, 17 March, several speakers questioned the real motives behind the suspension of local elections throughout the autonomous enclaves, accusing Arafat “of being inimical to democracy.”

Palestinian Legislative Council member Abdul Jawad Saleh pointed out that the Palestinian Authority had failed to promulgate laws safeguarding civil freedoms in Palestine.

He said the Legislative Council was dragging its feet and fighting an uphill battle against a despotic executive authority.

Ali Abu Hilal, an activist associated with the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine, pointed out that the PA leadership was not interested in seeing the authority of law take root in Palestine.

“The authority of law and separation of powers are not compatible with the PA thinking”, said Abu Hilal.

Yasser Arafat has ordered the suspension of municipal elections until further notice, ostensibly to avoid an embarrassing victory by the Islamist opposition.

Hebron faces severe water shortage

Occupied Jerusalem- Hundreds of thousands of Palestinians in the Hebron region are experiencing what seems to be the severest water shortage crisis ever.
According to Palestinians sources, the problem is acute in Dura, Yatta, Dahiriya, as well as in hundreds of villages and hamlets along the former armistice line separating the West Bank and the Palestinian territories occupied in 1948.
The problem, said the sources, is exacerbated even two main factors, first the scarcity of rainfall this winter which didn't exceed one third of its annual average, and the arrogation by the Israeli authorities of the virtually all water resources in the West Bank.
The Palestinian Authority stands almost completely powerless to help the Palestinian people overcome the problem.
In Dura for example the PA told townspeople it couldn't help them and suggested that they make arrangements with the Israeli occupation authorities to obtain drinking water.
One official at Dura municipal council commented on this saying "this is the first time in history a government asks its people to contact a foreign government for essential services."
In the meanwhile, water prices have skyrocketed in the black markets in the West Bank.
Palestinian sources said a cubic meter of water is sold in Hebron at $10 US dollars, probably the highest anywhere in the world.
The water crisis is expected to deteriorate even further in the weeks and months ahead, especially with the continuation of the drought.

Meanwhile, a study published in Palestine last month revealed that the Zionist regime arrogates 720 million cubic meters of Jordan River Waters, of which 650 million cubic meters are drinking water.

In addition, the Zionists get 50 million cubic meters of drinking water from the lake of Tibariya in northern Palestine.

The study, prepared by the two Palestinian geologists Sael Washahi and Dr. Hisham Oratani, also pointed out that the Zionist completely arrogated the Palestinians' share of the River Jordan's water since Palestinians are not allowed to reach the river or the strip of land adjacent to it.

Still more, the study revealed that the remaining water ending up in the Dead Sea is contaminated with poisonous industrial wastes, including cancer-causing chemicals.

The study comes at a time when Palestinians and Jordanians are embracing for the severest water shortage crisis ever this summer due to the severe drought that hit much of the Middle East this winter.

Worst summer

The head of the Water Department in the Palestinian Authority, Fadel Ka’awash, warned recently that Palestinians living under PA rule would experience an acute water shortage crisis, which he said “will be the severest ever.”

“If the Israelis deny us the amounts of water allocated to us in accordance with the Oslo Accords, many Palestinians will literally go thirsty this summer”, Ka’awash said in a press interview on 16 March.

Ka’awash pointed out that the water situation in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip is already ominous, saying that people in many localities were already storing water for the summer.

The Palestinian official said Israel controls over 80% of Palestinian waters, adding that Jewish settlements in the West Bank and Gaza consume the bulk of these waters.

He pointed out that while a single Palestinian receives an average of 30 litres of water per day, a Jewish settler receives an average of 800 litres per day.

According to the Oslo Accords, Israel is supposed to give the Palestinians 80 million cubic metres per year.

However, Ka’awash said, “We have been lied to; we have only received 8 million cubic metres since 1995.”

Head of University’s Student Council transferred
to hospital following torture by Zionists

Occupied Jerusalem - The head of Bir Zeit University’s Student Council, Iyad Habib, was rushed to hospital following several weeks of continuous physical and psychological torture at the hands of Zionist interrogators.

Palestinian sources said Habib was arrested seven weeks ago and had been ever since subjected to torture, including beatings, hooding and the notorious shaking technique known to cause brain dislocation.

The same sources said Habib collapsed last week after three days of relentless physical torture, forcing Zionist interrogators to rush him to hospital.

Habib was arrested at a Zionist roadblock while en route to the University.
The Israeli army routinely erects roadblocks and checkpoints outside the so-called “liberated” towns, e.g. area (A), to catch Palestinians who oppose the Oslo peace process and hold views incompatible with Zionist hegemony.

Most Palestinian detainees are sent to one of the many detention camps throughout Palestine to serve an open-ended sentence.

Editor says he resorts to self censorship
lest PA closes down his paper

Occupied Jerusalem- A Gaza-based journalist who was released from PA custody last month said he would resort to self-censorship in order to avoid closing his newspaper by Palestinian Authority police.

Ghazi Hamad, Editor-in-chief of the pro-Islamists weekly al Risala, said in a telephone interview from his home in Gaza, said he is singled out by PA police chiefs for "exaggerated suppression."

Hamad said PA police summon him every week for questioning about certain news reports or news items in the paper.

"Very often they object to us reprinting certain items from the dailies that are pro-PA, and when we protest, they tell us they don't treat all papers on equal footing."

Hamad said the PA, especially its Secretary-General Al Tayyeb Abdul Rahim, continually threatens to close down our weekly "if we don't go with flow."

He added that it was difficult to have a successful opposition paper under the PA since it would be subject to intermittent closure.

"Advertisers will not send their adds to newspapers that could be closed down any time by the police," he said, adding that this "forces journalists who wish to keep going to seek coexistence with the authority, and the result is agonizing self-censorship."
