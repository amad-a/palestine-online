Netanyahu's statement on West Bank is in the spirit of Oslo

By Yasser Zaatreh*

We really need to ask our fellow Palestinians who signed or supported the Oslo Agreement (many of whom were enraged over Netanyahu's pre-emptive proclamation that the West Bank is an integral part of 'Israel'), since when did 'self-autonomy' as a concept begin to imply the independence of the self-autonomous territory from the State which granted it?! Doesn't self-autonomy by definition merely involve the management of inhabitants, not the land they inhabit?

We should also ask those who signed or supported Oslo exactly where in the Agreement does it say 'withdrawal'? Isn't the exact term used 'redeployment'?

If the text of the Oslo Agreement grants no sovereignty on land but refers rather to the administration of the locals, what's so odd about Netanyahu handing over to the so-called Palestinian National Authority (PNA) only heavily populated Palestinian areas leaving for his Jewish settlers all else?!

Let's face it: when the researcher Israel Shahak commented four years ago that Oslo was not in substance any different from the formula of the collaborationist Village Associations (Rawabitt Al-Qurra) that 'Israel' tried to impose on Palestinians in the 1970s, he wasn't too far off the mark.

That's why Netanyahu's stonewalling doesn't really top off that of Shimon Perez. Both stalled for time to build or expand settlements.

The same thing goes for the proportion of lands in which the Israeli Army will be redeployed. As long as it's not heavily populated with Palestinians, its size will increase or decrease depending on Israel's mood, which is in turn allegedly determined by security considerations which the Oslo Agreement gave priority to.

But then the supporters of Oslo might object that the questions of sovereignty, settlements, borders, Jerusalem and refugees were all left to the final status negotiations. In fact, that is not even denied by Netanyahu. The real question though is whether leaving the door open for further negotiations will necessarily mean that their outcome will be dictated by Arafat, not the physical balance of forces on the ground that tend to favour the Israeli leadership.

Why won't those who signed that vague and loose agreement on our behalf ask themselves what guarantees they got for attaining sovereignty, Jerusalem, or other demands in the final status negotiations?

What are the actual pressures that will come to bear on the Israeli leadership to cause it to respond to those demands, especially since the Palestinian demands at stake are more or less the locus of a consensus among different Israeli political shades, where the differences among those shades have to do not with principle, but with the format of the self-autonomy that will alleviate the burden of managing the Palestinians?

Finally, we should question the current PLO leadership about what it has now that can guarantee even the implementation of Oslo, or lesser still, an effective response to Netanyahu's programme.

If the answer is 'nothing', then perhaps it's time for the PLO leadership to heed the advice of its Political Officer, Farouq Al-Qadoumi, to head right back to Tunisia.

American, Israeli and Palestinian figures

debate images and realities of Jerusalem

By Ghada Khouri*

Shortly after coming into office, Israeli Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu declared that the "battle for Jerusalem" had begun. Since then, the Israeli government has intensified efforts to rid Jerusalem of its Palestinian inhabitants while consolidating exclusive Israeli sovereignty over the Holy City. The battle is not only taking place on the ground, but also on the airwaves, where Israeli and Palestinian officials attempt to influence public opinion in their favour. Speaking at a conference sponsored by the Middle East Institute in Washington, D.C. on 16 January, prominent American, Palestinian and Israeli figures came together to contrast the realities and images of Jerusalem. While the ideas presented by the participants varied widely, they agreed that Jerusalem remains one of the most contentious issues between Palestinians and Israelis and that its future, at least partly, rests over which side succeeds in winning the hearts and minds of the American public.

Douglas Feith, co-Chairman of the Board of Advisers for the Jewish Institute for National Security Affairs, epitomized the vision of many of Israel's most ardent supporters by blaming the lack of progress in the peace process on the Arab parties. He argued that Arab rhetoric calling for Israel's destruction makes it impossible for Israel to make concessions. As a result, the conflict remains an existentialist one whereby the very survival of Israel is challenged by Arab rejectionists. Feith's depiction of Israel as a tiny State surrounded by hostile Arab neighbours, intent on destroying it, is all too common in the American media and the U.S. Congress.

The effectiveness of the pro-Israeli lobby in projecting this image led to the Jerusalem Embassy Relocation Act passed by Congress in 1995, a bill which outraged the Arab world. Feith justified the Act, which calls for the relocation of the U.S. embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem by 1999, as an assertion of U.S. support for Israel's right to exist and a recognition that Jerusalem embodies the Jewish State's struggle for legitimacy. The Act was designed to send a message to the Palestinians that their aspirations for Jerusalem are "unrealistically high," Feith said, as he dismissed the Palestinians' legal and historical claims to the city.

Rashid Khalidi, President of the American Committee on Jerusalem, sharply refuted Feith's arguments by pointing out that Arab hostility is based on real issues and not an "eternal wellspring of hostility toward the Jews." He criticized Feith's portrayal of the Arabs as intent on destroying Israel, pointing out that acceptance of United Nations resolution 242 by Israel's neighbours constitutes de facto recognition of Israel's right to exist.

According to Khalidi, the image of Jerusalem espoused by the U.S. Congress, the media and the public is based on several falsehoods. He argued that the "myths" perpetuated by the American press, which depict Jerusalem as the "united and eternal" capital of Israel, are based on the following false assumptions: that the Israeli public is unanimous in its belief that Jerusalem is "indivisible"; that the city's current municipal boundaries are natural; and that the city is not as important to Arabs, Muslims and Palestinians as it is to Israelis and Jews.

In reality, Jerusalem is a "deeply divided" and "segregated" city where East Jerusalem is "clearly Israeli-occupied territory," Khalidi said. Citing recent public opinion surveys held in Israel, Khalidi challenged the notion that Israelis are unyielding in their belief that Jerusalem is the indivisible capital of Israel. He pointed out that the current boundaries of Jerusalem are artificial in that they were expanded by Israel to include large portions of the West Bank. Since the boundaries of the city were changed in the past, he said, "they could be changed again."

The only viable solution to the problem of Jerusalem, according to Khalidi, is shared sovereignty, with East Jerusalem as the capital of a Palestinian State. Philip Wilcox, who served as Consul General in Jerusalem during his 31-year career in the U.S. Foreign Service, agreed, pointing out that Jerusalem is a "political capital and religious centre" for both peoples. He suggested that a lasting peace must address Arab grievances and correct the wrongs committed against the Palestinian population, half of which was expulsed in 1948, leading to a mass refugee problem.

However, the Palestinians' legitimate claims over Jerusalem seem to fall on deaf ears in the halls of Congress and U.S. newsrooms. The crisis-oriented American coverage has generally failed to place the Palestinian-Israeli conflict in perspective, often providing little analysis or historical background to current disputes. As a result, sporadic Palestinian acts of violence receive extensive coverage, while incremental and daily Israeli provocations are under-reported.

Palestinian journalist Daoud Kuttab, who heads the Institute of Modern Media at Al-Quds University, described life in Jerusalem as a "nightmare" for its Palestinian residents. While any Israeli can settle in Jerusalem at will, an unwritten Israeli policy seeks to drive out Palestinian Jerusalemites from the city of their birth by confiscating their residency permits. Jewish settlements, closures and Israeli-controlled entry permits for Palestinians seeking to visit Jerusalem have effectively severed the Holy City from the rest of the West Bank. Kuttab cited the case of his brother-in-law, who can only visit his Jerusalemite wife by day, then return to his home in Bethlehem by night. He is not permitted to permanently settle in Jerusalem. In turn, his wife would lose her residency rights in Jerusalem if she chose to move to Bethlehem. Like many Palestinian families, the couple has fallen prey to what Khalidi calls a systematic Israeli policy of "ethnic cleansing."

But Jerusalem is not only a city where Palestinians and Israelis are deeply divided politically and psychologically. It is also the site of a widening rift between secular Israelis and the Haredim, or ultra-orthodox. Ori Nir, correspondent for the Israeli daily newspaper Ha'eretz, expounded on this trend, which has led an increasing number of Israelis to leave the Holy City to settle in more modern, secular towns such as Tel Aviv.

According to recent surveys, 51% of first-grade Israeli children in Jerusalem are Haredim, signalling a dramatic increase in Jerusalem's ultra-orthodox population. As a result, Jerusalem is now the second poorest city in Israel. According to Nir, this is due to the fact that 70% of ultra-orthodox men "choose to be unemployed" in order to pursue religious studies. As a result, Jerusalemites pay the highest municipal taxes in the country to subsidize the Haredim, who now make up the largest class of welfare recipients. To secular Israelis like Nir, government policy aimed at altering the city's demographic make-up comes at a high price. "The best place in Jerusalem," he said, "is the exit to Tel Aviv."

As the Israeli government consolidates exclusive sovereignty over Jerusalem by changing facts on the ground, the American media have focused on the political demagoguery of Israeli and Palestinian leaders. So far, the Israeli point of view has dominated the debate over the future of Jerusalem. While the Arab parties cry foul, they have under-estimated the power of the media in shaping American public opinion and have failed to devote enough resources to countering the myths propagated by pro-Israeli groups.

Loren Jenkins, a senior foreign editor at National Public Radio and previous bureau chief of Newsweek in Beirut, blamed the one-sided reporting on Jerusalem partly on Arab politicians' failure to effectively present their case. He contrasted the poor performance of Arab leaders to the savvy public relations skills of Israeli politicians, pointing out that while Netanyahu is readily available to American journalists, obtaining an interview with Palestinian leader Yasser Arafat could take days. Netanyahu's effectivenein dealing with the media makes it difficult to "find an Arab voice that can compete at the same level," Jenkins said.

The at-times heated debate, which brought to the fore the disparity between the realities of life in Jerusalem and the images presented in the U.S. media, left little doubt that the Israelis are decidedly on the winning end of the battle over Jerusalem­a contest with intense repercussions over the entire Arab-Israeli peace process. As the power card rests with Israel, winning the fight on the ground is a remote possibility for the Palestinians. The battle taking place on the airwaves and in the newsrooms may be the only one offering real hope of success, especially in light of the fact that international law and the community of nations support Palestinian claims over the Holy City, including Palestinian sovereignty over Arab East Jerusalem. However, while Jewish lip service to Jerusalem is backed up with substantial political and financial support to pro-Israeli groups, Arab leaders have yet to meet the challenge of effectively countering the myths about Jerusalem. Judging from the near absence of a concerted Arab political strategy and media campaign on the issue of Jerusalem, critics wonder if they are even trying.

*The author is a freelance writer on Middle East issues.

Two Third World Examples of Democracy

By Mahmoud al Khatib

The first example took place in Turkey last month with the banning of the Islamic Welfare Party by the Constitutional Court. The secular military institution could not afford living behind the scenes, so in order to stay in the limelight, the Turkish army leaders buried democracy for the fourth time in the past four decades: three in military coups and the last a semi-military coup, which resulted in the downfall of the Erbakan-led coalition government by military pressure. Mr. Erbakan, who survived in the prime ministry for one year, was the first Turkish, as well as worldwide, Islamic prime minister in the aftermath of the First World War.

Mr. Erbakan's party, the largest political party in the country (a total membership of 5 million), obtained 158 seats in the 1995 parliamentary elections leaving the True Path, the ruling party, in second position with some 130 seats, which qualified the Welfares to form a coalition government with Mrs. Tansu Ciller's True Path. When Mr. Erbakan came to power, he asserted he would respect the secular regime and the political pluralism in the country. The army leaders seeking a military alliance with the Zionist State were afraid of democracy, not only because it could bring Islamists to power, but also because it threatened their own interests. They had been the actual rulers of the country over the past 60 years. The Islamists who came to power through democracy wanted to be the actual rulers and refused to remain in the shadows. Others, such as Prime Minister Mesut Yilmaz, the fragile man, accepted to play that role and to be a dummy in the hands of the army leaders. That's why he submitted to the army chiefs and continued his efforts to outlaw the Welfare Party, closing the religious middle schools and imposing stricter enforcement of dress codes in the public service, universities and schools.

But the banning of the Welfare Party, which obtained more than 6 million votes in the previous elections, and last month's decision by the education minister banning the wearing of Hijab (Islamic dress) by both teachers and female students in the secondary schools, will certainly provoke the Turkish people, given that 99% of them are Muslims. Who should be blamed if the millions of Muslim Turks, Welfares, Sufis or others went out to the streets to protest the inhuman measures taken by Mr. Yilmaz under pressure of the army leaders? Who should be blamed, the oppressed people or the tyrant leaders?

Turkey is an example of a country that swings between two worlds: the Islamic World, the natural environment for Turkey, which ruled the Islamic World for about four centuries; and the Western World. The European Community has rejected all attempts made by the secular Turks to join the European Union, not because the Turkish government, as Western leaders say, has violated the human rights of its people, but rather because it is a Muslim country even though it is ruled by a secular minority. Westerners are only concerned over democracy and human rights in their countries. They do not worry over the violations of human rights or over the non-democratic procedures perpetrated by their allies who rule many of the Islamic countries, especially if these procedures target Islamists and prevent them from enjoying the fruits of democracy. Turkey and Algeria are perfect examples to this fact.

*****

In Algeria, a new holocaust has been committed in a savage and primitive manner since 1992. As in Turkey, Islamists were victims of the impatient and greedy military institution, which could not stand seeing an Islamic party at the top of the new democracy.

The Islamic Salvation Front (FIS), a newly established party, ran for the first parliamentary elections in Algeria in 1991. Its candidates swept about 80% of the parliamentary seats declared in the first round, which infuriated the army leaders. Eventually, the army leaders forced President Shazli Ben Jideed to abolish the results of the elections and ban the FIS. The government escalated its measures against the party leaders and arrested most of them. Not even that was enough. The army generals obliged President Ben Jideed to resign when they felt he would not satisfy their plans to crack down on the Islamists.

The Algerian FIS was different than Turkey's Welfare Party, which is a moderate Islamic party that refuses to use violence against the government. FIS covert leadership formed the Islamic Salvation Army, which decided to confront the arbitrary measures taken by the government against them. That was the spark that ignited the ongoing civil war in Algeria six years ago.

The FIS militants targeted only army soldiers, policemen and Statesmen. In the past few months, the killings and sabotage actions mainly targeted civilians in general and women and children in particular. In many massacres, women and children were butchered like sheep, their bodies ripped open with axes and swords. In some mass killings, civilians, including infants, were burnt alive. The toll is estimated at 80,000 people, although the government reports only 26,000 having been killed in the past six years.

FIS condemned the killing of innocent civilians, asserting it has stopped its military attacks. It also announced a one-party cease-fire. Observers believe that FIS has nothing to do with the recent massacres. The government has accused another Islamic group called the Islamic Armed Group (GIA) of being involved in these massacres. Some reporters say large numbers of Algerian and foreign secret service agents penetrated GIA. Interviewed by several TV channels and newspapers, Algerian security officers, who fled the country and took refuge in Europe, revealed that some of the massacres were committed by the Algerian army and by the pro-government militia. This accusation was confirmed by a number of international human rights groups and by Amnesty International's Secretary General.

There is one reality that no one can argue with; it is that any Islamic group, however extremist it is, cannot accept the killing of innocent women and children or the burning alive of any person. These groups might have their own claims or justifications to kill or slaughter men or even mature women, a thing which we strongly reject, but they definitely have no argument for killing infants and children. This is a fact known by the common Muslims. No, we can never believe that any Islamic group would butcher women and children that way! We stick to the statements made by the international human rights organizations and Amnesty International, which all point an accusatory finger towards the government and the pro-government militias.

That's why I call for legal international investigation into the massacres committed and still being committed in Algeria. I do not call for a military intervention, but rather for a legal team whose only objective is to reveal the real criminals in Algeria who committed these barbaric massacres.

Why did the democratic West keep silent over the killings in the past six years? Why did they keep silent when the army leaders butchered democracy and removed the most popular political party in Algeria that was about to take power in that country?

Democracy is encouraged and welcomed by Westerners as long as it does not bring Islamists to power. What a hypocrisy!

"Kvetching" and the Politics of "Profiling"

By Ray Hanania

After more than 8 years, the United States government has finally unveiled its plan to counter the terrorist threat "against" America.

Although they won't release the details of the plan, asserting the need for security, it pretty much comes down to one simple procedure: Passengers who look Arab will be stopped. Those who do not, might be randomly searched.

The new policy, which takes affect this year, requires people who have Middle Eastern "looks" or whose travel patterns include Middle Eastern countries, to be taken aside at airports for an extensive search of their personal possessions, carry-on items and luggage.

A special computer will analyze information on passengers and identify those that need "special attention" at airports.

Millions of dollars have been spent on devising this plan, which advocates claim will stem the tide of airline terrorism -- the last such act occurred on July 17, 1996 when 230 Americans were killed in the on-flight explosion of TWA Flight 800. An exhaustive investigation of the explosion ruled out international terrorism, a missile, and suggests it was probably the result of a freak accident involving gaseous vapors from fuel tanks aboard the plane.

The practice of profiling will augment technological safeguards that already have been installed at airports, including the installation of 54 high-tech bomb detectors and 220 trace explosive detectors. Additionally, some 238 bomb-sniffing dogs will monitor passenger movements at US airports by the end of this year.

Still, the Federal Aviation Commission and a special US Commission on Terrorism feels that it needs something more.

Why profile passengers?

Most non-Middle Eastern Americans would not be encumbered by the profiling hassles. And, since most Middle Eastern Americans don't vote, who cares if the burden of terrorism falls on their shoulders?

Reasoned arguments against profiling continue to fall on deaf ears.

The most notable is the Tim McVeigh factor. You see, Tim McVeigh is the terrorist convicted of blowing up a federal office building in Oklahoma in April, 1996. Although the visceral reaction of the US Justice system was to roust dozens of Arab looking passengers traveling out of Oklahoma that day, it was an accidental encounter between an Oklahoma police officer who pulled over McVeigh's car because it didn't have a roper license, that resulted in McVeigh's arrest when bomb-making materials and weapons were found in his car.

Picking on the Arab is easy, in the United States. Certainly, it is easier than trying to disarm the hundreds of violent American militias that operate in the American hinterlands, or stripping them of their automatic weapons.

And, the United States has to pick on someone, when its policies simply fail to achieve their goal of protecting Americans from the acts of terrorism.

You see, when government policies fail, government, by its nature seeks out someone to blame, besides themselves.

The easiest target in America is the Arab, who lives in a disorganized cultural society fraught with internal political and social divisions, and an absence of qualified leaders.

The Israelis have a word to describe the only option that we, as Arab Americans, have in the face of this obvious form of discrimination: It's called "kvetching," or the act of whining out loud to no one in particular.

We, Arab Americans, live in a world where it is easier to criticize each other than it is to organize effectively and to speak out in support of constitutional rights that have been consistently stripped from us.

So, get ready to set aside your dignity at US Airports.

It's all that we Arab Americans have left to surrender these days.

 

Justice in the Eye of the Beholder

Several major American newspapers recently carried very expensive, full page advertisements entitled "If We Forget ..."

The ads reminded the world of the terrorist bombing of Pan Am Flight 103 which exploded in midair on Dec. 21, 1988, killed 259 passengers and 11 people on the ground in Lockerbie, Scotland.

And, the ads, alleged, two Libyan nationals, Lamen Khalifa Fhimah and Abdel Basset Ali Al-Meghrahi, are responsible. It offers a $4 million reward for information on the two suspects, who were indicted by a US court.

The United States used its power to force the United Nations to impose an embargo against Libya, demanding that the two suspects be extradited to England or the United States. Libya has refused, responding that the two should be extradited to a "neutral" country where they have a better than even chance of receiving a fair trial.

In this case, the United States government is not really interested in justice.

Libyan refusal to surrender the two suspects to America's kangaroo court --an American term used to describe a judicial system that has already made up its mind against a suspect -- has given the United States plenty of ammunition to punish Libya, which has remained a stalwart critic of Israel and American double standards.

Libya has offered to compensate the families of the victims, but the victims have refused.

You see, in America, the life of one American does not have a price tag. The life of a foreigner, however, can be evaluated in terms of dollars.

Five months before Pan Am Flight 103 crashed in a fiery explosion, gunmen on the USS Vincennes, ostensibly patrolling international waters off the coast of Iran, fired upon Iranian Airbus A300, destroying the commercial civilian aircraft and killing all 290 people on board.

The ship's commander claimed his crew "mistook" the giant commercial Boeing 747 plane for a much smaller, military F-14 fighter jet.

A hastily arranged investigation by the United States, blamed one "operations officer," and issued the lowest possible punishment, a letter of reprimand placed in the unknown officer's personnel file. Behind-the-scenes, US Officials passed on rumors that the Airbus had been pre-loaded with dead bodies, and intentionally sent to the war zone believing it would be shot down to create a situation to rebuke the Americans. (This was repeated often by family members of the victims on national TV.)

For more than four years, US officials insisted the Airbus was at fault and had veered out of Iranian airspace into international waters, and thereby justified the attack. Finally, in 1992, evidence surfaced that in fact, the commercial Iranian airline had been on course and was in Iranian airspace. It was the USS Vincennes that had been of course by more than 20 miles.

Additional evidence showed that the commander of the American cruiser, Capt.Will C. Rogers III, may have intentionally shot the commercial airline down, and that he was egging for such a military hit. The Vincennes had been called into the waters near Iran as a result of the Iran-Iraq war and they claimed tiny little Iranian gunboats had fired upon the massive US cruiser.

In all the bluster from then President Ronald Reagan, he never once expressed remorse for the murder of the 290 Muslim civilians.

The United States has never publicly apologized for the downing of the commercial plan, and have resisted all efforts by the Iranians to investigate Capt. Rogers and his crew.

However, the United States was quick to offer monetary compensation to the families of the 290 victims. More than $131 million was paid to sweep the controversy under the rug.

Iranian protests to the United Nations were muted by allies of the United States government.

No UN embargo was imposed on the United States.

No full page advertisements were purchased calling for the extradition of that American buckaroo, Will C. Rogers. All evidence suggests he knew it was a commercial Iranian airline, but wanted to bring it down anyway.

The families of the victims of Airbus A300 did not form a special organization to hire lawyers to exact a punishment from the Americans. They weren't given a forum on national American TV, as were family members of the Pan Am 103 disaster.

And, no court marshall was held to indict Will C. Rogers for a crime that he and the US Navy struggled to cover up these past 10 years.

Maybe the victims of Pan Am 103 downed off of Lockerbie, Scotland do deserve justice.

But do they deserve any more justice than the victims of Iranian Airbus A300?

Pluralism, women and economy­IAF's top priorities

There are so many laws and regulations in Israel which discriminate in favour of the persons defined as those 'who can immigrate in accordance with the Law of Return' that the subject demands separate treatment. We can look here at one example, seemingly trivial in comparison with residence restrictions, but nevertheless important since it reveals the real intentions of the Israeli legislator. Israeli citizens who left the country for a time but who are defined as those who 'can immigrate in accordance with the Law of Return' are eligible on their return to generous customs benefits, to receive subsidy for their children's high school education, and to receive either a grant or a loan on easy terms for the purchase of an apartment, as well as other benefits. Citizens who cannot be so defined, on other words, the non-Jewish citizens of Israel, get none of these benefits. The obvious intention of such discriminatory measures is to decrease the number of non-Jewish citizens of Israel, in order to make Israel a more 'Jewish' state.

Trust in Allah

By Lena Winfrey Sayyed

Some people in the world today, who consider themselves "modern", believe that real "truth" is in the eye of the beholder and that between truth and wrong there exist grey areas of leniency. These people are gravely mistaken, for truth does exist. The truth is with Allah, and He has sent Messengers and Messages to tell us the truth and to guide us. Allah says in the Qur'an, "Truth stands out clear from error" (2:256). When the world ends and we are all dead, the only truth and reality facing us will be Allah. So, we should seek the truth now and prepare ourselves for our future life.

However, being human, we let the life of this world sidetrack us. We are busy earning money, earning degrees, having children, amusing ourselves with the latest technology and we forget our duties to Allah; we actually live in a dream world. This life is like a short dream while the Hereafter is an eternal reality. We must wake up and not join the unbelievers who numb their senses with amusements in order to stop themselves from thinking. "The life of this world is alluring to those who reject faith, and they scoff at those who believe" (Qur'an 2:212).

We must keep in mind that this life is an exam from Allah, and if we do well, then we'll be in Paradise; however, if we refuse to worship our Creator and cause mischief in this life, then we'll be in the Hell-fire. We must realize the temptations of this life and how to avoid them. Always remember the advice Allah gave in the Qur'an, "That which is on earth We have made but as a glittering show for the earth, in order that We may test them­as to which of them are best in conduct. Verily what is on earth We shall make but as dust and dry soil (without growth or herbage)" (18:7-8).

Keeping this in mind, we should go through life as a traveler and pick up the supplies we'll need for the next life­good deeds. We have to keep this and our other purposes in mind. First, Allah created us to worship only Him. Secondly, He placed us on the earth as a vicegerent to support justice and to put down evil. We are to carry the truth throughout the world.

Some Muslims today say that it is impossible in today's world to carry out the Islamic mission. Many complain that Muslims are being assaulted from all sides. This is true, yet we still must stick to the mission of our lives and not despair. Allah tells us in the Qur'an, "Do not despair of Allah's Mercy" (39:53). There are many methods of jihad (struggle). We can speak, write, change things, protest and fight, if necessary. The greatest jihad is the personal jihad. We must change ourselves before we can change others. So, we must be great role models of the true Islam. If we really lived Islam in the way it should be lived, then according to Said Nursi, a Kurdish scholar during this century, "If we were to display through our deeds the perfections contained in the ethics of Islam and the truths of faith, the followers of other religions would definitely enter Islam, in masses; indeed, certain continents and States would also join Islam" (The Damascus Sermon).

Indeed, in today's world, to practice Islam is becoming increasingly hard. Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) predicted that one day practicing Islam would be like "holding hot coals" (Hadith). Yet, imagine the great reward we'd receive from Allah for our patience and strength during such hard times.

We must be courageous, brave and self-sacrificing. The next life is the most important because it'll last forever. So, we must be righteous and ready to serve Allah in any cause that we can. We must support the truth and put down injustice and oppression. The Prophet told us that "if we see something wrong, we should change it with our hand, but if we can't, then with our tongue, and if we still can't, then we must hate it in our hearts, which is the least of faith" (Hadith).

We should not make excuses and say that if things were easier, then we'd do them. We must always strive for righteousness, even if we fear the loss of our property or our own lives. "And there is the type of man who gives his life to earn the pleasure of Allah; and Allah is full of kindness to (His) devotees. Oh, you who believe! Enter into Islam whole-heartedly and follow not the footsteps of the Evil One, for he is to you an avowed enemy!" (2:207-208). The Prophet said, "The one most honourable in Allah's estimation is the one most pious" (Bukhari and Muslim).

On the Day of Judgment, Allah will ask us what we did with the possessions He gave us. Did we abuse them or did we use them to gain His pleasure and to work righteousness? The Prophet said, "Allah does not look at your forms or your possessions, but He looks at your hearts and your deeds" (Muslim).

We must weigh the important things in this life and never waste our time, talents or possessions. Everything should be used to gain Allah's pleasure. Each of us can do something for Islam and to help other Muslims, as well as other human beings, even if it is some small act. If we do so, we should know that Allah is always ready to reward us; no small deed goes unnoticed. Allah says, "Whoever works righteousness, man or woman, and has Faith, verily, to him will We give a new life, a life that is good and pure, and We will bestow on such their reward according to the best of their actions" (16:97), and "Oh, My servants who believe! Fear your Lord. Good is (the reward) for those who do good in this world. Spacious is Allah's earth! Those who patiently persevere will truly receive a reward without measure!" (39:10).

We should be open to do whatever we can to help those in need, whether it is the beggar, the poor, the orphan or the oppressed. We must speak out the truth, no matter to whom we are speaking. "The best striving (jihad) is uttering a word of truth before an unjust sultan" (Abu Dawud and Ibn Majah).

To perfect our faith, we must have unlimited trust in Allah, for He never forsakes his faithful servants. We must remember, no matter how much we fear the unbelievers and their armies or the wicked rulers or unjust men, that Allah is with those who truly have faith and trust in Him. Allah says, "Whoever rejects evil and believes in Allah has grasped the most trustworthy Hand-hold that never breaks, and Allah hears and knows all things. Allah is the Protector of those who have faith" (2:256-257).

We must know that Allah has all the Power. Miraculous things have happened in the past and in today's life. We only need a strong iman (faith). Allah sent angels to help the Prophet fight the unbelievers in the Battle of Badr, and though the Muslims were greatly outnumbered, they still achieved a victory. Even in this century, a great example of fighting against great numbers of soldiers and without sufficient weapons can be seen in the Battle of Karameh; the Palestinians and Muslims won.

Allah is always on the side of justice, and all we have to do is to join Him in this justice, then we'll truly be victorious­in this life and in the next.

The Miller

By E. Yaghi

Once, when imperialism was still the rage and its oppression felt around the world, in a certain land, honey bees buzzed and orange blossoms hung in clusters in the thick orchards of one man by the name of Abdul Haleem. He lived in relative peace and contentment on the same land that his forefathers had tilled with love and patience for eons gone by. He was sort of an aristocrat in his "Garden of Eden" and had many children who played for hours in the summer sun between their water pump and the dirt, taking turns rolling and fighting in the rich red earth and then showering in the sprinkling system all over again. Although Abdul Haleem was known to be kind and generous, he was also respected and feared by his neighbors and relatives and, for miles around, his reputation had spread about his daring feats of bravery and the honesty of his spoken word. One afternoon, while he was sitting on the porch of his house drinking in the scent of his orchard under the shade of a huge mulberry tree, a distant relative of his approached.

"Uncle," the young man said politely, "I've got a very urgent request. I hope you can help me." He stood wrenching his hands and fidgeted nervously.

"Well, what is it man? Speak up! I have no time for people who beat around the bush," the uncle said, his hazel eyes at work scrutinizing his nephew.

"I've come to ask for your protection. In the village where I live, no one can come or go without the consent and approval of our rich landlord who governs us. I must leave that village. I am a poor miller who is trying to support my family and business is very bad in my village. I know you to be brave and just. Please accompany me back to my home so that I may get my wife and children and move somewhere else."

Abdul Haleem could smell excitement in the air and he loved a challenge, besides, he never refused a request for help. "You have nothing to fear," he said happily, "I will go with you and see what I can do."

And he did. In the black of night when the only light was the pale yellow moon, he and the miller rode past watermelon patches, apple groves, fragrant citrus blossoms in thick wooded orchards while they listened to the laugh of the wild hyena, the howl of the mad dog, the hoot of the barn owl and the caw of the mockingbird. Although the British Imperialists forbade the native inhabitants weapons, Abdul Haleem carried his hidden gun in an old leather pouch. Every once in a while, he patted it to be sure that it was still there. He knew that if he was caught with a weapon, he would immediately be imprisoned so he had to be cautious. After some time, he and his companion approached the obscure village which lay quiet and sleepy. "We must go at once and get the landlord's permission for my leaving. Otherwise, he will surely punish me and probably even beat me," said the nephew.

"I told you not to be afraid," Abdul Haleem said brusquely. He was usually exceptionally good in dealing with men and more often than not, knew how to handle them. He went up to the landlord's house, knocked on the door, and after he had a few words with him, returned in victory to his nephew. The smiling miller embraced his uncle and placed his Judas kiss on his cheek and said, "Oh, Uncle, I'm so glad that you were with me. Now let's go and get my family."

"Wait a minute. Where are you planning to take them?" the concerned uncle queried.

He saw his nephew look at his feet dejectedly and then answer in a forlorn voice, "I really have no place to go. I am at your mercy!"

Abdul Haleem never refused anyone hospitality so he said, "you are welcome to live on my land as long as you want!"

It was a warm feeling to be of service to his poor relative. But he didn't have time to be emotional right now. He and his nephew proceeded to the small house which was home for a wife and a brood of children. Soon, they had the drowsy family and their meager possessions bundled up on an old creaky wagon and headed for Abdul Haleem's orchards. Days later after saturating his nephew with the best food and comfort, the good uncle gave him a small plot of land free where he could set up his mill and grind wheat. The two men drew up a verbal contract which went something like this, "I'll let you live here as long as you like on the condition that I, my brothers and my sister and their children grind our grain in your mill without paying any money."

"Oh, no, Uncle, I will grind the grain free for all your relatives, it is the least I can do to repay you."

But the uncle refused, saying that the entire family was too large and that the miller would never make any profit, so it was agreed that only the uncle's closest relatives could grind their grain free. A small but suitable house was then erected on the donated plot and soon the sound of heavy stones whetting together filled the orchard air. Everything went smoothly at first. But, Abdul Haleem's brother was unhappy with the arrangement. On a cool morning as the two sat outside on the porch, drank mint tea and smoked their hand rolled and home grown cigarettes, Abdul Haleem's brother said to him, "I warned you before, you are making a serious mistake. There will come a time when our nephew will cause trouble, just wait and see."

Summers came and went. The miller wasn't poor any more. Business was good and success had gone to his head. He forgot his uncle's generosity and kindness and started to complain to his wife. "I will never be rich if I keep grinding all uncles' family's grain free."

"Just refuse to do it then," said the wife matter of factly, forgetting that time when her husband's uncle had saved her and her family from misery.

So the coward who had once taken advantage of his uncle then refused to grind anyone's grain free. Abdul Haleem was furious when he found out what was going on. When he confronted his nephew he was answered with, "But Uncle, no one said they can't mill their grain here. They just have to pay like everyone else."

He had not reckoned dealing with his uncle's anger and inspite of his fear, he tried to gain support and advice from another leading family in the village. But to his chagrin he was told, "No, we won't help you against our good friend. How can you act this way after all he's done for you? He gave you land when you had no place to go. Have you no shame?"

However rather than letting his uncle's family use his mill free, he moved his business to another town but still kept some of his family on the gifted plot of land so that he could keep his claim to the area. Then one day, the invaders came and nothing remained the same. Everyone lost their homes and land, including the innocent and the greedy. Abdul Haleem's seed became refugees but later again prospered. But the nephew became poor once again and never succeeded. On a quiet day, if you look past the sprawling settlements of a people who confiscated a land not theirs and now don't love it as its former owners once did, if you listen carefully, you can still hear the old mill grinding, children laughing while they splash in the orchard fountains and smell Abdul Haleem's home grown and hand rolled cigarettes as he sits on his porch beneath his beloved mulberry tree in the land of milk and honey.

Gone are those beautiful scenes and innocent days that at one time seemed would last forever. Here to stay it appears is a tyrannical rule of oppression and bloodshed. The seed of Adbul Haleem has been dispersed across the four corners of the globe and forever is gone those carefree times when life was beautiful and simple.
