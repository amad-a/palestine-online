The Palestinian State and the 4 May ‘Bug’

By Mahmoud al-Khatib

This year the world will witness two determinative events that have a direct relation to time. The first is known as the ‘Y2K Bug’; computer experts warn that the globe will be destroyed during the first minute of the new year 2000 if Uncle Sam’s technology fails to find an urgent solution to this bug before the end of this year. The other is the ‘4 May Bug’; Chairman Arafat is expected to declare his Palestinian State on this date, which marks the end of the five-year transitional period of self-rule.

While we are fully confident that Uncle Sam will find a solution to the Y2K Bug, we are full of horror at the outcome of Mr. Arafat’s Statehood declaration. For he toured the four corners of the globe in order to secure for himself the real title of “President” and to record his funeral, which seems to be imminent, in the annals of the funerals of the “Greatest Presidents”!

The fourth of May, as Mr. Arafat’s subordinates say, is a sacred date for declaring the State, at least this is what they have been claiming for a long time now. Yes, it is sacred as long as Chairman, sorry, President Arafat wishes to call it. But what would his poor subordinates say if Mr. Arafat gave them the cold shoulder and decided to adjourn the Statehood declaration? For he received the same tone and same reply from the world leaders he met with during his last tour. They all advised him to postpone the Statehood declaration until after 4 May. By the time the date actually rolls around, it is expected that Mr. Arafat will adjourn it for six months. This means that the Palestinian bug will coincide with the Y2K Bug. At least one bug is better than two!

The Palestinians have earned the right to have their hearts in their mouths with the approach of 4 May. Although they have not yet tasted the State and have never experienced independence in their recent history, they have been feeling its bites and burns since 1994 when the PA’s leadership came from Tunis to Gaza. They are aware that under Mr. Arafat and his henchmen the newly born State would be hell. The Palestinians have the following impressionistic image of this State:

- A president who flies around the globe and has his term extended every 50 years in order to get a lifetime presidency, and whose main hobby is walking on red carpets at international airports parading the honour guard.

- A full jailhouse in every Palestinian town and refugee camp competing with each other on offering a five-star service to their ‘guests’. Such a service has not been introduced to Palestinian political detainees even by the Zionist enemies as was testified to by some international human rights bodies.

-  Widespread corruption decaying the civil and security bodies.

- Tribalism, which had not been experienced by the Palestinian People until those who came from Tunis brought it with them to stop the Islamist stream in the occupied territories.

There is no need to deeply explore the geo-political form of the State after the Palestinian ‘bug’. It is a State, but not a State; an entity but not that entity. It is a new form of a State that has no counterpart. It is even not expected to have a counterpart until the day of final judgement. But Arafat’s model State is fragmented atomic islands in a sea of Jewish settlements in the West Bank without geographic or political borders; without geographic or demographic unity among its fragmented units; without sovereignty; without natural resources but survives by American and European assistance; and with masked and real unemployment in all sectors except in the ten security bodies which serve the interests of the Zionist enemy.

Arafat’s story with the Palestinian State is similar to the parable of Juha and the Sultan: Juha wants his son to marry the Sultan’s daughter. Juha, his wife and his son have no objection to this marriage, but the Sultan, his wife and his daughter strongly reject it! Just like Arafat, the Arabs and their poor friends want the Palestinian State to be established, but those who are concerned and have the power—namely Israel, the United States and the West in general—do not want it and they even suppressed the Palestinian Juha before daring to ask for the bride’s hand.

Can the PA establish the State by force even though it abandoned the gun, which is the detrimental element in the struggle between the Right and Falsehood? Can this State be created by giving more Palestinian sacrifices to the Israelis and by arresting more Palestinians? Or can it be accomplished by giving more relinquishments regarding Palestinian land and legitimate rights? It is clear that the PA does not have a will to force its option because it is convinced of its disability. However, the Palestinian Authority can celebrate a real wedding only by releasing its people from jails, by reconciliation with its people, by clearing its security bodies of the collaboration with the Israeli Shin Bet, and by declaring a national Jihad programme to liberate the occupied land without rushing to get trifle fruits.

If Israel’s goal is to liquidate the Palestinian Cause and close the file after 50 years of battling to establish a Jewish foothold in Palestine, then surely the path they have taken thus far will realize that end. For Israel today is stronger than yesterday and free of external pressures that would want it to abide by its agreements with the PLO. The Jewish and Zionist lobbies in the United States and inside the White House are acting on its behalf. Therefore, the Palestinians should not let their legs be dragged into the Zionist swamp.

What the three candidates—Netanyahu, Barak and Mordechai—of the Israeli elections agree upon is not an ordinary Palestinian State, but rather a newly created form of relation with Jordan that is not available in the present political dictionaries. It is neither a confederation nor a federation. Israel wants to form an entity in which the Palestinians are scattered throughout their lands and in diaspora while holding Jordanian passports so that their lands can continue to be usurped by the machinery of the Jewish settlements. In other words, and regardless of the Jordanian rejection of this relation because of its destructive consequences on Jordan itself, Israel wants to create a Jordanian supremacy over the Palestinian People, and an Israeli supremacy over the Palestinian land. This is what any Israeli government agrees on and blesses, not the 4 May’s bug!

Arafat postpones statehood declaration in return for nothing

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat has apparently succumbed to American pressure and decided to postpone the declaration of Palestinian Statehood, originally designated for the fourth of May when the interim phase of the Oslo process comes to an end.

Arafat had hoped for some sort of American “guarantees and assurances” that would help him save face before his people whom he repeatedly promised to declare Statehood on the fourth of May.

For that purpose, the Palestinian leader visited the United States on 23 March and held talks at the White House with President Clinton and his top aides.

However, Arafat was reportedly disappointed by the American response to his pleas for “assurances and guarantees”, and, therefore, decided to mobilize further support for the Palestinian people’s right to self-determination and Statehood.

Seeing that the American tune was not harmonious with the Palestinian song, Arafat had reduced his rhetoric concerning “Statehood” to a mere plea to fill “the political vacuum” likely to arise at the end of the interim period of the peace process.

Leaving Washington virtually empty-handed, Arafat decided to send his deputy Mahmoud Abbas (Abu Mazen) back to Washington in mid-April to try to “formulate a common American-Palestinian approach to the issue of Statehood.”

However, reports from Washington suggested that the Americans were not willing to give Abu Mazen (viewed by the Israelis as the most dove-ish of all PA leaders) more than they had given to Arafat, namely some words of praise for the Palestinian commitment to the peace process and the various security agreements with Israel, and a renewed American commitment to bankroll the PA, whose coffers have been emptied by embezzlement, corruption and financial mismanagement.

However, it seemed Arafat did not want to put all his eggs in the American basket this time, or at the very least wanted to give an impression as such to the Americans.

In April, the Palestinian leader, probably the most peripatetic person in the world, flew from one country to the other in a bid to win recognition for Palestinian Statehood.

He visited as many as 20 countries in Europe, Africa and Asia, appealing to his hosts to recognize a Palestinian State in the West Bank and Gaza Strip once it is declared.

It is not clear how valuable this “verbal support” will be, especially if taken into account the fact that most of the States he visited had already supported the previous declaration of Statehood which was made by the PLO in Algeria in 1988.

PA retreats
It is clear by now that the question concerning the issue of Statehood is no longer whether there will be a postponement or not, but rather for how long that postponement will be, or, indeed, will it be open-ended as the American administration seems to suggest.

Indeed, while Arafat has not formally and openly said he would postpone the declaration of Statehood, statements to that end by his parroting aides have shifted 180 degrees in less than a week.

Those aides and “ministers”, who only a week ago vowed that there would not be any postponement of the Statehood declaration, have begun to speak of “our alienable right to Statehood," while deliberately dropping the declaration of Statehood from their statements.

Ahmed Abdul Rahman, whom Arafat gave the title “minister without portfolio”, said in a Gaza seminar on the subject of Statehood on 22 April that the declaration of Statehood was contingent upon a set of certain factors, and once these factors are at hand, “We will declare Statehood today.”

Abdul Rahman did not say what these factors were, but it was amply clear that what he had in mind was “American recognition” or, at least, American tacit approval.

Abdul Rahman claimed that the last thing the PA was concerned about was a probable Israeli veto of a Palestinian State.

However, it was the Israeli objection, or more correctly, Israeli veto, which is often disguised as “American reservation”, that has so far determined Arafat’s words as well as actions with disastrous implications to the Palestinian people and their national interests.

Unfortunately, this happened all along, in Cairo, Taba, Hebron, and finally miserably in Wye Plantation, when Arafat succumbed to Netanyahu’s concerted efforts to “lower Palestinian expectations to next to nothing”, as Netanyahu boasted on many occasions.

Now, the problem with Yasser Arafat is that he is willing and ready to do just about anything to secure American support for Palestinian Statehood. Hence, Arafat continued to “honour Palestinian commitments under the Wye agreement”, often by rounding up hundreds of Palestinian citizens and throwing them behind bars for many months without charge or trial, only to appease the Americans and please the Israelis.

This is done, despite and in the shadow of the frenzied Israeli land grab in the West Bank, which observers say is unprecedented since the signing of the Oslo Accords more than five years ago.

In the face of all of this, the United States does little more than just entertaining Arafat and asking him to be patient, and, significantly, demanding that he fulfill his “security obligations” vis-�-vis Israel.

And with Arafat playing the role of “the good mama’s boy”, e.g. accepting everything the U.S. does, the Israelis will complete their scheme of “finding indelible facts on the ground” until there is little or nothing to be worried about in case a Palestinian State is announced, because then it will be a State without substance, without much land, and with a lot of hollow symbols.

Palestinian Rejection of the UN Partition Plan

By Dr. Musil J Shihadeh*

I would like to examine here the much talked about Israeli myth that the Palestinian tragedy was a result of their rejecting UN resolution 181 for the Partition of Palestine. In other words, what would have happened had the Palestinians and the Arabs accepted the UN Partition Plan of resolution 181. Would Israel have accepted in good faith the same resolution and implemented it with good intentions?

To keep Israel’s image unblemished in the eyes of the world and to dismiss the contention that Israel was born in sin, Zionist apologists blame all the tragedies that befell the Palestinians on their rejection of the UN Partition Plan as embodied in resolution 181 of 29 November 1947. Such a theme would cloak Israel with urgently needed morality, and would justify Israel’s atrocities, massacres and the uprooting of the Palestinians in the war of 1947-1948 in order to take over their land. In retrospect, the Zionists claim that if the Palestinians had accepted the UN Partition Plan as the Zionist leadership did, none of the miseries that took place during the period 1947-1948 would have taken place—no wars, no victims, no refugees, no land expropriations—and the two peoples would have enjoyed peaceful coexistence for the last 50 years!

At the risk of intruding into the realm of speculation, let me explore the possibilities that could have evolved had the Palestinians and the Arabs accepted the UN resolution in its entirety. If however, the early Zionist plans were unveiled prior to the establishment of the State of Israel and if what had taken place on the ground was objectively compared with these plans, one would have to come to the inescapable conclusion that most of the Israeli premeditated plans involved violating resolution 181 in favour of acquiring as much land as possible while at the same time evicting the indigenous Palestinians from the same land. Let’s keep in mind here that the UN Partition Plan divided Palestine into approximately 55% for the Jewish State and 45% for the Arab State, even though the Arabs actually owned at least 90% of the land and represented a two-thirds majority of the population (Israel—An Apartheid State, by Uri Davis, chapter 1; The UN Commission on Palestine; JNF records; and the Israeli custodian of absentee properties established 1950). I would also like to point out here that the UN Partition prohibited the forced transfer of any inhabitant from any sector to the other. Assuming then that Israel was genuine and honest about accepting the UN Partition, the long-sought objective of establishing a Jewish State would become a farce, since any way you divide it, Palestine had an Arab majority population. It would be very hard to believe that a Jewish State would be named as such if the majority of the inhabitants in that sector were non-Jewish.

Notwithstanding the above, let us assume that a democratic Jewish State was established. In this case, the Arab majority would vote for Arab representatives to be placed in the Jewish Parliament and for an Arab for the position of Prime Minister—after all, no one can deny that in a free democratic society the majority rule. Aside from the name, the Jewish State would be in effect an Arab government with the majority of its inhabitants being also Arab. Since the UN Partition stipulates very clearly that no one from either sector would be dispossessed from his land, except for the common welfare, there would be no place for new Jewish immigration as most of the lands were under Arab ownership and control. Therefore, the UN Partition, if implemented honestly, would make the establishment of a Jewish State in Palestine nearly an impossible Jewish dream. Hence, to cling persistently to the actual establishment of a viable Jewish State, two preconditions had to be fulfilled: the expropriation of land from the indigenous Arabs and the devising of a system under which the Arab majority would become a minority. Since it would be ludicrous, if not insane, to expect anybody volunteering to give his land and property away in exchange for becoming a miserable refugee, somebody else has to do the job. In order for the Zionist leadership, then, to establish the Jewish State in a land whose majority of people and land are Arab, it has no other choice but to use force to evict the Arabs and take over their land and properties as many Jewish sources confirm.

Simha Flapan, Benny Morris, Ilan Pappe, Professor N. Neuberger and Norman Finkelstein are a few of the Israeli historians who confirm such a view. In fact, the uprooting of the Palestinians the result of premeditated plans with the Zionist leadership as far back as 1930, when even Ben-Gurion favoured the transfer of Palestinians in order to create a viable Jewish State. Mr. Yoram Ben Porath of the JNF and a Zionist leader exposes this further when he stated on 14 July 1972 (Yediot Aaronoth) that there is no such thing as Zionism, as colonization by the Jewish State, without the eviction of the Arabs and the expropriation of their lands! Haim Weizman (Exile and Return, page 117) concurs with a similar view when he stated that Israel should be as Jewish as England is English! Joseph Weitz, Deputy chairman of the JNF (1951-1973), stated as early as 1940:

“Among ourselves it must be clear that there is no place in the country for both peoples together...With the Arabs we shall not achieve our aim of being independent people in this country. The only solution is Eretz Israel, at least the west part of Eretz Israel, without Arabs...and there is no other way but to transfer the Arabs from here to the neighbouring countries, transfer all of them, not one village or tribe should remain, and the transfer must aim at Iraq, Syria and even Transjordan. For this purpose money will be found, much money; and only with this transfer could the country absorb millions of our brothers. There is no other alternative...One should investigate now the neighbouring countries in order to determine their capacity to absorb the Arabs of Eretz Israel.”

It’s no wonder, therefore, that plans such as Nachsen, Misparayim, Galilee, Dalet and others were prepared before the war to cleanse the Arabs from the entire Jewish sector. It is no wonder that massacres and uprooting took place in Deir Yassin, Haifa, Jaffa, Safad, West Jerusalem and hundreds of others to make sure that the Jewish State becomes indeed very Jewish! Mr. Rabin in his memoirs has also confirmed how he participated in the eviction of almost 60,000 unprotected Arab civilians in Lud and Ramleh after surrendering to the Zionist forces. He ordered that they start walking toward the East—women, children and old people!

We all have read the history of wars, which established an undeniable fact that most occupiers take over control of the country with their military presence, but how many would evict the indigenous people and replace them with their own people? In Palestine, the Zionists could not create a Jewish State while the majority of the people were not Jewish, as stated by the Israeli historian Benny Morris: “Any way you divide it, you would have a fifth column”, he stated in an interview with Dan Perry, the associated press (16 December 1997).

Again, if the Zionist claim is true, that the eviction took place because of the Palestinian rejection of the UN Partition, why would Israel practice similar evictions when the war was over and everybody was settled? Can Israeli history deny the expulsion of the inhabitants of Ikrit in Western Galilee and the village of Rama in 1949? Can Israel deny that on 5 November 1949 they evicted by force the inhabitants of K’far Anan from their homes and forced them to cross the borders into the West Bank? How about the forced evictions of the 700 inhabitants of K’far Yusuf on 28 February 1951, long after the 1948 war, who were loaded onto trucks and dumped on the Jordanian border? Should we thank the Israelis for supplying the transportation? Actually, in February of 1951 alone, the inhabitants of more than 13 villages were transported out of their homes and dumped on the Jordanian borders! Do all these forced evictions lend any credibility to the Israeli claim that it was not within the Zionist plans to evict the Palestinians?

A further proof of Zionist immoral history in Palestine is the Israeli practices in the West Bank. The expropriation of lands, the evictions, the demolitions, and the cruel military rule do not point to the illegal and immoral behaviour of the Zionists alone, but they confirm the evil practices of the past.

Using the same logic now, are the evictions to be continued because we are now rejecting the further partitioning of whatever is left of our homes and our lands? Are we to be “punished” again by further evictions for rejecting to be evicted?

It was normal for the Palestinians as much as for any people on this earth to reject the UN Partition, which allots the best part of their lands to foreign intruders, but what is not normal is to “punish” them so severely for such a normal attitude, by disinheriting them and turning them into tragic refugees. To claim that Israel should not accept them back in conformity with so many UN resolutions including the UN Partition because they might pose as a fifth column is an exercise in theatrics, because those Arabs who stayed did not become a fifth or a fourth column for that matter. The hard-core fact of the matter is that Israel was born in sin; it robbed the indigenous Palestinians of their homes and properties, and then claimed to the world in a very obvious chutzpah that it was the Palestinians’ fault. It’s like the convicted thief who does not stop stealing but resents being called a thief.

It should behoove Israel and its allies to realize that success in fooling the world would not bring it any closer to any real peace, as the proof lies in the non-peaceful status quo. To address the victims and to tell the truth of what has taken place might be the only first step toward real peace.

*The writer is a free-lance American of Palestinian origin whose writings and ideas have frequented the American media.

At the crossroads:

Between “reasonable” compromise and a just solution

By Dr. Daud Abdullah*

The five-year transitional period envisaged by the Oslo project has finally come to an end. It leaves in its wake a dark legacy of broken promises and political betrayal. Troubled as it were by a conflict between Israeli interpretation on the one hand and Palestinian compliance on the other, the project stumbled from one crisis to another. All told, the current crisis has arisen not so much from the powerlessness of the Palestinians as from Israel’s refusal to accept the principles of negotiation that are necessary for peace. This was evidently illustrated in the letter written by foreign minister Ariel Sharon to UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan one week after the signing of the Wye Memorandum.

Having gained Israel’s existence through the direct sponsorship and favour of the UN, it seemed natural that the newly appointed Israeli minister would reaffirm his country’s gratitude and indebtedness to the World Body. On the contrary, Sharon wrote, “Israel expects that the General Assembly will not put forth resolutions that prejudge the outcome of the final status negotiations, including the issue of Statehood. Moreover, I would like to express our view that anachronistic and outdated resolutions recycled and readopted by the Assembly year after year, which no longer reflect present realities and achievements, should be finally removed from the annual agenda and relegated to the legacy of the past.”

Although many observers in New York felt that the letter did not warrant a reply, the Palestinian Ambassador to the UN, Naseer al-Kidwa, quickly condemned it as a crude attempt by Israel to (once again) “defy international law and relevant United Nations resolutions.” These resolutions mainly refer to Jerusalem, the refugees, settlements, borders, human rights, Palestinian self-determination, and permanent sovereignty over the natural resources in the occupied territories. Sharon’s impassioned plea to revoke them confirmed a view which the opponents of Oslo have voiced consistently for years: that being the Israelis are neither committed to nor interested in a negotiated settlement based on international law.

On account of that, their presumption is one which maintains that there is no Palestinian territory. Hence, they do not acknowledge that Israel is an occupying power in the West Bank, Gaza Strip or Jerusalem. To them, the West Bank and Gaza Strip is not Palestinian territory, rather it is Judea, Samaria, and the Gaza ‘' district’. In other words, there was no Palestine. The only Palestinian territory they recognize is that which lies east of the Jordan river. This, no doubt, is one of the most dangerous obstacles to peace in our region. The unwillingness of the occupier to dismantle his settlements, withdraw from the territories, restore the status quo ante 1967 in Jerusalem, and allow the refugees to return are obvious clues to their real intentions. Perhaps the aging foreign minister should be reminded that even if the Palestinian Authority (PA) signs an unjust settlement, as he expects, it would not be final because only a just solution can be permanent.

Transition and settlements
Admittedly, the concept of a transitional period to Statehood is nothing new to the Palestinians. It was proposed as early as May 1939 in the British White Paper, which declared that “The objective of His Majesty’s Government is the establishment within ten years of an independent Palestine State…” And on other constitutional matters, the Paper also affirmed that “His Majesty’s Government therefore now declares unequivocally that it is not part of their policy that Palestine should become a Jewish State.” Sixty years having been spent on realizing the goals of Palestinian nationalism remain largely unaccomplished, albeit with one fundamental change: that being whereas in 1939 they had to contend with a single colonial power—namely Britain—today they have to engage other hegemonic forces in the United States, Europe, and their regional protectorate Israel.

Contrary to the views expressed by former Secretary of State James Baker that the transitional period would usher in a change of attitude that demonstrates a commitment to peace, the Oslo years have facilitated the intensification of Israeli settlement in the occupied territories. This was, of course, well in accordance with the foundation plan drawn up by Baker [and his senior aide Dennis Ross] and which the former presented to the American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) in May 1989, ostensibly for approval before it became national policy. On that occasion, Baker advised that the future settlement of the conflict should be directed toward the attainment of a “reasonable” compromise between Israeli annexation of the West Bank and Gaza and Palestinian Statehood in the area. This formula, he argued, provided ample scope for the Palestinians to realize their full political rights as well as for Israeli security.

Although Baker did not remain in charge at the State Department long after this, the core of his ideas were largely adopted by the Oslo project. While the transitional period denied the Palestinians sovereignty over the West Bank and Gaza, it enabled Israel to continue enjoying the fruits of its conquests there. In its relentless campaign to minimize the size of territory under Palestinian control, Israel increased the overall number of its settlers in the West Bank and Gaza by 12% between December 1996 and June 1998. Thus it now remains on the eve of the new millennium the only country in the world that is still expanding its frontiers and settling its citizens in the conquered lands. Sharon’s call to Jewish settlers on 15 November 1998 to “grab” unoccupied West Bank land so it does not fall under PA control reveals the depth of Israeli contempt for the rule of law. Given the fact that the UN International War Crimes Tribunal maintains that the construction of settlements in occupied territories is a war crime, the PA should, under the current circumstances, seriously consider legal action against both the Israeli and U.S. governments. Surely without the latter’s financial support Israel would not have been able to allocate $2.6 billion for the settlement of 800,000 Jews in the West Bank by the year 2010.

Shifting Europe
As the conflict enters this advanced and critical stage after Oslo, all the signs confirm that its final resolution will not be determined behind the closed doors of the Security Council. The dispossessed Palestinians who have been the victims of Zionist colonization since the dawn of the twentieth century are determined to play no less a role than their political leaders in defining the course of their history. Their monumental losses and suffering over the years have left them with an undying faith that only the full restoration of their rights will suffice. They have learned through bitter experience that the value of international agreements lies not only in their content but more importantly in their implementation. For this reason, they jealously reserve their right of veto on all matters which affect their higher national interests.

Endowed as they are with one of the highest literacy rates outside the industrialized world, the Palestinians are, on the whole, very capable of distinguishing between empty official rhetoric and meaningful political practice. Hence, their cautious welcome of the March 1999 declaration by the Heads of Government of the European Union affirming support for the “unqualified Palestinian right to self-determination including the option of a State.” Though cloaked in attractive language, the declaration was, nonetheless, typical of the inconsistency that has been a feature of European policy toward the Palestinian cause.

Significantly, while Brussels was proclaiming its support for the inalienable rights of the Palestinians, it was at the same time conferring upon Israel membership to its four-year Research and Development Programme. Previously, the EU had stubbornly withheld membership to Israel until progress was made on implementing the Wye memorandum. The March 1999 decision to reverse this policy has effectively cleared the way for the Jewish State to compete for research projects worth $16.4 billion. This sudden about-face by the EU will further undermine trust and erode confidence among the Palestinians. Their only explanation to this development is that since the Europeans would like to appear even-handed and impartial in their approach to the conflict, they could not support the legitimate right of the Palestinians to Statehood without offering concessions to Israel; even while the latter continues to violate its international treaty obligations!

Where to?
And what of the research funds? Will the environmentally conscious Europeans insist that it be used to save the holy land from pollution and destruction? Or, will they pander to Israeli “security needs” and allow it to be used for the development of weapons of mass destruction? At a time when there are no recycling bins for bottles, cans, plastics, or batteries in Jerusalem. Given their historic religious and cultural interests in this city, circumstances demand that the Europeans must do all within their power to stop the annual disposal of 3.5 million tons of untreated waste into landfills in Jerusalem. For how much longer will the occupying power be allowed to dump industrial waste, including poisonous heavy metals and salts, directly into the environment, damaging underground aquifers, rivers and streams? Surely by turning the financial and economic screws on Israel the EU can effectively play a major role in reversing this deadly trend and, likewise, protect its own interests in the holy land.

In the end, the issue is not about financial assistance, great or small. To the Palestinians it is a matter no less fundamental than that outlined by the founding fathers in the American Declaration of Independence. That “people seek historically that separate and equal status among the powers of the earth to which the laws of nature and nature’s God entitle them.” Unlike the usurpers of their land, they are totally convinced that the recognition which is won by sweat and blood is more likely to endure than that which is granted on a silver platter. Hence their continued rejection of the solutions based on pragmatism rather than legality.

To freedom-lovers throughout the world, occupied Palestine represents the last significant outpost of European colonialism. The struggle of its people to realize their national aspirations has been long, bloody and painful. As they stand at the crossroads of the twenty-first century, they see in the distance a promising future enlightened by resistance. Driven by the conviction that their condition will not change by the stroke of a pen or the passing of a resolution, they have resolved to take the long and certain road to success instead of the misleading short-cut of “reasonable compromise”. For it is only through a just solution that the dark consequences of 1948 be finally removed.

*The author is a researcher at the Palestinian Return Centre (London) and
Editor of its Return Review.

The Judaization of the Holy Land:

United States Policy Towards Jerusalem

By Dr. Ahmad Yousef*

(4-4)

The Zionist Lobby and the Judaization of Jerusalem
Judging by the development of U.S. policy toward Jerusalem, Israel has had success in managing its drive to gain U.S. support for Israel’s colonization policies in the Holy City. Israel was also able to devalue the UN resolutions concerning the legal status of Jerusalem as an occupied city by the fact that the rapid Judaization going on in Jerusalem is a violation of the Geneva Conventions and a blatant aggression against the United Nations and its resolutions.

This success is more than partially owed to the efforts of the Jewish community in the United States, with its extensive network of money and media. Through political action committees (PACs), the Jewish community has been able to collect millions to elect Congressmen who support Israel. The PACs collected $14 million to support candidates for the 1994 elections, and the funds raised for the presidential and Congressional elections of 1996 surpassed that by millions.

Richard Curtiss writes that the pro-Israeli committees are the single biggest financial contributors during U.S. election seasons compared to all other interest groups. Curtiss adds that most pro-Israeli PACs were founded by the board members of AIPAC. This Washington-based group is considered the biggest Israeli lobby.

The Jewish community is able to mobilize its vote for political reasons. Jews in the United States usually vote at a rate of 90%, while only 47% of the U.S. population at large votes. The importance of the Jewish vote is clear during any election, whether presidential or congressional.

If we think like presidential and congressional candidates eyeing the elections of the year 2000, it is obvious that the “Jerusalem agenda” will be a political bargaining chip. Candidates will use the idea of “Jerusalem is the capital of Israel” to win the votes of the Jewish community and the support of the pro-Israeli PACs. Most people believe that Vice President Al Gore, known for his absolute support of Israel, will follow Clinton in the presidency. If so, the project to Judaize Jerusalem politically will be accomplished using both the Republican dominated Congress and possibly another Democratic administration.

Conclusion
The clearest and most consistent characteristic in Washington’s policies toward Arabs and Muslims is belittlement and disregard. This administration understands that the way it is dealing with Arabs and Muslims will not serve its long-term strategic interests in the region. Clinton addressed the possibility of a backlash in the Arab and Islamic world in response to the drastic shift in American policy evidenced by the veto of a United Nations Security Council resolution condemning Israel for its plan to build settlements in East Jerusalem. Kosovo, the Roots of Conflict

Dr. Azzam Tamimi*

Without the need to strike any comparisons, the tragedy of Kosovo may go down in history as one of the most horrific in modern times. There have been numerous European tragedies this century; few, including that of Kosovo, are so dramatic as to have a significant impact on the future not only of Europe but also of the entire world. Experts pointed out right from the beginning that the issue of Kosovo is likely to be the most intractable of all the political conflicts in the Balkans. If war were to break out, it was predicted, the killing and destruction would be more intense than anything hitherto witnessed in the region. There may be little disagreement on this conclusion, though people may continue to disagree on the nature of the conflict that has led to this particular tragedy. The most obvious feature of the conflict in Kosovo may easily be said to be ethnic or religious. Some may contend that a Serb/Albanian or Christian Orthodox/Islamic divide can clearly be seen. Numerous arguments may be put forward to prove the point that ethno-religious tensions are to blame.

The popular view of the recent wars in Croatia and Bosnia was always that these were ethnic conflicts. Particularly in the case of Kosovo, it would seem that what we are dealing with is indeed a genuine ethnic conflict. The basic division is, in the first place, an ethnic one in the full sense. Unlike the different types of Bosnians, who are all Slavs and all speak the same language, the Serbs and the Albanians are linguistically quite separate. Together with the differences in language goes a range of other cultural differences, many of them linked to religion—the division between Serb and Albanian roughly coincides with the division between Eastern Orthodox and Muslim. With both language and religion setting people apart, all the conditions seem to be present for a primary conflict of peoples (1).

The Albanians of Kosovo today are in many ways a politically mobilized people, but religion has played almost no role at all in that mobilization. There is no Islamic political movement among the Albanians. Where religion is a factor in the present political situation is on the Orthodox side, which constantly employs religious rhetoric to justify the defence of ‘sacred’ Serbian interests. But this, according to Malcolm, is a classic example of religion being mobilized and manipulated for ideological purposes (2).

The roots of today’s conflict are traced to the nineteenth-century Serbian ideology, which created a cult of the medieval battle of Kosovo, as some sort of nationally-defining historical and spiritual event. Just as the French did in Algeria and the Russians in Chechenia, the Serbs embarked on an explicit programme of introducing Serb colonists to Kosovo throughout the inter-war period.

A memorandum sent to the Great Powers by Belgrade in early 1913 set out three justifications for Serbian rule in Kosovo: the moral right of a more civilized people; the historic right to an area which contained the patriarchate buildings of the Serbian Orthodox Church and had once been part of the medieval Serbian empire; and a kind of ethnographic right based on the fact that at some time in the past Kosovo had had a majority Serb population—a right which, according to the memorandum, was unaffected by the recent invasion of Albanians (3).

In emulation of Jewish nationalism as expressed by Zionism, the Serb nationalists claim that Kosovo is the ‘Jerusalem’ of the Serbs. This claim is belied by the fact that in no form of Christianity, including Eastern Orthodoxy, does a ‘holy place’ play any sort of theological role equivalent to the role of Jerusalem in the theology of Judaism.

Milosevic came to real power in Belgrade in 1987. Until then he had been an unknown Communist who ascended the ladder of power within the Party thanks to the influence of his wife. He might not have made it had it not been for the card of Serb nationalism he played at a time when the communist world seemed to crumble rapidly. Soon after becoming president of Serbia, Milosevic stripped Kosovo of its autonomy. Like the Pharaoh of Egypt, Hitler of Germany and many dictators of modern as well as ancient times, paranoia and greed determined Milosevic’s attitude and the destiny of the nation he had imposed himself upon. Like all such despots, Milosevic needed a source of legitimization for his criminal acts. Nationalism and religion have been, as in most other cases, the most convenient tools.

It is definitely not easy for the West to bomb a European country in the manner we have been witnessing every day for the past three weeks. How To Promote Civilizational Dialogue:
Some Policy Guidelines For The West And The Muslims

Ronald E. Neumann*

Despite clear statements since 1992, we are often asked: “What is your policy toward Islam?” Our clear choice is not to have such a single policy toward Islam because the perceived need for such a policy is wrong on facts and contrary to our values. We reject, and have rejected, the “clash of civilizations” thesis as a basis of policy. We do have policies toward States, against terrorism and violence, and in support of democracy. I would like to talk about these elements.

To accept the idea that the West is one broad, unified political construct and Islam is another would make it practically incumbent on the United States to have a policy toward Islam, as we do toward other large political constructs called nation-States or regional groupings. We do not accept that idea.

Let me be clear and emphatic: the United States of America does not and should not have a political policy toward Islam. Islam is first and foremost a religion—as with other great religions, it offers a prescription for an individual’s relationship with God and for the relationship among human beings themselves. The ways in which individuals, groups, or States work out the prescription lead to a wide variety of political actions which are a proper focus of policy, but which are not unified at all.

The United States of America does not have a policy directed at any of the world’s religions. We do not have a policy on Islam because, as Assistant Secretary Djerejian said in his 1992 Meridian House speech, with reference to political Islamist movements, “We detect no monolithic or coordinated international effort behind these movements.”

What I want to express clearly is that we do not see Islam as something intrinsically foreign, for which we need a foreign policy. It is a simple fact that there are millions of Muslims in the United States today and Islam is the fastest growing religion in the United States. It is one of the world’s great religions and a force of civilization. Recognizing that Islam is as American as any other religion practiced by our people, I need only point to the First Lady’s participation in the Eid al-Fitr activities at the White House this year and the President’s greetings to the American Muslim community on that occasion.

As a religion, Islam advocates many of the same values so many Americans hold dear: the centrality of religion to a well-lived life, high regard for the importance of family, and, in the form closest to the practice and sayings of the Prophet, toleration and respect for the beliefs of others. So, I repeat that the foreign policy issue is not Islam, but political actions in particular places by particular people.

A now famous theory argues that Islam, mostly outside the United States, is part of a large clash of civilizations.

The idea of a clash of civilizations has become a catch-all phrase thrown blithely about by people, only some of whom have actually read the articles and the book from which it comes. I do not really mean to caricature Professor Huntington’s work, but unfortunately it came along just as people were looking for a framework to analyze the post-Cold War turmoil they were seeing in the world.

Huntington’s work has been used by some to interpret the violence and turmoil that we were seeing in parts of the Arab world, although the Islamic world as a whole is much larger. This violence seemed to be peculiarly foreign and particularly frightening. In short, Huntington’s theories gave intellectual credibility to half-formed perceptions and feelings that many people already had about Islam and the possibility, if not inevitability, of a clash with the West.

This is a concept we have steadfastly rejected. Former National Security Advisor Anthony Lake was quite clear on this point in a 1994 speech to the Washington Institute. Referring directly to the clash of civilizations, he said, “We strongly disagree.…There is indeed a fundamental divide. But the fault line runs not between ‘civilizations’ or religions....No, it runs instead between oppression and responsible government, between isolation and openness, and between moderation and extremism. And it knows no distribution by race or creed.”

Furthermore, I submit that the idea of policy based on what people believe and think is fundamentally antithetical to basic American principles. Our history and laws have built an important division between, on the one hand, nearly absolute freedom to advance ideas and, on the other, sometimes quite stringent restrictions on the means that are legitimate to implement those ideas. We do not make policy on the basis of others’ thought because to do so could be destructive to the ideas and values we hold dear.

So, having spoken so much about what we do not have and do not accept in terms of policy, let me now speak more positively.

Islam becomes a foreign policy issue only because some people and countries exploit and abuse its rhetoric, ideas and symbols for actions that we do oppose. Islam, as a religion, does not prescribe any particular international policy, but some States and some groups within States have chosen to use their own interpretation of the religion as a justification for policy and practices of oppression and terrorism. It is these policies and practices that interest us, not their alleged religious justification. If these same policies and practices were justified by some other means, the will of the leader and his social theories, for example, or just on a more mundane assessment of national interest, our response would be the same.

Whatever the justification, we will always oppose those who:

1. Practice terrorism, oppress minorities, preach intolerance or violate internationally accepted norms of human rights, including the right to freedom of conscience or religion;
2. Oppose the peaceful resolution of disputes, especially that between Arabs and Israelis;
3. Use repression, violence, or terrorism to pursue their objectives.

In contrast, we support those who work toward political pluralism. By laying down these redlines, to use a word popular in the region, we are not talking about imposing a precise American model on anybody’s political system or foreign policy. Instead, we believe that certain values are universal, such as political participation and adherence to internationally accepted human rights norms. These values do not belong just to the United States, although we like to believe that we are a leading proponent. They belong to all of humanity. I would point out that many societies or political groupings that call themselves Islamic, and therefore lay claim to being just, accept that these values are as much a part of their cultural and political heritage as they are of ours.

It is clear that many of the young people who find their way into Islamist groups, and sometimes into extremism, do so because they see no other way to make a real difference in their own societies and political systems. There is a widespread sense of failure now attached to the political theories of the period of struggle for independence and the early post-independence period. More openness and political participation, along with globally oriented economic policies, would greatly reduce the appeal of extremism of whatever religious or ideological coloration. It is also unfortunate that, while some Muslim States are taking steps toward democracy, many Muslim States of the Middle East have slipped behind other regions of the world, both in terms of increasing political participation and participating in the best aspects of economic and informational globalization and development. This has heightened tensions but the phenomenon is global.

Some thinkers have also suggested that there is an inherent conflict between Islam and democracy. In that sentence, I may have oversimplified an argument for the sake of knocking it down, but the idea is essentially simplistic and deserves attack.

Islam, first of all, is not a monolith, even within the four schools of Sunni Islam and the variation with Shiite practice. Equally, perhaps even more important, while the Qur’an is eternal, its interpretation does evolve. We in government and in think-tanks must not treat Islam as static, any more than Christianity. Human interpretations of religious precepts change. They are changing today across the Muslim world. From independent Iranian thinkers, who are often silenced but continue to work and insist on their right to tell the truth as they see it, through struggles in Egypt to Algerian religious leaders whose supporters have paid in their blood the price of opposing violence, there are arguments for the harmonization of democratic and Islamic principles. For example, some thinkers earlier in this century were opposed to parliamentary democracy as a system incompatible with traditional consensus building in the Islamic community. That view is increasingly challenged today.

The debate over interpretation and the application of democracy is a huge debate, swirling throughout the Islamic world. Ideas of democracy are still contested. We cannot anticipate the debate’s conclusion. We should study it far more. But, at a minimum, we must not trivialize it into a few catch phrases, or profess to fully understand the process, while still in the middle of it.

One good example of the dynamism of Islam is the situation in Iran, in which some religious-political authorities are saying loud and clear that participation and rule of law are not only compatible with, but also intrinsic parts of, Islamic law and practice. It is important that we understand that this is not a repudiation of Islam but a critically important debate within the religion. So, while there is in the minds and hearts of the faithful, a belief that there is only one Islam, it is clear that there is a wide and changing variety of the practices associated with it. Many of these ideas are fully compatible with the values of the United States and internationally recognized principles. To put it simply, it is possible to be both a devout Muslim and a small “d” democrat, as long as one believes and acts as if this is so. It is precisely the actions of people and States to which we must respond and which we seek to influence, not their intellectual, emotional, or spiritual justifications. The justifications are ultimately none of our business, however, the actions must be.

The change in views of democracy are occurring in the Middle East. In Algeria, Morocco, Lebanon, Jordan, Kuwait and Yemen, there is movement toward more democratic expression and more influence on policy of elected representatives. Nowhere is the outcome clear. Nowhere do the lines of controversy put Islamists on only one side of the debate. Yet if the results are uncertain, there is far too much going on to ignore. And we are not ignoring it. It is a fact that we are struggling to find the right balance in support for our diverse interests. But it is also a fact that we are putting in place new programmes to support democratic development, progress toward the rule of law, and the emergence of still fragile civic societies. We must do this to be true to our principles and to build a more stable world in which are own interests can thrive.

I want to conclude with the views of President Clinton, who has probably done more to reach out to the Islamic world than any previous president. He told the Jordanian Parliament in 1994 that there is no clash of civilizations. But there are contests between forces that transcend civilization—a contest between freedom and tyranny, terror and security, bigotry and tolerance, and isolation and openness. We have chosen our side. Our task is to bring all the peoples of the region of whatever faith into the “camp of peace”.

Jewish History, Jewish Religion

The Laws Against Non-Jews

Part 15

By Israel Shahak

Saving of Life
This subject - the supreme value of human life and the obligation of every human being to do the outmost to save the life of a fellow human - is of obvious importance in itself. It is also of particular interest in a Jewish context, in view of the fact that since the second world war Jewish opinion has - in some cases justly, in others unjustly - condemned 'the whole world' or at least all Europe for standing by when Jews were being massacred. Let us therefore examine what the Halakhah has to say on this subject.

According to the Halakhah, the duty to save the life of a fellow Jew is paramount. It supersedes all other religious obligations and interdictions, excepting only the prohibitions against the three most heinous sins of adultery (including incest), murder and idolatry.

As for Gentiles, the basic talmudic principle is that their lives must not be saved, although it is also forbidden to murder them outright. The Talmud itself expresses this in the maxim 'Gentiles are neither to be lifted [out of a well] nor hauled down [into it]’. Maimonides explains:

As for Gentiles with whom we are not at war ... their death must not be caused, but it is forbidden to save them if they are at the point of death; if, for example, one of them is seen falling into the sea, he should not be rescued, for it is written: 'neither shalt thou stand against the blood of thy fellow’ - but [a Gentile] is not thy fellow.

In particular, a Jewish doctor must not treat a Gentile patient. Maimonides - himself an illustrious physician - is quite explicit on this; in another passage he repeats the distinction between 'thy fellow' and a Gentile, and concludes: 'and from this learn ye, that it is forbidden to heal a Gentile even for payment …’

However, the refusal of a Jew - particularly a Jewish doctor - to save the life of a Gentile may, if it becomes known, antagonise powerful Gentiles and so put Jews in danger. Where such danger exists, the obligation to avert it supersedes the ban on helping the Gentile. Thus Maimonides continues: ' ... but if you fear him or his hostility, cure him for payment, though you are forbidden to do so without payment.' In fact, Maimonides himself was Saladin's personal physician. His insistence on demanding payment - presumably in order to make sure that the act is not one of human charity but an unavoidable duty - is however not absolute. For in another passage he allows Gentile whose hostility is feared to be treated 'even gratis, if it is unavoidable'.

The whole doctrine - the ban on saving a Gentile's life or healing him, and the suspension of this ban in cases where there is fear of hostility - is repeated (virtually verbatim) by other major authorities, including the 14th century Arba'ah Turim and Karo's Beyt Yosef and Shulhan 'Arukh. Beyt Yosef adds, quoting Mairnomdes: 'And it is permissible to try out a drug on a heathen, if this serves a purpose'; and this is repeated also by the famous R. Moses Isserles.

The consensus of halakhic authorities is that the term 'Gentiles' in the above doctrine refers to all non-Jews. A lone voice of dissent is that of R. Moses Rivkes, author of a minor commentary on the Shulhan Arukh, who writes.

Our sages only said this about heathens, who in their day worshipped idols and did not believe in the Jewish Exodus from Egypt or in the creation of the world ex nihilo. But the Gentiles in whose [protective] shade we, the people of Israel, are exiled and among whom we are scattered do believe in the creation of the world ex nihilo and in the Exodus and in several principles of our own religion and they pray to the Creator of heaven and earth...Not only is there no interdiction against helping them, but we are even obliged to pray for their safety.

This passage, dating from the second half of the 17th century, is a favourite quote of apologetic scholars. Actually, it does not go nearly as far as the apologetics pretend, for it advocates removing the ban on saving a Gentile's life, rather than making it mandatory as in the case of a Jew; and even this liberality extends only to Christians and Muslims but not the majority of human beings. Rather, what it does show is that there was a way in which the harsh doctrine of the Halakhah could have been progressively liberalised. But as a matter of fact the majority of later halakhic authorities, far from extending Rivkes' leniency to other human groups, have rejected it altogether.

Desecrating the Sabbath to Save Life

Desecrating the sabbath - that is, doing work that would otherwise be banned on Saturday- becomes a duty when the need to save a Jew's life demands it.

The problem of saving a Gentile's life on the sabbath is not raised in the Talmud as a main issue, since it is in any case forbidden even on a weekday; it does however enter as a complicating factor in two connections.

First, there is a problem where a group of people are in danger, and it is possible (but not certain) that there is at least one Jew among them; should the sabbath be desecrated in order to save them? There is an extensive discussion of such cases. Following earlier authorities, including Maimonides and the Talmud itself, the Shulhan 'Arukh decides these matters according to the weight of probabilities. For example, suppose nine Gentiles and one Jew live in the same building. One Saturday the building collapses; one of the ten - it is not known which one - Is away, but the other nine are trapped under the rubble. Should the rubble be cleared, thus desecrating the sabbath, seeing that the Jew may not be under it (he may have been the one that got away)? The Shulhan 'Arukh says that it should, presumably because the odds that the Jew is under the rubble are high (nine to one). But now suppose that nine have got away and only one - again, it is not known which one - is trapped. Then there is no duty to clear the rubble, presumably because this time there are long odds (nine to one) against the Jew being the person trapped. Similarly: 'If a boat containing some Jews is seen to be in peril upon the sea, it is a duty incumbent upon all to desecrate the sabbath in order to save it.' However, the great R. 'Aqiva Eiger (died 1837) comments that this applies only 'when it is known that there are Jews on board. But ... if nothing at all is known -about the identity of those on board. [the sabbath] must not be desecrated, for one acts according to [the weight of probabilities, and] the majority of people in the world are Gentiles.' Thus, since there are very long odds against any of the passengers being Jewish, they must be allowed to drown.

Secondly, the provision that a Gentile may be saved or cared for in order to avert the danger of hostility is curtailed on the sabbath. A Jew called upon to help a Gentile on a weekday may have to comply because to admit that he is not allowed, in principle, to save the life of a non-Jew would be to invite hostility. But on Saturday the Jew can use sabbath observance as a plausible excuse. A paradigmatic case discussed at length in the Talmud is that of a Jewish midwife invited to help a Gentile woman in childbirth. The upshot is that the midwife is allowed to help on a weekday 'for fear of hostility', but on the sabbath she must not do so, because she can excuse herself by saying: 'We are allowed to desecrate the sabbath only for our own, who observe the sabbath, but for your people, who do not keep the sabbath, we are not allowed to desecrate it.' Is this explanation a genuine one or merely an excuse? Maimonides clearly thinks that it is just an excuse, which can be used even if the task that the midwife is invited to do does not actually involve any desecration of the sabbath. Presumably, the excuse will work just as well even in this case, because Gentiles are generally in the dark as to precisely which kinds of work are banned for Jews on the sabbath. At any rate, he decrees: 'A Gentile woman must not be helped in childbirth on the sabbath, even for payment; nor must one fear hostility, even when [such help involves] no desecration of the sabbath.' The Shu1han Anukh decrees likewise.

Nevertheless, this sort of excuse could not always be relied upon to do the trick and avert Gentile hostility. Therefore, certain important rabbinical authorities had to relax the rules to some extent and allowed Jewish doctors to treat Gentiles on the sabbath even if this involved doing certain types of work normally banned on that day. This partial relaxation applied particularly to rich and powerful Gentile patients, who could not be fobbed off so easily and whose hostility could be dangerous.

Thus, R. Yo'el Sirkis, author of Bayit Hadash and one of the greatest rabbis of his time (Poland, 17th century), decided that 'mayors, petty nobles and aristocrats' should be treated on the sabbath, because of the fear of their hostility which involves 'some danger'. But in other cases, especially when the Gentile can be fobbed off with an evasive excuse, a Jewish doctor would commit 'an unbearable sin' by treating him on the sabbath. Later in the same century, a similar verdict was given in the French city of Metz, whose two parts were connected by a pontoon bridge. Jews are not normally allowed to cross such a bridge on the sabbath, but the rabbi of Metz decided that a Jewish doctor may nevertheless do so 'if he is called to the great governor': since the doctor is known to cross the bridge for the sake of his Jewish patients, the governor's hostility could be aroused if the doctor refused to do so for his sake. Under the authoritarian rule of Louis XIV, it was evidently important to have the goodwill of his intendant; the feelings of lesser Gentiles were of little importance.

Hokhmat Shlomoh, a 19th century commentary on the Sbu1han 'Arukh, mentions a similarly strict interpretation of the concept 'hostility' in connection with the Karaites, a small heretical Jewish sect. According to this view, their lives must not be saved if that would involve desecration of the sabbath, 'for "hostility" applies only to the heathen, who are many against us, and we are delivered into their hands.. But the Karaites are few and we are not delivered into their hands, [so] the fear of hostility does not apply to them at all. In fact, the absolute ban on desecrating the sabbath in order to save the life of a Karaite is still in force today, as we shall see.

The whole subject is extensively discussed in the responsa of R .Moshe Sofer- better known as 'Hatam Sofer' – the famous rabbi of Pressburg (Bratislava) who died in 1832. His conclusions are of more than historical interest, since in 1966 one of his responsa was publicly endorsed by the then Chief Rabbi of Israel as 'a basic institution of the Halakhah'. The particular question asked of Hatam Sofer concerned the situation in Turkey, where it was decreed during one of the wars that in each township or village there should be midwives on call, ready to hire themselves out to any woman in labour. Some of these midwives were Jewish; should they hire themselves out to help Gentile women on weekdays and on the sabbath?

In his responsum, Hatam Sofer first concludes, after careful investigation, that the Gentiles concerned- that is, Ottoman Christians and Muslims- are not only idolators 'who definitely worship other gods and thus should "neither be lifted [out of a well] nor hauled down", ' but are likened by him to the Amalekites, so that the talmudic ruling 'it is forbidden to multiply the seed of Amalek' applies to them. In principle, therefore, they should not be helped even on weekdays. However, in practice it is 'permitted' to heal Gentiles and help them in labour, if they have doctors and midwives of their own, who could be called instead of the Jewish ones. For if Jewish doctors and midwives refused to attend to Gentiles, the only result would be loss of income to the former which is of course undesirable. This applies' equally on weekdays and on the sabbath, provided no desecration of the sabbath is involved. However, in the latter case the sabbath can serve as an excuse to 'mislead the heathen woman and say that it would involve desecration of the sabbath'.

Someone had run into Abu Ahmed’s small coffee shop where he had just served a steaming cup of Turkish coffee to an old friend and customer and screamed, “Abu Ahmed, come quick, Bassel’s been shot!”

He didn’t remember how his legs carried him to where his son’s blood trickled into the dust of the refugee camp. His heart fell with a large thump at the sight of his son sprawling. He saw his son’s life pumping out of him as his blood throbbed into the arid ground. Plants in Rafah were watered with blood, the blood of Palestinian youths.

The old man knelt down and grabbed the now matted dark mass of hair that Bassel had kept so well groomed before the shooting. The eyes of Bassel had rolled back but when Abu Ahmed put his head on the boy’s chest, he could feel his son’s heart still beating.

Immediately, an ambulance arrived at the scene and paramedics jumped down with a stretcher and carried the body of Bassel away. By now the boy’s family had arrived and the father, mother, and some of his sons piled into a car and sped off behind the ambulance. Suddenly they approached an Israeli roadblock near Khan Younis. An Israeli soldier shouted at the men saying, “get out of the car, all of you!”

The old man, his sons and his wife got out of the car. At once they were pounced upon by other Israeli soldiers who commenced to savagely beat them. Abu Ahmed’s wife screamed and begged. “Please, let them go. Our son has been shot in the head and may be dying!”

She got nowhere. Her screams of protest went unheard, her pleading unnoticed. After her husband and sons were beaten, they were ordered to clear a street of its roadblocks. They could do nothing but comply. Later, after the beaten family finally got back in the car and resumed their journey, they were once again stopped at another checkpoint near the Nasser Hospital in Gaza.

At last they arrived at the hospital. The family beaten and bloody rushed in to search for Bassel. Was he dead or alive? Bassel, 16, had been taken to the Pediatric Intensive care Unit to try to save his life. When his family entered the room, the youth had already been placed on life support systems and was unconscious. It had been decided to move the boy to an Israeli hospital and for some humane reason, a helicopter had been sent to transfer the wounded boy. But when Abu Ahmed and his family went to the landing pad to accompany the wounded Bassel, they were stopped by some armed Jewish settlers.

One of the settlers hissed, “Stop right here. Go no further!”

Other settlers grabbed his sons and began to beat them in front of their wounded and weary father. “You have almost killed my son,” said Abu Ahmed, “are you going to kill the others too?”

And, now somewhere in his sorrowful unconsciousness the old father saw the settlers finally allow the family to accompany the near-dying Bassel. But later he died at the hospital he was transferred to.

The old father didn’t want to wake up. What was there to wake up to? Bassel had died from a bullet wound to his head. A year later, Ahmed had now died too. He too had been shot in the head. The remaining three other sons were being held in administrative detention without a court hearing. Where was it all going to end and when would tragedy stop plaguing the old man and his family?

He drifted back to the real world, to the smooth soothing bed had had been placed on when he fainted. He drifted back to the smell of antiseptics and the dank smell of death, of Ahmed’s death. But the old man could not give up and die too though surely death was easier and perhaps heaven awaited such a good old man who had lost nearly everything in this world. Yet he had a fighting urge to live. He had to live, he had to try to help get his three living sons out of prison.

At first the Israeli army demanded that the body of young Ahmed could not be buried in the same cemetery next to the body of Bassel. But eventually, the body of Ahmed was placed next to Bassel’s. A huge funeral procession had accompanied Ahmed’s body and once again, a martyred son of the old man was laid to rest.

Abu Ahmed and his family are living symbols and heroes to other Palestinians. They are neither rich nor powerful. They are twice-over refugees with little money. But they are as noble as they come. Abu Ahmed and his family, both the living and the dead are heroes, the unsung heroes.

There are children in the morning

They are leaning out for love

They will lean this way forever...”
