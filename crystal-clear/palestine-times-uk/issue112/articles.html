Khalid Amayreh

The vindictive smear campaign by the Zionist establishment and its intelligence, police and media apparatus against the 1,250,000-strong Palestinian community in the Jewish State should not come as a surprise to anybody with even the most rudimentary knowledge of the annals of Israel’s treatment of its Arab “citizens.”

In fact, it would be sufficiently safe to say that Israel’s treatment of its Palestinian “citizens” was far worse than the way the White man had dealt with the American Indians in North America and with the Aboriginals in Australia. These communities of minorities came eventually, though somewhat belatedly, to be treated as “equal citizens,” at least at the official level.

In Israel, however, the Palestinians, the natives of the land, who had been toiling Palestine ages before Jewish immigrants set foot on Palestine in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, are still treated as third or fourth class citizens. In fact, they are not “citizens” at all, but merely “permanent residents.” The reason for that lies simply in the fact that the Palestinians are not Jews; they are “goyem.”

The Israeli treatment of its Palestinian community, which makes up nearly 20% of the population, can be described in many respects as “criminal.” Indeed, if there was justice in this world, a court of law would probably declare Israel, par excellence, a criminal State of the first degree.

The Jewish State had slaughtered the Palestinians, often en masse; it banished them from their homes, often at gunpoint; it destroyed their villages and hamlets and sought to obliterate the sites of these villages and hamlets; it even unearthed the bones of their ancestors; it destroyed their places of worship; and converted many mosques into “national museums” while leaving others to fall into disrepair.

The Jewish State sought with unrelenting determination to reduce the remaining Palestinian towns and villages in 1948-Palestine (Israel proper) into isolated and shut-in ghettos, resembling the ghettos in which Jews had lived prior to their usurpation of Palestine.

The reason for that is the Zionists’ almost morbid desire to check to the very minimum Palestinian demographic expansion so that Arab life in these ghetto towns and villages would eventually become unbearable so much so that the Palestinians, particularly the young generations, would contemplate leaving “Eretz Yisrael”…for good!

And when a Palestinian, whether in Israel proper or in the West Bank, ventures desperately to build a structure to shelter his children from the cold of the winter and the heat of the summer, the Zionist establishment immediately sends in its bulldozers to demolish the newly-built house, thus deepening people’s bitterness and inflicting additional emotional pain on the already tormented victim.

Has the State treated him well and he reacted not in kind?

For a State that treats part of its supposed citizenry as “unwanted” (I am not speaking here about words but rather actions) should not expect these citizens to demonstrate loyalty and devotion to that State. The Jewish State incited against its Palestinian citizens before they incited against the State, the State oppressed the Arabs before they complained…what else can the oppressed do?

The Jewish State is yet to recognize the existence of many Arab villages and hamlets that predated the establishment of Israel by decades, even hundreds of years. A Palestinian boy and the UN Security Council

Dr. Mohamed Khodr*

As I watched the expensive and extravagant three-day United Nations Millennial Summit in New York City held in September, I couldn’t help thinking of the large poster hanging in my home of two-year-old Muhammad Ali Abu-Swai (he’ll be five now if he’s alive, God willing).

On 4 August 1997, Israeli soldiers awakened Muhammad’s family and ordered them to leave. Within minutes, bulldozers demolished their home in the Ras Al-Amoud neighbourhood in Arab East Jerusalem leaving the family homeless. In the time-frozen picture of Muhammad, he is wearing a clean white shirt and yellow pants as he stands barefooted on the stones of what used to be his home. As if in prayer, his fists are clenched in front of his bowed head, with his eyes closed dripping with tears. Muhammad stands alone in this picture, with no family and no Israeli soldiers appearing, only his pained, fearful face and silent cry. Israel’s demolition of Muhammad’s home, life, dreams, security, education, health and future goes unnoticed by the world.

The guilty party is out of sight and out of mind. In Israel’s and America’s eyes, Muhammad has no one to blame but his great grandfather for not accepting the Partition of Palestine in 1948 imposed by the institution that arose out of the Holocaust—the United Nations. I kept thinking how intertwined are the lives of Muhammad and the United Nations.

The UN became the “psychotherapist” for Western guilt, colonialism and shame. UN resolution 181 that called for the Partition of Palestine became the “Prozac” for the colonial powers of the Security Council. The Security Council’s members are former and current Colonial powers. They are four mainly Christian nations and one officially atheist nation. All are nuclear powers with the most powerful armies who by might alone have become the world’s judges, jury and executioners. By simply raising their hands, they decide which country gets bombed, sanctioned, starved, expelled, or accepted. Security Council resolutions are usually implemented in the Southern Hemisphere, on poorer nations, often Islamic or Arab, and usually dark-skinned. Obviously, these five nations and their proxy countries are immune to Security Council resolutions and thus can oppress and murder their own civilians such as Russia in Chechnya or China’s oppression of dissidents and religious groups. The only other nation that is immune from complying with dozens of UN resolutions is–Israel.

As I watched the three-day UN Millennial Summit, I wondered whether anyone, especially the Arab and Islamic leaders, was thinking of Muhammad and his inhumane painful fate. Did they see the irony of celebrating an institution that in recent years has become the whipping rod for the sole superpower in the world to impose its hegemony on Arab and Islamic States thereby rendering their governments impotent and weak, clamouring for American attention, money and photo opportunities? Even Arafat, Muhammad’s leader, is reduced to carrying out Israeli security policies upon his own people.

Sixty Arab and Islamic leaders attended this summit. They were all sidelined as Clinton and Israel once again pressured Arafat to compromise. Ironically, none of the negotiations were based on the 70 UN resolutions adopted over the last 52 years. None of the Arab and Islamic leaders who represent a fifth of the world’s population and 50% of the world’s oil reserves, seized the opportunity of their presence at the UN to unite and demand that the UN implement its resolutions, that Israel accept international law, and that Jerusalem be shared.

Muhammad, when you grow up, you may wonder whose fault it is you lost your home. It’s somewhat complicated, but in a real sense, everybody failed you: from your own people, to the Arab and Islamic world, to a confluence of events in Europe and America that allowed a small Jewish community to build their influence and money on the victims of the Holocaust, to the United Nations and the entire world, and lastly to me. Can I do more as an Arab American and Muslim to ensure that the world “Never Forgets” the tears you shed on your occupied stolen soil? So many words have failed you, my little friend, from the UN Charter to the UN Declaration of Human Rights, to dozens of UN resolutions, including 181, 194 (right of return of Palestinians), 242, 338, 476 and 478 (declaring Israel’s annexation of Jerusalem “null and void”), to the Fourth Geneva Convention, to Israel’s human rights abuses reported by Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and even the U.S. State Department, to several international peace conferences, and finally the declarations of the Arab League and Islamic Conference.

Words, my friend, won’t feed you, clothe you, shelter you, give you an education, or a peaceful living. While we correctly blame Israel and America for your miserable fate, what is the culpability of your corrupt autocratic leadership who build rich mansions across from squalor refugee camps? Yes, Muhammad, Israel and the American Jewish community, with the support of Christian Fundamentalists, do control America’s foreign policy. How else would you explain $150 billion in American aid since 1949 to the 17th richest nation on earth? However, my friend, the Arab world has conveniently used Israel as an excuse for authoritative and undemocratic rule to cover its incompetence while enriching its leaders. The Arab world spends on average 7%-8% of its gross domestic product (GDP) on weapons while spending about 4% on education.

Israel refuses peace for ideological and expansionist reasons but just as likely peace could lead Israel to a civil war. While in the Arab world, peace may just lead to democracies and a window of opportunity for knowledge, prosperity and freedom while keeping our Islamic faith and tradition.

Muhammad, I pray that you and your generation do not repeat our mistakes, our passivity, our semi-attachment to Islam, our empty hyperbolic words and empty actions. I pray that you seek knowledge, truth and justice and hold firm to your faith. We have all failed you. Your only hope is to depend on Allah (SWT) and on yourself. Your pain will be heard and your tears will be wiped. No injustice ever endures. God bless you and keep you safe from the slings and arrows of outrageous fortunes. Palestine will be the 190th country to be admitted to the UN–an organization that is now “null and void.”

Beyond a declaration

Mustafa Al-Barghouti*

Two deadlines coincide on 13 September; the date set for the declaration of a Palestinian State, as well as for the end of the interim phase of the Oslo accord. The interim period was scheduled to end in May 1999, but was extended under the Sharm el-Sheikh agreement in the hope that the Israeli elections would succeed where six years of negotiations after Oslo had failed. The elections only complicated the situation further, however, giving rise to Barak’s hard-line strategy and his arrogant miscalculation of the Palestinians’ patience.

The significance of these twin deadlines should not be underestimated. Many Palestinian officials have begun to hint that the declaration of Statehood should be deferred once again. They avoid mentioning the fact that the end of the interim phase of the Oslo accords–the provisions of which are so prejudicial to the Palestinian people–happens to fall on the same day that was set for the declaration of Statehood. Those who advocate postponement base their arguments on the advice of the international community, fearful of a confrontation between Palestinians and Israelis. However, postponement also carries a price, in view of the damage it does to the credibility of the Palestinian Authority (PA).

But there are other reasons why this option should not be considered lightly. For the Palestinian Central Council (PCC), the question of whether and when to declare a State should be more than a tactical negotiating weapon. Statehood for the Palestinians is a sacred goal. Over 33 years, the struggle towards this end has come to form the lifeblood of the people, the pulse of their social and political institutions and the inspiration behind every project down to the schools and clinics in Palestinian villages, cities and camps. Statehood embodies the dream for independence and human dignity, for freedom from decades of tyranny and oppression under Israeli occupation, for the inalienable right to self-determination enjoyed by most other peoples on earth.

Only with Statehood will we be able to build a society unlike any that has existed in this region. The Palestinians will be able to prove to the world that Arabs, too, can meet the challenges of this age with dignity and honour, and create a model for a democratic developing nation that can be emulated elsewhere. An independent State is the only way to safeguard peace, which the Palestinian people have come to accept, despite the injustice of the fact that a State covering all the territories occupied since 1967 represents only 22% of the original land area of Palestine.

For these reasons, the PCC must absolve itself of all the obligations and restrictions under the interim agreement that prevent the establishment of a State and immediately begin to institutionalize the State on the ground. Practical measures, more than a mere declaration, will assert Palestine’s independence from Israel and from all provisions of the Oslo accords that restrict the assertion of Statehood. These measures entail putting the organic law into immediate effect, nullifying all restrictions on the legislative process, drawing up the national constitution and setting a date for its ratification, arranging for parliamentary elections, electing the members of the national council, setting a date for the municipal elections and beginning negotiations with neighbouring Arab nations over the definition of common borders.

Other measures will have both practical and symbolic importance. One is to revise the taxation structure and the value-added tax, and other such taboos under the Oslo accords. Another will be to remove the Israeli identity number from Palestinian passports and the humiliating term “travel document” from their covers. A third is for Palestinian authorities to issue building permits, particularly those that had been suspended, both within and outside “Area A,” which in turn would entail opening ministerial offices in Jerusalem. No one could accuse the Palestinians of seeking a military confrontation with Israel if we undertook the above-mentioned actions.

The Israelis must realize that the Palestinians have other options, including the calculated precipitation of a crisis, and that we are no longer the captives of the interim agreements. If we settle for anything less, Israeli opinion polls will remain the terms of reference of the peace process. In the same spirit, negotiations must be a tool to serve the struggle for our rights, rather than the struggle being a tool to serve the Palestinian negotiating strategy.

The meeting of the PCC presents the opportunity to liberate the Palestinians from the prejudicial terms of the Oslo accords. The world will sympathize with any constructive measures we take.

Israel must understand that the creation of a Palestinian State will not remain captive to the results of negotiations. Indeed, that stance should strengthen the Palestinian negotiating position. To drive this point home, every day from 14 September onward should bring new practical developments in the establishment of the independent Palestinian State, in everything from the promulgation of new laws to the construction of new roads.

The attention of the Palestinian leadership and all political forces must focus on the domestic front. All efforts must be made to encourage popular participation in the process of building the nation. Reforms are necessary to realize, among other things, the appropriate implementation of the Civil Service Law and the rule of law in general. It will also help when the people see that the leadership is actively engaged in the actual process of building their State, not just in negotiations over the declaration of Statehood.

Ten years ago, the PLO and the Palestinian national movement were the targets of a conspiracy to isolate and destroy them. Intensive popular action on the land of Palestine thwarted that conspiracy and brought the PLO leadership out of its isolation. Today, we have more powerful means at our disposal and more extensive international sympathy than were available when the defenseless Palestinian people rose to alter the course of history in December 1987. Still, let us remember that the path to victory begins with the determination to win.

• The writer is a Palestinian civil society activist

I want to return, therefore I am

Dr. Daud Abdullah*

Known to international police as a former leader of the terrorist Stern Gang, he became a Noble Peace Laureate after signing the Camp David Accords in September 1978. Hitherto, Menachem Begin was never recognized as a philosopher of sorts. Yet, writing in his book, The Revolt, he repackaged Descartes’ dictum ‘I think, therefore I am’ to illustrate the true nature and essence of the Zionist character. Hence his declaration ‘We fight, therefore we are.’

After narrowly escaping the massacre committed by Begin’s Gang in his own village, Balad el-Sheikh, in 1948, a disinherited Palestinian, Fawaz Turki, wrote movingly of the connection between the passion for Return and his own identity. Growing up in the camps of Beirut after the Great Nakba (Catastrophe), he recalled how the struggle for return had become the bedrock upon which Palestinian existence in exile was built. Thus he famously announced in 1977, ‘I want to return, therefore I am.’1

Having spent a quarter of a century in ghourba (exile), Turki concluded that it was virtually impossible for a Palestinian to be indifferent to what happens to his people. In his remarkable article entitled, “The future of a past”, he questioned how a Palestinian could claim he did not believe in the return. It would be sheer madness because the return in the Palestinian experience is more than territorial restitution and refugee repatriation. “It is, pure and simple, Palestinian selfhood.”

Two decades on, the gap between this vision of Palestinian selfhood and its actual fulfilment remains as wide as ever. Nothing emphasized this more convincingly than the recent Camp David summit which failed, as expected, to redress past wrongs and to give hope for the foreseeable future. Since the whole exercise was shrouded in a blanket of secrecy, one can only surmise that the fundamental cause for this fiasco had been America’s insistence that the Palestinians declare an end to the conflict, renounce their national rights to Jerusalem and repatriation, and accept less than 20% of historic Palestine with enclaves of armed Jewish settlers.

 Placing carts before the horses
In effect, the sole mediator of Middle East peace had in its supreme wisdom tabled a proposition that positioned the carts before the horses. A plan which not only calls for a closure of the conflict before the restoration of historic rights but worse still favours the occupier over the occupied. Toward this end, pro-Israeli think tanks and media machines have been cranked up to sell this latest package on offer. What is it? That Israel would share Jerusalem with the Palestinians while retaining sovereignty therein. It would recognize a “Palestinian law of return” to the West Bank and Gaza Strip but reserve the ‘right’ to decide who shall return to the Jewish State. And, similarly, it would support a compensation scheme instead of the right of return as enshrined in United Nations resolution 194.

Quite evidently, the Americans have decided to throw the full spotlight on Jerusalem, with all its complexity, history and religious passion. Given its now familiar mantra that both sides would have to make some compromises, Washington’s hope is that once some measure of recognition is granted to Palestinian aspirations in Jerusalem, it would be easier for the PA to compromise on the refugee issue. And so, the plan aspires to repatriate most of the refugees exiled in Lebanon to the West Bank and Gaza, naturalize those in Jordan and Syria, and permanently close the file thereafter. This is, to all intents and purposes, a settlement that seeks to substitute historic Palestine with a truncated mini-State in parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip and the Palestinian right of return with a “law of return” to the State of Palestine. The latter two, of course, are not the same.

Lurking dangers
No amount of gloss or spin would render this proposal palatable to the Palestinian people. True they demand Statehood and independence, but definitely not at the expense of other historic national rights. Whatever the hardships and pain, they are still not ready to dishonour the ultimate sacrifices made at Karameh, Tel el-Za’ater, and Sabra and Shatila. There are, evidently, huge costs and numerous dangers in imposing a settlement that ignores legitimacy and seeks to brush aside 52 years of struggle. One danger is that it would spawn a new wave of hatred, the type of which Fawaz Turki described:

“In the meantime I, a Palestinian from the refugee camps who acquired his past in exile, hate. I hate the world I come from. I hate what has been done to it. What has been done in it. I hate those who are doing the doing. I hate, above all, what is done in its name. Robbed of my right to be, like so many other Arabs, I am reduced to hating, which is my only control mechanism, the only hot iron that cauterizes the poison of my condition, the only channel through which I can siphon off my pain and anger.”

The issue at stake here is not, never was, and never will be one of local dissent against Jewish facts on the ground. The issue is about a people exercising their national rights in their ancestral land. The creation of a Palestinian State minus Jerusalem as its capital and the right of return is a sure recipe for disaster. Such a State that vaunts cosmetic political changes but preserves old economic, social and humanitarian injustices may easily go down the same violent path that South Africa has gone in the post-Apartheid era. In the West Bank and Gaza Strip where there is neither truth nor reconciliation, it would be difficult to imagine the depths of violence its society may descend into.

Although they never like to hear or admit it, policy makers in Washington are notorious for their dependency on an intelligence machinery that is incapable of analyzing political realities in global hot spots; Viet Nam and Cuba being two outstanding examples. Their unchecked presence in the West Bank and Gaza today is yet to confirm that lessons have been learnt from the past. Is the White House really unaware of the critical conditions and burgeoning arms trade in the refugee camps, or are they just hoping that the Palestinians would soon turn their guns on each other?! If it is true that the policy is to turn the revolutionary talent of the Intifadah veterans into the service of Israel’s “Russian mafia”, such a policy could easily backfire. Instead of enabling Israel and the West to realize their dream of a Mediterranean free trade area with an investment and consumer market of 700 million people, the policy may only ensure that many more years would be spent putting out the fires of wars and building bridges of trust, confidence and friendship.

Israel’s response to such dangers was summed up in characteristic arrogance by Prime Minister Barak after his return from Camp David. “It is time the Palestinians learned that not all their dreams are realizable.” Of course their realizations have become much more difficult after recognition was granted to the State of Israel before Palestinian national rights were restored. Their achievement has been further delayed after the Israelis violated every agreement signed in the last ten years, including Article IV of the Interim Agreement which recognizes the West Bank and Gaza Strip as “a single territorial unit.” Furthermore, as long as the free movement of Palestinians is impeded by Israeli military checkpoints, development would remain a pipedream of the self-deluded.

Toward Palestinian selfhood
Generally speaking, there is nothing wrong in making political errors. What is totally abhorrent though is the unwillingness to recognize errors, learn from them, and remedy their consequences. If the recent encounters at Camp David and its political fallout did not verify the inadequacy of America as an impartial and honest mediator, nothing will ever do so in the future. The clamour from every corner of the American political establishment to move their embassy to Jerusalem in open contravention of international law is enough to disqualify that country from supervising peace negotiations between Israel and the Palestinians. Surely if might is more important in adjudication than honesty and integrity then America should consider employing its Mafia bosses to sit as judges and jurors in its crime-infested cities.

In the aftermath of the second Camp David summit, one truth has emerged: that a new and radical approach is needed toward peace in the Middle East. The issues at stake here are far greater and much more important than the personal ambitions, needs and aspirations of any single individual, party or government. To put it another way, the history and future of a people cannot be bargained and sold as commodities on Wall Street. Moreover, the way one people perceive another is not always as important as how that people actually see themselves. For if the Americans and Jews still look down upon Palestinians in the same manner as Herzl, Weizmann and Jabotinsky did in the first half of the twentieth century, Palestinians certainly do not see themselves that way today.

Politicians influenced by such thoughts ask the Palestinians, why do you want to return to Israel? You would never be able to realize your ‘Palestinian-ness’ in Israel. They speak as if Israel existed before Palestine; denying the fact that Israel was carved out of and in Palestine. They ask, why would you want to come to a country where you would not feel a sense of belonging? As if the Palestinians are foreigners and the immigrants from Russia, the United States and Europe are the indigenous people. Is it not time, therefore, that someone told the Zionists and their supporters that the Palestinians are not demanding the right to return because they want to become Israeli citizens? When they think of return they think, above all, of the Palestine that was taken by force and illegally renamed Israel.

Even with a separate State in part or all of the West Bank and Gaza, the Palestinians who were evicted from Jerusalem, Haifa and Jaffa would still want to return to their homes. Their living and dying over the past 50 years were all for this. Few have expressed it as beautifully as Fawaz Turki:

“Yes. Yes. Yes, that is where I want to return. Where I want to be. At the gates of Jerusalem, Haifa and Jaffa. And when I knock on the door, I shall be there listing every blade of grass, every grain of sand, every floor, every shadow of every olive tree that I had left behind!”

American Zionism -- the real problem

Edward Said

In my opinion, the role of organized Zionist groups and activities in the United States has not been sufficiently addressed during the period of the "peace process," a neglect that I find absolutely astonishing, given that Palestinian policy has been essentially to throw our fate as a people in the lap of the United States without any strategic awareness of how US policy is in effect dominated, if not completely controlled, by a small minority of people whose views about Middle East peace are in some way more extreme than even those of the Israeli Likud. 

Let me give a small example. A month ago, the Israeli newspaper Ha'aretz sent over a leading columnist of theirs, Ari Shavit, to spend several days talking with me; a good summary of this long conversation appeared as a question-and-answer interview in the August 18 issue of the newspaper's supplement, basically uncut and uncensored. I voiced my views very candidly, with a major emphasis on right of return, the events of 1948, and Israel's responsibility for all this. I was surprised that my views were presented just as I voiced them, without the slightest editorializing by Shavit, whose questions were always courteous and un-confrontational. 

A week after the interview there was a response to it by Meron Benvenisti, ex-deputy mayor of Jerusalem under Teddy Kollek. It was disgustingly personal, full of insults and slander against me and my family. But he never denied that there was a Palestinian people, or that we were driven out in 1948. In fact he said, we conquered them, and why should we feel guilty? I responded to Benvenisti a week later in Ha'aretz: What I wrote was also published uncut. I reminded Israeli readers that
Benvenisti was responsible for the destruction (and probably knew about the killing of several Palestinians) of Haret Al-Magharibah in 1967, in which several hundred Palestinians lost their homes to Israeli bulldozers. But I did not have to remind Benvenisti or Ha'aretz readers that as a people we existed and could at least debate our right of return. That was taken for granted. 

Two points here. One is that the whole interview could not have appeared in any American paper, and certainly not in any Jewish-American journal. And if there had been an interview the questions to me would have been adversarial, hectoring, insulting, such as, why have you been involved in terrorism, why will you not recognize Israel, why was Hajj Amin a Nazi, and so on. Second, a right-wing Israeli Zionist like Benvenisti, no matter how much he may detest me or my views, would not deny that there is a Palestinian people which was forced to leave in 1948. An American Zionist for a long time would say that no conquest took place or, as Joan Peters alleged in a now-disappeared and all but forgotten 1984 book, From Time Immemorial (that won all the Jewish awards when it appeared here), there were no Palestinians with a life in Palestine before 1948. 

Every Israeli will readily admit and knows perfectly well that all of Israel was once Palestine, that (as Moshe Dayan said openly in 1976) every Israeli town or village once had an Arab name. And Benvenisti says openly that "we" conquered, and so what? Why should we feel guilty about winning? American Zionist discourse is never straight out honest that way: it must always go round and talk about making the desert bloom, and Israeli democracy, etc., completely avoiding the essential facts about 1948, which every Israeli has actually lived. For the American, these are mostly fantasies, or myths, not realities. So removed from the actualities are American supporters of Israel, so caught in the contradictions of diasporic guilt (after all what does it mean to be a Zionist and not emigrate to Israel?) and triumphalism as the most successful and most powerful minority in the US, that what emerges is very often a frightening mixture of vicarious violence against Arabs and a deep fear and hatred of them, which is the result, unlike Israeli Jews, of not having any sustained direct contact with them. 

For the American Zionist, therefore, Arabs are not real beings, but fantasies of nearly everything that can be demonized and despised, terrorism and anti-Semitism most specially. I recently received a letter from a former student of mine, who has had the benefit of the finest education available in the United States: he can still bring himself to ask me in all honesty and courtesy why as a Palestinian I let a Nazi like Hajj Amin still determine my political agenda. "Before Hajj Amin," he argued, "Jerusalem wasn't important to Arabs. Because he was so evil he made it an important issue for Arabs just in order to frustrate Zionist aspirations which always held Jerusalem to be important." This is not the logic of someone who has lived with and knows something concrete about Arabs. It is that of a person who speaks an organized discourse and is driven by an ideology that regards Arabs only as negative functions, as the embodiment of violent anti-Semitic violent passions. As such, therefore, they are to be fought against and if possible disposed of. Not for nothing was Dr Baruch Goldstein, the appalling murderer of 29 Palestinians who were quietly praying in the Hebron mosque, an American, as was Rabbi Meir Kahane. Far from being aberrations that have embarrassed their followers, both Kahane and Goldstein are revered today by others like them. Many of the most zealous far-right settlers sitting on Palestinian land, remorselessly speaking about "the land of Israel" as being theirs, hating and ignoring the Palestinian owners and residents all round them, are also American-born. To see them walking through the streets of Hebron as if the Arab city was entirely theirs is a frightening sight, aggravated by the defiance and contempt they display openly against the Arab majority.  

I bring all this up here to make one essential point. When after the Gulf War the PLO took the strategic decision -- already settled on by two major Arab countries before the PLO -- to work with the American government and if possible with the powerful lobby that controls discussion of Middle Eastern politics, they had made the decision (as had the two Arab states before them) on the basis of vast ignorance and quite extraordinarily mistaken assumptions.
The idea, as it was expressed to me shortly after 1967 by a senior Arab diplomat, was to surrender in effect, and say, we are not going to struggle any more. We are now willing to accept Israel and also to accept the US's determining role in our future. There were objective reasons for such a view at the time, as there are now, as to why continuing the fight as the Arabs had done historically would lead to further defeat and even disaster. But I firmly believe that it was a mistaken policy simply to throw Arab policy into the lap of the US and, since the major Zionist organizations are so influential everywhere in the United States, into their lap as well, saying, in effect, we won't fight you, let us join you, but please treat us well. The hope was that if we conceded and said, we are not your enemies, as Arabs we would become their friends. 

The problem is with the disparity in power that remained. From the viewpoint of the powerful, what difference does it make to your own strategy if your weak adversary gives up and says I have nothing further to fight for, take me, I want to be your ally, just try to understand me a bit better and then perhaps you will then be fairer? A good way of answering this question in practical and concrete terms is to look at the latest turn of events in New York's senatorial race, where Hillary Clinton is competing with Republican Ric Lazio for the seat now held by Daniel Patrick Moynihan (D), who is retiring. Last year Hillary said that she favored the establishment of a Palestinian state and, on a formal visit to Gaza with her husband, embraced Soha Arafat. Since entering the senatorial race in New Yorshe has outdone even the most right-wing Zionists in her fervour for Israel and opposition to Palestine, even going so far as to advocate moving the US embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem and (more extreme) advocating leniency for Jonathan Pollard, the Israeli spy convicted for espionage against the US and now serving a life sentence. Her Republican antagonists have tried to embarrass her by depicting her as an "Arab-lover" and by releasing a photograph of her actually embracing Soha. Since New York is the citadel of Zionist power, attacking someone with such labels as "Arab-lover" and "friend of Soha Arafat" is tantamount to the worst possible insult. All this despite the fact that Arafat and the PLO are openly declared American allies, recipients of US military and financial aid, and in the security field the beneficiaries of CIA security support. In the meantime, the White House released a photo of Lazio shaking hands two years ago with Arafat. One blow clearly deserves another. 

The real fact is that Zionist discourse is a discourse of power, and Arabs in that discourse are the objects of power -- despised objects at that. Having thrown in their lot with this power as its surrendered former antagonist, they can never expect to be on equal terms with it. Hence the degrading and insulting spectacle of Arafat (always and forever the symbol of enmity to the Zionist mind) being used in an entirely local contest in the US between two opponents who are trying to prove who of the two is the most pro-Israeli. And neither Hillary Clinton nor Ric Lazio is even Jewish. 

What I shall discuss in my next article is how the only possible political strategy for the US so far as Arab and Palestinian policy are concerned is neither a pact with the Zionists here nor one with US policy, but a mobilized mass campaign directed at the American population on behalf of Palestinian human, civil and political rights. All other arrangements, whether Oslo or Camp David, are doomed to failure because, put simply, the official discourse is totally dominated by Zionism and, except for a few individual exceptions, no alternatives to it exist. Therefore all peace arrangements undertaken on the basis of an alliance with the US are alliances that confirm rather than confront Zionist power. To submit supinely to a Zionist-controlled Middle East policy, as the Arabs have done for almost a generation now, will neither bring stability at home nor equality and justice in the US. 

Yet the irony is that there exists inside the US a vast body of opinion ready to be critical both of Israel and of US foreign policy. The tragedy is that the Arabs are too weak, too divided, too disorganized and ignorant to take advantage of it. I shall discuss the reasons for that as well in my next article since my hope is to try to reach a new generation that may be both puzzled and discouraged by the miserable, denigrated place in which our culture and people are now located, and the constant sense of indignant but humiliating loss that all of us experience as a result.

Why are we panicking?

The ongoing struggle of “Israeli Arabs”

Ramzy Baroud

With the rise of every new day’s sun, the Palestinian residents of Israel prove that their identity is not as divided as some may think, nor has their “integration” into the Israeli society left them with multiple allegiances and a sense of “loyalty schizophrenia”. It is not Israeli research and census bureau studies that determine the “Israeli Arabs’” stand on the political map of the Middle East, but it is the daily assurances of the one million Palestinians who have remained unmoved from historical Palestine which shapes and stubbornly refuses all attempts to undermine its struggle for recognition and equal civil rights.

The 1948 war and the establishment of an Israeli State over occupied Palestinian land resulted in much more than the expulsion of Palestinian refugees to the West Bank, Gaza Strip and neighbouring countries. It also brought about a tragic new reality to those Palestinians who remained. The establishment of Israel created refugees within historical Palestine, inflicted discrimination, military administration and an iron-fist policy.

In 1966, the Israeli military administration was abolished, and the remaining Palestinian communities within Israel were supposedly integrated into Israel’s economic, social and political arena. But in reality, such integration has failed to actualize, not only through statistics and numbers, but also through Israel’s perception of its Arab population and through this population’s perception of itself.

In the same way Israel’s government has long denied the existence of such a nation called Palestine, it has also denied the existence of a large segment of the Arab population within Israel, by denying the existence of their homes and villages. The 90,000 residents of 176 unrecognized Arab villages, also known as “phantom villages”, are a loud reminder of Israel’s disregard for even the most basic of human rights, even for those who are supposedly its own citizens. Those living in these phantom villages have no rights today, as they have never had for over the past five decades.

The rest of Israel’s Arab population escaped the tragic fate of those who belonged to these unrecognized villages, they too had their own “unrecognized” rights and privileges. It is difficult enough for “Israeli Arabs” to live in a country that was built on the ruins of their own land, but Israel’s insistence on its Jewishness and its routine persecution of Palestinians in the nearby West Bank and Gaza Strip was very poor incentive for “Israeli Arabs” to feel like citizens of Israel, with equal rights and equal responsibilities.

The Jewishness of Israel reflected direct discrimination against the mainly Muslim and Christian Arab population. Systematic Israeli discrimination resulted in a near complete confiscation of Arab Israelis’ land, under-funded public facilities, house demolitions, job and religious discrimination, and more.

Decades of living in Israel has not converted Palestinians into loyal Israelis. To the contrary, Israel’s oppression of its one million Arabs pushed “Israeli Arabs” to cling to who they really are: Palestinians.

With every Land Day (Israel’s Independence Day), or with any other occasion that symbolizes Palestinian pride or Israeli oppression, “Israeli Arabs” take to the streets, waving Palestinian flags, chanting in Arabic, and confirming their Arab identity. As angrily, brutally and inhumanly as Israeli soldiers crack down on protestors in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, the Israeli police interfere, to put down the protests of the “Israeli Arab” population in Nazareth or Kfar Kana.

The Palestinian tragedy remains unsolved. Indications of its continuation are highlighted in the slums of Gaza and desperate refugee camps in South Lebanon. We ought to remember, however, that the tragedy which first began in historical Palestine in 1948 never came to a close there either. It continues, intensifies and spreads. Houses in unrecognized Arab villages continue to be demolished. Their owners and the entire village protest with each demolition, and injured and beaten villagers fall in anger and despair. Dozens of “Arab Israelis” were wounded in the last Israeli Independence Day celebration, as is the case on almost every Independence Day; 70,000 “Israeli Arabs” gathered on 15 September to confirm their commitment to the Aqsa Mosque and to the Palestinian struggle as a whole.

Kfar Kasim elementary school students march to school where an Israeli flag hangs. Covering the walls inside the poorly funded school are 49 photos bearing the beautiful faces of 49 villagers from the village of Kfar Kasim who were savagely massacred by Israeli soldiers in 1956. The faces, the memories they carry, the killing of many more, the demolished houses, the unrecognized villages, the gathering of thousands in support of Palestinian rights in Jerusalem and many more great and simple signs scream with an undeniable truth: the “Israeli Arabs” are first and foremost part of the unfolding Palestinian story, the tragedy. “Israeli Arabs” are by no means living with a “divided identity”; they are simply Palestinians who, like all other Palestinians, were denied their identity.

Quest for Justice

Judith Stone

I am a Jew. I was a participant in the rally for the Right of Return to Palestine. It was the right thing to do. I’ve heard about the European holocaust against the Jews since I was a small child. I’ve visited the memorials in Washington, DC and Jerusalem dedicated to Jewish lives lost, and I’ve cried at the recognition to what level of atrocity mankind is capable of sinking.

Where are the Jews of conscience? No righteous malice can be held against the survivors of Hitler’s holocaust. These fragments of humanity were in no position to make choices beyond that of personal survival. We must not forget that being a survivor or a co-religionist of the victims of the European holocaust does not grant dispensation from abiding by the rules of humanity. “Never again” as a motto rings hollow when it means “never again to us alone.”

My generation was raised being led to believe that the biblical land was a vast desert inhabited by a handful of impoverished Palestinians living with their camels and eking out a living in the sand. The arrival of the Jews was touted as a tremendous benefit to these desert dwellers. Golda Mier even assured us that there “is no Palestinian problem.”

We know now this picture wasn’t as it was painted. Palestine was a land filled with people who called it home. There were thriving towns and villages, schools and hospitals. There were Jews, Christians and Muslims. In fact, prior to the occupation, Jews represented a mere 7% of the population and owned only 3% of the land.

Taking the blinders off for a moment, I see a second atrocity perpetuated by the very people who should be exquisitely sensitive to the suffering of others. These people knew what it felt like to be ordered out of their homes at gunpoint and forced to march into the night to unknown destinations or face execution on the spot. The people who displaced the Palestinians knew firsthand what it meant to watch your home go up in flames, to surrender everything dear to your heart at a moment’s notice. Bulldozers leveled hundreds of villages, along with the remains of the village inhabitants, the old and the young. This was nothing new to the world.

Poland is a vast graveyard of the Jews of Europe. Israel is the final resting place of the massacred Palestinian people. A short distance from the memorial to the Jewish children lost to the holocaust in Europe, there is a leveled parking lot. Under this parking lot is what’s left of a once-flourishing village and the bodies of men, women and children whose only crime was taking up needed space and not leaving graciously. This particular burial marker reads: Public Parking.

I’ve talked with Palestinians. I have yet to meet a Palestinian who hasn’t lost a member of their family to the Israeli Shoah, nor a Palestinian who cannot name a relative or friend languishing under inhumane conditions in an Israeli prison. Time and time again, Israel is cited for human rights violations to no avail. On a recent trip to Israel, I visited the refugee camps inhabited by a people who have waited 52 years in these “temporary” camps to go home. Every Palestinian grandparent can tell you the name of their village, their street and where the olive trees were planted. Their grandchildren may never have been home, but they can tell you where their great-grandfather lies buried and where the village well stood.

The press has fostered the portrait of the Palestinian terrorist. But, the victims who rose up against human indignity in the Warsaw Ghetto are called heroes. Those who lost their lives are called martyrs. The Palestinian who tosses a rock in desperation is a terrorist.

Two years ago I drove through Palestine and watched intricate sprinkler systems watering lush green lawns of Zionist settlers in their new condominium complexes, surrounded by armed guards and barbed wire in the midst of a Palestinian community where there was not adequate water to drink and the surrounding fields were sandy and dry. University professor Moshe Zimmerman reported in the Jerusalem Post (30 April 1995), “The [Jewish] children of Hebron are just like Hitler’s youth.”

We Jews are suing for restitution, lost wages, compensation for homes, land, slave labour and back wages in Europe. Am I a traitor of a Jew for supporting the right of return of the Palestinian refugees to their birthplace and compensation for what was taken that cannot be returned? The Jewish dead cannot be brought back to life and neither can the Palestinian massacred be resurrected.

David Ben Gurion said, “Let us not ignore the truth among ourselves...politically, we are the aggressors and they defend themselves...The country is theirs, because they inhabit it, whereas we want to come here and settle down, and in their view we want to take away from them their country...”

Palestine is a land that has been occupied and emptied of its people. It’s cultural and physical landmarks have been obliterated and replaced by tidy Hebrew signs. The history of a people was the first thing eradicated by the occupiers. The history of the indigenous people (only 52 years old) has been all but eradicated as though they never existed. And all this has been hailed by the world as a miraculous act of God.

We must recognize that Israel’s existence is not even a question of legality so much as it is an illegal fait accompli realized through the use of force while supported by the Western powers. The United Nations missions directed at Israel in attempting to correct its violations have thus far been futile.

In Hertzl’s “The Jewish State”, the father of Zionism said, “...We must investigate and take possession of the new Jewish country by means of every modern expedient.”

I guess I agree with Ehud Barak (3 June 1998) when he said, “If I were a Palestinian, I’d also join a terrorist group.” I’d go a step further, perhaps. Rather than throwing little stones in desperation, I’d hurl a boulder.

Hopefully, somewhere deep inside, every Jew of conscience knows that this was no war; that this was not God’s restitution of the holy land to it’s rightful owners. We know that a human atrocity was and continues to be perpetuated against an innocent people who couldn’t come up with the arms and money to defend themselves against the Western powers bent upon their demise as a people.

We cannot continue to say, “But what were we to do?” Zionism is not synonymous with Judaism. I wholly support the rally of the Right of Return of the Palestinian people.

The Issue of Jerusalem

E. Yaghi

That less than 50% of Palestinian land is left to the Palestinian people seems not to be an issue with those who have been condemning Palestinian President Yasser Arafat’s reluctance to cede any more of East Jerusalem to the Israelis.
