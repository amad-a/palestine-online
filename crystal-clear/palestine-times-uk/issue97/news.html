Tens of Thousands turn out for Hamas rally in Nablus

Nablus (Occupied West Bank) – From Khalid Amayreh

Belying PA and Israeli allegations that support for Hamas was plummeting, over 50,000 Palestinians converged at Nablus Friday, 25 June, to mark the annual anniversary of the Prophet Muhammed's birthday.

The bulk of the huge multitude were young Palestinians from the city itself and surrounding rural areas, which shows that Palestinian youngsters are, for the most part, still looking to Hamas for the future.

The thousands of Islamist young men and women shouted in unison "God is Great", "Jerusalem is ours forever" and "Hamas! We are your soldiers."

Huge portraits of Islamic Holy Places and verses from the Holy Qur’an, emblazoned in beautiful calligraphy, decorated the site of the rally.

Several speakers saluted the huge rally, including Allama (intellectual authority) Sheikh Yousef al-Qaradawi, who spoke via telephone from Qatar.

Qaradawi called on all Palestinians to stand united in the face of their enemy in defense of their homeland and Holy Places.

"I urge all the sons and daughters of Palestine to stand united in the face of the common enemy," he said.

He added, "Whether you are members of the police force or ordinary citizens, you are all believers in God, and must stand united in this confrontation against this sinister enemy that thrives on our division and disunity."

Qaradawi urged Muslims worldwide to pay more attention to the message of the Prophet and to seek to translate it in their daily life and in all aspects of their societies.

"We are not only celebrating the birth of the Messenger; we are celebrating the birth of the message, so let us get incentive from this occasion to increase our awareness of the message and the Messenger."

Hamas leaders, for their part, urged the Palestinian Authority to take real, sincere and earnest action to cement national unity among Palestinians.

"It is never enough to wish for national unity; it is never enough to speak about it; you must, we must, work for it and sacrifice for it," said Jamal Salim, a prominent Islamist leader in the Nablus area.

Salim said Hamas was always extending its hands to the Palestinian Authority for national reconciliation.

Hamas, he said, always placed national interests above all considerations.

"We will not allow schism to divide us, and we will not allow Palestinian blood to be shed, and we will continue to be the safety valve for our people and our cause."

Salim also pointed out to the "creeping cancer of the settlements," saying, "We will not succeed in checking the growth of this malignancy if we don't unify our ranks and make available to us all alternatives."

Finally, Hamas lauded the recent agreement in Algeria between the FIS and the Algerian government.

"We give our blessing for national reconciliation in Algeria and we express our solidarity with the Islamist movement in Algeria and the Algerian President and urge them to open a new chapter that would stop bloodshed in the sisterly country."

Sheikh Hamed al-Beitawi, the grand Mufti of Nablus, and Head of the Palestine Ulema Association, warned against "giving up even one iota of Palestine."

"This is an Islamic land with a decision from God…this is the sacred land to which the Prophet made his journey by night; this is al-Aqsa Mosque; this is the first Kibla…and the third of the holiest shrines in all of Islam."

Other Islamist speakers called on the PA to uphold the rule of law and guarantee peoples' rights and civil liberties.

Several rallies were due to take place throughout the West Bank and Gaza Strip in commemoration of the solemn occasion, including one at al-Aqsa Mosque in East Jerusalem.

PA Attacks NGOs

Occupied Jerusalem- Special to PT

The Palestinian Authority has launched a smear campaign against non-government organizations (NGOs) operating in the West Bank and Gaza Strip, accusing the heads of these bodies of theft and corruption as well as besmirching the image of "our people's struggle."

PA justice minister Freih Abu Maddin, who spearheaded the campaign, branded NGOs as “a bunch of thieves”, “fat cats" and "a band of foreign agents who are working against Palestinian national interests."

"These organizations are serving suspicious goals and their overall role in our country is by no means constructive," said Abu Maddin.

Abu Maddin told the PA's Voice of Palestine (a mouthpiece for Chairman Yasser Arafat) that NGOs, particularly the so-called “human rights organizations”, were only acting at their masters' and financiers' beck and call.

He claimed some NGO directors receive a monthly salary reaching as high as $10,000, a sum, he said, equals the salaries of ten judges combined.

The same charges were parroted several times by PA printed and electronic media, which charged that NGOs were playing a negative role in the march of the Palestinian struggle and were purposefully distorting the image of the Palestinian Authority.

These charges, including allegations of financial irregularities, were vehemently denied by Iyad al Sarraj, a prominent human rights activist in the Gaza Strip who accused the PA of "launching a discrimination attack on NGOs and human rights organizations for political reasons."

Sarraj argued that the PA, particularly the Justice Minister, was seeking to punish and discredit human rights organizations for their role "in exposing the many flaws and violations of human rights and the rule of law under the PA."

He challenged Abu Maddin to name the organization whose director receives a monthly salary of $10,000.

"We reject this indiscriminate vilification of NGOs. If the PA has evidence of any financial irregularities or wrong-doing against a given organization, it should name the villain organization," Sarraj told the Palestinian daily al Quds on 13 June.

Basem Eid, Head of the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, welcomed any sincere efforts by the PA to make sure that NGO operations are in full conformity with the law.

In return, however, Eid demanded that the PA "put its house in order" and "open its doors to human rights organizations if indeed it has nothing to hide."

"NGOs have nothing to hide, and the PA should have nothing to hide as well, especially concerning human rights and the upholding of the rule of law."

The vitriolic attack came two days after Agance France Presse (AFP) circulated a report from Gaza, describing the PA justice system as "primitive" and suggesting that PA officials squandered $100 million that had been earmarked for rebuilding the Palestinian system.

The report quoted the Special UN Coordinator in the Gaza Strip as saying that there is still no real separation of powers in the PA regime and that a thorough reconstruction of the PA justice system was needed in order to meet requirements for a modern civil society.

Campaign against NGOs politically motivated

The PA campaign against NGOs seems to be first and foremost politically motivated.

This is evident from the fact that the ten or so human rights organizations were singled out from more than 1,000 NGOs currently operating within the autonomous enclaves and covering a whole range of activities from agriculture to public health.

Indeed, the largest NGO in the occupied territory is the Palestinian Red Crescent Society, headed by Fat’hi Arafat, PA President Yasser Arafat's brother.

The finances of the Red Crescent never truly came under scrutiny despite widespread charges of theft, embezzlement and misspending.

The reason for that is, of course, Arafat's proximity to Arafat.

The PA, which is in itself infested with corruption, nepotism and theft, in addition to embezzlements amounting to hundreds of millions of dollars, ostensibly aims to discredit human rights bodies following the adage "offense is the best defense."

Indeed, in the absence of a free press and a competent judicial authority, human rights organizations become the premier watchdog monitoring the Palestinian Authority.

It is true that the PA often finds such a watchdog annoying� even disturbing� hence the interest to vilify the NGOs, with the apparent intent to discredit human rights organizations, particularly the more outspoken amongst them.

Moreover, the campaign comes amidst persistent demands from human rights organizations for the publication of the Palestinian General Comptroller's Report.

According to reliable Palestinian sources, Arafat has suppressed the publication of the report, arguing that it is politically damaging to release the report at this time.

Two years ago, a report issued by the General Comptroller' s Committee revealed the disappearance of over $300 million, nearly a third of the entire PA budget, eventually forcing the PA cabinet to resign.

However, the discredited ministers, whose names the report mentioned in connection with the scandal, were reinstated or promoted by Yasser Arafat in a new cabinet reshuffle a few months later.

Amnesty says PA and Israel torture
detainees, violate human rights

Occupied Jerusalem - The London-based Amnesty International has accused Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority and the Zionist regime of using torture on a wide scale and of grossly violating human rights.

In both cases, Palestinians are the victims of these violations.

In a report covering the year 1998 and published last month, Amnesty International said the Zionist regime continued to detain Palestinians without charge or trial for prolonged periods of time.

Moreover, Amnesty pointed out that Palestinian suspects were given lengthy prison sentences based on confessions extracted from them through torture. "Torture and ill-treatment continue to be officially sanctioned and used systematically during interrogation of detainees," the report said.

Finally, the Amnesty report pointed out that Israeli security forces killed 20 Palestinians in vague circumstances over the course of 1998.

The report also castigated the PA for employing torture against political detainees, which resulted in three deaths in 1998. The report blamed the PA for hundreds of arrests on political grounds, detentions without charges or trials, grossly unfair trials by State security courts, widespread torture of detainees, and the carrying out of death sentences on three people.

PA chairman Yasser Arafat often justifies the gross violations of his people's human rights and civil liberties by his security forces on the grounds that Israel and the United States are not interested in seeing true democracy and rule of law prevail in the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

Meanwhile, the printed and electronic media operating within the Palestinian autonomous enclaves have chosen to ignore the scathing criticisms of PA human rights records by "Amnesty International," and instead focused on the human rights organization's criticisms of the Zionist regime.

The three Palestinian dailies, al-Quds, Alayyam, and al-Hayatul Jadida featured prominently Amnesty's charges that Israel extracts confessions from Palestinian detainees under torture and uses these confessions as the main evidence in disingenuous trials.

The Zionist regime employs a variety of torture techniques, including hooding, beating, electric shocks, sleep deprivation, as well as the notorious shaking technique which causes brain dislocation and hemorrhage.

The law in the Zionist regime sanctions torture (physical and psychological).

The premier torture means employed by the Palestinian Authority is beating on the head.

Three Palestinian inmates died of torture in PA jails in 1998.

Meanwhile, Palestinian prisoners in Israeli jails and political detainees in PA jails intend to go on an open-ended hunger strike to protest their continued detention, Palestinian sources said.
The sources said the prisoners and detainees would probably start their hunger strike by the first of July.
The prisoners and detainees, of whom Islamists make up the vast majority, accuse the PA and the Zionist regime of reneging on earlier promises to release them.
The prisoners went on a protracted hunger strike last year to pressure Israel and the PA to release them.
The PA promised the detainees that they would be released and their files would be studied and re-evaluated.
However, very few prisoners were released and the detainees, most of whom haven't been accused of any wrong doing, accused the PA of deceiving them.
The PA incarcerates over three hundred Hamas and Islamic Jihad activists, mainly to appease Israeli security whims and to prove to the United States that it is fighting terrorism.

Speakers at "Palestine at the Crossroads"
conference lash out at Yasser Arafat

Occupied Jerusalem (24 June) - Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority (PA) received some of the severest criticisms ever during the three-day conference entitled, "Palestine at the Crossroads", organized by the Arab Though Forum at Beir Zeit University from 21-23 June.

A multitude of academics, intellectuals, experts and politicians, including PA officials, took part in the symposium.

Papers presented during the conference and speeches by guests covered a wide array of subjects, including diagnoses of the present state of the peace process, the election of Ehud Barak as Israel's Prime Minister, the expansion of Jewish settlements, the state of the Palestinian economy, the future of the Oslo process, Palestinian Statehood and a critique of the Palestinian PA administration.

The strongest criticisms came from Salim al-Za’anoun, who heads the Palestinian National Council (PNC). He lashed out at the current PA leadership for its corruption, incompetence, autocracy and despotism during the plenary session of the conference.

Za’anoun, an ally of Yasser Arafat but a reluctant supporter of the peace process, pointed out that the justice system within the self-rule areas was utterly underdeveloped and even primitive, adding that its overall shape was much worse than it had been 40 years ago when the Gaza Strip was under the Egyptian administration.

Za'anoun pointed out that Palestinian courts and jurists were being harassed by some junior officers in the PA security agencies, adding that in contrast a single judge of the Israeli High Court was able to prevent the Israeli Prime Minister from closing down Orient House a few weeks ago.

"I am ashamed of what is going on in our justice system," said Za'anoun, with bitterness clear in the tone of his voice.

He wished well for the newly appointed Attorney-General Zuheir Surani and President of the High Court Radwan al Agha saying, "I hope you will not be afraid; I also hope they will not face what their predecessors had faced."

(The former A-G Fayez Abu Rahma and former High Court President Qusay Abadleh resigned a year and a half ago after the PA executive authority refused to carry out court decisions to release political prisoners who had been incarcerated for prolonged periods without charge or trial.)

Finally, Za'anoun castigated the PA for ignoring the decisions and recommendations of the Legislative Council (PLC) and the Palestine National Council (PNC), saying the PLC made tremendous efforts in enacting new laws and legislation, and preparing the Basic Law, only to be ignored by the executive authority.

"When the PLC prepared the Basic Law, the executive authority refused it arguing that only the PNC and Central Council were qualified to promulgate a draft of the Basic Law. However, when the two councils did just that, the executive authority said nobody except the Arab league could be entrusted with preparing the Basic Law."

He warned that unless the PA put its own house in order, the Palestinians wouldn't deserve a State and any amount of foreign and American aid wouldn't benefit the Palestinian cause.

Ahmed Qrei', speaker of the PLC, refrained from voicing direct criticisms of Arafat. However, he warned that reverting to "business as usual" with the Zionists while the expansion of Jewish settlements continued unabated and at a frantic pace was a stupid and disastrous policy.

"There is no way Palestinians can return to the negotiations table with Israel while the Jewish bulldozer is pulverizing our land," he said.

Never the less, it is all well-known that Qrei' and his PLC don't make policy in the Palestinian Authority, hence his views to that effect shouldn't be treated as much more than public recommendations to Arafat, the sole decision maker in the PA.

Ziad Abu Amre, a political science professor and a Legislative Council member, accused Arafat's PA of killing Arafat's PLO.

"The leadership that signed the Oslo Accords," he said, "did so in order to save the PLO from extinction and to put an end to attempts to create an alternative leadership."

He added that "paradoxically, as we see it manifestly clear, it is the PA that is killing, replacing and corroding the PLO."

"The PLO," he argued, "is in a state of gradual death and there is no way it can be revived since it achieved none of the goals it was created to achieve…it failed its raison d'�tre."

On the economic level, most of the economic experts who took part in the conference agreed that it was virtually impossible to develop a meaningful and prosperous Palestinian national economy as long as "everything remained in the hands of the Israeli occupation."

Hisham Oratani, Professor of Economics at the Najah University in Nablus, called on Palestinians to have "a moment of accountability with themselves" concerning the economic situations in the Occupied Territories.

He pointed out that Israel was still in control of the Palestinian economy in the West Bank and Gaza Strip by virtue of controlling border crossings, taxes, the labour market and all natural resources, including the land itself.

This economic subservience, he said, couldn't and wouldn't produce political independence, adding that the Palestinian economic future looked very bleak, indeed.

The PA minister of Commerce and Industry, Maher Al Masri, agreed with Oratani.

He imputed the current economic anarchy in the self-rule areas to the fact that the Palestinians were in effect under the Israeli occupation.

"As long as we don't have control over the land, there is no way the Palestinian Authority can achieve real development."

He further predicted that it was becoming increasingly difficult to rectify economic conditions in the occupied territories because the Palestinian issue "has reached the realm of the unknown since the expiry of the interim agreement."

On the third and final day of the conference, some speakers revisited some of the topics raised in the plenary session, particularly those pertaining to the absence of the rule of law under the PA and the failure of Yasser Arafat to bring about a genuine separation of powers in the autonomous enclaves.

Azmi al-Shueibi, a PLC member, demanded that "everybody, including Arafat himself and the security agency, be under the law not above it."

"Without fixing our justice system, nothing will work," he said.

The conference issued a final statement summing up its works, calling on the PA to respect the sovereignty of the law, justice, accountability, transparency and sound public administration, saying without these, there could be no meaningful Palestinian State.

The statement also demanded that there should be certain mechanisms guaranteeing "our people's right to exercise their inalienable rights on their national soil to back up UN resolutions supporting these rights.

The conference was another resonant vote of no-confidence in Yasser Arafat's Authority at a time when the Palestinian public opinion began seriously drifting away from the PA leadership and demanding political reform and genuine democracy.

PA justice system in disarray

Occupied Jerusalem- From Khalid Amayreh

Palestinian Authority President Yasser Arafat has apparently taken some promising steps toward the restoration of the rule of law within the autonomous enclaves.

This week, Arafat appointed Zuheir Surani, a veteran Gazan jurist and lawyer of reportedly impeccable credentials, a new Attorney-General.

Surani, 63, succeeds Fayez Abu Rahma, who resigned from the Attorney-General office he held a year and a half ago in protest against "undue interference" in the administration of justice by the executive authority and the refusal of PA security agencies to carry out court orders.

The appointment of Surani at the helm of the Palestinian judicial system coincided with the appointment last week of Radwan al-Agha as Head of the PA High Court, a post that had also been vacant for over 16 months due to the resignation of the former chief judge Qusay al-Abadleh, virtually for the same reasons cited above.

The Palestinian jurist community and human rights organizations have generally lauded the two appointments as "a step in the right direction."

Raji Surani (a cousin of the new A-G) said he hoped the two appointments would deliver the Palestinian justice system from "the catastrophic conditions" it is undergoing now.

However, Surani warned that the new A-G and Chief Judge would eventually meet the same fate as that of their predecessors if the executive leadership, e.g. Arafat, refuses to come to terms with the fact that the law is above everybody, including Arafat himself and his generally undisciplined security agencies.

"I am not sure if the new Attorney-General has received assurances from Arafat that he will be empowered to establish the rule of law," said Raji Surani of the Palestinian Independent Commission on Human Rights, adding with a skeptical whiff in his voice that "if he hasn't, then he won't make much of a difference." The new A-G and the Chief Judge will face a justice system that is on the verge of collapse.

In mid-May, the Palestinian Bar Association (PBA) asked its members to refrain from litigation for three days in order to show their displeasure with the "judicial paralysis," currently prevalent in the self-rule areas.

In one instance, the single judge assigned to serve over 120,000 citizens in the city of Jenin in the northern West Bank had to shut down his office, telling a multitude of awaiting litigants to go home "because the court is closed until further notice."

Earlier, the head of the Palestinian Bar Association, Abdul Rahim Abu al-Naser, appealed to Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat to take meaningful steps to save the entire justice system from complete collapse.

"We are sending out an outcry to the officials to save the justice system," said Naser in statements published in leading Palestinian newspapers.

Among the flaws cited by Naser were the permeation of lawlessness in society, the vacancy of the Attorney-General’s Office for the second consecutive year and the utter indifference with which the security apparatus view court rulings.

However, what Naser omitted to say was the fact that there was simply no "Palestinian law" in operation in the autonomous enclaves since the so-called Palestinian laws currently functioning in the West Bank and Gaza Strip are in fact a confluence of Ottoman, British, Jordanian and Egyptian laws, in addition to the hundreds of Israeli military orders, issued over the course of the past 30 years solely to serve the interests of the Israeli occupation.

Needless to say, much of this legislation is no longer relevant and should be defunct, since it was introduced to fit circumstances that are no longer pertinent.

Numerous obstacles to administration of justice
Numerous manifestations underscore the judicial disarray, ranging from understaffed courts and under-trained lawyers to widespread interference in the administration of justice by the 15 or so security agencies answerable to PA President Yasser Arafat.

According to Rani Abdo, a Palestinian lawyer from Jenin, the security agencies often act as a parallel "judicial authority," effectively undermining the credibility of the courts and seriously corroding public trust in the entire justice system.

"Many of the security chiefs don't understand the concept that the authority of law is paramount and that nobody should be above the rule of law."

Another lawyer, Saber al-Tawil, pointed out that "the PA leadership is not interested and certainly not serious about upholding the rule of law, since this would seemingly be incompatible with the parochial interests of some influential operatives in the executive authority."

Tawil warned that if the present situation continued, the justice system would collapse completely, and the Palestinian society would drift menacingly to anarchy and chaos, eventually bringing about the downfall and collapse of the Palestinian Authority itself.

He pointed out that a growing number of Palestinians are abandoning the courts in favour of other avenues, such as the traditional tribal forums where "justice" is quick "and without all these complications."

Lawyers’ diagnoses of the justice system are by no means an overstatement.

In fact, the PA justice minister, Freih Abu Maddin, used very strong terms to describe the dire state of affairs, saying the justice system was undergoing a "nakba" or catastrophe.

The term "nakba" is normally reserved for describing the loss of Palestine to Zionist Jews in 1948 and the consequent dispersion of Palestinians throughout the region and the world.

Arafat: Law of the Land
Given that Arafat holds all the reins, controls all the money and takes all the decisions, the chairman effectively is the law of the land in the guise of the Palestinian Authority, either directly by issuing all sorts of "presidential decrees" or indirectly by acquiescing to the mainly unlawful activities and behaviour of his senior and junior security chiefs.

In part, Arafat's "judicial autocracy" stems from the utter contradiction and incompatibility between the need to meet the "security commitments" he took it upon himself to carry out under the Oslo Accords, and later under the more scandalous Wye River Memorandum on the one hand, and the requirements for building a modern society where people's rights and liberties ought to be upheld irrespective of any other considerations.

Needless to say, these fetters and crippling restrictions explain why political detainees and prisoners of conscience are locked up in PA jails for periods ranging from six months to four years (roughly the same period of time other Palestinian "administrative detainees" would serve in an Israeli jail, virtually for the same suspicious charges, e.g. active political opposition to the Oslo process.

It must be made clear that the matter goes beyond planning and carrying out acts of violence against Israeli targets to include "all acts of incitement" against the Israeli occupation authorities. And, in the final analysis, it is the Israelis who have the right to determine what constitutes "incitement" and decide whether a given incitement is "harmful", e.g. when it is carried out by Hamas, or "innocuous," or "tolerable" when it is carried out by Fatah. In the former case, Arafat is expected, in accordance with the agreements and other secret and tacit understandings, to lock up the practitioners of the harmful incitement, such as Abdul Aziz al-Rantisi, who has been locked up in a PA jail for over 16 months, without charge or trial, simply because "his detention is necessitated by the agreements."

Does this mean that Arafat is acting at Israel's beck and call? The continued detention of hundreds of political activists (without charge or trial) suggests he is.

Never the less, the security agreements with Israel can't be blamed for all PA actions and behaviour that contravene the letter and spirit of the existing laws.

For example, a recent case in Gaza, where a citizen of the Jabalya refugee camp, Ayman Amasi, nearly lost his life as a result of the torture to which he was subjected at a Gaza police lockup, sheds some light on the lawlessness permeating throughout the PA enclaves.

Amasi was arrested on 15 May by the Gaza Criminal Investigation Squad in connection with a theft and rape crime he had allegedly committed six months earlier while working in the Israeli Arab village of Tira.

Amasi, who had been completely acquitted by an Israeli court after genetic and blood tests found no connection between him and the crime alleged, was beaten mercilessly on the head, resulting in a brain hemorrhage from which he is still recuperating at Gaza's Shifa hospital.

But why would the Gaza police re-arrest a Gazan citizen on charges and crimes of which he was acquitted by an Israeli court?

According to Basem Eid, Director of the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group, Abdul Wahab Darawshe, a former Arab Knesset member, had telephoned his friend Yasser Arafat and requested that Amasi be arrested and punished for "his crime" at Tira.

Arafat immediately acted on Darawshe's call, ordering his Gaza police chief Gazai Jabali to apprehend and punish Amasi for the crime of which he was supposed to be innocent.

Still worse, when Amasi maintained that he was innocent and his police "torturers/interrogators could not extract any confessions from him, they offered to let him free in return for 5,000 Jordanian dinars, roughly US$ 7,000.

However, when Amasi told the story to his visiting brother, which eventually found its way to the media, the torturer got very furious with him.

That fury found expression in a savage beating to all parts of Amasi’s body, especially the head, rendering him unconscious.

After Amasi was transferred to hospital half-dead, Jabali claimed in a statement to the press that he had tried to commit suicide, a claim vehemently denied by Amasi's family and human rights activists.

One police officer at Jabali's office, who spoke off the record, commented half-seriously that "Amasi was punished enough for his crime, and that should satisfy Darawshe."

Amasi was released from the Shifa hospital on 1 June.

He is now appealing to international human rights organizations as well as all "those to whom justice means something" to help him receive justice.

The Amasi story didn't end right there. Ghazi Hamed, Editor-in-chief of the Gaza-based Islamist weekly, al Risala, was arrested last month for reporting on the case and publishing testimonies, including an affidavit from Amasi's Israeli lawyer, refuting Jabali's "assertions" that Amasi committed rape and theft in Tira.

When asked by this correspondent why he ordered the arrest of Hamed, Jabali said Hamed "was caught red-handed while he was committing a crime."

Asked what his crime was, Jabali said, "He was caught falsifying facts, disseminating lies and doubts about the credibility of the Palestinian justice system as well as trying to protect a hideous criminal."

PA official: the Occupied Territories
are a farm for the Israeli economy

Bassam al Shaka'a, a member of the PLO executive committee and mayor of the West Bank town of Nablus, argued that the Palestinian economy is an economy based on providing certain services.

He added that without having control over the borders, the Palestinian economy would remain "a farm for the Israeli economy."
In 1994, the PA signed the "Paris Agreement" with Israel, which many experts say, consolidated Israeli economic control of the Palestinians.

Poll: 71% of Palestinians believe PA is corrupt

Occupied Jerusalem - An opinion poll conducted last month by the Nablus-based Palestinian Studies and Research Center found that the bulk of Palestinians believe the PA is corrupt.

The results of the poll showed that up to 71% of Palestinians in the autonomous enclaves believe that there is widespread corruption throughout PA institutions; 66% said the corruption would stay and even increase in the future; and 76% of the respondents said they believed the chief means of getting a job was through favouritism, nepotism and bribery.

On PA and democracy, only 27 % of respondents said they believed the PA was democratic.

The poll also showed that 75% of the Palestinians believed they couldn't freely criticize the PA.

Only 20% of the respondents said there was "press freedom" under the Palestinian Authority.

Palestinian detainee loses memory as a result of torture

Occupied Jerusalem - Palestinian sources said Amjad Yousuf Ibrewish, 25, lost his memory after he was subjected to severe physical and psychological torture at the hands of Israeli interrogators.

The sources said Ibrewish, who is from the town of Dura, 10 miles south-west of Hebron, was arrested by the Israeli army four weeks ago on suspicion of affiliation with Hamas.

Ibrewish reportedly was placed in solitary confinement and subjected to a variety of torture techniques including hooding, shaking, sleep deprivation and beating.

These forms of torture resulted in the death of several Palestinian detainees in the past. Israeli interrogators routinely employ torture against Palestinians arrested on "security charges."

The Zionist regime is the only country in the world that sanctions by law the use of torture against political detainees.

Israeli army Rabbi says Jews must
avoid rescuing Arabs on Saturdays

Occupied Jerusalem - The Chief Rabbi of the Israeli army has reportedly endorsed an edict by a junior army rabbi in which he ruled that it was inadmissible for Jews to "rescue or give first aid to non-Jews on Saturdays."

The chief rabbi reportedly said the edict was perfectly in harmony with the essence of the Jewish Halacka or Law. The junior rabbi said that Jews are enjoined not to seek to rescue or help non-Jews on weekdays, adding that doing so on Saturday would be a greater sin.

The junior rabbi, Avijai Rotinsky, argued that "there is no room for human feelings here, because one must allow for an absolute separation between the rules of Halacka and one's feelings."

Hamas wins in Gaza Teachers' election

Occupied Jerusalem - Supporters of the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) emerged the winners in Tuesday's Teachers' Union election in the Gaza Strip, sources in Gaza said.

The Islamic bloc, which represents Islamist forces in the Gaza Strip, reportedly controlled the executive council of the UNRWA Teachers Union, receiving the lion's share of seats in the Union's three sectors: the teachers sector, the services sector and the workers sector.

The Islamist electoral victory comes only two weeks after Hamas' loss in the Engineering Union's election in the Gaza Strip, which observers attributed to "professional" rather than “political” considerations.

Hamas is the chief political and ideological rival of the PLO headed by Yasser Arafat.

The movement has come under unrelenting pressure from Israeli and PA security forces, forcing the movement to significantly reduce the volume of its armed struggle against the Zionist occupiers.

However, observers in Palestine believe Hamas could resume attacks against targets in the Zionist regime at any time, depending on the political situation in the Occupied Territories and the region.

Occupied Jerusalem - Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat has reportedly decided to withhold the release of the General Comptroller's report, apparently because it contains scathing criticism for his administration.

Palestinian sources close to the PA Legislative Council intimated recently that Arafat instructed the Council's sub-committee responsible for preparing the report not to release it to the media.

The sources did not give any reasons for this step, but one councillor confided that the report revealed widespread theft and corruption throughout the PA administration. Two years ago, a similar report forced Arafat to reshuffle his cabinet under public pressure.
