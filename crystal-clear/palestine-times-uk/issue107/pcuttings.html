Press Cuttings

The Times (UK), 28 April 2000

Ehud Barak appears ready to make an historic breakthrough in the Middle East peace process by taking a first step towards officially recognising a Palestinian state.

The Israeli Prime Minister is reportedly preparing a resolution for his Cabinet which would offer the significant concession as long as Yassir Arafat, the Palestinian leader, abandoned his threats to declare unilaterally an independent state by September.

The offer, which is an attempt to breathe life back into the peace talks, would lead to recognition of Palestinian statehood if the newly created country was established in agreement with Israel.

Barak would likely run into trouble with right-wing elements in his Cabinet who would oppose such a plan.

Ha'aretz, 25 April 2000

The Shin Bet security service has recommended to (Israeli) Prime Minister Ehud Barak that all prisoners identified with the Fatah organization and with other groups that are part of the Palestinian Authority be released as part of the negotiations with the PA.

The recommendation includes prisoners convicted of murdering or wounding Israelis. Apart from isolated cases, Israel has so far refrained from releasing Palestinian prisoners "with Israeli blood on their hands" within the framework of the Oslo process.

The position of the Shin Bet, as presented to Barak, draws no distinction between the prisoners on the basis of their actions, but only on the basis of their organizational affiliation. While the Shin Bet recommends the release of all the Fatah prisoners, irrespective of what they were convicted of, it also believes that all Hamas and Islamic Jihad prisoners should remain incarcerated until they finish serving their prison terms.

The Washington Post, 23 April 2000

Ain Helweh, Lebanon- More than fifty years after Israel's birth, some 70,000 stateless Palestinians remain behind the concrete walls of this refugee camp in southern Lebanon, living along muddy streets and warren-like alleys with no sure sense of where they, their children or their grandchildren will abide.

If trouble starts after Israel withdraws from southern Lebanon this summer, local officials, diplomats and others say it is most likely to emanate from here, or from among the estimated 200,000 other Palestinian refugees, about 10,000 of them trained and armed, who live scattered across Lebanon in a dozen camps and several settlements where faith in the future has run low.

The Boston Globe, 21 April 2000

Washington- ..Visiting the White House about a week after Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak was there, Arafat met in the late afternoon with Clinton after holding preliminary talks with Secretary of State Madeleine K. Albright.

The first message the Clinton administration passed to Arafat was one the Palestinian leader clearly wanted to hear: US mediators will be more active than they have been in recent months in trying to shape the talks.

..But Washington's second message to Arafat was probably less welcome: that it is foolish to hold out for long-cherished goals and is instead time to cut a deal that both sides can live with, even if it falls far short of their aspirations.

The New York Times, 21 April 2000

Jaba, West Bank- Peace effort or no, it is still considered a badge of honor in Palestinian society to be a martyr for the cause.

But no one ever expected Hilmiya al-Tous, mother of 12, to become one. She was not militant; she was not even political.

Her death on the night of March 20 got lost in a shuffle of momentous events. The next day, Israel withdrew from a sizable chunk of the West Bank and Pope John Paul II arrived in the Holy Land. It was Mother's Day in the Arab world, and Mrs. Tous was quietly buried by her children while her husband, Mahmoud, lay unconscious in a hospital bed.

Mr. and Mrs. Tous were shot by an Israeli soldier or soldiers -- she in the head, he in the back -- as they were driving away from an Israeli checkpoint.

The Los Angeles Times, 19 April 2000

Jerusalem-Six days after the (Israeli) Supreme Court rejected the practice, (Israeli) Prime Minister Ehud Barak on Tuesday ordered new legislation that would allow Israel to hold enemy fighters as pawns to gain information on missing or captured Israeli soldiers.

At the same time, Barak and his security Cabinet granted the release of 13 of 15 Lebanese detainees whose case was the subject of the Supreme Court's landmark ruling last week. The men, most of whom have been held for more than a decade, some without charges, are due to go free today before the beginning of Passover at sundown.

..Barak said Israel must reserve the right to keep prisoners if doing so might provide information on Israeli soldiers who disappeared in battle. The proposed legislation would transfer the power of determining a detainee's fate from the courts to the executive branch, most likely the Defense Ministry--which Barak heads.
