PA cracks down on critics following
dissemination of scathing petition

Occupied Jerusalem – From Khalid Amayreh

In what seems to reflect a fledgling but growing contention between the official Palestinian establishment and the nationalist-liberal opposition, the PA reacted strongly earlier in December to silence and punish a group of intellectuals and civic leaders who had earlier disseminated a petition castigating “PA corruption and tyranny and calling on the masses to rise up to save the homeland before it is too late.”

The petition, which appeared on 27 November, described the six years that have passed since the signing of the Oslo Accords as “a terrifying scenario of deception and mendacity, the outcome of which will be undoubtedly felt deeply and painfully in the final status talks.”

“We were promised a State whose capital is Jerusalem, repatriation of the refugees, dismantling of the settlements, freedom for the prisoners and an economic transformation which would make of us the Singapore of the region; but what we see is that more of our land has been usurped, settlements expanded, and plots against the refugees’ cause accelerated rapidly behind the scenes and the gates of prisons locked on our sons; neither Jerusalem was recovered, nor Singapore’s advent came about, and the people are now divided into two groups—one oppressing and extorting; and a larger one grieving and looking for a deliverer.”

The petition accused the PA of adopting a modus operandi of “graft, corruption, humiliation and exploitation of the Palestinian people” and “of turning the Oslo process into a formula whereby the Palestinian cause is liquidated in return for some corrupt PA elements getting rich.”

However, what infuriated the PA leadership most was the petition’s direct and audacious attack against Yasser Arafat, the ultimate red-line and sacrosanct cow in the PA regime.

“The PA President,” said the leaflet, “has widely opened the doors for the opportunists to spread corruption throughout the country,” adding that, consequently, “our economic conditions have worsened, social bonds have weakened, and our health, educational and judicial institutions ruined.”

Finally, the signatories called on the masses to rise to correct the Palestinian march before it is too late.

“The bell of danger must be sounded in every village, town and refugee camp; in every corner and lobby; even in every shop, home and office because the homeland is being sold out and the citizen is betrayed and decimated.”

The 20 signatories of the document included 9 legislative council (PLC) members, 3 university professors, 3 physicians and 5 leftist and centrist political activists.

Among the signers were the wheelchair-bound former mayor of Nablus, Bassam al-Shaka’a, who lost both legs in a car bomb planted by Jewish terrorists in 1981; Abdul Sattar Kassem, who was the target of an assassination attempt by pro-Arafat elements in 1994; and Ahmed Qatamesh, the West Bank leader of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine, who was freed from an Israeli jail earlier this year after spending six and a half years of “administrative detention” without charge or trial.

Kassem, Qatamesh, and seven other signatories, who are not PLC members, were arrested on 28 and 29 November, immediately after their signing, while Shaka’a and Wahid al-Hamdulla, the former mayor of Anabta (a town of 20,000 near Nablus) were placed under house arrest.

According to PLC member Husam Khader, one of the co-signers, the petition was leaked to the press prematurely, as the original intention was to get it signed by tens of thousands of Palestinians in order, as he said, to “send a clear message to Arafat that enough is enough.”

Khader, a popular Fatah leader who enjoys widespread support in the large refugee camp of Balata near Nablus, sounded unapologetic and uncompromising.

“We felt we had to choose between loyalty to the President and loyalty to the homeland,” Khader said in a telephone interview on 28 November.

The same tone was reiterated by another defiant co-signer, Hassan Khreisha, chairman of the PLC Human Rights and Oversight Committee, who said there was no reason to shy away from criticism of PA President Yasser Arafat if and when needed.

“What is of more value, the leader or the homeland? I believe the homeland is larger than the leader…whoever he may be.”

A third co-signer, PLC member Abdul Jawad Saleh, was even more defiant.

He told over 2,000 college students at Beir Zeit University on 29 November that “if we have to choose between Arafat and the homeland, we will chose the homeland.”

No distinction between PA excesses and Israeli occupation’s crimes

Initially, the PA described the petition as “a divisive sheet besmirching our national struggle and undermining our national interest.”

PA minister without portfolio, Ahmed Abdel Rahman, criticized the petitioners, saying they made no distinction between PA excesses and the Israeli occupation’s crimes.

“Yes, there are excesses, but the Israelis are ultimately responsible for most of our difficulties.”

However, the PA leadership, which has threatened to remove parliamentary immunity from the nine PLC members who co-signed the leaflet to pave the way for their arrest and prosecution for incitement, is facing a real dilemma dealing with the matter.

Legally speaking, stripping a law-maker of his parliamentary immunity would require the approval of two thirds of the 88-member Legislative Council, a task informed Palestinian sources opine will not be easy for Arafat.

But even if the PA President fulfilled his ostensible (but not necessarily genuine) threat and did succeed in stripping the nine councillors of their parliamentary immunity, that could spell trouble for his leadership and seriously undermine his image among Palestinians.

The councillors, it must be remembered, are generally prominent nationalist leaders in their respective communities and enjoy significant support among the Fatah rank and file.

Moreover, most of them belong to large Palestinian clans, such as Al-Shawwa in Gaza, and al-Masri and Shaka’a in Nablus, and any stringent measure by the PA against them could draw a boomerang effect from these clans.

Nonetheless, the PA leadership would also lose much if it allowed the petitioners to emerge victorious from this showdown, because then the burgeoning protest movement against “corruption and tyranny” would gain momentum and acquire additional strength, the last thing Arafat would like to see in this sensitive juncture when the PA and Israel are negotiating the fundamental final status issues of the Arab-Israeli conflict.

One close confidant of Arafat told a weekly newspaper that the Palestinian leadership would be fighting a losing battle if it did not start listening to the “heartbeats of the ordinary man in the street.”

A few days before the petition was published, hundreds of indigent Gazans took to the streets to protest the high prices of flour, the main strategic commodity in the Occupied Territories.

The protesters attacked “the hidden mafia that is sucking our blood,” and only stopped short of attacking the Palestinian leadership itself.

The protest did force Arafat to order a significant decrease in the prices of flour and fuel, a key demand of the protesters.

It may now be prudent for the PA to try to let things calm down, because escalation will be in nobody’s interest.

However, calming things down per se will not make the crisis disappear if genuine reform is not soon forthcoming.

Text of the leaflet that angered Yasser Arafat

Occupied Jerusalem - The following is an unofficial translation of the leaflet that was supposed to be signed by tens of thousands of Palestinians were it not for the arrest of its original signatories.

“The homeland is calling”

We are pained by what is happening on the sacred land of Palestine, of displaying utter disregard for the people and selling out their rights.

We, as ordinary citizens, motivated by our loyalty to the homeland, feel compelled to speak out to you in order to work together to correct the lamentable state of affairs that we have reached.

Following Oslo (the signing of the Oslo Agreement in 1993), the Palestinian leadership claimed the accords would enable us to establish a State whose capital is Jerusalem, repatriate the refugees, dismantle the settlements, free the prisoners and pave the way for an economic transformation that would make us the Singapore of the region. Indeed, some of us were so deceived that we took to the streets to celebrate the false victories.

However, six years after Oslo, more of our land has been usurped, settlements expanded, plots against the cause of the refugees are accelerating rapidly behind the scenes, and the gates of prisons are locked on our sons; neither Jerusalem was recovered, nor Singapore’s advent came about, and the people were divided into two groups—a small one that is oppressing and extorting, and a larger one grieving and looking for a deliverer.

It is a horrible scenario of deception and mendacity, the outcome of which will undoubtedly be felt deeply and painfully in the final status talks.

Apart from that, the Palestinian Authority has adopted a modus operandi of graft, corruption, humiliation and exploitation of the Palestinian people. Indeed, the Oslo Agreement now appears to be the vehicle for the selling-out of the homeland in return for some corrupt and corrupting elements within the PA as a means to their getting rich.

The PA president has opened the doors widely for the opportunists to spread corruption in the larger Palestinian street. The economic conditions have worsened, social bonds have become weaker, the moral values of our people have become lax, and destruction has befallen many of our health, educational and judicial institutions.

Hence, the bells of danger must be sounded in every village, town and refugee camp; in every corner and lobby; even in every shop, home and office. The homeland is being sold out and the citizen is being betrayed and decimated.

So, let us stand together in the face of this tyranny and corruption. The oppression will not be stopped save at the hands of the oppressed, and the oppressor will only be stopped through strength and determination of the faithful.

Torture is continuing in Israel
despite High Court’s decision

Occupied Jerusalem – PT

Physical and psychological torture of Palestinian detainees by Israeli interrogators is continuing unabated in Israeli jails and detention centres, according to human rights organizations operating in the occupied territories.

According to prisoners’ testimonies, the Shabah method (covering a detainee’s head with a stinking bag for prolonged periods) and many forms of beating are still being used on a large scale in Israeli prisons.

On 9 December, the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group (PHRMG) reported that three young men from Bethlehem sere severely tortured in the Russian Compound lockup in Jerusalem.

According to the PHRMG report, Muhammed Ali Hameed Souman, 17, from Breitha’a al-Ta’amreh near Bethlehem, was subjected to intermittent torture for several weeks, apparently for the purpose of extracting confessions from him relating to his political affiliation.

Souman was arrested in his home on 24 August and taken to the Russian Compound where he was interrogated for 55 days. The first 25 days he was tortured severely. They covered his head with a dirty bag and forced him to sit in painful positions (Shabah) for long periods. Furthermore, they forced him into a very small and narrow cupboard where he could not move at all. The pain of torture forced Muhammed to confess to charges relating to his membership in the Islamic Jihad movement. His lawyer was not allowed to meet with him until 40 days after his arrest.

Ali Issa al-Wahsh, 20, also from al-Ta’amreh, was also arrested on 24 August. He was interrogated for 70 days, during which time he said he was beaten and tortured severely. He was not allowed to use the toilet for some days; his health deteriorated, but he was not taken to hospital nor received medical care. As a result of the torture, al-Wahsh was forced to confess that he was a member of the Islamic Jihad organization.

A third youth subjected to torture was Ra’ed Ahmed Salem al-Hamri, 19, also from al-Ta’amreh. He was arrested at his home on 21 August 1999. He spent the first night in the Atsion jail, and then was transferred to the Russian Compound lockup where he spent 62 days under interrogation. He testified that he was beaten and tortured and that Israeli interrogators put burning cigarettes on his body and forced him to sit in painful positions. During the interrogation, he was hospitalized at “Pikur Holem” on 22 September as his health deteriorated badly during the torture. Al-Hamri was accused of being a member of the Islamic Jihad, and as a result of the torture, he confessed to the charges.

Several other cases of torture have been reported at the prisons of Nafha, Beir al-Sab’a and Askalan.

According to the Palestinian human rights organization “Tadamun”, or solidarity, Israeli prison officials and interrogators are increasingly resorting to beating and solitary confinement.

In early December, Israeli prison police even shot tear gas inside the small cells of Palestinian inmates, causing several of them to faint.

Tadamun quoted some prisoners as saying that prison police often deliberately provoke the prisoners in order to create a pretext to beat and punish them.

In September, the Israeli High Court ruled that the employment of torture against detainees is illegal.

The ruling said the use of various interrogation techniques by the General Security Service (GSS) is illegal, including torture or ill-treatment of detainees during interrogation. The international community and human rights organizations welcomed the decision.

Following the ruling, the GSS suspended the use of harsh forms of torture for some time.

However, it is clear now that the ruling is not being implemented and that torture is still the chief mode of operation in Israeli jails.

Two Hamas fighters martyred

Hebron – PT

Israeli undercover soldiers attacked with anti-tank rockets on 13 December a house in the village of Beit Awwa, 20 kilometres west of Hebron, in which two Hamas fugitives were hiding.

The two fugitives are Iyad al-Battat, who the Israeli authorities claim is responsible for several attacks on Israeli targets, and Nader Masalmeh, a local village activist who was at the house.

The Israeli army claimed the two Hamas fugitives started the shooting, prompting the soldiers to return fire and kill them both.

However, eyewitnesses dismissed the Israeli account as “an exposed lie” and “mere propaganda.”

According to Palestinian sources, the Israeli army cordoned off the village, situated along the former armistice line between Israel and the West Bank, several hours before the shooting, which meant that the Israeli undercover units were intent on spilling blood in Beit Awwa.

The eyewitnesses pointed out that hundreds of Israeli soldiers were involved in the ambush, in addition to a helicopter gunship, using anti-tank rockets against Battat, who was armed with a single AK-47 rifle.

Israeli officials boasted about the ambush and even suggested that the PA helped with intelligence information.

Israeli Deputy Defense Minister Ephraim Sneh told the Israeli radio on 15 December that “we made a promise to kill them within a year and I am happy to say that we fulfilled our promise.”

It is not clear if the PA security agencies were involved in the incident.

Hamas sources said prior to the incident, Preventive Security Chief Jebril Rajoub had urged Battat’s father to convince his son to surrender himself because “he is going to be liquidated.”

Moreover, a PA security patrol erected a roadblock at an intersection leading to Beit Awwa and advised Palestinians not to proceed to the village in light of the Israeli operation.

Hamas vowed to retaliate. A movement’s spokesman in Gaza, Ismael Abu Shanab, said the Israeli ambush was unprovoked, suggesting that Israel violated an undeclared truce between the two sides.

Many of Hamas fighters were assassinated or ambushed by the Israeli army, probably in connivance with PA forces.

However, there have been consistent reports that Hamas is rebuilding its military wing, making use of the wide public disillusionment with the Oslo process among the Palestinians.

However, Hamas’s “tactical setback” does not seem to have an effect on the Movement’s popularity in the West Bank and, indeed, on its traditional stance on the peace process.

Hamas founder and spiritual leader, Sheikh Ahmed Yassin, said Hamas would “continue to cling to the path of resistance until we restore our rights.”

Yassin, who was speaking on 17 December during a rally in Gaza marking the twelfth anniversary of Hamas foundation, told over 30,000 supporters that he had no doubt that the current peace process would collapse because it is not based on justice and it is unaccepted by the vast majority of Palestinians.

“They are trying to liquidate the Palestinian question under the cover of the so-called peace process, but we will not allow them to do so because we have legitimate rights that ought to be recovered.”

Israel dictates her stance to Arafat

Occupied Jerusalem - Israeli and Palestinian Authority (PA) negotiators have again failed to reach an agreement on the implementation of the second redeployment stipulated in the revised Wye River Memorandum, or what is known as the Sharm el-Sheikh agreement.

For despite a spate of optimistic statements by Israeli and PA officials that progress was being made, effectively no progress was reported.

Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak and PA leader Yasser Arafat met in Ramallah on 21 December in an additional effort to get the talks going.

However, one PA official described the talks as “general” and “lacking in detail.”

The fact that the two sides were not only talking but thinking at cross-purposes became apparent in the subsequent meeting between PA negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat and his Israeli counterpart Odid Eran in West Jerusalem on 22 December.

Ureikat told reporters following the meeting that no real progress was made. He accused the Israelis of “putting up an intransigent position” and of “creating a false impression that progress was being made when in fact the Israeli position is unchanged.”

In fact, there seemed a certain inconsistency in the statements of PA officials concerning the meeting between Barak and Arafat. Arafat’s advisor, Nabil Abu Rudeina, had described the meeting as “friendly and serious” and that “its results would become apparent in a few days.”

However, Abu Rudeina did not give any details, nor said which side retreated from its stance on the second redeployment, or whether Israel agreed to take Palestinian objections to the redeployment map into account.

Earlier, Gaza Preventive Security Chief Muhammed Dahlan accused Israel of contravening the Sharm el-Sheikh agreement.

Dahlan was right. The Israeli government seemed insistent upon carrying out the redeployment as it saw fit without any consideration for the Palestinian views or demands.

This stance was reiterated by Israeli Foreign Minister David Levy, who attended the Ramallah meeting. He pointed out that Israel did not give any concessions to the Palestinians, suggesting that it was up to the PA to retreat and accept the Israeli offer “as it is.”

He repeated the Israeli claim that Israel alone had the exclusive right to determine the scope and extent of the redeployment, as well as all matters pertaining to the release of Palestinian prisoners.

According to Palestinian observers, the Israeli tactic is to make the PA rotate in an empty circle of meetings and encounters in the hope that this would eventually frustrate Yasser Arafat and push him to budge, as he has always done under such circumstances.

The Palestinian position is actually further weakened by the effective American connivance with Israel over the redeployment impasse.

Indeed, when U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright visited Palestine recently, she actually lent tacit support for the Israeli position.

Far from endorsing the Palestinian position, which Arafat had hoped she would, Albright said the parties alone should settle their differences without external interference.

In reality, this amounts to giving Israel the right and freedom to “impose and dictate” its position on an increasingly vulnerable and autocratic Arafat.

Meanwhile, Arafat, who is now preoccupied with the Bethlehem-2000 celebration, is supposed to meet with U.S. President Bill Clinton probably in the second week of January. (Arafat considers a mere meeting with the President of the United States a great achievement.) In this meeting, pundits believe Arafat will announce his acceptance of the Israeli plan for redeployment, and, still more, may give additional concessions concerning the final status talks.

In return, it is expected that Clinton, who is becoming a lame duck, will confirm American commitment to the peace process and support for the Palestinians’ right to self-determination.

On the ground, these statements would practically mean nothing, as they were uttered several times in the past but have had little or no effect on the actual pro-Israeli American position.

Meanwhile, the Israeli paper Ma’ariv disclosed on 23 December that Israel prepared a policy paper whereby no more than 50% of the West Bank would be handed over to the Palestinian Authority in the context of a final peace arrangement between the two sides.

The other half of the West Bank, according to the document, would be annexed to Israel.

The document seemed to be genuine and authentic.

In fact, Odid Eran, who represents Israel in both the interim phase and final status talks with the Palestinians, actually conveyed this position to his Palestinian counterpart Yasser Abed Rabbou on 19 December.

He reportedly told him that Israel was planning to annex most of the Jewish settlements in the West Bank in the context of the final settlement.

In light of this fact, it is very likely that the current on-again/off-again talks between the two sides would actually collapse, especially if a breakthrough is reached on the Syrian track.

And in case this actually happens, Arafat would likely resort to unilaterally declaring Palestinian Statehood on the 10%-20% of the West Bank and Gaza Strip he now runs.

As to what will happen next, the coming months will tell.

Arafat dreads progress on Syrian track

Occupied Jerusalem - Despite public statements to the contrary, the Palestinian leadership has been clearly worried about the resumption of peace talks between Israel and Syria and the effect of an increasingly possible Israeli-Syrian peace treaty on the Palestinian question.

Indeed, since U.S. Secretary of State Madeleine Albright, who visited the region in the second week of December, announced the resumption of the talks between the two implacable enemies, the Palestinian issue has been, more or less, placed on the back burner.

Moreover, Albright herself, along with U.S. envoy Dennis Ross, utterly failed to get the “PA-Israeli tracks” moving again, having been virtually deadlocked by Israel’s utter refusal to consider Palestinian demands regarding the transfer of an additional small territory to partial PA administration.

In fact, aside from the usual diplomatic pleasantries, Albright effectively rebuffed the Palestinian Authority, which had been hoping to see the American Secretary of State pressure Barak to soften his position on redeployment and settlements.

“The parties themselves ought to resolve their outstanding differences,” said Albright, effectively rendering a veto power to Israel in interim-phase and final status talks with Israel.

This and other similar statements, needless to say, were a great disappointment for the Palestinian leadership which began to seek a face-saving exit out of this embarrassing predicament.

Palestinian anxiety well-founded

Palestinian anxiety over the resumption of talks between Syria and Israel is well-founded.

Palestinian Authority Yasser Arafat is now watching the Palestinian track take a backseat, not only as far as the United States is concerned but also in terms of Israeli concentration and willingness to achieve real progress in the final status talks with the Palestinians.

In a clearer political language, this means that the Palestinian track will be effectively frozen, or stalemated, owing to Israel’s preoccupation with talks with Syria and unwillingness to “give out concessions on two fronts concomitantly.”

This, coupled with the fact that the Israeli-Palestinian talks are already rotating in an “empty circle”, as Arafat himself earlier described the talks, means that the “status quo,” with all its crises and predicaments, will continue to prevail.

The most conspicuous aspect of this unbearable “status quo,” as far as the Palestinians are concerned, is, of course, the continued expansion of Jewish settlements in the West Bank and to a lesser degree in the Gaza Strip as well.

In real terms, even if Israel and the PA do hold intensive talks, it is unlikely that Ehud Barak will take any meaningful decision to freeze the construction of additional Jewish settlements and the confiscation of Palestinian land in the occupied territories.

PA officials, including Arafat, rightly dismissed Barak’s decision to freeze the building of further settlements during the duration of talks with the Palestinians (for 90 days) as “a propaganda ruse.”

The announcement to that effect, says PA negotiator Sa’eb Ureikat, is vacuous as the number of settlement projects is so vast that building could go on for at least five more years unabated.

Palestinian and Israeli sources alike point out that there are currently over 6,000 settlement units under construction. In addition, Barak’s promise to freeze new settlement tenders does not pertain to Jerusalem.

Stepped-up expansion of settlements

It goes without saying that a prolonged freeze on the Palestinian-Israeli track will mean a stepped-up expansion of Jewish settlements, if only to blackmail the PA into agreeing to rejoin the talks on Israel’s terms.

However, if the PA refused, and it became apparent that the two sides would not reach a framework agreement by February 2000, the erstwhile designated date for reaching such an agreement, a crisis-atmosphere and frustration would prevail over the Palestinian arena, particularly if a real breakthrough was reached between Syria and Israel.

And, in case that breakthrough is consummated with a peace treaty leading, for example, to the establishment of normal diplomatic and economic relations, not only with Syria, but with the rest of the Arab countries supporting the peace process, e.g. Saudi Arabia and the Gulf States, the Palestinian position would certainly be further undermined.

Indeed, these prospects must look terribly ominous to the Palestinian leadership, which has mistakenly calculated that the Arab regimes would not normalize with Israel before the Palestinian issue was completely resolved.

In any case, one should remember that the bulk of Arab regimes are despotic in nature and are more answerable to the United States than to their own masses and will consequently normalize with Israel with or without the achievement of a breakthrough on the Palestinian track.

Furthermore, the PA, which is now extremely unpopular among Arab masses due to its connivance with the Zionists against Palestinian Islamist groups, will not be able to bank on popular Arab support since the regimes are unlikely to allow any serious popular movement to undermine the long-awaited peace with the Jewish State.

In the final analysis, a situation in which Israel enjoys “peace relations” with all its neighbours (Jordan and Egypt along with Syria and Egypt) would make Arafat’s regime as vulnerable as ever to Israeli dictates and bullying.

And in a situation like this, the beleaguered Palestinian leadership could resort to either of two scenarios:

First, creating a crisis situation for the purpose of forcing the United States to re-focus attention on the Palestinian track, thus proving to all parties concerned that “there can be no peace and stability in the region without the Palestinians.” This crisis could take the form of large-scale demonstrations and disturbances in various parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip, particularly the so-called “friction spots.”

However, these tactics were tried in the past, and Israel always succeeded in defusing them by giving Arafat some symbolic gestures but no substantive achievements on the ground.

Notwithstanding, if the crisis persisted and Arafat continued to fail to make any real achievements, he would very likely declare a Palestinian State with a tacit approval from Israel and the United States and support from the Arab countries.

This in effect would be a belated recognition on Arafat’s part that Oslo was a failure as a small Palestinian entity on a small part of the West Bank, even if recognized by Israel, would virtually leave the Israeli occupation in the bulk of the West Bank and the settlements intact. In other words, the status quo will be frozen indefinitely.

The other scenario could take the form of Arafat giving far-reaching concessions to Israel in the form of accepting a small Palestinian entity on 60%-70% of the West Bank, without Jerusalem, on the ground that “we have no choice” and “this is all we could do under current circumstances.”

In this case—Arafat’s de facto capitulation to Israel—the United States would very likely provide his regime with generous financial assistance and help strengthen his security agencies in order to enable him to crush popular protests against the sell-out. (The U.S. has been silent on the arrest of the signatories of Petition-20.)

Finally, the imposed settlement will be absolutely oblique, inherently unfair and by no means durable or acceptable to present or future Palestinian, Arab or Muslim generations. It will be like a confession under duress, or marriage under duress, meaning it would not last very long.

Israel bars hundreds of Palestinian
pilgrims from leaving for Mecca

Occupied Jerusalem- The Zionist occupation authorities, which controls all border crossings from and to the occupied territories, have been baring hundreds of Palestinians from travelling to Mecca for "Omra" or lesser pilgrimage.

Palestinian sources said most of the returned pilgrims were college students and lecturers, professionals, and generally young people below the age of 30.

According to Mansur Hasan, of the West Bank town of Jenin, he was turned back because of his opposition to the peace process.

"They told me at the Allenby Bridge (the main border crossing between Jordan and the West Bank) that the Shabak (the Israeli domestic intelligence Service) didn’t allow me to travel," adding that "I really don't know the reason.”

"Mansur said while at the Allenby Bridge waiting to have the exit visa, he was taken to a small chamber where Shabak officers questioned him on his political convictions and views about the Oslo process, Hamas, the Palestinian Authority and Israel. "They told me that I would never be allowed to leave the West Bank as long as continue to incite against Israel."

Mansur described the Israeli measures against Palestinian pilgrims as "unethical" and "incompatible with the universally observed freedom of religion."

"Where in the world a person destined to visit the holy places of his faith is turned back…this is certainly the highest degree of injustice," he said, siting verses from the Holy Quran. The same story was told by Ahmed al Sheikh also from the town of Jenin, in the northern part of the West Bank. He related that as he sought to leave for Saudi Arabia via Jordan, he was interrogated by Shabak officers stationed at the Allenby bridge and told that he was not allowed to proceed to Mecca because his two sons were in prison on charges of resisting the Zionist occupation.

"They told me that they would take me, my wife, and our daughter to the slaughter house (interrogation chambers) and would deface my daughter if we didn't confess that we were not going to bring money for Hamas from Saudi Arabia. "Al Sheikh expressed profound indignation and bitterness at the fact that this violation of a man's religious freedom takes place in the shadow of the so-called peace process.

"What peace! What is the meaning of this process when we are denied the right to perform a religious duty."

Israel tells PA it wants to annex settlements

Occupied Jerusalem- The Palestinian Authority reacted angrily to the declared Israeli desire to annex parts of the West Bank and Gaza Strip where Jewish settlements have been built since 1967.

Palestinian sources said Chief Israeli negotiator to the final status talks, Odid Eran, told his Palestinian counterpart Yaser Abed Rubbo of the Israeli stance during the talks they held in Ramallah on 20 December.

Another Palestinian dies in PA
custody, apparently of torture

Ramallah - A Palestinian human rights organization disclosed that another Palestinian died in a Ramallah prison earlier last month, apparently as a result of torture.

The Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group (PHRMG) said Mahmud Hasan al-Bajjali, 33, and a father of three children, died in PA custody in Ramallah on 6 December under “vague circumstances.”

According to a PHRMG report, Bajjali died after four years in prison without trial.

He was arrested by the Israeli authorities on 6 November 1995 and charged with a criminal offense. After his arrest, Bajjali spent one year in an Israeli jail; then he was transferred to the Palestinian Authority prison in Nablus where he spent two years before he was taken to Ramallah.

Bajjali reportedly did not suffer any illness and he had no complaints.

The death of Bajjali is the 22nd death in PA custody so far.

Zionist regime detains 28 Palestinian
students returning from abroad

Gaza - The Israeli occupation authorities, which tightly control all crossing points from and to the Palestinian self-rule enclaves, reportedly arrested 28 Palestinian students at the Rafah border crossing while returning home from abroad.

Palestinian sources described the arrests as “provocative” and “carried out in order to inflict pain and suffering on the students and their families.”

Last month, the Zionist authorities arrested Mustafa Juma’a, 26, while returning from Cairo, having finished his studies in Turkey.

According to his mother, Israeli officers manning the crossing point summoned her son for questioning, and she waited for him for three hours before she was told that he “was under arrest.”

She said she was absolutely sure that the Zionists arrested her son for no reason other than to harass him and to break their wills.

According to Ziyad Awajeh, the PA liaison officer in Gaza, the Zionist authorities have arrested 28 students at the Rafah crossing point since the beginning of the year.

Syrian Muslim Brothers: Palestine is
the heart of the conflict with Israel

Occupied Jerusalem- The Muslim Brothers of Syria came out strongly against the resumption of talks between Israel and Syria, describing a prospective peace treaty between Syrian and Israel as “a capitulation” and “a sell-out of Arab and Muslims rights in Palestine.”

The group, some of whose leaders are based in London, said in a leaflet faxed to news agencies on 22 December that any agreement concluded between the Syrian government and Israel would be “illegitimate since it would go against the collective will of the Syrian people.”

The leaflet pointed out that the Palestinian question was, is and will always be the center of the conflict with the Jewish state.

Nine Palestinian prisoners tortured in Beer Sheba

Occupied Jerusalem - Nine Palestinian prisoners, held in solitary confinement at the Israeli Beer Sheba detention centre, have reportedly been subjected to torture and beatings by Israeli prison officials and Shabak agents.

According to information obtained from the human rights organization, al-Tadamun (solidarity), one of the prisoners, Zaher Jebrin, nearly died last month as a result of a combination of maltreatment and lack of medical care.

Jebrin’s lawyer, Maher Al-Zurba, quoted Jebrin as saying that a prison doctor gave him a medication that causes an allergic reaction for him, in turn causing a serious deterioration in Jebrin’s health.

According to Al-Zurba, nine other Palestinian political prisoners were still detained in solitary confinement.

“They are living under harsh conditions and subject to maltreatment at the hands of Israeli prison officials.”

One of the prisoners, Taha Shakhshir, was quoted as saying that he was “beaten savagely” and that “tear gas was shot at me inside his small chamber.”

Israel still allows torture to be employed against Palestinian detainees despite a ruling against torture by the Israeli High Court.
