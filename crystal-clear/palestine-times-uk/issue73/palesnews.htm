Jewish Groups Plan to Take Over Al Masjidul Aqsa

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Several messianic Jewish groups have been performing specific rituals in advance of the "re-establishment of the Third Holy Temple" at the site of Al Masjidul Aqsa in Jerusalem, Israeli sources revealed on Friday.

According to the Hebrew daily, Ha'aretz, the leader of one of these groups, Yehuda Itzioni, has been actively organizing "exercises and rehearsals" on slaughtering sacrificial animals in preparation for the "rebuilding of the Holy Temple following the erasure of the Al Aqsa Mosque."

In 1980, Itzioni was involved in a conspiracy to blow up the Dome of the Rock and the Al Aqsa Mosque along with several other terrorists belonging to what was then labelled "the Jewish Underground." However, all the terrorists were given presidential pardon and subsequently released, only to resume their diabolical plans against Islam's third holiest shrine.

Ha'aretz pointed out that Itzioni and hundreds of his followers slaughtered a ram recently at the Jewish settlement of Karmi Tsur in the Hebron area. The ram was reportedly cut up into several pieces following an ancient tradition attributed to custodians of the Temple's altar when making their sacrificial offerings to the deity.The rituals were filmed by the Canadian Television station (CTV) as part of a documentary on "Extremist Jewish Religious Trends in Israel."

According to "Ha'aretz", the rituals lasted for three hours, without the slightest interference by the police. The film included an interview with Itzioni during which he told CTV correspondent in Israel Diana Lewis that "the Aqsa Mosque is the wrong building in the right place." Itzioni added that "my followers are growing in number on a daily basis, and that a large movement is now being crystallized around the idea of the Jewish right in rebuilding the Temple."

A member of the group, who introduced herself as Banina, said she believed that an imminent war would take place after which the al Aqsa Mosque would be erased and the temple rebuilt. Indeed, most of the Jewish groups associated with Religious Zionism believe that an internecine war on an unheard-of scale (e.g. one that would leave tens of millions of casualties) is a necessary precondition for the coming of the Messiah who would save the Jewish people and bring about global Jewish domination or Redemption.

In 1980, a Jewish Underground member who was implicated in planting bombs in the vicinity of Al Masjidul Aqsa told Israeli interrogators that he thought the would-be-demolition of the Mosque would have infuriated hundreds of millions of Muslims the world over. The terrorist added that he reasoned Muslim rage would lead to a war which in all likelihood would escalate into a world war in which the scale of casualties would be formidable enough to promote the process of Redemption of the Jews and of the Land of Israel. As all Muslims would by then disappear, everything would be ready for the coming of the Messiah.

In March, a group of rabbis became euphoric following their success in producing a "completely red heifer" at the Kfar Hasideem settlement, the first of its kind in 3,000 years. The rabbis, some of whom were seen examining the heifer skin using magnifying lenses, announced that the ashes of the cow, when immolated, would bring about the perfect purification of the sages, which then would herald the rebuilding of the Temple Mount.

Not Anecdotal

It is imperative to stress that these dangerous ideas are by no means anecdotal in nature or confined to a small group of eccentric believers. In fact they formulate both the very ideology of many hundreds of thousands of Israelis, including most settlers, followers of the National Religious Party (Mifdal), Gush Emunim, Kach, Kahane Hay, and perceivably the gate-keepers of Israel's huge nuclear arsenal.

In light of the above, the world, particularly Muslims, should not be surprised if they wake up one day to the grim news of the destruction of Al Aqsa Mosque at the hands of these dangerous terrorists who pose an unparalleled menace to world peace.

Israeli Piracy in Hebron

Occupied Hebron- From: Khalid M. Amayreh

In line with its brutal maltreatment of Palestinians, the Israeli army raided a farm in Hebron on 22 June, beating and arresting Nabil al Natshe, 39, a local businessman and former deportee to Southern Lebanon.

According to eyewitnesses, two helicopter gunships hovered over the farm located at the Singer outskirt within area (A) presumably to photograph the farm. Soon afterwards, hundred of soldiers raided the site and savagely beat up al Natshe, handcuffed him, and took him away to an unknown destination.

Al Natshe's son, 15 year-old Naim, said the "criminal soldiers beat up my father before our eyes, but we were helpless to do anything because there were so many soldiers with so many guns."

Al Natshe's uncle, Issa, described the sudden raid and the mistreatment of his nephew as "an act of piracy and abduction that is in line with characteristic Israeli savagery and brutality." Moreover, Isa expressed profound bitterness at the Palestinian Authority's powerlessness to stop Israeli terrorism against the Palestinians even within

areas which are supposedly under exclusive PA control.

"You see, they (PA police) cannot do anything to stop the Israelis...It seems they have carte blanche to do as they wish in our 'liberated' areas," said the 60-year-old man.

Palestinian sources pointed out that the operation, which lasted for two hours, proceeded from the nearby settlement of Hagay on the southern outskirts of Hebron opposite the farm.

Hagay, like the tiny Jewish enclave in Hebron, has a few Jewish families, but settler leaders hope to seize more Palestinian land in the vicinity of the settlement for future expansion.

Al Natshe was among the 415 Islamist deportees (deported to Lebanon by Yitzhak Rabin in 1992). In 1996, he was detained without trial by the Palestinian Authority and remained in custody for over a year.

It is not known whether al Natshe's brutal arrest by the Israeli army was carried out in connivance with the PA security apparatus.

Lately, the Israeli army resorted to abducting "undesirable" Palestinians on the roads, at checkpoints or even in the streets. During the month of June, Israeli soldiers abducted Sheikh Abdul Majid Atta, Imam of the Dheishe Refugee Camp's mosque as he stepped out of the Wakf Department building in Bethlehem. Atta's whereabouts are not known.

Earlier in the month of June, two buses carrying Hebron University students were "abducted" near the Beit Fajjar intersection, 13 miles north of Hebron. The buses and the passengers were held hostage for several hours in the sizzling summer heat.

Eventually, the buses were let go only after several students were taken to a detention camp ,without charge or trial.

Bulk of Palestinians Believe PA is Corrupt

By Khalid Amayreh

Occupied Jerusalem- Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat ordered an "investigation" into the large-scale corruption scandal currently engulfing much of the PA administrative apparatus. On 8 June, Arafat appointed a team of PA officials and associates, headed by his personal secretary Al Tayyeb Abdul Rahim (merely a mouthpiece for the Chairman), to investigate the Control Board report which spoke of widespread financial corruption throughout the PA hierarchy and alleged that as much as $340 million were squandered (roughly half the declared PA 1996-1997 budget).

According to Abdul Rahim, the investigating team, which he said has Arafat's full confidence, will meet with officials whose names were cited in the Control Board report in order to establish the truth. The ordered investigation, however, is expected neither to establish the truth nor to convince a skeptical Palestinian public to place its trust where it doesn't belong. The investigating team, after all, is largely made up of people who are themselves part of the establishment and therefore inherently inappropriate to carry out an objective investigation. More important, many Palestinians already scoffed at the very act of ordering an official inquiry into the massive scandal which a high-ranking finance ministry official called "the norm rather than exception." The official, who agreed to speak to the Palestine Times on condition of anonymity, said Arafat knew all along of and about all these "excesses."

"I can even dare say that the Chairman himself turned a blind eye to at least some of the embezzled money," intimated the official. If indeed the euphemistically worded "excesses" were the "norm" rather than the "exception," it would then be fairly reasonable to conclude that the corruption scheme was so overwhelming that any routine, even if well-intentioned, investigation would be utterly inadequate to tackle the matter in any genuine manner. Added to that is the fact that the investigating team (in order to carry out a thorough investigation) would have to meet with (or more correctly confront) thousands of officials and civil servants, not to mention the far more unbridled and inaccessible security lords and countless VIPs who view any questioning of their "credibility" as a common threat that ought to be repulsed at any cost.

On 17 June, a public opinion survey conducted by the pro-PA Nablus-based Research Center showed that 72% of the Palestinians believed that corruption was rampant throughout the PA administrative apparatus. Fifty-two per cent of the respondents actually opined that all PA ministers be fired immediately. In fact, corruption within the PA seems to be "very well protected," using the words of a Ramallah municipal council official. Recently, the head of a Hebron institution was told to stop inquiring about an embezzled sum of money allocated for his institution because "we have here big whales and little fish, and you know what could happen to the small fish if they don't behave carefully." Hearing this sombre note from a central PA figure in Ramallah to whom he presented his grievances, the Hebron manager decided to become "pragmatic" lest he be "devoured" by a big whale or arrested on charges of "endangering national interests."

Al Kidra Fired

Under heavy public pressure to produce "action" rather than "words," the Palestinian Authority did act on 4 June, though belatedly, by sacking the ill-reputed Attorney-General Khalid Al Kidra. Arafat fired his hand-picked AG reportedly after it became amply clear that the latter had been involved in a series of serious violations pertaining to his sensitive job, including charging bail, releasing prisoners and pocketing the money without receipts.

Al Kidra was reportedly placed under house arrest immediately after he announced his "resignation" for bad health. Indeed, Palestinian jurists and human rights activists, as well as many ordinary citizens, heaved a sigh of relief at the dismissal of the widely-despised 70-year-old former Fatah activist.

Palestinian officials intimated to the Palestine Times on 9 June that Al Kidra had become "an intolerable liability" to the PA and to Chairman Arafat. "The man had become a stigma attached to the judicial system and therefore he had to be removed," said a PA Ministry of Justice official. On 5 June, the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens' Rights issued a statement expressing profound satisfaction at the sacking of Al Kidra. The statement, entitled "Victory for Justice," said the "presence of this man at the top of this crucial office of Attorney-General did an incalculable disservice to the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian people at large." The statement went on saying that "he always twisted the arm of the law, violated its letter and spirit, made undue interference in court proceedings, concocted charges against innocent people and, not the least, ignored and even defended human rights violations by PA security agencies."

"All this," the statement concluded, "made him a premier obstacle impeding the administration of justice." Al Kidra, who came to be hated by young Fatah activists for his "excessive hypocrisy and sycophancy", is particularly blamed for the deterioration of press freedom and expression with the autonomous enclaves. In 1995, he ordered the closure of the pro-Hamas weekly "al Watan" and its sister publication, "al Istiklal", of the Islamic Jihad. In both cases, Al Kidra accused the two papers of "incitement" and of

"endangering paramount Palestinian national interests." Such nebulous and concocted charges were repeatedly evoked by Al Kidra to justify the prolonged detention without charge or trial of journalists, writers and human rights activists.

"I never felt that he ever tried to uphold the law," said Ghazi Hamed, editor of the Gaza-based Islamist weekly, "al Risala". During the intifada, Al Kidra was shot in the leg by a disgruntled Fatah activist following widespread rumours that he embezzled large sums of money destined for young intifada activists. He has not fully recuperated from that injury.

Other critics opined that Al Kidra was the monkey rather than the organ grinder. "His main duty was to carry out Arafat's instructions as well as please and appease the Chairman. Paradoxically, he ended up harming his boss," said a clerk at the Gaza Municipal Council who expressed satisfaction at Al Kidra's dismissal. "The person 's gone, but the phenomenon is still there," muttered the Gazan clerk, heaving a sigh of ambivalence. The mixed feeling is rife among Palestinians.

Sexual Gum

In the context of the ongoing propaganda war between the PA and the Netanyahu government, Palestinian officials last week accused Israel of spreading the so-called "sexual gum" among Palestinian female teenagers throughout the West Bank and the Gaza Strip. According to a PA Food and Drug Board report, Israel has been smuggling defective and hazardous consumer products into the autonomous enclaves, including the lust-arousing chewing gum which reportedly causes sexual arousal in female teenagers and infertility to women of child-bearing years. The report, published in major Arabic papers on 31 May, pointed out that seven brands of the elastic substance were discovered in retail shops and roadside stands in several Palestinian towns. Nahed al Nakhal, the Board director, said some of the gum wrappers bear a warning saying "not recommended for girls below the age of 18," while other wrappers bear the phrase "made in Spain." Nakhal argued that it was highly unlikely the "suspicious product" came from abroad, adding that the most likely source of the gum was Israel.

The PA official, who holds a Ph.D. in microbiology, pointed out that Israel had always sought to "dump all kinds of defective and potentially dangerous consumer products into the West Bank and the Gaza Strip."

"They seek to achieve two sinister goals: harming our people and making money doing that," he said in an interview on 31 May with the Arabic daily, al Hayatul Jadida. Last year, samples of the "sexual gum" were taken to Egypt for thorough analysis. The results showed that the gum contained, among other things, the hormone progesterone, a steroid hormone that induces sexual arousal in women. High levels of the hormone, however, could cause infertility to married women.

The Palestinian media treated the subject with ample attention, warning young girls to be careful. Moreover, school principals were exhorted to educate schoolgirls on the matter in the hope of increasing public awareness of "this cunning scheme."

Report on press freedom under PA

More human rights abuses

The following is a comprehensive report by the Palestinian Human Rights Monitoring Group ( PHRMG) on the status of press freedom under the Palestinian Authority.

"Everyone shall have the right to freedom of expression: this right shall include freedom to seek, receive, and impart information and ideas of all kinds regardless of frontiers either orally, in writing, or in print in the form of art or through any other media of his choice." (Article 19 (2) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.

"Press and printing are free. Furthermore, freedom of opinion should be entitled to every Palestinian individual who attends the absolute right to express his opinion in a free manner either verbally, in writing, photography, or drawing, as a different means of expression and information." (Article 2 (1995) Palestinian Press Law.

According to the Palestinian Press Law, the right to a free and independent press is guaranteed. This legal protection has not been translated into the existence of a truly free and independent Palestinian press. Our assessment of the status of freedom of the press and of expression is that it is limited, often by bodies with no legal authority to do so-particularly the security services and the President's office. This freedom is also limited by the practice of self-censorship, practiced by journalists, editors and publishers.

To collect information for this report, the PHRMG interviewed journalists and editors from three daily newspapers. Without exception, those interviewed insisted on anonymity, stating the fear that their employers will take measures against them, or that they will be harmed by security agencies of the PA. This fear has a basis in fact: In 1996, Journalist Samir Hamato was held in detention for seven months, without being charged or interrogated. It remains unclear what prompted his arrest; Hamato himself says that he has no idea what led to his lengthy detention, or what caused his release. Hamato is a supporter of Hamas. In another case, Editor Maher Al Alami, was detained for eight days for placing a story about Yasser Arafat on page 8 of Al Quds newspaper instead of on the cover page, as requested by the office of President Arafat.

Newspaper Closure

Newspapers published by Hamas and the Islamic Jihad were also closed in 1996, in the wake of the bomb attacks in Israel in February and March of that year. A local newspaper, "Jenin" was shut down, and its editor, Imad Abu Zahara, briefly jailed and threatened with further imprisonment in October, 1996.

The pro-Jordanian newspaper An Nahar closed on January 1. The immediate reason was for financial reasons. However, the PA had carried out a lengthy campaign of harassment against An Nahar, resulting in distribution difficulties and withdrawal of major advertisers, leading up to the financial crisis. Two measures stand out: the closure of An Nahar for forty days in August and September of 1994, was followed by a steep drop in advertising revenue since both advertisers and readers AHD moved on to other newspapers, and newsstand vendors were approached by individuals who "recommended" that An-Nahar be "displayed less prominently."

Harassment of Journalists

In the first three months of 1997, no senior journalist was arrested. We have heard unverified reports of journalists in Gaza being held by the security forces for one-way 'consultation" during which the journalists were told to "behave." The reports are unverified because none of them have been willing to cooperate with human rights groups out of fear of reprisal from the PA.

Khalid Amayreh, a journalist with UAE Satellite Television, gave testimony about his harassment, which is similar to the cases mentioned above. On March 22, he was told to come to the PSS headquarters in Dura. There, the officer in charge yelled at Amayreh, accusing him of suggesting that Jibril Rajoub was taking orders from Ami Ayalon, head of the Israeli General Security Service (Shabak). Amayreh had reported on a meeting held between the two men, a meeting which was widely reported in local and international media. After threatening him with imprisonment, the officer told Amayreh to sign a document stating that there was no intent to "libel anybody or place someone in a bad light." The next day, two senior officials of the PA attacked Amayreh on a Voice of Palestine program. The officials were" Mutawikil Taha (Director-General of the PA Ministry of Information) and Jibril Rajub, who called Amayreh "insane." Such comments intimidate journalists, who fear being the target of official criticism and harassment.

During the celebrations in Ramallah after the release of the last women prisoners from Israeli prisons (Feb. 11), a journalist was pushed and threatened with arrest by members of Yasser Arafat's security guard. Arafat invited a group of journalists to enter a room with him. A security guard prevented the journalists from following. One of the journalist told the guard, "if you won't let me enter the room with Arafat, then I am going home and there won't be a story." The guard then called other guards to take the journalist aside. The guards took him aside, pushing and behaving in a threatening manner. After the intervention of other journalists, the situation was resolved peacefully. The incident is symptomatic of the contempt many members of the security forces hold for journalists.

When asked, most of our interviewees expressed the opinion that arrests are no longer necessary, since the effect of past intimidation has been successful in silencing criticism. In any case, journalists from the three major newspapers stated that they are aware of "red lines" that must be not be crossed for fear of reprisals from either management or the security forces.

Official Cooperation

Three daily newspapers are published in PA: Al Quds, AL Hayat al Jadia and al Ayyam. The situation of the daily press is an important tool for the assessment of the freedom of the press. Despite its weakened condition, the daily press remains the main channel for the distribution of information and opinion. AlQuds is of special significance, as it is the Palestinian "newspaper of record." According to the JMCC, public support for the three dailies is as follows: Al Quds: 52.6%, Al Ayyam: 8.6% and al Hayat al Jadida is 7.6%.

Various authorities practice censorship and unacceptable intervention in the press. All of this intervention is in violation of the press law quoted above, approved by Arafat in 1995. The authorities control the content of the daily press through existing relationships with the owners, publishers, and chief editors of the papers. This relationship is based on outright ownership of a newspaper by senior PA officials, participation of PA officials in senior positions on a newspaper staff, and in the case of AL Quds, a close relationship between the chief editor and PSS head Jibril Rajub.

The close connection between al Ayyam and al Hayat al Jadia and the PA seems to allow those publications more latitude and freedom to criticize than AL Quds. It seems as though newspapers openly connected to the PA are seen as loyal, and therefore permitted to criticize-within certain limits of course. Al Quds on the other hand, as the most veteran and respected newspaper, must be restrained since its relationship with the PA is based more on intimidation than on a close and voluntary relationship with the authorities.

Al Quds newspaper: PSS chief Jibril Rajub is in near daily contact with al Quds chief editor. The two men apparently coordinate with articles appear on the front page, and discuss the contents of stories that are critical of the PA, or one of its agencies. The PSS head has been witnessed at the offices of Al Quds numerous times in 1997. This close relationship has resulted in a situation in which the goals of the PA (as seen by the head of one of a powerful security agency) and the editorial perspective of the newspaper are hardly ever in conflict. The newspaper functions as a cheerleader to Arafat. There is little critical coverage on major political issues, or a stage for alternative views. The situation of the Palestinian press is quite similar to the semi-official Egyptian press.

The office of Arafat continues to interfere in the editorial process of newspapers. Editors are told which stories to include and where to place them. If "requests" made in the past were not honored, the presidential office often expresses dissatisfaction. In the case of stories that are known to the journalists, and show a serious problem with a security agency, senior official , or Arafat himself, the newspaper is asked to wait with the story until the situation is resolved. Then the story will include a resolution of the problem, thus turning originally critical news into a success story.

Self Censorship and cheerleading

The PA has been successful in instilling a climate of fear and intimidation on the community of journalists in Palestine. One result is the practice of self censorship by journalists and editors, along with the direct manipulation of the media described above.

This media manipulation is often hard to point at. An example is the coverage of political activity. In the three daily newspapers, during January-March 1997, one rarely sees mention of political activities of any organization except Fatah. Statements by opposition leaders are published routinely, as are paid advertisements announcing political events. Another example is the coverage of human rights violations by leaders, but without coverage of the violations themselves.The Al Baba case is an exception, as it was covered extensively.

The media in Palestine is not in a position to determine public opinion or to spark public action. Rather, these roles are played by leaders-in all levels of society-with the authority to dictate opinions and actions. The source of authority is mostly external, coming as it does through hierarchical channels. Journalists, as members of Palestinian society, are subjects within this political culture more than they are autonomous and independent actors. The exceptions to this merely prove the rule.

Palestinians Heave Sigh of Relief

Following Attorney-General's Sacking

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

Lawyers, judges and human rights activists, as well as many ordinary Palestinians, heaved a sigh of relief following the sacking of the widely despised Attorney-General Khalid al Kidra by Palestinian Authority Chairman Yasser Arafat on 6 June. Arafat reportedly fired al Kidra after the 70-year-old had "outlived his usefulness" and became an "intolerable liability," in the words of a PA Ministry of Justice official who asked for anonymity. "The man became an inseparable stigma attached to the judicial system of the Palestinian Authority. He had to be removed," intimated the same official.

On 6 June, the Palestinian Independent Commission for Citizens' Rights issued a statement expressing profound satisfaction at the sacking of al Kidra. The statement, captioned "Victory for Justice," said the presence of this man in the crucial post of the Attorney-General did an incalculable disservice to the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian people at large. "He always twisted the arm of the law, violated the letter and spirit of the law, made undue interference in court proceedings, concocted charges against innocent people, and, not the least, ignored and even defended human rights violations by PA security agencies."

"All this," the statement went on, "made him a premier obstacle impeding the administration of justice."

Al Kidra, a veteran Fatah activist who came to be hated by the Movement's younger members for his "excessive hypocrisy and sycophancy" is particularly blamed for the deterioration of Press freedom and expression within the PA enclaves. In 1995, he ordered the closure of the pro-Hamas weekly "al Watan" and its sister, "al Istiklal", which was published by the Islamic Jihad organization.

In both cases, al Kidra accused the two papers of "incitement" and "endangering paramount Palestinian national interests." Such vague charges were often evoked by al Kidra to justify and repress the frequent detentions without charge or unjustified trials of journalists, writers and human rights activists. "I never felt that he ever tried to uphold the law," said Ghazi Hamed, Editor of the Gaza-based Islamist weekly "al Risala".

Al Kidra was also unpopular within Fatah. During the intifada, he was shot in the leg by a disgruntled Fatah activist following widespread rumors that he embezzled large sums of money destined for young intifada activists. He has not fully recuperated from that injury.

Other critics opine that al Kidra did only what his ultimate boss, Yasser Arafat, told him to do. "His main duty was to appease and please Arafat, and, paradoxically, he ended up hurting and harming Arafat," said a clerk at Gaza municipal council who expressed satisfaction at al Kidra's dismissal.

For his part, al Kidra denied that he was sacked by Arafat, arguing that he submitted his resignation to Chairman Arafat for reasons having to do with his deteriorating health.

However, whether al Kidra resigned or was made to resign, his disappearance from the Attorney-General's office is a cause for relief among most Palestinians.

Hamas Receives Lion's Share of Student Vote in Jerusalem

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

The Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) achieved a big victory at the student elections on 16 June at al Quds University, winning 31 seats or 61% of the 51 contested seats which make up the student general assembly at the University. Four student blocs participated in the election, including "al Quds al Muslima" (Islamic Jerusalem), the pro-Fatah Shabiba, the leftist Action Front, and the moderate Islamic Independence bloc.

According to results announced on the night of 16 June, al Quds al Muslima, a coalition of Hamas and Islamic Jihad supporters, won 31 seats, followed by its chief rival, the Shabiba bloc, which won 16 seats. The remaining three seats went to the leftists.

According to Dr. Ali Khashan, who supervised the election, 70% of the student body at the college cast their ballot. Hamas achieved remarkable victories this year at universities and institutes of higher learning within the autonomous enclaves, which observers say reflects growing public disenchantment with Yasser Arafat's Palestinian Authority and the peace process as a whole. Moreover, many more Palestinians have come to lose confidence in the integrity of the PA following the disclosure two months ago that hundreds of millions of dollars of public money had been embezzled by high-ranking PA officials.

On 16 June, a public opinion poll in the West Bank and the Gaza Strip revealed that 52% of the Palestinian people thought the PA ministerial board had outlived its usefulness and should, therefore, resign immediately. Moreover, the survey, which was conducted between 5-7 June using a randomly-chosen sample of 1,317 respondents, showed that 63% of the Palestinians believed that corruption was rampant within the PA administrative hierarchy.

In the Gaza Strip, where the PA is believed to be stronger, a staggering 74% of the respondents said corruption was increasing. The survey was conducted by the Nablus-based Research Center, which is reportedly financed, at least partially, by the Palestinian Authority.

Outbreak of Meningitis Hits Gaza Strip

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Palestinian Authority health officials declared a state of emergency throughout the autonomous enclaves on 11 June in an effort to combat an acute outbreak of meningitis in the Gaza Strip and parts of the West Bank.

Riyad al Zanoun, who holds the health portfolio in the PA, revealed in a press conference on 11 June that as many as 350 cases of meningitis were hospitalized in the Gaza Strip during the past few days.

Meningitis occurs as a result of an inflammation of the three membranes that envelop the brain and spinal cord and is caused by either a viral or bacterial infection. Al Zanoun said 150 cases were treated and released after full recovery, while as many as 200 cases remained in hospitals for further treatment.

The Palestinian official exhorted the public not to panic, stressing that the disease was mostly of the relatively benign viral kind, not the more serious and potentially fatal bacterial variety. He called on the public to thoroughly wash and rinse fruits and vegetables and not to drink contaminated water.

Many Palestinian physicians voiced increasing pessimism at the deteriorating state of public health within some of the autonomous enclaves. Dr. Ghazi Ikteit, a General Practitioner in Dura (about 12 miles south-west of Hebron) disclosed that he had been recently receiving scores of sick children suffering from various kinds of infections in their digestive systems. Ikteit opined that the infections resulted for the most part from drinking contaminated water.

Dura, a town of 25,000, reportedly has been having an acute water shortage this summer, forcing townspeople to fetch water from exposed and antiquated wells dating back to the Ottoman era. The town's mayor, Mahmud al Nammura, admitted that the municipal council was completely powerless to solve the problem. "The Israelis are stealing our water and they control the tap, and the Palestinian Authority can't help us, hence we are going thirsty; this is the problem," complained al Nammura with a tone of frustration and hopelessness in his voice.

Injured Palestinian children find hope through Relief Group

By Ian Lucas

Jamal Shaheen was a normal 12-year-old boy playing in a park in the West Bank town of Nablus when he saw a soda can nearby. Picking it up, he noticed a white powder-like substance inside. "When I looked closely, it exploded," Jamal recalls. The blast amputated both hands and took an eye, leaving the boy permanently disabled.

Last year, an American relief group called the Palestine Children's Relief Fund heard of Jamal's situation from a doctor who saw him, in Nablus. They contacted his family and learned that Jamal was not receiving the treatment he needed. Though a bright boy, Jamal still needed quality artificial hands and occupational therapy to teach him how to use them.

In March, the P.C.R.F. brought Jamal and Nourhan Matar, a 5-year-old burned refugee girl from the Gaza Strip for treatment in the U.S. Nourhan was treated in Florida while Jamal was sent for treatment in Philadelphia. Working with the Arab community, Jamal has been well supported socially and medically in the Shriners Hospital.

Jamal is only one of many Palestinian children that have benefitted from free medical care in the U.S. Since 1990, the P.C.R.F. has brought over 80 injured children from Palestine and Lebanon for treatment in the U.S. "We have helped to treat gunshot victims from the Intifada, bombing victims from camps in Lebanon, kids with terrible burns and children born with diseases and need corrective surgery," says PCRF vice-president and co-founder Sue Nasir. "We have been fortunate to be able to do something positive for Palestine at a period when we know only suffering and oppression."

Indeed, from the American doctors and hospitals who provide the children free treatment to the host families who house them while they are being treated, the P.C.R.F. depends on the humanitarian spirit of many people concerned. "When you approach people to help on a humanitarian level, most are happy for the chance to help a suffering child," says Nasir. "People don't see these kids as Palestinian or Arabs but as human beings who need help. We have shown the Americans that we care about our own children and also that we are victims of occupation, not the terrorists that the media makes us out to be."

The P.C.R.F. was co-founded by Steve Sosebee, an American journalist who was covering the story of a family in Hebron who had been bombed by passing Israeli soldiers. "When I saw the boy in the hospital without legs and a hand, I had to do something to help him,'' says the young American. "I felt guilty for my government's behavior in the Middle East, and as a writer I felt my efforts to expose what was really happening in Palestine were futile. By arranging treatment for this boy in America, I at least felt I did something to assist those who have given their blood for freedom."

Sosebee did more than just help a single boy from Hebron. Seeing many other children in need, he co-founded in 1991 the Palestine Children's Relief Fund with Sue Nasir to start bringing children who could not get adequate care in the Middle East. "I saw so many children who were injured and not being treated there," Sosebee recalls. "The P.C.R.F. was established as a way to fill this void in the relief work for Palestinians. No one was helping the individual cases who could not be treated there."

Since 1991, the P.C.R.F. has been the only organization sending injured Arab youth to the U.S. for free medical care. In 1994, the P.C.R.F. expanded to include injured children in war-torn Lebanon. Many Lebanese and Palestinian children were in need of care outside of the country and the P.C.R.F. has already treated a dozen such children in the U.S. More injured youths from Israeli bombings in south Lebanon are coming for treatment later this year.

In addition to treating injured Arab children, the P.C.R.F. also ships of medical supplies and equipment to institutions in Palestine and Lebanon. "We want to do what we can to improve the quality of medical care so no children need to travel for treatment," says Sosebee. "But until that day, we will remain devoted to the individual kids who cannot be treated in their homeland."

This summer, the P.C.R.F. has several injured youths going to the U.S. for treatment. These include a 9-year-old boy shot in the head by soldiers, a 14-yearold boy who lost his legs from an Israeli bomb, a 15-year-old girl whose parents were killed and she was burned in an Israeli attack in south Lebanon and other children in need of medical care that is not available to them in the Middle East. The P.C.R.F. also has several shipments of medical supplies to go to the Middle East in the coming months. They are seeking volunteers and support for these efforts.

Conversion Bill May Endanger Israeli Government

Occupied Jerusalem- PT

A brewing controversy in Israel over "who is a Jew" may endanger the very survival of the Israeli government, Israeli sources said on Monday, 16 June. The controversy is centred around a proposed draft law that would recognize as legitimate those conversions to Judaism performed by Reformist and Conservative Rabbis, which has been vehemently opposed by the influential Orthodox Rabbinate as well as most religious and ultra-religious political parties in Israel.

On Sunday, 15 June, leaders of the Reformist and Conservative sects met extensively with Israeli politicians from across the political and religious spectrum, including Prime Minister Benyamin Netanyahu, in an effort to expedite the passing of the conversion bill to rectify the erstwhile inferior religious status of those affected individuals in Israel. While Netanyahu refused to appear as siding with the "heretic"

Reformists and Conservatives, he nevertheless reportedly promised to see to it that a compromise acceptable to "all parties" be worked out.

Earlier, Israeli Trade and Industry Minister, Natan Sharansky (currently facing charges of connections with the powerful Russian mafia) vowed to quit the government if the conversion bill was not passed. Netanyahu met with Sharansky on 16 June in an attempt to resolve the matter, but reportedly to no avail. Sharansky had argued that failure to pass the bill would cause a spilt between Israel and the world, especially American Jews, the bulk of whom are affiliated with the Reformist and Conservative sects.

According to Reformist leaders, Netanyahu is seeking to produce an "impossible formula" whereby conversions by non-Orthodox Rabbis would be recognized as legitimate while at the same time enable the Orthodox establishment to retain its veto power over the conversion issue.

According to Reuven Hammer, head of the Rabbinical Assembly of Israel, the Netanyahu government would work out a formula by which Reformist and Conservative converts can be registered as Jews, but would, at the same time, give the Orthodox camp the feeling that these people were not exactly registered as Jews.

During the second week of June, hostilities between the Orthodox and the non-Orthodox camps developed into a near show-down when ultra-religious activists spat on and shouted "Nazis" at a group of Reformist and Conservative Jews who were praying at the Wailing Wall in East Jerusalem. Eventually, the non-Orthodox Jews were forced to flee the site after police failed to protect the group from the wrath of Orthodox hoodlums who hurled stones at the "heretics" as well as "Goyem" Arabs nearby.

The incident, not unprecedented, was half-heartedly condemned by Israeli officials. Premier Netanyahu described it as "terrible" but refused to denounce the perpetrators and their supporters.

Palestinians: Conditions at Concentration Camp Reached Explosion Point

Occupied Jerusalem-PT

Conditions at the Meggido detention camp near Jenin, where nearly 600 Palestinians are detained by Israel as "hostages," have reached the explosion point, a report issued by the Jerusalem-based Prisoner Club revealed last month.

The report disclosed that Israeli "Special Forces" stormed the detention camp several times in the last few weeks, severely beating up detainees who were protesting draconian Israeli measures at the camp. According to the report, which was published in leading Arabic newspapers in the territories, scores of prisoners and the so-called "administrative detainees" were either injured or suffered from gas suffocation during the storming of the camp by an IDF crack unit.

Israeli troops reportedly raided the camp at least three times in the last three months, with the most violent and savage attack taking place on 19 March, resulting in as many as 20 injuries among the detainees.

According to information smuggled from the maximum-security camp, Israeli soldiers in full combat gear stormed detainee tents, beating them "in a bestial manner," and setting their clothes and belongings on fire. Moreover, Israeli soldiers equipped with gas masks threw as many as 50 gas bombs throughout the camp, causing asphyxiation to many detainees. Five detainees reportedly fainted, and one, who apparently had cardiac problems, collapsed unconscious consequently.

The Prisoner Club report blamed the Palestinian Authority for not paying sufficient attention to the detainees cause.

"We, once again, appeal to Chairman Yasser Arafat to take immediate action to save our brothers from the claws of Israeli savagery and barbarism," said the report. The report pointed out that the Israeli army sought to conceal its "genocidal and disgraceful practices against the defenseless detainees by cutting off the camp from the outside world."

On Tuesday, 17 June, the Israeli Prison Authority issued a statement admitting that conditions at Meggido were not "idealistic." The statement said that hundreds of administrative detainees (held without charge or trial) were being transferred to other prisons.

Meggido Prison Chief Nera'am Gold-Brom told the Hebrew daily "Ha'aretz" on 17 June that "substandard conditions contributed to disturbances and the death of several inmates at the camp." Gold-Brom also revealed that 592 detainees were being detained at Meggido; 66% of them are affiliated with the Islamic Resistance Movement (Hamas) and the rest are associated with Fatah and other secular Palestinian movements.

Hebron - Sources of the Palestinian Prisoner's Friends Society revealed that Israel had been detaining Emad Zakzouk since June 1995 although it had announced his death two years ago.
