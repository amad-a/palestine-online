Press Cuttings

 

The New York Times, October 24, 1998
The Middle East peace agreement gives the CIA a new and potentially risky role in safeguarding security for Israel and the Palestinian Authority. The pact empowers intelligence operatives to referee disputes involving the handling of suspected terrorists, to monitor who is arrested and jailed by Israeli and Palestinian authorities and to help establish sophisticated and reliable border checkpoints. While the CIA has quietly been performing many of these tasks for the last three years, Friday's accord brings those covert activities in from the cold and thrusts the CIA into the center of the Middle East peace effort, making the CIA responsible for monitoring and verifying the toughest provisions of the agreement.



The Los Angeles Times, Saturday, October 24, 1998
Settlers have threatened to work to topple Netanyahu's coalition over withdrawal from the West Bank, holding angry demonstrations for the last several days. "We are not going to give up and sit quiet," said Benny Kashriel, the mayor of Maale Adumim, a settlement in the West Bank just east of Jerusalem. "Netanyahu told us he would stand strong against American pressure, and he did not." Aharon Domb, a settler leader, was
even angrier, using language reminiscent of rhetoric used against then-Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin before his 1995 assassination by a Jewish extremist. "The agreement emerging in Washington is an agreement of betrayal of the ideal and the people who sent Netanyahu to the head of the government," he told Israel Radio.

The Guardian, October 24, 1998
Binyamin Netanyahu denounced the Oslo agreement within days of its signing in 1993 as "an enormous lie". He was against the peace process when he was in opposition, and twisted it once he reached office. Since he became Israel's prime minister the basic problem has been that Netanyahu, who alone could deliver what was necessary for peace on the Israeli side, has not wished to do so. During more than a week of frenetic and sometimes histrionic negotiations in the United States, Netanyahu has not shifted from his basic strategy, which is that the peace process as originally envisaged should not and will not be carried through. There should therefore be no illusions about the agreement which has been reached in the Maryland hills, no talk of compromises and still less of breakthroughs.

Ha'aretz, October 23, 1998
Relations between Hamas and the Palestinian Authority, including the personal relationship of Arafat and Sheikh Yassin, have seriously deteriorated in the past year. When Sheikh Yassin was in prison, and when he was freed a year ago, Arafat treated the Hamas leader with great respect. Following his release, the Sheikh toured Arab capitals and was given a king's welcome, arousing Arafat's jealousy. During his visits to the Arab capitals, the Sheikh made disparaging comments about the Palestinian leadership.. Two Hamas leaders, Dr. Abdel Aziz Rantisi and Dr. Ibrahim Maqadmeh, were arrested by Arafat's people and have been in jail ever since. They are not alone. Dozens more (some say more than 100) Hamas activists have been put in Palestinian prisons without trials.

Ha'aretz, October 23, 1998
Netanyahu expressed concern that those who supports an agreement despite the need to give up parts of the homeland and its attendant dangers, would revolt to the point of refusing to serve in the army when the next hostilities with the Palestinians flare up. He added that "the number of people who refuse to serve will grow to an unprecedented size." At another meeting with the settler leadership late on the Saturday before his departure, Netanyahu said that if it is inevitable that the
government fall now, he would prefer that it be the right that topples it rather than the left. He added that the public opinion polls show that he has a good chance of being re-elected.

The Boston Globe, October 22, 1998
Arafat, who has denounced Netanyahu for years, sent flowers for the Israeli leader's birthday, but the two did not talk face to face. After Netanyahu said he was considering leaving, Arafat called him and appealed to keep the talks going and reach an agreement, Palestinian officials said. Officials said the Israeli ultimatum could have been just posturing designed to shake up the Palestinians and Americans. Just as important, Americans familiar with the talks said, Netanyahu has to play to a small group of hard-liners back home who demand that he snub Clinton and hold on to the remaining portions of the West Bank.
